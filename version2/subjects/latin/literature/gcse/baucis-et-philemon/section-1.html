Content — what is happening
  • Jupiter and Mercury arrive in Phrygia in human disguise — Jupiter in mortal form, Mercury without his winged sandals. The scene is set for a test of hospitality.
  • They approach a thousand houses seeking shelter. Every one rejects them — the doors are shut and bolted against them.
  • One small, humble cottage receives them: thatched with reeds, the home of an elderly couple, Baucis and Philemon, who have lived and grown old there together.
  • The couple's equality is introduced: they share everything, obey each other and command each other — the perfect image of a harmonious marriage rooted in simple contentment.
Style — language & effect
Iuppiter Jupiter Initial Position
The first word of the passage is the king of the gods. Ovid announces divine presence immediately — this is not a story about ordinary travellers. Placing Iuppiter emphatically at the start mirrors the fanfare of epic openings and signals that what follows will be extraordinary.
In an exam: "The emphatic initial position of Iuppiter immediately establishes the divine presence and signals the theoxeny (divine visitation) motif that will structure the narrative."
Atlantiadēs descendant of Atlas Patronymic
Mercury is named not as Mercury but as "descendant of Atlas" — a Greek-style patronymic borrowed from Homer. Ovid uses it to elevate the register: this is proper epic diction, not domestic storytelling. The elaborate circumlocution also emphasises that even in disguise, these are figures of immense divine lineage.
In an exam: "The patronymic Atlantiadēs employs epic naming conventions, lending the domestic narrative an elevated, Homeric register while identifying Mercury through his divine genealogy."
caducifer wand-bearing Compound Epithet
A compound adjective — "wand-carrying" — that immediately identifies Mercury through his famous attribute, the caduceus. These compound epithets are a hallmark of epic style. The word is doing double duty: naming Mercury and keeping the elevated register Ovid has established with Atlantiadēs.
In an exam: "The compound epithet caducifer identifies Mercury through his divine attribute while employing a typically poetic word-formation that elevates the register."
positīs ālīs having set aside his wings Ablative Absolute
An ablative absolute compressing a whole subordinate clause into two words. Mercury has removed his winged sandals to pass as mortal — the detail is economically embedded in the grammar. The effect is deliberate concealment: the gods are incognito, and only the reader knows who is really standing at each door.
In an exam: "The ablative absolute positīs ālīs economically conveys the god's transformation, suggesting conscious concealment of divine markers for the mortal disguise."
mīlle domōs… mīlle domōs a thousand houses… a thousand houses Anaphora
The same phrase — mīlle domōs — opens line 3 and recurs at line 4, but with opposite verbs: adiēre (approached) and clausēre (shut). The repetition makes the universal rejection feel crushing and comprehensive. The same thousand houses that were sought refused entry. The parallelism of the language mirrors the parallelism of the situation.
In an exam: "The anaphora of mīlle domōs emphasises the comprehensive rejection the disguised gods face, highlighting the community's failure of hospitality through emphatic repetition."
adiēre they approached Syncopated Perfect
A shortened (syncopated) perfect — adiērunt compressed to adiēre for metrical elegance. These syncopated forms are a feature of Latin poetry, fitting the hexameter more smoothly. The same technique appears with clausēre and cōnsenūēre later in the passage.
In an exam: "The syncopated perfect adiēre maintains the elevated poetic register characteristic of Ovidian epic narrative."
tamen however Pivotal Conjunction
A single word carries the entire narrative pivot. After the crushing repetition of a thousand rejections, tamen signals everything changes. One house was different. The brevity of the pivot — just one word — makes it more powerful: Ovid creates the turn without fanfare, trusting the word to do its work.
In an exam: "The adversative tamen marks the crucial narrative pivot from universal rejection to singular welcome, creating suspense and highlighting the exceptional hospitality to follow."
parva quidem small, it is true Concessive
Ovid is upfront about the cottage's poverty — parva quidem acknowledges the smallness directly. The concessive quidem ("admittedly, it is true") prepares the reader for a contrast: physical modesty will shortly be offset by moral generosity. The honesty about the cottage's limitations makes the warmth inside it more striking.
In an exam: "The emphatic parva quidem acknowledges material poverty while the concessive quidem prepares for the moral contrast — physical modesty paired with spiritual abundance."
pia dutiful / pious Moral Epithet
Pia is the highest Roman moral commendation — it encompasses duty to the gods, to family, and to guests. Applying it to Baucis at the moment of her introduction tells the reader immediately: this woman is good. Her pietas is precisely what will earn divine favour when the impious neighbours face punishment.
In an exam: "The epithet pia immediately establishes Baucis's moral character. Her pietas — duty to gods, family, and guests — contrasts with the impietas of the hostile neighbours and foreshadows her salvation."
Baucis… Philēmōn Baucis… Philemon Named Subjects
After the anonymity of "a thousand houses," Ovid finally gives us names. These two individuals — with their Greek Phrygian names — stand out precisely because everyone else has been faceless. Naming them personalises the story and signals their importance: they are not background figures but the moral centre of the poem.
In an exam: "The naming of Baucis and Philēmōn individualises the couple against the anonymous mīlle domōs. The Greek names provide local colour for this Phrygian setting."
parīlī aetāte equal in age Ablative of Description
Two words conveying a lifetime of partnership. They have been together since youth — matched in age, matched in everything. The ablative of description packs biographical detail into the briefest possible space, which is characteristic of Ovid's economy. The equality implied here anticipates the final line's antithesis.
In an exam: "The ablative of description parīlī aetāte economically establishes the couple's lifelong partnership, emphasising their shared journey and mutual devotion."
illā… illā in that cottage… in that cottage Anaphora
The same word repeated — that cottage, that cottage — drums home the couple's rootedness. They married there, they grew old there, their whole life has been in this one small place. The anaphora creates a rhythm of contentment: no restlessness, no ambition, just two people in one unchanged space.
In an exam: "The anaphora illā… illā emphasises the cottage as the complete setting for their shared life, reinforcing themes of constancy and humble contentment."
cōnsenūēre they grew old together Compound Verb
The con- prefix ("together, jointly") carries the emotional weight. They did not just age — they aged as one. A single syncopated perfect verb — compressed from cōnsenūērunt — quietly contains their entire shared history. It is one of Ovid's most economical strokes of characterisation.
In an exam: "The compound cōnsenūēre, with its con- prefix emphasising joint action, encapsulates the couple's lifelong partnership in a single syncopated perfect."
pārentque iubentque they both obey and command Antithesis
The closing words of the introduction are a paradox: they obey and command simultaneously. In a Roman household these roles were rigidly separate — masters ordered, servants obeyed. Baucis and Philemon have dissolved that hierarchy. The -que… -que polysyndeton links two opposites into one seamless relationship. It is a witty, epigrammatic summary of a happy marriage.
In an exam: "The antithetical pairing pārentque iubentque, linked by polysyndeton, creates a memorable paradox encapsulating the couple's egalitarian relationship and closes the introduction with epigrammatic wit."
