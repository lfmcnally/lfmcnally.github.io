Greece
The sanctuary and the temple
The sanctuary
  • a sanctuary was a holy area in a city or town containing a temple and an altar dedicated to a god or gods
  • the sanctuary was usually separated from the rest of the city by a wall
  • a sanctuary could contain temples and altars to many gods — it did not have to be dedicated to one deity alone
  • sanctuaries often had a fresh water source at the entrance for worshippers to wash before entering
  • it was important that holy sites were not polluted — pollution discouraged the favour of the gods
  • a person could be polluted by: contact with a dead person, contact with a new-born baby, or having committed homicide
  • pollution could last several days or be removed immediately with a wash
The function of a Greek temple
  • a temple was a building dedicated to a god — its primary purpose was to house the cult statue of that deity
  • it is best understood as a house for the god — a residence on earth for when the god left Mount Olympus
  • only priests were allowed inside the temple itself — the majority of worshippers never entered the building
  • the altar stood outside the temple; this was where sacrifice and offering took place — the most communal act of worship
  • during festivals the altar could involve the whole city
The temple building
  • temples came in many sizes but usually followed the same rectangular plan
  • most were orientated east to west — the entrance faced east, towards the rising sun
  • the temple stood on a solid foundation plinth which also provided steps up to the building, raising it above ground level
  • the colonnade ran around the outside of the temple, supporting the roof
  • the cella (or naos) was the main room housing the cult statue
  • the opisthodomos was a rear room used to store the city's treasures and offerings to the gods
The sanctuary and temple together created a carefully managed boundary between the human and divine worlds. The water at the entrance, the wall separating the sanctuary from the city, the restriction of the inner temple to priests alone — all of these control who gets close to the god and in what condition. Pollution was not a moral failing but a ritual state: it could be removed, but it had to be taken seriously. The altar outside, not the temple inside, was where most Greeks actually worshipped — the temple was the god's house, not a place of congregational worship.

Greece
Temple decoration
Where temples were decorated
  • the two main areas of decoration on a Greek temple were the pediments and the friezes
  • sculpture could relate to the city in which the temple was built or to the Greeks as a whole civilisation
  • the choice of decoration was always deliberate — it made a statement about the god, the city, and its values
Pediments
  • the pediment was the triangular space at either end of a temple
  • it could contain sculpture or be left blank
  • the triangular shape created a challenge for sculptors — figures had to decrease in size towards the corners; some sculptors used reclining figures or animals to fill the narrow ends naturally
The Ionic frieze
  • a continuous strip of stone or marble running all the way around the temple
  • good for telling a continuous story or including many characters in one narrative
  • exemplified in the Parthenon — the Ionic frieze ran 540 feet around the entire building
The Doric frieze
  • a continuous strip of stone divided into square spaces called metopes
  • each metope contained its own individual scene — good for showing several stories or several scenes from one story
  • exemplified in the Parthenon (outer colonnade) and the Temple of Zeus at Olympia (twelve labours of Heracles)
The choice between Ionic and Doric frieze was not just aesthetic — it was narrative. A Doric frieze with individual metopes suits a series of self-contained episodes (the twelve labours). An Ionic frieze suits a single unfolding scene (the Panathenaic procession on the Parthenon). The pediment suited myths with a clear central moment — a god standing in judgement, a hero at the peak of his action. Every surface of a Greek temple that could carry sculpture did carry sculpture, and every choice of subject was deliberate.

Greece
The Parthenon — prescribed source
Prescribed source facts view temple
The Parthenon · Acropolis, Athens
  • Date: 447–432 BC
  • Commissioned by: Pericles
  • Architect: notably Phidias, Iktinos, and Kallikrates
  • Dedicated to: Athena Parthenos ("the Virgin")
  • Material: marble (Pentelic marble from nearby Mount Pentelicus)
  • Style: Doric (outer colonnade) and Ionic (inner frieze — unusual combination) view capital
    Doric capital — the plainest of the orders
  • Location: the Acropolis, Athens
  • Function: temple and treasury (the opisthodomos housed the Athenian Empire's treasury)
  • Significance: the building and its decoration symbolised the power, wealth, and piety of Athens
Context — why it was built
  • in 480 BC the Persians sacked Athens and burned the Acropolis, destroying the existing religious buildings
  • after the Persian Wars, Athens became the head of the Delian League — a naval alliance that effectively became an Athenian Empire
  • the leading politician Pericles moved the League's treasury from the island of Delos to Athens and used the money to fund a massive building programme
  • the focal point of this programme was the Acropolis — already a sanctuary dedicated to Athena
  • the Parthenon was the centrepiece: a statement of Athens' power, piety, and cultural supremacy
Plan
  • the Parthenon was enormous: 69.5 metres long and 30.9 metres wide
  • it shared the features of a standard Greek temple: orientated east to west; stood on a three-stepped base; surrounded by a colonnade; had a naos and opisthodomos
  • its unique feature: behind the external colonnade stood another row of columns supporting an Ionic frieze — an unusual addition that increased the grandeur of the building and provided another surface for sculpture
Decoration — the pediments
  • the pediments are heavily fragmented; we rely on Pausanias (second century AD) for their subject matter
  • Eastern pediment (above the entrance): the birth of Athena — springing fully armed from the head of Zeus; chosen because it celebrated Athens' patron goddess at the most prominent point of the temple
  • Western pediment: the contest between Athena and Poseidon for possession of Athens — Athena planted an olive tree, Poseidon struck the ground with his trident and produced a saltwater spring; the gods judged in Athena's favour
  • you will not be shown images of the original pediments in the exam as they are too fragmented — but you must know the stories and why they were chosen
Decoration — the friezes
  • Outer Doric metopes: ninety-two metopes on the outer colonnade depicting four mythological battles — the Gigantomachy (gods vs giants), the Amazonomachy (Greeks vs Amazons), the Centauromachy (Lapiths vs Centaurs), and scenes from the Trojan War; all represent the victory of civilisation over barbarism view metope
    Parthenon Doric metope · a Lapith and a Centaur
  • Inner Ionic frieze: 540 feet long, running around the entire building; depicted the Panathenaic procession — the great festival of Athena held every four years in Athens; showed gods, humans, animals, and chariots in a continuous scene view frieze
    Parthenon Ionic frieze · the Panathenaic procession
The cult statue of Athena view reconstruction
Athena Parthenos · modern reconstruction of Phidias's cult statue
  • inside the cella stood a 13-metre statue of Athena designed by the architect Phidias, added around 438 BC
  • it was a chryselephantine statue — made of gold and ivory attached to a wooden frame (chrysos = gold; elephantine = ivory); the ivory represented her skin, the gold her clothing
  • Pausanias describes it: upright, wearing a tunic to her feet; a Sphinx on the helmet; griffins in relief on either side; the head of Medusa on her breast in ivory; holding a statue of Victory; a spear in the other hand; a shield at her feet; a serpent near the spear
  • the statue no longer exists — chryselephantine statues were made in parts so they could be melted down if the city needed money; the Victory in her hand was solid gold and was melted down in times of financial crisis and replaced in prosperity
  • the same sculptor, Phidias, also designed the cult statue of Zeus at Olympia
The Parthenon was never simply a temple. It was a treasury, a war memorial, a political statement, and a monument to Athenian identity all at once. The decoration makes this clear: the birth of Athena and the contest with Poseidon assert Athens's divine connections; the mythological battles on the metopes frame Athens as the defender of civilisation against barbarism — a reference to the recent Persian Wars as much as to ancient myth. The Panathenaic procession on the inner frieze brings the living city into the temple itself. Every surface tells the same story: Athens is chosen, Athens is powerful, Athens is pious.

The Temple of Zeus at Olympia — prescribed source
Prescribed source facts view reconstruction
Reconstruction of the Temple of Zeus at Olympia
  • Date: 472–456 BC (begun 472 BC; completed 456 BC; cult statue added 448 BC)
  • Architect: Libon
  • Dedicated to: Zeus
  • Material: local limestone (not marble — unlike the Parthenon)
  • Style: Doric view capital
    Doric capital — the plainest of the orders
  • Location: the Altis — the sacred area at the centre of Olympia containing its religious buildings
  • Function: temple and treasury
  • Significance: the building and its decoration symbolised the importance of Zeus, Heracles, Pelops, and the Greeks
The altar — older than the temple
  • the Altar of Zeus at Olympia predates the temple by around 200 years — it was present from at least 776 BC when the Olympic Games were believed to have been founded
  • this shows the relative importance of the altar over the temple in Greek religion — worship happened at the altar, not inside the building
  • Pausanias describes the altar: built from the ash of thigh bones of animals sacrificed to Zeus, daubed with a paste of ash and water from the river Alpheius; by Pausanias's day it was 7 metres high — accumulated over roughly 925 years of sacrifice
  • the main sacrifice at the Olympics was a hecatomb — 100 oxen — held on the third day of the games
  • only men could ascend to the highest part of the altar; even outside the festival, private individuals and the Eleans sacrificed there daily
Plan view plan
Floor plan — Temple of Zeus at Olympia
  • the temple was 64.1 metres long and 27.7 metres wide — smaller than the Parthenon (69.5m)
  • it shared the features of a standard Greek temple: orientated east to west; stood on a three-stepped base; surrounded by a colonnade; had a naos and opisthodomos
  • built in local limestone rather than marble — plastered over to give a smooth finish
Decoration — the eastern pediment view pediment
East pediment · Oenomaus, Pelops & Zeus · Olympia Museum
  • depicts the myth of Oenomaus and Pelops — the chariot race that led to the founding of the Olympic Games (see 1.2)
  • Zeus stands at the centre as judge — appropriate for the god of justice at his own temple
  • on either side: the elder King Oenomaus and the younger Pelops; beside them the key female figures — Hippodamia and Sterope; then horses; reclining figures at the corners representing Olympia's two great rivers, the Kladeos and Alpheios
  • the sculptor uses the natural body shape to follow the triangular shape of the pediment
Decoration — the western pediment view pediment
West pediment · the Centauromachy, Apollo at the centre · Olympia Museum
  • depicts the Centauromachy — the mythical battle between the Lapiths and Centaurs
  • the myth represented the victory of civilisation over barbarism — a popular motif in temple architecture (also on the Parthenon metopes)
  • Apollo stands at the centre of the western pediment, his arm outstretched
Decoration — the metopes
  • the twelve labours of Heracles are sculpted onto twelve metopes — six above the front entrance, six above the back
  • their presence on a temple dedicated to Zeus emphasises Heracles's connection to Zeus and to the site of Olympia (see 1.2 for detail on individual metopes)
The cult statue of Zeus view reconstruction
Zeus at Olympia · reconstruction of Phidias's cult statue
  • inside the temple stood a 13-metre chryselephantine statue of Zeus, designed by Phidias — the same sculptor as the Athena in the Parthenon
  • Pausanias describes it: Zeus seated on a throne; a garland of olive shoots on his head; a Victory of ivory and gold in his right hand; a sceptre ornamented with every kind of metal in his left hand, topped with an eagle; sandals of gold; robes of gold embroidered with animals and lilies
  • in front of the statue was a pool of olive oil — this maintained the ivory and created a shimmering reflection of light on the statue
  • the statue no longer exists — like the Athena, it was a chryselephantine statue and was eventually melted down
The Temple of Zeus is a useful comparison with the Parthenon because they are so similar in form but so different in effect. Both are Doric temples, both have chryselephantine cult statues by Phidias, both use their decoration to tell stories about their patron deity and the values of the site. But the Parthenon is built in gleaming Pentelic marble at enormous expense as a statement of imperial power; the Temple of Zeus is built in local limestone at a sanctuary whose most important feature — the altar — predated the temple by two centuries. At Olympia, the building was secondary to the worship. At Athens, the building was the statement.

Greece
Priests and religious officials
Priestshiereus and hiereia
  • Greek priests were called a hiereus (pl. hiereis); priestesses a hiereia — the name means "one who sacrifices to a god"
  • the priesthood was a temporary civic role — no special training was required; some served for a single festival, some for a year, others for life
  • a priest's job was to perform the correct ritual at the correct time and maintain the temple — not to preach, teach, or provide pastoral care
  • gods were usually attended by priests; goddesses by priestesses — with exceptions (the temple of Apollo at Delphi had both)
  • a Greek citizen would hope to serve as priest of their local god at least once in their lifetime — it was a mark of civic commitment
The mantis
  • a mantis (pl. manteis) was a Greek soothsayer — a person who could predict the outcome of future events
  • they read omens in two main ways: the entrails of sacrificed animals, and the flight of birds (augury)
  • if any blemishes or imperfections were found in the entrails, the signs were bad
  • the mantis was so important that one would accompany an army before battle to read the omens
  • some manteis built careers on consistent accuracy — the more reliably they predicted, the more closely they were followed
The Greek priest was not a spiritual leader in any modern sense. They did not guide the community's morality or interpret scripture. Their role was entirely practical — perform the right ritual at the right moment and maintain the god's house. The mantis served a different function: they bridged the gap between the human world and the divine by reading the signs the gods sent. Together, the priest and the mantis covered the two directions of the relationship with the gods — the priest spoke to the gods through ritual; the mantis listened to what the gods were saying back.

Blood sacrifice
Overview view vase
A Greek sacrifice scene on a vase
  • the main offering to the gods was a blood sacrifice — the ritual killing of an animal
  • offerings could also include gifts of money, songs, and war booty — but animal sacrifice was the most important
  • the animal had to be the right one for the god — different gods required different animals; wealthier occasions meant larger animals
  • the most expensive sacrifice was the hecatomb — at least 100 oxen, performed at major festivals such as the Great Panathenaia and the Olympic Games; at approximately 8,000 drachmae (34 kilograms of silver) this was no small expense
  • if anything went wrong during the sacrifice, the whole process had to be restarted — a frightened animal was a bad omen
Stage 1Preparation
  • participants wore best clothes and garlands of leaves or twigs
  • the animal's horns were gilded if it had any; attendants led it to the altar
  • a maiden carried a basket containing barley grain — and hidden beneath it, the sacrificial knife
  • musicians played to reduce the chance of the animal taking fright — a scared animal meant starting again
  • once at the sanctuary, participants cleansed themselves with water and gathered around the altar; the fire was lit
  • the chief sacrificer poured water on the animal's head to make it nod — its apparent consent was essential
  • the chief sacrificer uttered a prayer to the god; participants threw their grain into the fire to confirm their participation
Stage 2The kill
  • the chief sacrificer took the knife from the basket and removed some of the animal's hairs, throwing them into the fire
  • the animal's throat was cut; if large, a bowl collected the blood; if small, the animal was held above the altar so the blood poured directly onto it
  • women performed a ritual scream at the moment of death — marking the animal's passage from life to death
Stage 3Sharing the sacrifice
  • none of the animal went to waste
  • the god's portion — thigh bones wrapped in fat — was burned on the altar while wine was poured on the fire; this is the portion the gods received because of the myth of Prometheus, who tricked Zeus into choosing the bones over the meat
  • the entrails were read for omens by the mantis
  • the remaining meat was cooked and shared among participants first, then the wider community
  • meat was rarely eaten in the ordinary Greek diet — the sacrifice was one of the few occasions most Greeks ate it at all
  • the animal's skin was given to the sanctuary
The sacrifice was not simply about killing an animal for a god. It was a carefully managed ritual that fed the community, created a shared moment of contact between the human and divine worlds, and involved the whole city when held at a festival. The requirement that the animal appear to consent, the hidden knife, the ritual scream — every detail has a purpose. A frightened animal means the gods are not pleased; a smooth, willing sacrifice means the relationship is in good order. The communal sharing of the meat after the ritual bound the participants together as much as it honoured the god.

Rome
Roman temples
The sanctuary
  • Roman sanctuaries followed the same basic principle as Greek ones — a holy area separated from the city containing a temple and altar
  • the sanctuary could have temples and altars to many gods
  • a fresh water source at the entrance allowed worshippers to purify themselves before entering
  • pollution was taken seriously — it could anger the gods
The function of a Roman temple
  • like Greek temples, a Roman temple was primarily a house for the god — it housed the cult statue
  • unlike the Greeks, Roman priests were not assigned to a specific temple or god — a key difference
  • the altar was the most important part of the sanctuary for the general public — all sacrifice and offering took place there
  • the altar could exist in its own right without a temple (e.g. the Ara Pacis)
The temple buildingsimilarities and differences with Greece
  • Roman temple design was based on a mix of Etruscan and Greek styling
  • like Greek temples, Roman temples were usually orientated east to west
  • key difference: a Roman temple stood on a podium (a high platform) with steps only at the front — you could only enter from the front; Greek temples had steps on all four sides
  • the colonnade stood on the podium and ran around the temple — but the side and rear columns were semi-engaged (half-embedded in the wall) rather than free-standing; only the front columns were fully free-standing
  • the main room was the cella, containing the cult statue
  • the focus of the Roman temple was therefore strongly on the front façade — it was designed to be seen head-on, not walked around
The podium is the most important difference between Greek and Roman temples. A Greek temple could be approached and viewed from all sides — its colonnade was free-standing all the way round. A Roman temple directed your approach: the podium meant there was only one way in, and the semi-engaged columns at the sides and rear meant the building was designed to be seen from the front. Roman temples were about display and hierarchy as much as worship — the front of the temple faced the forum or street, presenting itself to the city.

The Temple of Portunus and the Pantheon — prescribed sources
The Temple of Portunusprescribed source facts view temple
Temple of Portunus · Forum Boarium, Rome
  • Date: 120–80 BC
  • Dedicated to: Portunus, god of harbours (earlier wrongly called the Temple of Fortuna Virilis — "manly fortune")
  • Material: tufa (local stone) and limestone, covered with plaster to give the effect of marble
  • Style: Ionic view capital
    Ionic capital — identified by its scrolled volutes
  • Location: the Forum Boarium (cattle market), between the Palatine and Aventine Hills, next to the Tiber — an area that was a harbour in Roman times
  • Significance: one of the best preserved Roman temples; stands next to the circular Temple of Hercules Victor and the site of the Great Altar of Hercules
The Temple of Portunusplan and features
  • one of the best existing examples of a typical Roman temple
  • stands on a podium with steps only at the front — Etruscan influence
  • at the front: a porch with free-standing columns — Greek influence
  • at the sides and rear: semi-engaged columns attached to the cella wall — a variation on the Greek style
  • the altar no longer exists, but like Greek temples it stood at the foot of the temple steps
The Pantheonprescribed source facts view temple
The Pantheon · Rome
  • Date: AD 125 (rebuilt by Hadrian; the original was commissioned by Augustus and built by his general Marcus Agrippa between 27 and 25 BC, but burned down in AD 80)
  • Commissioned by: Hadrian
  • Dedicated to: debated — Pantheon means "all gods" (Greek: pan = all, theos = god); possibly dedicated to all gods, or possibly named for its domed shape resembling the heavens
  • Material: marble, brick, and concrete
  • Style: Corinthian view capital
    Corinthian capital — the most ornate, with acanthus leaves
  • Location: the Campus Martius (Field of Mars) in Rome
  • Significance: one of the best preserved ancient Roman buildings; its rotunda and oculus were feats of engineering unique in the ancient world
The Pantheonwhy the name is debated
  • the Roman historian Cassius Dio wrote in the second century AD that the name was already being debated in his time
  • one view: named because it contained statues of many gods, including Mars and Venus
  • Dio's own view: named because its vaulted roof resembles the heavens
  • the debate itself is useful evidence — it shows that even ancient Romans were not certain of the original purpose
The Pantheonplan and unique features
  • from the front the Pantheon follows the usual Roman plan: stands on a small plinth; free-standing columns at the front; semi-engaged columns at the rear of the porch
  • its unique feature: a rotunda (circular space) at the rear, capped with a dome
  • at the top of the dome is an oculus — a circular opening approximately 9 metres wide, the only source of natural light inside the building
  • the Pantheon provided the first example of this type of building — a remarkable feat of engineering
  • the circular plan allowed for several statues of gods to be placed around the interior cella
The Pantheondecoration and inscription view inscription
The Agrippa inscription on the frieze
  • the pediment shows signs of having once contained sculpture — now lost
  • Hadrian restored the original inscription from Agrippa's building on the frieze: M. Agrippa L.f. cos. tertium fecit — "Marcus Agrippa, son of Lucius, built this when consul for the third time"
  • by restoring Agrippa's inscription rather than adding his own, Hadrian was linking himself to Augustus and presenting the rebuilding as an act of piety rather than personal glorification
The Pantheon is the most important evidence that Roman temples did not always conform to a standard plan. The Temple of Portunus shows what a typical Roman temple looked like — podium, front steps, semi-engaged columns. The Pantheon shows what Roman engineering could achieve when the usual rules were set aside. The dome and oculus were without precedent: no building in the ancient world had attempted this on such a scale. That the building still stands largely intact nearly 1,900 years later is itself a testament to the engineering achievement.

Rome
Religious officials
Priests — general role
  • Roman priests worked to uphold the state religion by maintaining the pax deorum — the "peace of the gods" (pax = peace, deus = god)
  • this meant uttering the correct prayer, completing the appropriate sacrifice, and fulfilling priestly roles at festivals
  • unlike modern priests, Roman priests did not preach a moral code — it did not matter how a Roman behaved in private, provided the pax deorum was maintained
  • the relationship between gods and humans is captured in the Latin phrase do ut des — "I give so that you give"; offer sacrifice and offerings, and the gods will grant favour in return
  • unlike Greek priests, Roman priests were not assigned to a specific temple or god
  • priests veiled their heads with their toga when performing religious duties
  • Roman priests worked part time — the rest of their time was spent in business and politics
  • becoming a priest could greatly advance a political career — many ambitious politicians served as priests at some point in their lives
The Pontifices view statue
Augustus as Pontifex Maximus · veiled head (Via Labicana)
  • the pontifices were the most important college of priests in Rome — usually numbering fifteen
  • the college was headed by the Pontifex Maximus — a lifelong post giving its holder great power; the name means "bridge builder", possibly referring to the priest's role as mediator between humans and gods
  • from Rome's founding the Pontifex Maximus was elected from existing pontifices; from Augustus's election in 13 BC, the post was always held by the emperor
  • the main roles of the pontifices: protection of temples; regulation of burial and inheritance laws; supervision of the religious calendar — control of the calendar gave the priesthood immense power over when things could happen in Rome
The Augurs
  • an augur's main job was "taking the auspices" — reading the flight of birds, the behaviour of animals, or the direction of thunder to work out the will of the gods
  • taking the auspices was commonplace before battles, marriages, and business transactions
  • augurs carried a curved staff called a lituus
  • the story of Rome's founding illustrates the role: Romulus and Remus each took auspices to decide who would name and rule the new city — Remus saw six vultures; Romulus saw twelve; each side claimed victory (Remus by being first, Romulus by number); the dispute ended in violence and Remus's death
  • like other priestly roles, augurs were accused of reading signs in favour of political allies — manipulation cannot be ruled out, which is why politics and religion in Rome were so closely tied
The Vestal Virgins view statue
Statue of a Vestal Virgin
  • the Vestal Virgins were a college of six priestesses — the only female priesthood in Rome; they served the goddess Vesta
  • they lived in the House of the Vestals in the Roman Forum — the hub of social and political life in Rome
  • Selection: when a position became available, the Pontifex Maximus selected a new priestess from eligible girls aged six to ten; she had to be fit and healthy with two living parents; Vestals were taken from Rome's most prestigious families
  • the role lasted at least thirty years and required a vow of chastity — taking up most of a girl's life
  • Key duty: protecting the sacred flame of Vesta in her temple; the flame was believed to have been brought from Troy by Aeneas; if it went out, Rome would fall
  • the temple of Vesta also housed important state documents — treaties and the wills of leading Romans (Julius Caesar and Mark Antony both kept their wills there; Octavian retrieved Antony's will and read it to the senate, using its contents to justify war)
  • Vestals also made the mola salsa — a mix of salt and flour sprinkled on the heads of sacrificial animals at state sacrifices
  • Privileges: front row seats at certain games; allowed to own property, make wills, and vote — rights other Roman women did not have
  • Punishments: if the flame went out, the responsible Vestal was whipped; if she broke her vow of chastity, she was buried alive; the man responsible was whipped to death
The Haruspex view model liver
The Liver of Piacenza · a bronze model liver used by haruspices
  • the haruspex (pl. haruspices) was of Etruscan origin — unlike the augurs, who had Greek roots
  • a haruspex specialised in reading the entrails of sacrificial animals, consulted at important sacrifices such as festivals and battles
  • the process: watch how the animal fell when killed; examine the smoke and flames when it was burned; then read the entrails — the most important of which was the liver
  • haruspices used tools such as a model liver as a guide — the different parts of the liver meant different things; they checked its consistency and looked for abnormalities such as blood spots
Roman religion was inseparable from Roman politics. The Pontifex Maximus controlled the calendar — meaning he controlled when elections, trials, and business could take place. Augurs read the omens before every major public event — and could declare the omens unfavourable to delay or block political proceedings. The Vestals held state documents that could make or break political careers (as Antony's will shows). In Rome, serving as a priest was not a spiritual vocation but a political tool. The pax deorum was the justification — keep the gods happy and the state prospers — but the mechanisms for doing so were entirely in the hands of Rome's political elite.

Rome
Blood sacrifice
Overview
  • Roman sacrifices followed the same general procedure as Greek sacrifices — blood sacrifice was the most common form of offering
  • the procedure was so important that if anything went wrong, the whole process had to be started again
  • in a state sacrifice a pontifex led the ceremony; in a smaller sacrifice, a private citizen
  • one of the most common Roman sacrifices was the suovetaurilia — a pig, a sheep, and an ox sacrificed together; used when purifying something such as new land or buildings; before the sacrifice the animals were led in procession around whatever needed purifying
Stage 1Preparation
  • all participants ensured they were clean and well-dressed so as not to pollute the sacrifice
  • the animal was prepared: horns were gilded if it had any; ribbons were tied to its tail or horns
  • attendants led the animal to the altar — it was a good omen if it went willingly
  • the presiding priest veiled his head with his toga
  • flute players joined the procession to obscure any noises that might frighten the animal
Stage 2The kill
  • at the altar the priest sprinkled mola salsa (salt and flour, made by the Vestal Virgins) on the animal's head, followed by wine — causing it to nod; like the Greek sacrifice, the animal had to appear to consent
  • the priest uttered a prayer to the god, offering the animal into their care
  • a popa struck the animal on the head with a wooden rod to stun it
  • a cultrarius slit the animal's throat — it was important that the animal die with a single blow
Stage 3Sharing the sacrifice
  • the god received their share first — the priest uttered a prayer stating the god's name and the reason for the sacrifice
  • the haruspex read the entrails for omens, then the entrails were cooked and offered to the gods
  • the remaining meat was cut up, cooked, and shared with the wider community
  • in a state sacrifice there was a strict hierarchy: priests ate first, then the senate, then other elite citizens, and so on
  • in a private sacrifice, only the participants shared the meat
  • like Greece, meat was not a common part of the Roman diet — the sacrifice was one of the few occasions most Romans ate it
The Roman sacrifice was almost identical in structure to the Greek — preparation, kill, sharing; the animal's consent; the god's portion first; communal eating. The key differences are in the officials involved and the hierarchy of the sharing. In Rome, a haruspex rather than a mantis read the entrails; a popa and cultrarius performed the kill under the priest's direction; and the sharing of the meat reflected the rigid social hierarchy of Roman society. The suovetaurilia — pig, sheep, and ox together — shows how Roman sacrifice could also serve a civic function: purifying land and buildings as well as honouring the gods.

Greece & Rome
Temples compared
Similarities
  • Both had a sanctuary — a holy area separated from the city by a wall, with a fresh water source at the entrance
  • Both used temples primarily to house the cult statue — the temple was the god's house, not a place of congregational worship
  • Both orientated temples east to west
  • Both had a colonnade, a cella, and an altar outside the temple
  • Both treated the altar as the central place of worship — more important than the temple building itself (shown most clearly at Olympia, where the altar predated the temple by 200 years)
  • Both used temples to make political and religious statements about the power of the city or state
Differences
  • Podium: Roman temples stood on a podium with steps only at the front — you could only enter from one direction; Greek temples had steps on all four sides and could be approached from any angle
  • Columns: Roman temples used semi-engaged columns at the sides and rear — attached to the wall; Greek temples had fully free-standing columns all the way round
  • Focus: Roman temple design emphasised the front façade — designed to be seen head-on from a forum or street; Greek temples were designed to be viewed from all sides
  • Priests: Greek priests were assigned to a specific temple and god; Roman priests were not — they served the state religion as a whole
  • Materials: the Parthenon used expensive Pentelic marble; the Temple of Portunus used local tufa covered with plaster to imitate marble; the Pantheon used brick and concrete with marble facing
  • Innovation: Roman temples could depart radically from the standard plan — the Pantheon's rotunda and oculus have no Greek equivalent
The podium is the clearest single difference between Greek and Roman temple design. It tells you something important about how each culture understood the relationship between a temple and its city. A Greek temple could be walked around, studied from every angle, approached by anyone from any direction — it existed in the round. A Roman temple directed your approach, funnelled you up the front steps, and presented a single monumental façade to the forum. Greek temples were objects of beauty seen in space; Roman temples were statements of power seen from the street.

Greece & Rome
Officials compared
Similarities
  • Both had priests whose primary role was to perform the correct ritual at the correct time — not to preach or provide moral guidance
  • Both used soothsayers to read omens — the Greek mantis and the Roman augur both read the flight of birds and animal entrails
  • Both linked the priesthood to civic life — being a priest was a public role, not purely a spiritual one
  • Both cultures believed that neglecting correct ritual could bring harm to the community
Differences
  • Assignment: Greek priests were assigned to a specific temple and god; Roman priests were not — they served the state religion generally
  • Gender: Greece had no equivalent to the Vestal Virgins — the only formal female priesthood in Rome; Greek priestesses existed but had no equivalent college or state institution
  • Political power: Roman priestly colleges had direct political power — the pontifices controlled the calendar; augurs could declare omens unfavourable to block public business; the Vestals held state documents; nothing equivalent existed in Greece
  • Origin: the haruspex was of Etruscan origin — a distinctly Roman official with no Greek equivalent; the mantis was Greek
  • The emperor: from Augustus, the emperor was always Pontifex Maximus — fusing religious and political authority at the highest level; Greece had no equivalent fusion of state and religious leadership
The most striking difference is political. In Rome, the priesthood was a mechanism of state power — controlling the calendar, reading omens before every major public act, holding documents that shaped political careers. In Greece, the priesthood was a civic duty rather than a political tool. A Greek mantis might accompany an army, but they did not control when elections could be held. Rome institutionalised religion in a way that gave the priestly colleges real power over public life — and from Augustus onwards, that power was concentrated in the emperor himself.

Greece & Rome
Sacrifice compared
Similarities
  • Both used blood sacrifice as the primary act of worship
  • Both required the animal to appear to consent — water poured on the head to make it nod; a frightened animal was a bad omen and the sacrifice had to restart
  • Both burned the god's portion on the altar first — the god was served before the humans
  • Both used communal sharing of the meat as a way of binding the participants together
  • Both cultures ate meat rarely in daily life — the sacrifice was one of the few occasions ordinary people ate meat
  • Both used soothsayers to read the entrails after the kill — the mantis in Greece, the haruspex in Rome
  • Both treated a failed sacrifice as a serious religious problem requiring the whole process to restart
Differences
  • Officials at the kill: in Rome a popa stunned the animal and a cultrarius slit the throat — specific named officials; in Greece the chief sacrificer performed the kill themselves
  • Mola salsa: the Roman sacrifice used mola salsa (salt and flour made by the Vestal Virgins) sprinkled on the animal's head; Greece used barley grain
  • Hierarchy of sharing: in Rome the sharing followed a strict social hierarchy — priests first, then senators, then elite citizens; in Greece the sharing was among participants and then the wider community without this formal hierarchy
  • Suovetaurilia: Rome had the specific combined sacrifice of a pig, sheep, and ox for purification purposes — no direct Greek equivalent
  • Entrail-reading: in Rome this was done by a haruspex of Etruscan origin using specific tools including a model liver; in Greece a mantis read entrails or bird flight, without the same formalised toolkit
The two sacrificial traditions are strikingly similar at their core — the consent of the animal, the god's portion burned first, the communal meal. This is not surprising given how much Roman religion borrowed from Greece. The differences are mostly Roman additions: the mola salsa made by the Vestal Virgins, the popa and cultrarius as specialist officials, the rigid social hierarchy in the sharing of the meat. Each Roman addition reflects something broader about Roman society — the importance of the Vestals to state religion, the Roman tendency to formalise and institutionalise, the hierarchy that structured every aspect of Roman public life.
