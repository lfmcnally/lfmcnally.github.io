GCSE Classical Civilisation · Myth and Religion · Topic 1.8

Journeying to the Underworld

Revision notes — Greece and Rome. The Homeric Hymn to Demeter and Ovid’s Metamorphoses 10.

1 Key vocabulary
Greece
epithet
A repeated descriptive phrase used with a name in oral poetry — e.g. Demeter with the lovely hair. Helped singers remember; signals what aspect of a god is being emphasised.
oral tradition
Passing stories by word of mouth before writing. The Homeric Hymns were sung aloud to audiences across the Greek world.
chthonic
Relating to the gods of the earth and underworld — in contrast to the Olympian gods of the sky. Hades and Persephone are chthonic.
Erebus
The dark region of the underworld; the word the Hymn uses repeatedly for where Persephone is taken.
Eleusis
The town where Demeter takes refuge during her grief; later the centre of the Eleusinian Mysteries, secret religious rites in her honour.
pomegranate
The fruit whose seed Hades tricks Persephone into eating — binding her to the underworld for a third of every year.
ambrosia & nectar
The food and drink of the immortal gods. Demeter refuses them during her mourning as a sign of her grief.
Olympus
The mountain home of the chief gods. Demeter abandons it in rage, refusing to return until Persephone is released.
Rome
lyre
Orpheus's stringed instrument, a gift from Apollo. Its music could move gods, beasts, stones, and even the powers of the dead.
Hymen
The god of weddings. He attends Orpheus's wedding but the omens are bad — his torch will not light and produces tears.
Taenarus
A cave at the southern tip of the Peloponnese; one of the traditional gates to the underworld. Orpheus enters here.
Avernus
A lake near Naples thought to be another entrance to the underworld. Ovid uses the ‘vale of Avernus’ as the threshold back to the upper world.
shade
The faint, bloodless soul of a dead person — a weak version of the living being. Eurydice is described walking haltingly as a shade.
the Furies
Three goddesses of vengeance in the underworld. Their faces are wet with tears at Orpheus's song — Ovid calls this a ‘first time’.
naiad
A water nymph. Eurydice is walking with naiads when the snake bites her on her wedding day.
Styx
The principal river of the underworld. Orpheus ‘dared to go down to Styx’ — Ovid uses the river to stand for the whole realm.
2 Context

Greece — the Homeric Hymn to Demeter

One of thirty-three Homeric Hymns — short poems dedicated to the gods. Called ‘Homeric’ because they share Homer's style, but authorship is uncertain. Dated to the 7th–6th centuries BC. Part of an oral tradition — sung aloud before being written down.

The Hymn does two things at once: it explains why the seasons exist, and it explores the relationship between gods and humans. The gods are powerful, but they need humans — without worship and sacrifice, the gods lose their honour.

The prescribed lines
  • Lines 1–30: Persephone's abduction
  • Lines 33–104: Demeter searches
  • Lines 301–390: Demeter's rage; the pomegranate
  • Lines 390–474: Reunion; the bargain; the seasons

Rome — Ovid's Metamorphoses

Publius Ovidius Naso (43 BC–AD 17/18) — one of Rome's greatest poets. The Metamorphoses, written around AD 8, is a 15-book epic retelling myths from the creation to Ovid's own time. Its unifying theme is transformation.

The prescribed passage is Metamorphoses 10.1–64 — the story of Orpheus and Eurydice. Romans inherited their underworld largely from the Greeks but renamed key figures (Hades → Pluto; Persephone → Proserpina) and linked its entrances to real Roman geography.

The prescribed lines
  • Lines 1–17: The wedding; Eurydice's death; the descent
  • Lines 18–39: Orpheus's appeal to Pluto
  • Lines 40–58: The song's effect; Eurydice released
  • Lines 59–64: The fatal turn
3 Characters
Greece — Homeric Hymn to Demeter
Demeter
‘with the lovely hair’
Goddess of the harvest. Mother of Persephone. Her grief becomes a famine that nearly destroys mankind. Refuses to return to Olympus until her daughter is found.
Persephone
‘the slender-ankled’
Daughter of Demeter and Zeus. Abducted while picking flowers. Tricked into eating the pomegranate seed; becomes Queen of the Underworld for a third of every year.
Hades
‘Ruler of Many’, ‘Host of Many’
Brother of Zeus. King of the Underworld. Abducts Persephone with Zeus's consent. Smiles ‘grimly’ when ordered to release her — but obeys. Secretly gives her the pomegranate.
Zeus
‘loud-thundering’, ‘far-seeing’
King of the gods. Arranged the abduction without consulting Demeter. Forced to intervene when the famine threatens mankind — and the gods' source of worship.
Hermes
‘Slayer of Argos’
Messenger of the gods. One of the few who can travel freely between the worlds. Sent by Zeus to bring Persephone back from Hades.
Helios
God of the sun
Sees everything from his chariot above. Tells Demeter what Zeus arranged. A key witness — and one who defends Zeus's authority.
Hecate
Goddess of magic and night
Heard Persephone's cries from her cave. Brings the news to Demeter and accompanies her in the search. Becomes Persephone's companion afterwards.
Rhea
Mother of the Olympians
Sent by Zeus to fetch Demeter back to Olympus. Delivers the final terms of the bargain — Persephone spends a third of the year below, two thirds above.
Rome — Ovid's Metamorphoses
Orpheus
‘the poet of Rhodope’
Son of Apollo (or Oeagrus) and the muse Calliope. His lyre was given by Apollo. Music can move gods, beasts, and even stones. Descended to the underworld out of love for Eurydice.
Eurydice
‘the newly wedded bride’
Orpheus's wife. Killed on her wedding day by a snakebite. Spends most of the text as a silent shade. Her final word to Orpheus is a last ‘farewell’.
Pluto
‘lord of the shadows’
Roman name for Hades. King of the ‘joyless kingdom’. Moved by Orpheus's song and appeal; agrees to release Eurydice on one condition.
Proserpina
‘his royal bride’
Roman name for Persephone. Queen of the underworld. Orpheus reminds her of her own abduction to appeal to her sympathy — a deliberate rhetorical move.
4 The plots

Homeric Hymn to Demeter

1
Persephone is picking flowers in the Plain of Nysa. Zeus has arranged her abduction without telling Demeter.
2
Hades emerges through the earth in a golden chariot and carries her off. Persephone cries out — only Hecate and Helios hear her.
3
Demeter searches for nine days, neither eating nor drinking. Helios reveals the truth: ‘no other immortal is to blame but Zeus himself.’
4
Demeter disguises herself as an old woman and takes refuge at Eleusis. She abandons Olympus and withholds the harvest from the earth.
5
Famine threatens to destroy mankind. Zeus sends Iris first, then the other gods. All fail to persuade Demeter. She refuses to return or let crops grow ‘before she saw with her own eyes her fair-eyed girl.’
6
Zeus sends Hermes to Erebus. Hades ‘smiled grimly’ but obeys. He secretly gives Persephone a pomegranate seed before she leaves.
7
Reunion: Persephone leaps from the chariot. But Demeter immediately suspects she has eaten: ‘Surely you haven’t eaten anything below?’
8
The bargain: Persephone spends a third of the year in the underworld, two thirds with Demeter. Rhea delivers the terms. Demeter at once releases the harvest — ‘all the broad earth was heavy with flowers and leaves.’

Ovid, Metamorphoses 10.1–64

1
Orpheus and Eurydice's wedding. Bad omens: the god Hymen attends but his torch will not light — it smokes and produces tears.
2
Eurydice is bitten by a snake while walking with naiads on her wedding day. She dies immediately.
3
Orpheus descends through the cave at Taenarus, crossing the Styx, passing the recently buried dead and Cerberus.
4
He sings his appeal before Pluto and Proserpina. He uses three arguments: (1) love compelled him, (2) Eurydice will return here anyway, (3) if refused, he will stay and die too.
5
The effect of his song: all punishment stops — Tantalus, Ixion, Sisyphus, Tityus, the Belides all pause. The Furies weep for the first time.
6
Pluto agrees. The condition: Orpheus must not look back until they have left the vale of Avernus, or the gift is void.
7
On the steep, dark, silent path upward, ‘afraid she was no longer there’ — Orpheus turns. Eurydice drops back instantly.
8
Her final word is a last ‘farewell’ that ‘scarcely reached his ears.’ Ovid's ironic question: what could she complain of, ‘except that she had been loved?’

The underworld in the Hymn

The Hymn gives a partial picture. It is dark (Erebus, ‘the murky darkness below’), ruled by Hades and Persephone from a domestic household, and bound by strict rules — eating food there keeps you there. The dead themselves are barely described. Olympian gods cannot freely enter — Hermes must be specially sent.

The key insight
The underworld in the Hymn is defined by separation and rule, not by geography. What matters is the binding power of the pomegranate, the separation of the realms, and the authority of the gods over each other.

The underworld in Ovid

Ovid's underworld is far more furnished — he names the rivers, Cerberus, Tartarus, the five punishment figures, and the Furies, all within 64 lines. The dead are ‘bloodless spirits’ and ‘weightless throngs’. It is linked to real Roman geography. Ovid assumes his audience already knows the Greek tradition.

Aeneid 6 (supplementary)
Virgil's Aeneas descends to meet his father Anchises. The underworld here serves Rome's national destiny — dead souls await rebirth as great Romans. Aeneas's mission is political; Orpheus's is personal.
7 Themes and what the texts reflect about ancient culture

Greece — the Hymn

The gods need humans. The Hymn's central theological point: without human worship, the gods lose honour and power. Zeus intervenes not just out of concern for Demeter — but because famine would end sacrifice. This is why the crisis matters to Olympus.

Maternal grief. Demeter's love for Persephone is portrayed as an absolute force — stronger than Zeus's commands, stronger than the gods' authority. It is her refusal that gives her bargaining power.

Explanation of the natural world. The myth explains the seasons: Persephone’s time below = winter; her return = spring and harvest. Greek myths frequently served to explain the world around them.

The importance of ritual. The myth is connected to the Eleusinian Mysteries — secret religious rites at Eleusis involving death, rebirth, and what happens after death. Hades explicitly states that those who don’t worship Persephone with ‘sacrifices, reverently performing rites’ will face punishment.

Rome — Ovid

Love as a civilising force. Orpheus's love for Eurydice moves even the rulers of the dead — the Furies weep ‘for the first time.’ Love is presented as the most powerful force in the universe.

Love as fatal weakness. At the same time, love is what destroys Orpheus. He turns back because he fears for Eurydice — and in that moment of doubt and longing, he loses her. Ovid is interested in transformation, and Orpheus is transformed by loss.

Ovid's irony. ‘What could she complain of, except that she had been loved?’ This is typical of Ovid — a detached, clever voice commenting on human feeling. It makes the ending more unsettling, not less.

The underworld as real geography. By linking the underworld to actual Roman places (Avernus, Taenarus), Ovid and the Roman tradition treated death as something that happened at identifiable locations — not an abstract realm.

8 Greece vs Rome — comparison
Aspect Greece — the Hymn Rome — Ovid
Who descends Hades ascends to take Persephone. Then Hermes (a god) is sent to retrieve her. Orpheus (a mortal/hero) voluntarily descends out of love.
Driving force A mother's grief and divine politics — Demeter, Zeus, and the fate of mankind. Romantic love — one individual’s grief for his wife.
Underworld detail Minimal. Erebus, Hades’s household, the binding power of food. The dead themselves barely appear. Detailed. Named rivers, Cerberus, Tartarus, five punishment figures, the Furies. A fully furnished realm.
Geography The underworld is ‘below’ — reached by openings in the earth. Abstract location. Linked to real Roman places — Taenarus, Avernus. Treated as geographically real.
The ruler Hades — obeys reluctantly, smiles ‘grimly.’ Acts under Zeus’s authority. Pluto — ‘the lord of the shadows, who rules the joyless kingdom.’ Moved by Orpheus's song and appeal.
Outcome A bargain — Persephone returns for two thirds of the year. The seasons are explained. Order restored. Tragedy — Orpheus fails because of his own love. No restoration. Eurydice is lost for good.
What the myth is for Explains the seasons; celebrates the relationship between gods and humans; connected to the Eleusinian Mysteries. Explores love, loss, and the power of art. Fits Ovid’s theme of transformation. More literary, less ritual.
Tone Solemn and hymnic — a poem of praise to Demeter with serious theological stakes. Ironic and literary — Ovid comments on the action from a distance. Pathos mixed with wit.
9 Exam tips
On 1–3 mark questions
Name specific details — ‘Hades smiled grimly’, ‘the Furies wept for the first time’, ‘a third of the year below.’ Vague answers like ‘the underworld was dark’ get no marks. Precision always scores.
On significance questions
Always take one more step. Don’t just say what happened — explain why it mattered. ‘Demeter stopped the crops growing, which meant humans died, which meant the gods received no sacrifices’ is three steps; most students stop at one.
On source-based questions
Quote directly from the prescribed lines. A specific quotation earns the AO1 mark; your explanation of what it shows earns the AO2 marks. Never quote and stop — always develop.
On comparison questions
Don’t just describe each text separately. Pick a point of similarity or difference and develop it with evidence from both. The examiner wants to see you thinking across both cultures at once.
On ‘what does this tell us about ancient culture’
The Hymn reflects Greek beliefs about the seasons, the importance of worship, and the relationship between gods and humans. Ovid reflects Roman interest in love, transformation, and the geography of death. Always anchor your answer to the specific text.
Common mistakes to avoid
Confusing Greek and Roman names (Hades/Pluto, Persephone/Proserpina). Saying Persephone ate the pomegranate before the abduction — it happens just before her release. Saying Orpheus looked back on purpose — Ovid says it was from fear and love, not carelessness.
