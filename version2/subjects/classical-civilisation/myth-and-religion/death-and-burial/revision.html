Greece
The funerary process
Immediately after death
  • eyes and mouth closed by a family member
  • body washed, perfumed, and wrapped in a long white shroud
  • a coin (obol) placed on the mouth — payment for Charon, the ferryman to the underworld
  • death caused ritual pollution (miasma) — a bowl of water placed outside the house so visitors could purify themselves on leaving
Prothesisthe laying out — days 1–2
  • body laid out in the home for two days so friends and family could pay their respects
  • women led the mourning — they performed the lament: cut their hair, dressed in black, wailed, beat their chests and flailed their arms
  • leading the mourning was one of the few formal public roles open to women in ancient Athens
  • prothesis scenes appear on Athenian geometric amphorae — useful visual evidence for the ritual
Ekphorathe funeral procession — day 3, before dawn
  • held before dawn on day three — to limit the spread of miasma through the streets
  • body carried from the home to the burial ground on a wagon or by pallbearers
  • women, children, and men all took part, accompanied by an aulos (double-flute) player
  • all burials took place outside the city walls — to keep pollution away from the city and its religious spaces
  • in Athens, the main burial ground was the Kerameikos, just outside the northwest walls
Burial or cremation
  • both were acceptable — a sacrifice took place at the grave; blood released into the earth as an offering to Hades and Persephone
  • grave goods buried with the body — food, oil flasks (lekythoi), jewellery, personal items
  • wealthier families commissioned a sculptor to carve a stele — a relief showing the deceased as they were in life
  • neglecting a grave stele was a serious dishonour to the family
  • a thirty-day period of mourning followed; family visited the grave on days 3, 9, and 13, then annually
The stelai — key examples
  • Stele of Hegeso (c.410–400 BC, Kerameikos) — shows Hegeso seated, examining jewellery from a box held by a slave girl; domestic, peaceful scene
  • Stele of Dexileos (c.394–390 BC, Kerameikos) — shows a young cavalryman on horseback striking a fallen enemy; combat scenes were unusual on stelai
  • Dexileos's inscription gives exact birth and death dates — possibly to prove he died before the Thirty Tyrants, distancing the family from that regime
  • both stelai are visual sources — use them to discuss Greek attitudes to death, status, and memory
The whole process is built around two ideas: respect for the dead and protection of the living. The coin for Charon, the ritual water bowl, the dawn ekphora — all of these manage the boundary between the living and the dead. Without proper burial, a soul could not cross the Styx. That gave burial a weight that went far beyond social custom.

Greece
Festivals of the dead
Anthesterialate January · three days · linked to Dionysus
  • Day one: the dead were believed to rise from the underworld and roam the earth; wine from the previous year was opened; libations poured to Dionysus
  • Day two: drinking contests held; families poured libations at the graves of their ancestors
  • Day three: offerings made to Hermes (psychopomp — guide of souls, one of only two gods able to travel between the worlds); the living were not permitted to eat any of the food offered; the day ended as the dead returned to the underworld
  • Dionysus also had access to the underworld — all other Olympians were barred
Genesialate September · one day · from genos = family/lineage
  • a festival of ancestors — not just the recently deceased but the whole family line going back generations
  • Athenian families travelled to the burial grounds outside the city
  • graves decorated with woollen ribbons tied around the stele
  • offerings of food and a blood sacrifice made to all dead ancestors
  • reinforced the bond between the living family and their dead lineage
The Anthesteria is about the recent dead briefly returning to the world of the living; the Genesia is about all ancestors being honoured collectively. Together they show that the Greek duty to the dead was ongoing — not just a one-off funeral but a permanent relationship between the living and the dead. The fact that the living could not eat the food offered on day three of the Anthesteria shows the boundary between the living and dead had to be strictly maintained even when it temporarily dissolved.

Rome
The funerary process
Immediately after death
  • just before death, a relative captured the last breath with a kiss
  • those present then called out the deceased's name — if they died alone, this naming ritual was carried out ceremonially afterwards
  • body washed, perfumed, and dressed in the deceased's finest clothes — a final display of status
  • a coin placed on the mouth as payment for Charon
Laying out in the atriumdays 1–8
  • wealthy families laid out the body in the atrium (main reception room) for eight days
  • friends and relatives visited to pay their respects; women of the family performed the lament
  • less wealthy Romans relied on funeral clubs — social clubs paid into monthly; the club covered funeral costs and provided mourners when a member died
  • clubs were often trade-based (bakers', blacksmiths' guilds); larger clubs maintained shared tombs
  • Pliny's letter about the haunted house (7.27) shows that a body left unburied meant the spirit could not rest — and the whole community suffered
The funerary processionday 8
  • a public event — family, slaves, freedmen of the household, and flute and horn players
  • family members wore wax funerary masks of ancestors, kept normally in the family shrine — symbolising the dead being welcomed into the afterlife by their line
  • wealthy but small families hired professional mourners and even actors to mimic the deceased as they were in life
  • the grander the procession, the higher the family's social standing
Burial and the Manes
  • burial took place outside the city walls — law required it (the Twelve Tables)
  • wealthy families buried in tombs along the roads into towns — so passers-by would see the monument and pay their respects
  • the super-rich used elaborately carved sarcophagi decorated with mythological scenes
  • after burial, a marble bust of the deceased was placed in the home
  • the dead became Manes — deified ancestor spirits who needed to be fed; families left food and wine offerings at the tomb regularly
  • the Persephone sarcophagus (Capitoline Museums, c.230–240 AD) shows how Romans used mythology to give meaning to death — the myth of abduction and return suggested hope of afterlife
The funerary masks are one of the most distinctively Roman details. By having family members wear the faces of ancestors in the procession, the whole family history became visible at once. A Roman funeral for a wealthy family was not just about grief — it was a public statement about who the family were, how far back they went, and what they stood for. The masks had no Greek equivalent.

Rome
Festivals of the dead
Parentalia13–21 February · nine days · domestic festival
  • during this period: marriages forbidden, temples closed, no official business
  • a blood sacrifice by a Vestal Virgin on the first day — the only public element
  • otherwise a domestic, family-focused festival — not a state occasion
  • for the first eight days, families brought offerings to the tombs of their ancestors
  • Ovid records what the dead wanted: pietas over costly gifts — a tile wreathed with garlands, meal, salt, bread soaked in wine, violets
  • on the ninth day, the family gathered for a shared meal at home — to settle quarrels and repair the family bond
Lemuria9th, 11th, 13th May · to ward off evil spirits
  • unlike the Parentalia, the Lemuria was held to ward off evil spirits of the dead — restless, dangerous spirits, not honoured ancestors
  • the head of the household rose at night, washed his hands in spring water
  • threw black beans behind him nine times without looking back, saying 'with these beans I redeem myself and my family'
  • the spirits were thought to gather the beans and leave the house
  • marriages and official business were also forbidden during the Lemuria
The Lemuria sits oddly next to the calm, dignified Parentalia. Getting up at midnight to throw beans doesn't look like organised religion — it looks like fear. It shows that the Romans, just like the Greeks, believed the unburied or improperly remembered dead were dangerous. The two festivals together cover both sides of the relationship: honouring those properly remembered, and protecting against those who were not.

Greece & Rome
Why proper burial mattered
Greece
Rome
Without burial the soul could not cross the Styx — it wandered for 100 years
Without burial the spirit became a restless, dangerous spirit — the Lemuria was specifically to deal with these
The obol (coin) on the mouth paid Charon — no coin, no crossing
A coin also placed on the mouth — same belief, same practice
Failing to bury the dead was deeply impious and dishonoured the family
Funeral clubs existed so no one went without a proper burial — a community structure
Antigone's story shows how sacred the duty was — she risks her life to bury Polynices
Pliny's haunted house: an unburied body left a ghost that disturbed the whole community until buried at public expense
Both cultures believed that burial was a necessity, not just a kindness. An unburied body caused real problems — for the soul that could not move on, and for the living who had to deal with the restless spirit it left behind. That shared belief is why burial was treated as a religious obligation in both Greece and Rome, not merely a social custom.

Greece & Rome
Wealth & status
Greece
Rome
Elaborate carved stelai — with scenes and inscriptions — marked wealthy graves
Sarcophagi and large tomb monuments lined the roads — the bigger the tomb, the wealthier the family
More grave goods, more professional mourners, more elaborate lekythoi for the rich
The pompa (procession) was public — more ancestor masks = longer family history on display
The prothesis could be grander and longer for wealthier families
Hired professional mourners and actors to swell the procession and mimic the deceased
Both: the quality, scale, and location of a burial reflected the family's wealth and standing — burial was a public statement as much as a private ritual
In both Greece and Rome, a funeral was visible to the community. The grave stele on a Greek roadside or the pompa processing through Roman streets sent the same message: this family matters. The difference is that Rome made it even more explicitly public — the funerary masks parading the family's ancestors, the tombs lining the roads where everyone would see them. Greece was grand; Rome was theatrical.

Greece & Rome
Burial outside the city
Greece
Rome
Death caused miasma (ritual pollution) — the dead had to be kept outside to protect the city and its sacred spaces
The Twelve Tables (Roman law) forbade burial inside the pomerium (city boundary)
The ekphora before dawn limited the spread of miasma through the streets
Tombs lined the roads outside cities — the dead remained visible to passers-by, even from outside
Main Athenian burial ground: the Kerameikos, outside the northwest walls
Wealthy families placed tombs along major roads so monuments would be seen — visible commemoration
Both cultures separated the dead from the living — but for slightly different reasons. For the Greeks, the primary concern was pollution: a dead body made the city impure. For the Romans it was also law. The key difference is the Roman roadside tomb: the dead were placed exactly where everyone would pass them, turning burial into a form of ongoing public commemoration. Separated from the city, but never invisible.

Greece & Rome
Festivals of the dead — compared
Greece
Rome
Anthesteria — late January, three days, linked to Dionysus; spirits walk among the living
Parentalia — 13–21 February, nine days; domestic, family-focused; offerings at tombs
Genesia — late September; honours all ancestors, generation after generation; graves decorated with woollen ribbons
Lemuria — 9th, 11th, 13th May; to ward off evil spirits; head of household throws black beans
Both festivals honour the dead and protect the living — the Anthesteria involves smearing doors with pitch to keep spirits out
The Lemuria has no Greek equivalent — it shows a distinctly Roman fear of dangerous unburied spirits
Neglecting the dead could cause harm to the living — the festivals kept the relationship in good order
The Parentalia ended with a family meal — Cara Cognatio — balancing mourning with celebration of the living
In both cultures, festivals to the dead served two purposes at once: honouring the dead and protecting the living. The Greeks and Romans both believed that neglected dead could cause harm. The key difference is the Lemuria — getting up at midnight to throw beans is not solemn commemoration, it is fear management. Rome had a more explicit tradition of fearing the dangerous dead alongside honouring the good ones.
