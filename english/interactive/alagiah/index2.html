Step 3 ‚Äî Discussion Questions
Why does Alagiah say he saw "a thousand" faces ‚Äî but then focus on just one?
Paragraph 1"I saw a thousand hungry, lean, scared and betrayed faces... but there is one I will never forget."
AnswerSaying "a thousand" shows how many people are suffering. But picking out just one face makes it feel personal and important. It also makes us curious straight away ‚Äî which face? What was different about it? It's Alagiah's way of drawing us in from the very first line.
Why does Alagiah compare looking for shocking images to needing a drug?
Paragraph 3"The search for the shocking is like the craving for a drug: you require heavier and more frequent doses the longer you're at it."
AnswerA drug addiction is something you can't control ‚Äî it numbs you and makes you want more and more. Alagiah is saying that journalists become used to seeing terrible things, and it stops shocking them. He's also using "you" ‚Äî meaning us. He's saying the audience at home keeps demanding more dramatic images too, so we're part of the problem.
Alagiah has seen horrible things all day. Why does a smile affect him more than any of it?
Paragraphs 9‚Äì10"It was the feeble smile that goes with apology, the kind of smile you might give if you felt you had done something wrong."
AnswerThe man was apologising ‚Äî for being poor, for being ill, for being seen like this. But he hadn't done anything wrong. Alagiah realised that he himself was the one who should feel embarrassed: he was standing there healthy and well-fed, filming someone at their lowest point. The smile flipped everything around.
Why does Alagiah describe the old man who keeps his hoe beside him even as he is dying?
Paragraph 8"the old and dying man who keeps his hoe next to the mat with which, one day soon, they will shroud his corpse, as if he means to go out and till the soil once all this is over."
AnswerThe hoe shows the man still has hope ‚Äî or at least the idea of a future. He refuses to give up. Alagiah is telling us that these people are not just sad victims. They have pride, identity, and hope even at the end. That makes the world's failure to help them feel even more wrong.

Step 2 ‚Äî A Model Paragraph
Read this together ‚Äî where is the analysis? The highlighted words show you how the sentence stem is used. The key part is the last bit: "which makes the reader feel..." That is where the marks are.
Alagiah uses a metaphor to admit something uncomfortable about journalism. He compares the need for shocking images to "the craving for a drug" ‚Äî suggesting that journalists become addicted to seeing suffering and need more of it to feel anything. The word "craving" is important because it suggests Alagiah has lost control ‚Äî like someone who can't stop themselves. This makes the reader feel uncomfortable, because we realise that his numbness is partly caused by us: we, the audience, keep watching and demanding more dramatic images from home.
What this paragraph does well It names a technique (metaphor), uses a precise quotation, picks out a single word ("craving"), and always connects to how the reader feels.
The golden rule Don't stop at the quotation. Finding the quote is not the analysis ‚Äî explaining what it does to the reader is where the marks are.

How does Alagiah use a comparison to admit something uncomfortable about journalism?
Paragraph 3"The search for the shocking is like the craving for a drug: you require heavier and more frequent doses the longer you're at it."
Hints"Craving" ‚Äî you crave something you can't help wanting. "Doses" ‚Äî this is a word for medicine or drugs, not human suffering. "You" ‚Äî Alagiah points the finger at the reader, not just himself. Try: the word "you" suggests... which makes the reader feel...
What is the effect of Alagiah admitting he felt "revulsion" ‚Äî and then saying it again?
Paragraph 7"My reaction to everyone else I met that day was a mixture of pity and revulsion. Yes, revulsion."
HintsRevulsion means disgust ‚Äî a strong feeling of being repelled. Journalists usually pretend to be neutral. Saying "Yes, revulsion" ‚Äî repeating it ‚Äî shows Alagiah knows it will shock us and he means it. Try: the repetition of "revulsion" suggests... which makes the reader feel...
Why does Alagiah use the same word for the woman's wound and for the woman herself?
Paragraph 5"It was rotting; she was rotting."
Hints"Rotting" is a word for objects ‚Äî fruit, wood, matter. Using it for a person is harsh and deliberate. Alagiah is saying the wound and the woman are now the same thing. The two short clauses with the semicolon feel like two equal facts being stated coldly. Try: the word "rotting" suggests... which makes the reader feel...
How does the description of the smile make the reader feel responsible?
Paragraph 10"It was the feeble smile that goes with apology, the kind of smile you might give if you felt you had done something wrong."
Hints"Feeble" ‚Äî he barely has strength to smile. "You might give" ‚Äî Alagiah uses "you" again, pulling the reader in. We recognise this smile from our own lives ‚Äî the awkward apology for something that isn't your fault. Try: the word "feeble" suggests... which makes the reader feel... Or try: the phrase "you might give" suggests...
How does Alagiah use powerful language to show that the suffering isn't the victims' fault?
Paragraph 7"sucked of its natural vitality by the twin evils of hunger and disease"
Hints"Sucked" ‚Äî something is actively draining the body, like a vampire. "Twin evils" ‚Äî calling hunger and disease "evils" makes them sound like villains. This takes the blame away from the people and puts it on forces they can't control. Try: the word "sucked" suggests... which makes the reader feel...
Why does Alagiah describe this famine as "quiet" and "lonely" rather than dramatic?
Paragraph 4"a famine of quiet suffering and lonely death"
HintsFamines are usually reported as loud emergencies with dramatic footage. "Quiet" and "lonely" suggest the opposite: people dying with no witnesses, no help, no camera. "Lonely" is especially powerful ‚Äî it sounds like the world has looked away. Try: the word "lonely" suggests... which makes the reader feel...

What does "structure" mean? Structure just means: how has the writer organised their writing? Where do they start? Where do they end? What do they put in the middle? What do they save until the reader is ready for it? Every choice about order affects how we feel as we read.
Step 1 of 4
Step 2 ‚Äî How is the Text Built?
Discuss each one ‚Äî then reveal the note
Opening ‚Äî why promise "one face" in the very first sentence without telling us which one?
Paragraph 1"I saw a thousand hungry, lean, scared and betrayed faces... but there is one I will never forget."
NoteThis is a hook. Alagiah makes us curious straight away ‚Äî which face, and why? "A thousand" compared to "one" makes the single face feel special and important. We have to keep reading to find out. It also sets up Alagiah as a professional who has seen everything ‚Äî so the one face that breaks through must be something extraordinary.
The pivot ‚Äî paragraph 6 is only one sentence. Why does it get its own paragraph?
Paragraph 6"And then there was the face I will never forget."
NoteGiving one short sentence its own paragraph makes the reader pause. Everything before this was about physical suffering ‚Äî bodies, wounds, death. Everything after it is about feelings and moral questions. Paragraph 6 is the dividing line between those two halves. Its shortness gives it enormous weight ‚Äî it forces a full stop in our reading.
The big question ‚Äî why does this question come in paragraph 11 rather than at the start?
Paragraph 11"If he was embarrassed to be found weakened by hunger and ground down by conflict, how should I feel to be standing there so strong and confident?"
NoteIf Alagiah had asked this question at the beginning, it would feel empty ‚Äî we haven't seen anything yet. By paragraph 11, we've seen the faces, heard the smell, felt the shame of the smile. The ten paragraphs of suffering mean the question hits us hard when it arrives. The structure earns the question.
The ending ‚Äî why does Alagiah suddenly speak directly to the man in the final sentence?
Paragraph 13"So, my nameless friend, if you are still alive, I owe you one."
NoteFor the whole text, Alagiah has described the man in the third person ‚Äî "he", "him", "the face". Now he speaks to him directly: "my nameless friend". This shift makes it feel personal and human ‚Äî the man is not just a subject to be observed. "I owe you one" sounds like something you'd say to a friend, not a stranger. It's Alagiah's way of saying: this encounter changed me.

[1]I saw a thousand hungry, lean, scared and betrayed faces as I criss-crossed Somalia between the end of 1991 and December 1992, but there is one I will never forget.

[2]I was in a little hamlet just outside Gufgaduud, 'a village in the back of beyond, a place the aid agencies had yet to reach.' In my notebook I had jotted down instructions on how to get there. 'Take the Badale Road for a few kilometres till the end of the tarmac, turn right on to a dirt track, stay on it for about forty-five minutes ‚Äî Gufgaduud. Go another fifteen minutes approx. ‚Äî like a ghost village.'

[3]In the ghoulish manner of journalists on the hunt for the most striking pictures, my cameraman and I tramped from one hut to another. What might have appalled us when we'd started our trip just a few days before no longer impressed us much. The search for the shocking is like the craving for a drug: you require heavier and more frequent doses the longer you're at it. Pictures that stun the editors one day are written off as the same old stuff the next. This sounds callous, but it is just a fact of life. It's how we collect and compile the images that so move people in the comfort of their sitting rooms back home.

[4]There was Amina Abdirahman, who had gone out that morning in search of wild, edible roots, leaving her two young girls lying on the dirt floor of their hut. They had been sick for days, and were reaching the final, enervating stages of terminal hunger. Habiba was ten years old and her sister, Ayaan, was nine. By the time Amina returned, she had only one daughter. Habiba had died. No rage, no whimpering, just a passing away ‚Äî that simple, frictionless, motionless deliverance from a state of half-life to death itself. It was, as I said at the time in my dispatch, a vision of 'famine away from the headlines, a famine of quiet suffering and lonely death.'

[5]There was the old woman who lay in her hut, abandoned by relations who were too weak to carry her on their journey to find food. It was the smell that drew me to her doorway: the smell of decaying flesh. Where her shinbone should have been there was a festering wound the size of my hand. She'd been shot in the leg as the retreating army of the deposed dictator took revenge on whoever it found in its way. The shattered leg had fused into the gentle V-shape of a boomerang. It was rotting; she was rotting. You could see it in her sick, yellow eyes and smell it in the putrid air she recycled with every struggling breath she took.

[6]And then there was the face I will never forget.

[7]My reaction to everyone else I met that day was a mixture of pity and revulsion. Yes, revulsion. The degeneration of the human body, sucked of its natural vitality by the twin evils of hunger and disease, is a disgusting thing. We never say so in our TV reports. It's a taboo that has yet to be breached. To be in a feeding centre is to hear and smell the excretion of fluids by people who are beyond controlling their bodily functions. To be in a feeding centre is surreptitiously to wipe your hands on the back of your trousers after you've held the clammy palm of a mother who has just cleaned vomit from her child's mouth.

[8]There's pity, too, because even in this state of utter despair they aspire to a dignity that is almost impossible to achieve. An old woman will cover her shrivelled body with a soiled cloth as your gaze turns towards her. Or the old and dying man who keeps his hoe next to the mat with which, one day soon, they will shroud his corpse, as if he means to go out and till the soil once all this is over.

[9]I saw that face for only a few seconds, a fleeting meeting of eyes before the face turned away, as its owner retreated into the darkness of another hut. In those brief moments there had been a smile, not from me, but from the face. It was not a smile of greeting, it was not a smile of joy ‚Äî how could it be? ‚Äî but it was a smile nonetheless. It touched me in a way I could not explain. It moved me in a way that went beyond pity or revulsion.

[10]What was it about that smile? I had to find out. I urged my translator to ask the man why he had smiled. He came back with an answer. 'It's just that he was embarrassed to be found in this condition,' the translator explained. And then it clicked. It was the feeble smile that goes with apology, the kind of smile you might give if you felt you had done something wrong.

[11]Normally inured to stories of suffering, accustomed to the evidence of deprivation, I was unsettled by this one smile in a way I had never been before. There is an unwritten code between the journalist and his subjects in these situations. The journalist observes, the subject is observed. The journalist is active, the subject is passive. But this smile had turned the tables on that tacit agreement. Without uttering a single word, the man had posed a question that cut to the heart of the relationship between me and him, between us and them, between the rich world and the poor world. If he was embarrassed to be found weakened by hunger and ground down by conflict, how should I feel to be standing there so strong and confident?

[12]I resolved there and then that I would write the story of Gufgaduud with all the power and purpose I could muster. It seemed at the time, and still does, the only adequate answer a reporter can give to the man's question.

[13]I have one regret about that brief encounter in Gufgaduud. Having searched through my notes and studied the dispatch that the BBC broadcast, I see that I never found out what the man's name was. Yet meeting him was a seminal moment in the gradual collection of experiences we call context. Facts and figures are the easy part of journalism. Knowing where they sit in the great scheme of things is much harder. So, my nameless friend, if you are still alive, I owe you one.
