Menander, Σαμία

Moschion's Prologue Narrative · Lines ~35–57
Context. Moschion, a young Athenian, addresses the audience directly in the prologue. His adoptive father Demeas has a Samian mistress called Chrysis. The girl next door, Plangon, lives with her mother (her father Nikeratos is away abroad with Demeas). Moschion is about to explain how he got Plangon pregnant at the Adonia festival, and what he did about it. The opening lines of this section are badly damaged on the papyrus, so the first few entries are fragmentary.

The Fragmentary Opening

Lines ~35–38 (fragmentary)
].φέροντʼ ἰδὼν
]..τ̣ος προσετίθην πανταχοῦ
].ησθε πρὸς τὸν γείτονα
]α συνθλάσας τὸ σημεῖον σφόδρα
[…seeing someone carrying…] I attached myself everywhere […] towards the neighbour […] having crushed the seal/token utterly

These lines are badly damaged. The left margin of the papyrus is lost, so we only have the ends of lines. We can pick out a few things:

ἰδών (having seen) and προσετίθην (I was attaching myself) tell us Moschion was involving himself in something. πανταχοῦ (everywhere) suggests enthusiasm. τὸν γείτονα (the neighbour) points us towards the family next door.

The Two Households

Lines ~39–40
φ]ιλανθρώπως δὲ πρὸς τὴν τοῦ πατρὸς
Σαμί]α̣ν̣ διέκειθʼ ἡ τῆς κόρης μήτηρ, τά τε
…and the mother of the girl was disposed kindly towards my father's Samian woman, and…

The sentence structure is worth noting: the adverb φιλανθρώπως comes first, foregrounding the warmth of the relationship, while the subject (ἡ τῆς κόρης μήτηρ, "the girl's mother") is held back. The phrase τὴν τοῦ πατρὸς Σαμίαν is a characteristically Greek periphrasis — Moschion avoids naming Chrysis directly and instead identifies her through her relationship to his father and her origin.

Lines ~41–42
πλεῖστʼ ἦν παρʼ αὐταῖς ἥδε, καὶ πάλιν ποτὲ
αὗται παρʼ ἡμῖν.
For the most part this woman [Chrysis] was at their house, and from time to time they were at ours.

A beautifully balanced pair of clauses showing reciprocity: ἦν παρʼ αὐταῖς ἥδε // αὗται παρʼ ἡμῖν. Notice the chiastic structure CHIASMUS — "she was at theirs" is mirrored by "they at ours". The pronouns shift neatly: αὐταῖς (them, fem.) → ἡμῖν (us). This back-and-forth visiting is what creates the opportunity for the plot.

The Adonia Festival

The Adonia
The Adonia was a women's festival in honour of Aphrodite's mortal lover Adonis. Women would carry "gardens of Adonis" — pots of quickly sprouting, quickly dying plants — up onto rooftops, then spend the night feasting, dancing, and mourning Adonis' death. It was one of the few occasions when Athenian women could gather freely and stay up all night. In comedy, it is regularly associated with female licence and sexual opportunity. For Moschion's narrative, it provides the perfect dramatic cover.
Lines ~42–44
ἐξ ἀγροῦ δὴ καταδραμὼν
ὡς ἔτυχέ γʼ εἰς Ἀδώνιʼ αὐτὰς κατέλαβον
Having run down from the country, as it happened, I found them at the Adonia…

Moschion insists this was accidental: ὡς ἔτυχε, "as chance would have it". The audience would have enjoyed the irony. Note the pile-up of participles (καταδραμών) leading to the main verb (κατέλαβον) — a very natural way of narrating in Greek, where participles set the scene and the finite verb delivers the key action.

Lines ~44–46
συνηγμένας ἐνθάδε πρὸς ἡμᾶς μετά τινων
ἄλλων γυναικῶν· τῆς δʼ ἑορτῆς παιδιὰν
πολλὴν ἐχούσης οἷον εἰκός,
…gathered here at our place with some other women; and since the festival involved a good deal of fun, as you'd expect…

The genitive absolute GEN ABS — τῆς ἑορτῆς παιδιὰν πολλὴν ἐχούσης — is worth parsing carefully: the festival (gen.) + having (gen. participle) + much fun (acc. object of the participle). The aside οἷον εἰκός ("as you'd expect") is wonderfully knowing — Moschion nudges the audience with a wink.

Lines ~46–48
συμπαρὼν
ἐγινόμην οἶμαι θεατής· ἀγρυπνίαν
ὁ θόρυβος αὐτῶν ἐνεπόει γάρ μοι τινά·
…being present, I became, I think, a spectator; for their noise was causing me a certain sleeplessness.

The self-conscious οἶμαι ("I think") is a lovely comic touch — Moschion pretends to be vague about his own role. He was merely a θεατής, a "spectator"! The word ironically echoes the audience's own position in the theatre. And his excuse for being up all night? The noise was keeping him awake. The audience knows where this is heading.

Lines ~49–50
ἐπὶ τὸ τέγος κήπους γὰρ ἀνέφερόν τινας,
ὠρχοῦντʼ, ἐπαννύχιζον ἐσκεδασμέναι.
For they were carrying gardens up onto the roof, dancing, celebrating all night, scattered about.

A wonderful snapshot of the Adonia in action. Three imperfect verbs in quick succession — ἀνέφερον, ὠρχοῦντο, ἐπαννύχιζον — paint the scene as vivid, ongoing action, like a camera panning across the rooftop. The final participle ἐσκεδασμέναι ("scattered") is significant: the women are spread out in separate groups, which means a young man could find a girl alone.

The Confession

Lines ~51–52
ὀκνῶ λέγειν τὰ λοίπʼ, ἴσως δʼ αἰσχύνομαι
οἷς] οὐδὲν ὄφελός ἐσθʼ· ὅμως αἰσχύνομαι.
I hesitate to say the rest — perhaps I'm ashamed, though there's no use in that. Still, I am ashamed.

The repetition of αἰσχύνομαι is the emotional heart of this passage. Moschion first qualifies it with ἴσως ("perhaps"), then, after acknowledging that shame is pointless (οὐδὲν ὄφελος), repeats it without qualification: ὅμως αἰσχύνομαι — "all the same, I am ashamed." The rhetoric of hesitation PRAETERITIO is a device for telling the audience everything while pretending reluctance. It works here because the shame also seems real.

Lines ~53–54
ἐκύησεν ἡ παῖς· τοῦτο γὰρ φράσας λέγω
καὶ τὴν πρὸ τούτου πρᾶξιν.
The girl became pregnant — for by saying this, I am also telling you the deed that preceded it.

After all the build-up, Moschion delivers the fact in four words: ἐκύησεν ἡ παῖς. Then comes a brilliantly self-aware aside: by mentioning the pregnancy, he says, he has also communicated the act that caused it (τὴν πρὸ τούτου πρᾶξιν), without having to describe it explicitly. The euphemism does double work, sparing his blushes while making the audience complicit in understanding what happened.

Lines ~54–57
οὐκ ἠρνησάμην
τὴν αἰτίαν σχών, ἀλλὰ πρότερος ἐνέτυχον
τῆι μητρὶ τῆς κόρης, ὑπεσχόμην γαμεῖν
...ὅταν ἐπανέλθηι ποθʼ ὁ πατήρ.
I did not deny it when I received the blame; rather, I went first to the girl's mother and promised to marry her, whenever my father should return.

Moschion presents himself in the best possible light. Three key points emerge: he did not deny responsibility (οὐκ ἠρνησάμην); he took the initiative (πρότερος, "first"); and he promised marriage (ὑπεσχόμην γαμεῖν). The promise is conditional on his father's return from abroad, since in Athenian society a son needed his father's consent to marry. This sets up the central tension of the play: Demeas must agree, and the rest of the Samia is about the chaos that ensues when he comes home and draws the wrong conclusions about whose baby it is.

What happens next
Demeas returns and discovers that there is a baby in his house — and he assumes it belongs to Chrysis and Moschion, his own son and his own partner. His resulting fury at what he sees as a double betrayal drives the plot. The whole comedy turns on this misunderstanding, which Moschion's prologue has carefully set up for the audience: we know the truth from the start, and we watch the characters stumble through the consequences of not knowing it.
