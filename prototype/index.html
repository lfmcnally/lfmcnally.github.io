The ablative case

The ablative is the “Swiss-army-knife” case of Latin. It has no single English equivalent — instead it does a whole family of jobs, most of which English handles with the little words by, with, from and in. Master the jobs, and half of GCSE translation falls into place.

1 · What the ablative does

Where the nominative marks the subject and the accusative the object, the ablative marks the circumstances of an action — the tool used, the companion, the place, the time, the cause. Because Latin has no words for “by/with/from”, the ablative ending itself carries that meaning.

Source · seen in Chapter 12
servus gladiō dominum necat.
“The slave kills the master with a sword.” — gladiō is ablative: the instrument. No word for “with” appears; the ending does the work.

2 · The four uses you must know for GCSE

Instrument (“by/with”) — the means by which something is done: sagittā vulnerat, “he wounds with an arrow”. Place where (“in/on”), usually with in: in silvā, “in the wood”. Time when/within which: tertiā hōrā, “at the third hour”. Accompaniment (“with” a person), with cum: cum amīcīs, “with friends”.

The trap GCSE candidates fall into is translating every ablative as “with”. Ask instead: is this a tool, a place, a time, or a companion? The right little word follows from the job.

Revision cascade — write these five lines from memory
  • Ablative = the by/with/from case — circumstances, not subject or object.
  • Instrument: no preposition — “by/with a thing”.
  • Place where: in + ablative.
  • Time when: no preposition — “at/in/within”.
  • Accompaniment: cum + ablative — “with a person”.
💡
Memory hook: “I PACT with the ablative” — Instrument, Place, Accompaniment, Cause, Time.

3 · Spotting it in the endings

In the singular the ablative usually ends in a long vowel — -ā, -ō, -e/-ī, -ū — and in the plural it shares its ending with the dative: -īs, -ibus. When you meet an ambiguous form, let the sense and any preposition decide.
