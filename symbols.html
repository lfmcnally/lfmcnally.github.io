I · The Myth

The Centauromachy: Lapiths and Centaurs

The Lapiths were a mythical tribe of men who lived in Thessaly, a region of northern Greece. They were initially ruled by Ixion. Zeus, wishing to honour him, invited Ixion to the table of the gods on Olympus. Rather than showing gratitude, Ixion took a liking to Hera. To trick him, Zeus fashioned an image of Hera out of the clouds. Fooled, Ixion coupled with the cloud, and the next rains to fall gave birth to the Centaurs. The Centaurs were therefore, strictly speaking, the children of the Lapith king, half-brothers to the Lapiths themselves.

Unlike the Lapiths, the Centaurs were savage beings. They lived in caves, hunted wild food, fought with rocks, and had no understanding of the arts of civilised men: crafts, hospitality, and religion. The contrast was total. The Lapiths were settled, ruled by a king, bound by the customs of civilised men; the Centaurs were wild, rootless, and violent.

A Note on Ixion

The first murderer and the origin of barbarism

Ixion's crimes did not end with his attempt on Hera. According to myth, he was also the first man to kill a kinsman, murdering his own father-in-law rather than paying the bride-price. For this and for his attempt on Hera, Zeus punished him by binding him to a fiery wheel that turns forever in Tartarus. The Centaurs, born from Ixion's lust for Hera, inherited their father's wildness and violence. The myth therefore builds barbarism into their very origin.

The battle at the wedding

The word Centauromachy comes from the Greek centaur plus machy, meaning battle. It refers specifically to the battle between the Centaurs and the Lapiths at the wedding of the Lapith king Pirithous, son of Ixion, to Hippodamia. As Plutarch explains:

"When Pirithous was about to marry Hippodamia, he asked Theseus to come to the wedding... Now he had invited the Centaurs also to the wedding feast. And when these were flown with insolence and wine, and laid hands upon the women, the Lapiths took vengeance upon them."

— Plutarch, Theseus 30.3

After a long battle, Theseus and the Lapiths either killed or chased off the Centaurs from the wedding. Herodotus, however, offers a different version; he claims the war was already in progress when Theseus arrived to aid the Lapiths.

Key Detail

Why the unmixed wine matters

Civilised Greeks always drank their wine mixed with water. The Centaurs, being uncivilised, drank it neat and became drunk and violent. This one detail encapsulates the whole symbolic weight of the myth: the Centaurs have no idea how to behave at a ceremony, and their barbarism ruins a sacred occasion. For a Greek audience, mixing wine was not a minor table habit; it was a mark of civilisation itself.

The Lapiths, the story goes, had innocently invited their half-brothers to a wedding, seeking to heal the damage done by Ixion. Instead, they became the victims of an unprovoked attack by the uncivilised Centaurs. The story came to represent civilisation versus barbarism.

II · Symbolism

Civilisation versus Barbarism

The Centauromachy might have remained a colourful wedding-feast anecdote were it not for the Persian Wars. The Greeks defeated the Persians at Plataea in 479 BC, and from that point forward the Persians were seen as the image of the barbarian. The Greeks took pride in depicting their victory over them, and one of the most effective ways of doing so was through the visual language of myth.

By placing the Centauromachy on their temples, the Greeks could celebrate their historical victory over the Persians through a mythic parallel. The Centaurs, half-human and uncivilised, stood in for the Persian invader. The Lapiths, Greek and civilised, stood in for the Greeks themselves. This substitution worked precisely because myth and history were not sharply separated for a Greek audience; a temple that celebrated Greek victory over Centaurs celebrated Greek victory over Persians too.

Why Athens especially

The symbolism held particular weight for Athens. In 480 BC, the Persians had sacked the city and razed the earlier temple to Athena on the Acropolis. When Pericles rebuilt the Parthenon beginning in 449 BC, the inclusion of the Centauromachy on its metopes carried a clear message to the Greek world: Athens would never give up, and her gods had taken revenge on those who had destroyed her.

There was a second Athenian dimension to the myth. Theseus, the great Athenian hero, had fought alongside the Lapiths at the wedding. His presence tied the mythic battle directly to Athens, giving the Centauromachy an even greater civic importance for Athenian viewers. Theseus was already regarded as the founder-hero of Athens; seeing him on a temple metope fighting a Centaur was to see Athens herself striking down the barbarian.

Two myths, one message

The Centauromachy did not stand alone. Greek sculptors repeatedly paired it with the Amazonomachy, and sometimes with the Gigantomachy (the battle of the gods against the Giants) and the Trojan War. These four battles form a matched set of civilisation-versus-barbarism myths, each with its own flavour but each carrying the same basic meaning. A temple that displayed several of these mythical battles at once, as the Parthenon did, turned the whole building into an argument about what Greek civilisation meant and who its enemies were.

III · The Parthenon

The Parthenon Metopes

The Parthenon was ringed with ninety-two metopes, the square panels that sit between triglyphs on a Doric frieze. Each of the four sides of the temple showed a different mythical battle:

  • South side: the Centauromachy (32 metopes)
  • West side: the Amazonomachy
  • North side: the Trojan War
  • East side: the Gigantomachy, the battle of the gods and the Giants

Of the 92 metopes, thirty-two depicted the Centauromachy, and they occupied the southern side of the building. These are the best-preserved of the Parthenon metopes, though they remain heavily fragmented. The thirty-two Centauromachy metopes are often referred to in exam sources simply as 'the south metopes'.

The work of sculpting did not fall to one man. A whole team of sculptors was employed, and because of this there is a great deal of variation in the quality and finish of the individual panels. Some metopes are beautifully balanced and carved with real anatomical confidence; others feel stiffer, or awkward in their composition. This is worth remembering when you come to judge any one metope: it was not designed in isolation but as part of a huge team effort under time pressure.

Viewing from below

The metopes were positioned around ten metres up on the temple, which means they were always viewed from beneath. This affected how the sculptors designed them. A rearing Centaur towering over a fallen Lapith becomes far more dramatic when the viewer is looking up from ground level; the beast appears to loom out of the panel, and the drama of the moment of defeat is heightened.

The sculptors also had to work with a square space roughly 1.2 metres on each side. Each metope creates its own compositional challenge. The figures need to fill the space without leaving awkward blank areas, while still allowing the scene to be read clearly from a distance.

Viewing Experience

High relief, seen from below

Because the metopes were so far above the viewer, sculptors often used a mix of high and low relief in the same panel. High relief brought the key figures forward into visibility; low relief kept secondary details from overcrowding the scene. The original metopes were also painted in bright colours and had metal additions: bronze weapons, reins, and fittings, now entirely lost. What we see today is the pale marble skeleton of what was once a bold, colourful scene.

Materials and the Doric order

The Parthenon was built almost entirely of Pentelic marble, a fine white stone quarried from Mount Pentelicus in Attica. The metopes sit within a Doric frieze, the zone above the columns on a Doric temple, made up of alternating triglyphs (vertical grooved panels) and metopes (square decorative panels). On an undecorated Doric temple, the metopes might be left plain or painted; on the Parthenon, every one of the ninety-two was carved with relief sculpture. This was unusually ambitious, and only possible because Athens had the wealth and craft resources of the whole Delian League behind her.

IV · Analysis

Analysing a Parthenon Metope

To see how the three sculptor's objectives, recognisability, realism, and filling the space, work in practice, look at one of the south metopes showing a rearing Centaur above a fallen Lapith. This is the example discussed in the OCR textbook.

Creating a recognisable scene

The topic makes this relatively easy for the sculptor. The Centaur dominates the scene; you cannot mistake a half-horse figure for anything else. Although the weapon the Centaur was originally carrying is now lost, the sense of a battle can still be clearly read through the figure standing triumphant over the Lapith. Since the metopes were viewed from around ten metres below, a rearing Centaur appears to loom directly over the viewer, and this heightens the drama of the scene.

Creating realism

The sculptor succeeds in creating a powerful sense of action. The Centaur's left arm is carved in low relief while the main body is carved in high relief, a deliberate technique that helps the carving stand out when viewed from below. A semi-circular mark above the right shoulder suggests the arm originally held a pot, possibly a wine vessel, which would tie directly into the unmixed-wine theme of the myth. The front legs of the Centaur held above the fallen Lapith, combined with the rear leg muscles and raised tail, capture the Centaur as he rears to trample his enemy.

The Lapith is caught in the moment of defeat. His body lies awkwardly twisted; this is because he has landed on his arms, which are now missing from the sculpture. His right leg is raised while his left leg balances on the leg of the Centaur. Although his face is lost, we can easily imagine a look of anguish.

Filling the space

The rearing Centaur with open arms successfully fills the upper portion of the metope, forming a dynamic 'Z' shape with the Lapith's twisted body below. The sculptor has additionally draped a wild animal skin over the Centaur's arm to fill the right-hand side of the metope. This doubles as characterisation: the skin reflects the beastlike, uncivilised nature of the creature. The twisted body of the Lapith, with its bent knee, fills the bottom of the panel.

Exam Technique

Writing about success

When the exam asks how successfully a sculptor has depicted a scene, you need to form an opinion, not just describe. That means judging each of the three objectives in turn, recognisability, realism, and filling the space, and offering a view on how well each has been achieved. Pick out specific features (the rearing pose, the low-relief arm, the animal skin) and tie each to an objective. A balanced answer might praise the realism but note that the loss of the weapon weakens the recognisability of the battle.

V · Olympia

The Temple of Zeus at Olympia

The Parthenon was not the only Greek temple to use the Centauromachy. The western pediment of the Temple of Zeus at Olympia showed the same battle on a monumental scale. Where the Parthenon broke the story into thirty-two individual metopes, the pediment at Olympia presented the whole scene as a single, unified composition within the triangular gable end of the temple.

This produces very different effects. A metope shows you one duel; a pediment shows you the whole battle at once. The pediment gives the sculptor the space to develop a complete narrative, to show the moment of greatest tension, with figures reacting to one another across the whole composition, but he must do so within a cramped triangular frame that narrows to nothing at the corners.

The challenge of a triangle

Pediments pose a very particular problem: they taper. A tall standing figure fits nicely in the centre, but the corners demand figures who are crouching, falling, or lying down. At Olympia, the sculptor solved this by placing the central god, either Apollo or Zeus, tall and calm at the apex, with the wild, violent struggle of Lapiths and Centaurs packed beneath him on either side. Towards the corners, figures lie wounded or dead, their bodies fitting the narrowing space. The composition therefore has a moral shape as well as a visual one: the god presides serenely above while chaos is contained beneath.

Greeks who visited Olympia for the Olympic Games would have seen the temple and its pediments as one of the great sights of the Greek world. Placing the Centauromachy here, at the central sanctuary of Zeus, where all Greeks gathered, made the symbolism national, not merely Athenian.

VI · The Amazonomachy

The Amazons and the Attic War

The Amazons were a mythical race of women who came from Asia Minor, the western two thirds of what is now modern Turkey. They stayed clear of men except when they needed to reproduce, at which point they coupled with men from a neighbouring tribe. If a woman gave birth to a son, the baby was killed; if the child was a girl, she was raised by her mother and became an Amazon.

Ancient writers disagreed on the details of the Amazons and offered differing accounts of their battles. Their one constant was that they always sided against the Greeks. The term Amazonomachy refers to the mythical battles between Greeks and Amazons.

Two great battles

There were two major Amazonomachies. The first was Heracles' ninth labour, his battle with the Amazon queen Hippolyte for her belt. The second was the Attic War, which followed the events of that labour. Enraged that Heracles had brought back an Amazonian bride for Theseus as a gift, the Amazons declared war on Athens itself. Theseus and Heracles together led an army of Athenians against them.

The Attic War made the Amazonomachy a specifically Athenian story. An invasion of Athens by a foreign army from the east, repelled by Theseus and Heracles, was an almost too-perfect mythic parallel for the Persian invasion of 480 BC. Sculpting this myth on a temple was therefore not just decorative; it was a way of saying: the city has been invaded before by an eastern enemy, and the city has triumphed before.

Like the Centauromachy, the Amazonomachy came to represent Greek triumph over foreign peoples. The Amazons, being both foreign and female-led, were a natural symbolic stand-in for the Persian enemy: wild, Eastern, and ultimately defeated by Greek heroism.

Pairing Battles

Why Centauromachy and Amazonomachy often appear together

Both myths carry the same core meaning: Greek civilisation triumphing over a wild, uncivilised enemy. Placing them side by side on a single temple reinforces that message twice over. The Parthenon uses both (Centauromachy on the south, Amazonomachy on the west). Bassae pairs them on its internal frieze. Together they form a kind of double proof: whoever the enemy is, wild half-men from the north, or warrior women from the east, civilised Greeks win.

VII · Bassae

The Temple of Apollo Epikourios

Bassae was a small town roughly 36 miles southeast of Olympia, in the western Peloponnese. As a small and geographically isolated town, it may seem strange that its people built such a prestigious temple. Pausanias, however, offers an explanation:

"Of the temples in the Peloponnesus, this might be placed first after the one at Tegea for the beauty of its stone and for its symmetry. Apollo received his name from the help he gave in time of plague, just as the Athenians gave him the name of Averter of Evil for turning the plague away from them."

— Pausanias, Description of Greece 8.41.7

The temple was begun in 450 BC by Iktinos, the same architect who helped design the Parthenon. This may explain the admiration it received for its design and sculpture. It was dedicated to Apollo Epikourios, meaning 'the helper', because Apollo had helped the locals through a plague.

A temple of three orders

Bassae is architecturally extraordinary. It is one of the earliest Greek buildings to combine all three classical orders in a single structure:

  • Doric on the exterior, in the conventional fashion, six columns on the front and fifteen along each side
  • Ionic columns lining the interior of the naos
  • Corinthian, a single column marking the entrance to the innermost shrine, and the earliest known example of the Corinthian order in Greek architecture

This is daring. Most Greek temples stuck to a single order. Bassae uses all three within one building. It also has the wrong orientation: most Greek temples face east, so the cult statue catches the rising sun. Bassae faces north-south, apparently because the narrow mountain site left no other option. Iktinos solved this by adding an extra door in the east wall, which might have let morning light reach the cult statue at a sideways angle.

A frieze unlike any other

The Amazonomachy at Bassae was twinned with the Centauromachy and depicted on a continuous frieze that ran around the inside of the temple's naos, the inner chamber. This is unique in Greek architecture. Rather than having to walk around the outside of the temple to see the sculpture (as at the Parthenon), a viewer at Bassae could stand in one place and take in the whole narrative in a single turn of the head.

The frieze itself is made up of 23 marble slabs, running 31 metres long. Unlike the rest of the temple, which is built from grey local Arcadian limestone, the frieze is carved in marble, a deliberate choice of prestige material for the piece of sculpture that mattered most. Of the 23 slabs, 12 show the Amazonomachy and 11 show the Centauromachy. The twenty-three slabs are now in the British Museum, where they are displayed in Gallery 16 alongside casts that attempt to reconstruct the original sequence.

The choice of battle scenes suited the space well. Battles provide busy, interesting compositions for a viewer to explore, and the symbolism of Greek victory was a fitting topic for a small town that wanted to celebrate its Greekness.

The scene runs around the east and south-east ends of the frieze and shows the Heraclean Amazonomachy, Heracles' battle with the Amazons for the belt of Hippolyte. Heracles can be seen on one of the south blocks; he is identifiable by his lion-skin cloak.

Architectural Note

Why an internal frieze?

Most Greek temples placed their sculpture on the outside of the building: on metopes, pediments, or external friezes. An internal frieze was unusual. It meant the sculpture was designed to be read as a single continuous narrative rather than a series of discrete scenes, and it created a more intimate experience for anyone entering the temple. The viewer became part of the space the frieze enclosed. For a small town like Bassae, it also made the temple feel more precious; the most important sculpture was hidden inside, for those who had made the journey up the mountain.

VIII · Analysis

Analysing the Bassae Frieze

The same three sculptor's objectives apply to a frieze as to a metope. The space, however, is very different: rather than a square panel, the sculptor is working along a long horizontal band. This brings different compositional challenges.

Creating a recognisable scene

On the Amazonomachy blocks, the sculptor has successfully created a scene in which a man fights two women. The Amazons are frequently depicted with one bare breast, a convention of Greek art that makes them immediately identifiable; this can be seen on the horseback Amazon. The presence of horses further anchors the scene: Amazons are traditionally shown riding, and this helps the viewer read the narrative at a glance.

Creating realism

The drapery on the right-hand Amazon correctly depicts the movement of the woman towards the left. The shallow relief on both the horseback Amazon and the Greek suggests a snapshot mid-flight: the horseback Amazon tries to flee from the Greek. The horse, although clearly in action, is rather small in comparison to the human figure, a common convention in Greek art, where human bodies dominate even scenes of mounted combat.

Filling the space

The sculptor uses action cleverly to fill the space. The left- and right-hand characters mirror each other in pose, their bent knees suggesting movement while fitting them neatly into the frame. Although small, the rearing horse in the centre fills the entirety of the central area. The sculptor has also made a shrewd narrative choice by depicting the Amazon being pulled from her horse by the Greek; this allows her body to fit neatly into the rectangular space of the frieze while also telling the story of her defeat.

Composition Tip

Mirroring and the rectangle

On a long, rectangular frieze, two common techniques help fill the space. The first is mirroring, placing figures in matching poses at either end, framing a central scene. The second is central anchoring, placing a dominant figure, often a horse or rearing combatant, in the middle. The Bassae sculptor uses both techniques together on the Amazonomachy blocks, and they are worth looking for when judging any frieze composition.

IX · Comparison

The Three Monuments Compared

The Centauromachy appears on all three of our set monuments, the Parthenon, the Temple of Zeus at Olympia, and the Temple of Apollo at Bassae. Each places it differently, and each uses the myth to slightly different ends. A strong exam answer will often use one to illuminate another.

How each monument uses the Centauromachy
Parthenon Temple of Zeus at Olympia Temple of Apollo at Bassae
Location of sculpture South metopes (external, Doric frieze) Western pediment (external) Internal frieze, inside the naos
Format 32 separate square panels Single triangular composition Continuous 31m horizontal band (23 slabs)
Viewing experience From below, ~10m up, whilst walking around From below, whilst approaching the temple From the centre of the naos, turning the head
Material Pentelic marble Marble figures, limestone elsewhere Marble frieze; rest of temple in limestone
Political meaning Athenian victory; Theseus as founder-hero Panhellenic victory at Zeus' sanctuary Local pride; Greekness celebrated by a small town

The contrast is instructive. The Parthenon splits the myth into discrete moments, each metope a separate duel. The pediment at Olympia unifies the whole battle into a single frozen instant. The Bassae frieze spreads the battle continuously around an interior space, dissolving the boundary between individual scenes entirely. Three monuments, three different ways to stage the same mythic battle, and three different kinds of meaning made possible by the choice of architectural setting.

X · Reference

Analytical Framework & Glossary

When the OCR exam asks you how successfully has the sculptor depicted the Centauromachy (or Amazonomachy)?, it expects you to apply a clear analytical framework. The sculptor had three objectives to fulfil when designing a metope or frieze. Your job is to judge how well each has been met.

The Three Sculptor's Objectives

Recognisability · Realism · Filling the Space

Objective One Create a scene that could be easily recognisable
Objective Two Create realism in the scene
Objective Three Fill the space so as not to leave big blank areas
Exam Tip Form your own opinion based on all three criteria

Glossary of key architectural terms

Metope
A square panel between triglyphs on a Doric frieze. The Parthenon had 92 of them, viewed from around ten metres below. The south metopes (32 of them) showed the Centauromachy.
Triglyph
The vertical grooved panel that alternates with metopes on a Doric frieze. Its three grooves are thought to recall the ends of wooden beams from earlier timber temples.
Pediment
The triangular gable end at either end of a temple. The Centauromachy appeared on the western pediment at Olympia. The triangular shape forces the sculptor to use reclining or crouching figures towards the corners.
Frieze
A continuous horizontal band of sculpture. A Doric frieze is divided into triglyphs and metopes; an Ionic frieze is continuous. The Bassae frieze is unusual because it ran around the inside of the temple.
Naos
The inner chamber of a temple, usually housing the cult statue of the god. The Bassae frieze is inside the naos, above the Ionic columns.
Peristyle
The outer colonnade of columns that runs around a Greek temple. Bassae's peristyle has six columns at the front and fifteen down each side (hexastyle).
High relief / Low relief
Sculpture that projects strongly from the background is in high relief; sculpture that projects only slightly is in low relief. The Parthenon metopes use both in the same panel to help figures read clearly when viewed from below.
The three orders
Doric (plain, sturdy, no base, cushion-like capital), Ionic (slender, with a scrolled volute capital), and Corinthian (slender, with an ornate acanthus-leaf capital). Bassae is the first Greek temple known to use all three.
Centauromachy
κενταυρομαχία — from centaur plus machy, meaning 'battle'. The mythical battle between Lapiths and Centaurs.
Amazonomachy
ἀμαζονομαχία — the mythical battle between Greeks and Amazons.
