Introduction

Why do we study the Odyssey?

✦

We do not read the Odyssey only because it is a great story. We read it as a way into the ancient Greek world.

✦

For early Greece we have very few written sources; epic poems like this are among the richest evidence we have for how people lived, thought and believed.

✦

From it we can reconstruct their values (hospitality, honour, fame), their gods and religion, and their society – kingship, the household, women, and slaves.

✦

It must be read with care: it is a poem, not a report. It idealises, exaggerates, and blends memories of different ages.

✦

So we treat it as evidence to be questioned: what can it tell us about its world, and what can it not?

IN SHORT

We study the Odyssey as a source, not just a story – a way to reach a world we can access in almost no other way.

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 02
Introduction

Who was Homer?

✦

Homer is the name traditionally given to the poet of the two great early Greek epics, the Iliad and the Odyssey.

✦

Almost nothing about him is certain: tradition imagined a blind bard, perhaps from Chios or Smyrna, but there is no reliable evidence he was a single person.

✦

The poems reached something like their final form around the 8th–7th century BC.

✦

They look back to a much older heroic age – the Trojan War, remembered from the Bronze Age, centuries before.

✦

For us, ‘Homer’ is best treated as the voice of the poem, whoever stands behind it.

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 03
Introduction

How the poem was made

✦

The Odyssey grew out of a long oral tradition: songs composed and performed aloud by bards, and handed down, long before writing.

✦

Milman Parry showed the poems are built from formulae – repeated, metre-shaped phrases – the fingerprint of oral composition.

✦

The ‘Homeric Question’ asks whether one poet or many made the poems, and when they were written down. It has never been settled.

✦

The poem is composed in a strict metre, dactylic hexameter – the metre of epic.

✦

We look more closely at how oral style works inside the text in Lesson 7.

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 04
Introduction

Read in one order, lived in another

The order we read (the poem)

Books 1–4 – the Telemachy: Ithaca, and Telemachus’s search for news.

Books 5–8 – Odysseus freed from Calypso, and the Phaeacians.

Books 9–12 – Odysseus narrates his wanderings, as a flashback.

Books 13–24 – the homecoming, the disguise, and the reckoning.

The order it happened (the story)

1. The fall of Troy.

2. The wanderings: the Cyclops, Circe, the Underworld, the Sirens, the cattle of the Sun.

3. Seven years held by the nymph Calypso.

4. The Phaeacians carry him home to Ithaca.

5. Ithaca: the reckoning – while, all along, Telemachus grows and the suitors gather.

WHY IT MATTERS

The poem begins in the middle (in medias res). Homer starts near the end, keeps the hero absent to build mystery, and lets Odysseus tell his own story.

Lesson 1

Xenia: the guest–host code

LEARNING INTENTION

Understand xenia as the code governing host and guest, and why it drives the poem.

SUCCESS CRITERIA

Define xenia and its obligations on both sides; judge a host or guest against the code.

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 06
The Concept

What is xenia?

✦

Ritual guest-friendship: the bond of hospitality between a host and a guest or stranger.

✦

Sacred: Zeus Xenios protects guests and strangers, so to break xenia is to offend the gods themselves.

✦

Reciprocal: both host and guest have duties – the bond runs two ways.

✦

Hereditary: guest-friendships pass down families and are honoured for generations.

✦

Practical: in a world with no police or states, hospitality is how strangers travel safely and status is shown.

DEFINITION TO KEEP

Xenia is the sacred, reciprocal code of hospitality between host and guest, protected by Zeus.

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 07
The Rules

What each side owes

The host must

Welcome the stranger before asking who they are.

Give a seat, water to wash, food and drink first.

Offer a bath and a bed for the night.

Entertain, and give guest-gifts (xeinia).

Provide safe escort onward (pompe), and protect the guest.

The guest must

Be courteous and grateful, and respect the household.

Not overstay, and not abuse the host’s generosity.

Reciprocate – return hospitality and gifts in kind.

Honour the bond, and remember it in future.

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 08
The Skill

Judging a host against the code

The checklist

1. Welcome before questions.

2. Seat, wash, and feed first.

3. Only then ask who they are.

4. Exchange news and stories.

5. Guest-gifts, then safe escort.

Run any host through these steps; note which they keep and which they break.

Apply it – two verdicts

The suitors

Break nearly every step: guests who consume the host, never leave, give nothing. The anti-guests.

Nestor at Pylos

Keeps every step: feeds first, asks after, then bed, bath and escort. The model host.

THE MOVE

Measure a host against the steps, then reach a judgement.

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 09
Significance

Why xenia drives the poem

✦

It is the organising value of the poem’s world – the test of who is civilised and who is savage.

✦

The plot is a chain of hosts, and we keep score: the Phaeacians (ideal), the Cyclops (monstrous), Eumaeus (humble ideal), the suitors (abusers).

✦

Theoxeny: gods and kings travel in disguise, so how you treat a stranger might be how you treat a god – or Odysseus himself.

✦

The central conflict is a hospitality crime (the suitors); the ending is hospitality justice (the reckoning).

✦

It links to kleos: good hosts win fame and reward, while abusers earn punishment.

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 10
Consolidation

BOOK 1

Telemachus welcomes Athene

Hospitality comes before curiosity: he greets, seats and feeds the stranger before asking who she is.

A host protects his guest – he seats her apart so the suitors’ noise and insolence will not trouble her.

Pointed contrast: the suitors are guests devouring the very house they sit in.

BOOK 3

Nestor at Pylos

The full type-scene: welcomed into the feast, fed, and only afterwards asked who they are.

Hospitality is bound up with piety – the strangers are drawn into a sacrifice to Poseidon.

Nestor becomes the benchmark every later host is measured against.

BOOK 4

Menelaus & Helen at Sparta

The principle stated aloud: Menelaus rebukes a servant for hesitating to admit strangers.

Guest-gifts matter: giving generously is part of the host’s duty.

The guest’s side too: Telemachus courteously declines the horses. Courtesy runs both ways.

Lesson 2

Nostos: the homecoming

LEARNING INTENTION

Understand nostos as the poem’s goal and organising idea.

SUCCESS CRITERIA

Explain what Odysseus seeks and what blocks him; tell a successful return from a failed one.

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 13
The Concept

What is nostos?

✦

Nostos means ‘homecoming’ – the return home from Troy. The Odyssey is the story of one man’s nostos.

✦

It is more than arriving: it means recovering everything home stands for – his wife, his son, his throne, his identity.

✦

The poem opens with the goal stated: every other Greek hero is home, and Odysseus alone is kept from his return.

✦

Nostos is the engine of the plot – everything is measured by how near or far he is from home.

DEFINITION TO KEEP

Nostos is the hero’s return home – and the restoration of his whole world.

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 14
The Problem

What he seeks, and what blocks him

What Odysseus seeks

Ithaca and his household.

Penelope, his wife.

Telemachus, his son.

His throne and kingship.

His name and identity – to be himself again.

What blocks him

Poseidon’s anger, for blinding the Cyclops.

Calypso, who holds him on her island.

The sea, and the storms it throws at him.

Temptations that make men forget home (the Lotus, Circe).

His crew’s recklessness – and the suitors waiting at home.

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 15
The Skill

Returns that succeed, and returns that fail

The poem deliberately sets homecomings side by side, so we can compare them.

Nestor

A safe, swift return to Pylos – the straightforward nostos.

Menelaus

A long, wandering, wealthy return – delayed but successful.

Agamemnon

Murdered by his wife and her lover on the day he came home – the fatal return, the warning.

Odysseus

The longest and hardest, but the one that succeeds completely.

THE MOVE

Judge any homecoming – did the hero return, and did he recover his world, or lose it?

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 16
Consolidation

After annotating – notes for your booklet

BOOK 1

The goal stated

The proem and divine council name the aim at once: every hero home but Odysseus.

Nostos is set up as the single thing the whole poem drives towards.

BOOKS 3–4

The return-stories

Nestor and Menelaus recount their own returns, so Telemachus (and we) can compare them.

A nostos can be swift, or long and wandering – and still succeed.

BOOKS 1, 3–4

The fatal homecoming

Agamemnon’s murder on his return is raised early as the dark example.

A foil planted now, to be set against Odysseus’s success later.

Lesson 3

Kleos & timē: fame and honour

LEARNING INTENTION

Understand the values a Homeric hero lives by.

SUCCESS CRITERIA

Define kleos and timē; explain how the wish for fame shapes a character’s choices.

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 19
The Concept

Kleos and timē – what a hero lives for

✦

Kleos means ‘glory’ or ‘fame’ – above all, the fame that lives on after death, sung by poets. It is a hero’s only immortality.

✦

Timē means ‘honour’ – the public worth a man is given, shown in tokens: a seat at the feast, a share of spoils, gifts, respect.

✦

This is a shame-culture: what others think of you is everything, and to lose honour is worse than death.

✦

A hero acts, and risks his life, to win kleos and to defend his timē.

DEFINITION TO KEEP

Kleos = lasting fame in song. Timē = honour, and the respect shown to you now.

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 20
In the Telemachy

How the wish for fame shapes choices

✦

Athene fires Telemachus with the example of Orestes, who won undying fame by avenging his father – a model for the son.

✦

Telemachus’s journey is itself a search for kleos: he seeks news of his father’s fame, and by acting begins to earn his own name.

✦

Odysseus’s kleos is already sung – his cunning at Troy is famous; at Sparta, Menelaus and Helen recall his glory.

✦

The fear of a shameful name drives behaviour as much as the hope of a glorious one.

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 21
Consolidation

After annotating – notes for your booklet

BOOK 1

The poem as an act of kleos

The Muse is asked to sing the hero – the Odyssey is itself the fame it describes.

Song is how kleos survives; the bard is its keeper.

BOOK 1

Win a name, as Orestes did

Athene holds up Orestes’ vengeance as the path to fame, to spur Telemachus.

Kleos is set before the son as the goal to grow towards.

BOOK 4

Odysseus’s Trojan glory

At Sparta his cunning at Troy is recalled by those who fought beside him.

His kleos already exists; the poem shows him living up to it.

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 23
Lesson 4

The gods, fate & responsibility

LEARNING INTENTION

Understand how the gods act, and how Zeus frames human responsibility.

SUCCESS CRITERIA

Describe Athene, Zeus and Poseidon; explain the Orestes paradigm and its view of choice.

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 24
The Concept

The gods in the Odyssey

The gods are constantly involved in human affairs – helping, hindering, disguising themselves, steering events.

Athene

Odysseus’s and Telemachus’s patron – goddess of wisdom and craft; she guides, disguises herself, and puts courage into mortals.

Zeus

King of the gods; guarantor of justice and of xenia; he oversees the balance of fate.

Poseidon

Odysseus’s divine enemy – god of the sea, father of the Cyclops; his anger keeps the hero from home.

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 25
The Idea

Fate, the gods, and human choice

✦

The poem opens with a council of the gods, and Zeus’s first speech sets the terms.

✦

He points to Aegisthus, warned by the gods not to murder Agamemnon and court his wife – who did so anyway, and was killed by Orestes.

✦

Zeus’s point: mortals bring suffering on themselves, beyond their fated share, through their own recklessness.

✦

This is the Orestes paradigm: the gods may warn, but humans choose – and are responsible for their choices.

✦

It sets the pattern: the suitors and Odysseus’s crew are both warned, both persist, and both pay.

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 26
Consolidation

After annotating – notes for your booklet

BOOK 1

The council of the gods

Athene pleads Odysseus’s case; Zeus answers by framing human responsibility.

The gods set the plot moving – but insist mortals author their own ruin.

BOOK 1

Poseidon away

He is feasting among the Ethiopians, which is why the other gods can act now.

Divine help and divine anger both shape the timing of events.

BOOK 1

Gods in disguise

Athene works as Mentes, then Mentor – divine help is often hidden.

How you treat a stranger may be how you treat a god.

Lesson 5

Mētis: cunning intelligence

LEARNING INTENTION

Understand mētis as Odysseus’s defining quality, set against biē (force).

SUCCESS CRITERIA

Define mētis; predict where cunning will matter.

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 29
The Concept

Mētis – cunning intelligence

✦

Mētis means cunning intelligence: resourcefulness, craft, the ability to think your way out of danger.

✦

It is opposed to biē – brute force or strength.

✦

Odysseus is the hero of mētis: the proem calls him the man ‘of many turns’ (polytropos), of many wiles.

✦

Where other heroes win by strength, Odysseus wins by wit.

DEFINITION TO KEEP

Mētis = cunning and resourcefulness. Biē = brute strength. Odysseus is the hero of mētis.

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 30
In the Telemachy

The hero of wit, not force

✦

Achilles, the hero of the Iliad, is defined by biē – unmatched force. Odysseus is his opposite: defined by mind.

✦

His most famous feat is a trick: the Wooden Horse, which took Troy where ten years of fighting could not.

✦

Athene, goddess of craft, is his natural ally – she and Odysseus are ‘two of a kind’, both schemers.

✦

In the Telemachy, Telemachus must begin to learn his father’s cunning to survive the suitors.

Lesson 6

Disguise & recognition

LEARNING INTENTION

Understand disguise and recognition as a driving mechanism of the poem.

SUCCESS CRITERIA

Identify a recognition scene and its token; explain how disguise creates dramatic irony.

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 35
The Concept

Disguise and recognition

✦

Disguise: a character hides their true identity. Recognition (anagnorisis): that identity is discovered or revealed.

✦

Recognition usually turns on a token – a sign that proves who someone is (a family likeness, a scar, a bow, a secret).

✦

This pairing is one of the engines of the poem – the whole second half runs on it, and it begins here in Book 1.

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 36
Why It Matters

What disguise does

✦

Disguise creates dramatic irony: the audience knows what the characters do not, which builds tension.

✦

It is also a test: how people treat a disguised stranger reveals their true character – this links back to xenia and theoxeny.

✦

Recognition scenes are the emotional high points of the poem – the moments of reunion and revelation.

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 37
In the Telemachy

Disguise and token in Books 1–4

✦

Athene disguises herself as Mentes, then Mentor – divine disguise, guiding Telemachus while he never knows he speaks with a goddess.

✦

Recognition by likeness: at Sparta, Helen and Menelaus know Telemachus at once – the token is his striking resemblance to Odysseus.

THE PATTERN TO WATCH

Disguise – token – recognition, for the rest of the poem.

Lesson 7

Storytelling, bards & oral style

LEARNING INTENTION

Understand how the poem shows its own telling, and how oral epic works.

SUCCESS CRITERIA

Spot formulae and epithets; explain the internal bards (Phemius, Demodocus).

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 41
The Concept

An oral poem

✦

The Odyssey began as oral poetry – composed and performed aloud by singers (bards) long before it was written down.

✦

Remarkably, the poem contains bards within it, showing how epic was performed: Phemius in Ithaca, and later Demodocus among the Phaeacians.

✦

Song is powerful: it preserves kleos, and it moves its listeners to tears.

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 42
The Mechanics

The marks of oral style

Formulae

Repeated phrases and whole lines, used to fit the metre and help the singer compose as he performs.

Stock epithets

Fixed descriptive tags – ‘rosy-fingered Dawn’, ‘wise Telemachus’, ‘much-enduring Odysseus’.

Type-scenes

Repeated patterns for recurring events – a hospitality scene, a feast, an arming, a sacrifice.

WHY REPETITION

Repetition helped both the poet remember and the audience follow.

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 43
The Poet Within the Poem

Phemius, and song’s double edge

✦

Phemius sings for the suitors in Book 1 – of the Greeks’ bitter homecomings from Troy.

✦

Penelope, grieving, comes down and begs him to choose another song; Telemachus insists the bard may sing what he will.

✦

The scene shows song’s double power: it entertains, but it also wounds those it touches.

✦

Later, Odysseus himself becomes a storyteller, narrating his own adventures (Books 9–12).

Consolidation

Lesson 8

Women in society: Helen

LEARNING INTENTION

Understand how the poem first represents women and their power.

SUCCESS CRITERIA

Characterise Helen; set up the female thread to be developed later.

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 47
The Concept

Women in the Odyssey

✦

The Odyssey is unusually full of powerful, complex women – Penelope, Helen, Calypso, Circe, Nausicaa, Arete, and the goddess Athene.

✦

Their power takes many forms: queenly authority, divine or magical power, cunning, and control of hospitality.

✦

The thread begins here – with Helen at Sparta, and a first glimpse of Penelope on Ithaca.

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 48
In the Telemachy

Helen at Sparta (Book 4)

✦

Restored to Menelaus, Helen appears poised, queenly and self-possessed – the cause of the war now presiding over a peaceful hall.

✦

She knows Telemachus at once by his likeness to Odysseus, and slips a grief-easing drug (nepenthe) into the wine so the company can bear to remember Troy.

✦

She and Menelaus then tell two contrasting stories of Odysseus at Troy – hers flattering to herself, his recalling how she nearly betrayed the Greeks in the Wooden Horse.

✦

The poem keeps Helen deliberately ambiguous: charming and intelligent, yet her part in the war is never quite settled.

Classicalia · Topic 1 — The Telemachy 49
The Thread

Setting up the women to come

✦

Helen is the first of the poem’s women; the others will be measured against her and against each other.

✦

Above all, Penelope – glimpsed in Book 1, sent from the hall by her son – will become the counter-model: the faithful wife against Helen’s ambiguity, and later against Clytemnestra’s treachery.

✦

Watch how each new woman sharpens our view of the others.
