Source A — Pliny the Younger, Letters (c. AD 100)
"It is the month of December, and the city is in a sweat. Loose behaviour is given official licence. Everywhere there is the sound of great preparations, as if there were some real difference between the Saturnalia and ordinary days."

Using Source A and your own knowledge, explain what the Saturnalia involved and why it was important to the Romans. [8 marks]

AO1 (4 marks): accurate knowledge of the Saturnalia. AO2 (4 marks): how well you use and analyse the source.

Mark scheme
AO1 — Knowledge (4 marks)

Award 1 mark for each accurate point about the Saturnalia, up to 4. Accept any of the following:

  • Held on / began on 17 December
  • In honour of Saturn, god of agriculture / time / wealth
  • Celebrated Saturn's golden age of equality
  • Opened with a public sacrifice at the Temple of Saturn
  • Sacrifice performed ritu Graeco — heads uncovered
  • Saturn's cult statue had its woollen bonds untied
  • Public banquet followed; cry of 'Io Saturnalia!'
  • Masters served their slaves at dinner
  • Slaves could speak freely
  • Gambling (normally illegal) was permitted
  • People wore the synthesis rather than the toga
  • A mock king (Saturnalicius princeps) was chosen
  • Gifts exchanged on final day: sigillaria (pottery figures) and cerei (wax candles)
  • Private dinner parties (cenae) held throughout
AO2 — Source use and analysis (4 marks)

Award marks for how well the answer engages with Source A:

  • 1–2 marks: makes simple reference to the source (e.g. "the source says loose behaviour was allowed")
  • 3 marks: explains what the source shows about the Saturnalia's character — e.g. discusses "official licence" as evidence for the role reversal or relaxation of normal rules
  • 4 marks: analyses the source's perspective or limitations — e.g. notes Pliny's slightly ironic tone ("as if there were some real difference"), using this to discuss whether the reversal was genuinely meaningful or merely symbolic

Lesson Summary
1

The Saturnalia honoured Saturn, god of agriculture and time, and celebrated his mythical golden age of equality.

2

It began on 17 December with a public sacrifice at the Temple of Saturn, performed in the Greek rite (ritu Graeco) — heads uncovered, the opposite of normal Roman practice.

3

Saturn's cult statue had its woollen bonds untied; a public banquet followed with the cry 'Io Saturnalia!'

4

The social order was reversed: masters served slaves, gambling was permitted, and a mock king (Saturnalicius princeps) presided.

5

Private celebrations included dinner parties, dice games and gift-giving (sigillaria and cerei) on the final day (Sigillaria).

6

The reversal was temporary — it arguably reinforced Roman hierarchy by demonstrating the master's power and generosity, and made the system more stable.

📐 Key terms
Saturnalia Festival honouring Saturn, held 17 December
ritu Graeco In the Greek manner — heads uncovered during sacrifice
aerarium State treasury — housed in the Temple of Saturn
Saturnalicius princeps The mock king chosen to preside over festivities
sigillaria Small pottery figures given as gifts on the final day
cerei Wax candles given as gifts — symbolised returning light
synthesis Informal dining garment worn instead of the toga
