3.2 In Verrem (Against Verres)

üìö A-Level Classical Civilisation ‚è±Ô∏è 90 min üìä Politics of the Late Republic

Understanding In Verrem: The Speech That Made Cicero

In 70 BC, Marcus Tullius Cicero stood up in a Roman courtroom to prosecute Gaius Verres for extortion. What happened next changed Roman politics forever. This wasn't just another trial - it was a DEFINING MOMENT for the Republic, exposing systemic corruption and establishing Cicero as the greatest orator of his age.

The speech we're studying is called IN VERREM I - the "First Action" against Verres. It's the opening speech of the prosecution, delivered BEFORE any witnesses were called. In normal Roman trials, this would be followed by a long defence speech, then witness testimony, then more speeches. But Cicero had other plans.

What You Need to Know About This Trial

The Crime: Verres was accused of stealing 40 MILLION SESTERCES from Sicily during his governorship (73-71 BC). To put that in context - that's enough money to pay 40,000 soldiers for a YEAR.

The Stakes: This wasn't just about one corrupt governor. It was about whether the senatorial class could police itself, or whether Rome's entire judicial system was rotten.

The Key Players

  • Cicero (prosecutor): 36 years old, rising politician with something to prove
  • Verres (defendant): Former praetor and governor of Sicily, protected by powerful friends
  • Hortensius (defence lawyer): The most famous orator in Rome - until this trial
  • The Metelli: One of Rome's most powerful families, backing Verres

Why Study This Speech?

  • Masterful Rhetoric: Displays virtually every technique known to ancient oratory
  • Historical Impact: Forced judicial reform through the Lex Aurelia
  • Legal Innovation: Revolutionary approach to Roman trial procedure
  • Understanding Cicero: Where his legendary career truly began

How Roman Trials Worked

To understand Cicero's speech, you need to know the basics of Roman legal procedure:

The Court

The quaestio de repetundis handled extortion cases. This was a permanent court established to deal with provincial governors accused of stealing from their provinces.

Presiding Magistrate

A praetor oversaw proceedings but didn't decide guilt - that was the jury's job. In this case: Manius Acilius Glabrio, chosen by lot.

The Jury

Since Sulla's reforms (81 BC), juries were ALL SENATORS. About 50-75 jurors were selected by lot and then subject to challenges from both sides. They voted guilty/not guilty by secret ballot.

Penalties

For extortion, the penalty was repayment of stolen money plus exile. Not death, but political and social destruction. The convicted lost citizenship, property, and any hope of returning to Rome.

The Problem

Senatorial juries repeatedly acquitted wealthy senators despite clear evidence. The system was widely seen as corrupt and broken - senators protecting their own class interests rather than delivering justice.

What Happened After This Speech

After delivering In Verrem I, Cicero called witnesses. For NINE DAYS, Sicilians testified about Verres' crimes. The evidence was so overwhelming that Hortensius realised he couldn't win. He didn't even bother making a defence speech - traditionally the high point of any Roman trial.

Verres fled into exile before the verdict. Cicero had won without needing his second speech. But he'd prepared FIVE MORE SPEECHES detailing Verres' crimes (the Actiones Secundae), and he published them anyway. These became literary classics and cemented his reputation.

The Speech Structure

The published version of In Verrem I has 56 chapters, which we'll study in groups:

  1. Opening (1-4): Establishes stakes and Cicero's ethos
  2. Schemes (5-13): Exposes attempts to rig the trial
  3. Crisis (14-21): Argues the trial will decide Rome's future
  4. Strategy (22-37): Explains why Cicero will use witnesses first
  5. Corruption (39-47): Lists Verres' crimes systematically
  6. Final Appeal (48-56): Demands justice and reform
Opening: Chapter 1 - The Stakes

The Opening Move: Establishing Authority

Cicero opens by defining what this trial is REALLY about. Not just one corrupt governor - but the entire reputation of Rome's legal system.

"I believe, judges, that it is not hidden from anyone that at this moment the eyes of all nations are turned toward the outcome of this trial, to see what integrity and resolution there will be in the senatorial order under this excellent law and this excellent jury panel."
üîç Rhetorical Analysis

ETHOS - Authority Building

"I believe...it is not hidden from anyone": Creates sense of shared knowledge. Not telling them something new - reminding them of what everyone already knows. Makes rejection harder (how can you deny what "everyone" sees?).

PATHOS - Pressure

"The eyes of all nations": International scrutiny. Not just Romans watching - the WHOLE WORLD. Creates enormous pressure on the jury. Acquitting Verres = humiliating Rome globally.

LOGOS - Logical Framework

"Under this excellent law and this excellent jury panel": Flattery with a trap. He's praised the law and the jurors. Now if they acquit Verres, they prove the law and jury are NOT excellent. Forces them to convict to maintain their reputations.

üìå Context: "All Nations"

Who's Watching: Rome's provincial subjects (Greeks, Sicilians, Spaniards, etc.) and foreign kingdoms.

Why It Matters: Rome's power depended partly on perception of fair governance. If governors could plunder provinces without punishment, Rome's authority would collapse. Provincial revolts were expensive and dangerous.

Recent History: The Social War (91-88 BC) had shown what happened when Italian allies felt mistreated. Romans were nervous about provincial loyalty.

What Cicero Is Doing

  • Frames trial as test of Senate's integrity
  • Creates international audience (raises stakes massively)
  • Flatters jury while boxing them in
  • Establishes that acquittal = failure, not neutrality

Why It Works

  • Senators care deeply about their class reputation
  • International dimension makes it more than personal dispute
  • Flattery makes direct contradiction awkward
  • Sets up "reform or collapse" dichotomy from sentence one
Chapter 1 of 4
Opening: Chapter 2 - Verres' Crimes Summarized

The Catalogue of Corruption

Having established the stakes, Cicero immediately lists Verres' crimes. Not in detail yet - just a devastating summary to show the scale.

"You have before you a man who is an embezzler of public funds, an abuser of Asia and Pamphylia, a thief of the city's rights and courts, a disgrace and shame of Sicily, a ruin of the province."
üîç Rhetorical Analysis

ASYNDETON - List Without Connectors

Notice there's NO "and" between the crimes. This creates a RAPID-FIRE effect:

  • "Embezzler of public funds" (financial crime)
  • "Abuser of Asia and Pamphylia" (violence)
  • "Thief of the city's rights" (property crime)
  • "Shame...of Sicily" (moral failing)
  • "Ruin of the province" (complete destruction)

Why No Connectors? Creates rapid-fire effect. Crimes pile up relentlessly. If he'd written "embezzler AND abuser AND thief," it would slow down. This way, each crime HITS before you've processed the previous one.

Strategic Variety: Different types of crimes show Verres isn't ONE kind of criminal - he's EVERY kind.

üìå Asia and Pamphylia

Asia: Roman province in western Turkey, one of Rome's wealthiest territories. Verres served there as legate in 80s BC.

Pamphylia: Coastal region of southern Turkey.

Why Mention Them: Establishes Verres has been criminal for DECADES. Sicily wasn't the beginning - just most recent example. Showing pattern of behavior spanning 15+ years.

Progressive Escalation

  1. "Embezzler" - Takes money (crime against state)
  2. "Abuser" - Hurts people (crime against persons)
  3. "Thief" - Steals rights (crime against law itself)
  4. "Disgrace" - Moral corruption (crime against values)
  5. "Ruin" - Total destruction (crime against civilization)

Each crime is WORSE than the last. Ends with "ruin" - complete destruction.

Strategic Positioning

Not Detailed Yet: This is just a trailer. Full evidence comes later.

Overwhelm Early: Jury thinks "surely not ALL of this?" Creates doubt about defense before it starts.

Set Expectations: Jury now expects comprehensive proof. Cicero must deliver - but he's confident he can.

Chapter 2 of 4
Opening: Chapter 3 - Three Missions

Cicero's Triple Purpose

Cicero now reveals his THREE goals for this trial. Notice how he presents them as civic duties, not personal ambitions.

"I must ensure, firstly, that the reputation of the judicial order may be restored to the Roman people and to foreign nations; secondly, that favor and goodwill may be returned to all these men; thirdly, that foreign nations and allied kings may be satisfied."
üîç Rhetorical Analysis

TRICOLON - Three-Part Structure

Three parallel goals, carefully ordered:

  1. "Restore...reputation" - fixing internal credibility
  2. "Return to favor" - fixing relationship with citizens
  3. "Satisfy foreign nations" - fixing international reputation

ETHOS - Selfless Motivation

Notice what's NOT mentioned: Cicero's own career. Everything framed as duty to Rome. This is strategic - appearing self-interested would undermine his authority. Instead, he's the humble servant of justice.

ASCENDING CLIMAX

Moves from internal (Senate's reputation) ‚Üí citizens ‚Üí international community. Each level BIGGER than the last. Creates sense of mounting importance.

üìå "The Judicial Order"

What It Means: The senatorial class who made up juries since Sulla's reforms in 81 BC.

Why "Reputation" Matters: Multiple recent trials had resulted in obviously corrupt acquittals. The Senate's credibility for fair judgment was at historic low. Cicero is saying "This is your chance to fix that."

The Threat: If senators can't judge fairly, others (equites) might demand jury duty. This happened - later in 70 BC, the Lex Aurelia created mixed juries.

What Cicero Claims He'll Do

  • Restore Senate's reputation for fair judgment
  • Rebuild trust between elite and people
  • Show foreigners Rome still governs justly
  • Prove the system can punish corrupt senators

What He's Really Doing

  • Making conviction about Senate's survival
  • Threatening jury with public anger if they acquit
  • Positioning himself as Senate's savior
  • Creating enormous pressure through multiple stakeholders

Chapter 3 of 4
Opening: Chapter 4 - The Personal Connection

Why Cicero Took This Case

After establishing the stakes and his goals, Cicero explains his personal connection to Sicily. This builds his credibility (ethos) while creating an emotional bond with the victims.

"The Sicilians, a most friendly people and most ancient allies, have entrusted their entire fortunes and welfare to my integrity and dedication. They believe that I alone was chosen by the Roman people to be the one through whom they might recover what they have lost."
üîç Rhetorical Analysis

ETHOS - Trust & Selection

"Entrusted...to my integrity": Shows Sicilians CHOSE him. Not self-promotion - they recognized his worth. Creates sense of duty and validates his authority.

"I alone was chosen": Emphasizes his unique position. Nobody else could do this. Positions him as indispensable to justice.

PATHOS - Vulnerable Victims

"Most friendly people and most ancient allies": Sicilians aren't rebels or enemies - they're Rome's FRIENDS. Makes Verres' crimes more horrific (he victimized Rome's loyal allies).

"Their entire fortunes and welfare": Everything is at stake. Not just money - their survival. Creates urgency and sympathy.

üìå Cicero's Connection to Sicily

His Background: Cicero had served as quaestor (junior magistrate) in western Sicily in 75 BC. He'd treated Sicilians fairly and won their trust.

Why This Matters: Governors who served well in junior posts were remembered. Sicilians approached Cicero BECAUSE of his reputation from 5 years earlier.

Strategic Advantage: Cicero knows Sicily, knows witnesses, understands local grievances. He's not an outsider investigating - he's a trusted advocate who already has credibility.

Building Credibility

Historical Connection: Served in Sicily 5 years ago - not a stranger

Chosen by Victims: They came to him - validates his role

Personal Duty: Can't abandon people who trusted him

Modest Framing: Presents himself as servant, not glory-seeker

Strategic Positioning

Establishes Standing: He has RIGHT to bring this case

Creates Obligation: Must succeed or betray victims' trust

Humanizes Victims: Not abstract "provincials" - real people he knows

Pre-empts Attacks: Defense can't claim he's an outsider or opportunist

Summary: The Opening's Function (Chapters 1-4)

Chapter 1: Stakes - This trial determines Senate's credibility

Chapter 2: Crimes - Brief catalogue of Verres' corruption

Chapter 3: Goals - Three missions (domestic/civic/international)

Chapter 4: Authority - Why Cicero is the right prosecutor

Overall Effect: By end of Chapter 4, Cicero has established WHAT is at stake (Rome's reputation), WHO the victims are (Sicily), WHY he's prosecuting (duty and trust), and WHAT he aims to achieve (reform). The jury understands this is not a normal trial.

Chapter 4 of 4
Schemes: Chapters 5-6 - The Delay Conspiracy

Exposing the Plan to Rig the Trial

Having established the stakes in Chapters 1-4, Cicero now drops a bombshell: the defense has been ACTIVELY CONSPIRING to manipulate the trial. This is a dangerous accusation - he's naming powerful Romans as criminals before the case has even been heard.

"They believed that when Hortensius was consul, with those men as praetors who had been elected through his efforts, they would have no difficulty. When they saw this hope was shattered because of the diligence of the praetor Glabrio, they immediately made use of another plan. They wanted to drag out the case in whatever way possible so that it could be pleaded before Marcus Metellus. They supposed that this Marcus Metellus would be, not only friendly to them, but even completely devoted to the defense of Verres."
üîç Rhetorical Analysis

LOGOS - Specific Evidence

"When Hortensius was consul": Not vague timing - SPECIFIC date (January 1st, 69 BC). Makes conspiracy concrete and verifiable, not speculation.

"With those men as praetors who had been elected through his efforts": Names conspiracy members. Hortensius got his friends elected as praetors. Coordinated corruption at highest level.

PATHOS - Revealing Persistence

"When they saw this hope was shattered...they immediately made use of another plan": Shows desperate adaptation. First conspiracy failed, so they IMMEDIATELY tried another. This persistence = consciousness of guilt.

"Drag out the case in whatever way possible": "Whatever way possible" shows no limits. Will use ANY method to delay. Desperation indicates fear of fair trial.

ETHOS - Exposing Bias

"Marcus Metellus would be...completely devoted to the defense of Verres": Not just "friendly" - COMPLETELY DEVOTED. Destroys any pretense of impartial trial. If case reaches Metellus, verdict is predetermined.

üìå Who's Who - The Conspiracy

Hortensius: Quintus Hortensius Hortalus - Rome's most famous orator, defending Verres. Would become CONSUL on January 1st, 69 BC (highest Roman office).

Glabrio: Manius Acilius Glabrio - current praetor presiding over this trial. Reputation for fairness. NOT in Verres' pocket.

Marcus Metellus: Member of powerful Metelli family. Had been elected praetor for 69 BC. Would take over extortion court if trial delayed.

Why This Matters: If trial delayed into 69 BC, Hortensius (as consul) + Metellus (as praetor) could control proceedings. Defense needs TIME to manipulate system.

The Defense's Strategy

Phase 1 (Failed):

  • Wait until Hortensius becomes consul (Jan 69 BC)
  • His friends become praetors simultaneously
  • Use their combined influence to manipulate trial

Phase 2 (Current Plan):

  • Delay trial by ANY means necessary
  • Drag proceedings into 69 BC
  • Get Marcus Metellus as praetor (friendly judge)

Cicero's Counter-Strategy

Expose the Conspiracy:

  • Name names publicly (can't hide now)
  • Explain exact timeline (shows premeditation)
  • Reveal backup plans (shows guilt)

Create Urgency:

  • Trial MUST finish under Glabrio
  • Any delay = victory for corruption
  • Speed becomes moral imperative
"They thought that because certain men who had been elected praetors were completely devoted to Verres' defense and because they had bought Hortensius' consulship at a great price, they would be able to steal and plot and bribe their way out of any danger."
üîç Key Phrase: "Steal and Plot and Bribe"

Criminal Vocabulary: Three ACTIVE criminal verbs. Not "defend" or "argue their case" - STEAL, PLOT, BRIBE. Defense's methods = criminal behavior.

Asyndeton (no "and"): Creates rapid-fire accusation. Crimes pile up without pause. Makes defense seem frantically corrupt.

"Bought Hortensius' consulship": Shocking claim. Implies consulship itself was purchased. Suggests corruption at HIGHEST level of government.

Effect on Jury: If everything Cicero says is true, acquitting Verres = participating in the conspiracy. Puts pressure on jury to distance themselves from this corruption by convicting.

Chapters 5-6 (Part 1 of 3)
Schemes: Chapters 10-11 - The Bribery Evidence

Following the Money

Cicero now provides SPECIFIC EVIDENCE of bribery. He's not just making vague accusations - he's tracking Verres' stolen money and showing exactly how it's being used to buy acquittal.

"They distributed a huge amount of money throughout Sicily during my investigation there; they shared out cash in Rome itself, both for influencing the choice of prosecutor and for corrupting the jury panel for this entire trial. When these attempts were detected and brought to light by my diligence and hard work, they turned to another method. Large sums of money were given out; men were sent around to certain tribes; the distribution took place at the house of a certain nobleman; in short, they thought that everything could be accomplished and arranged through money."
üîç Rhetorical Analysis

LOGOS - Geographic + Systematic Scope

"Throughout Sicily" + "in Rome itself": Bribery in TWO locations hundreds of miles apart. Shows well-funded, organized operation with geographic reach. Not isolated incident - coordinated campaign.

Multiple purposes listed: Money used for corrupting witnesses (Sicily), influencing prosecutor selection (Rome), bribing potential jurors (Rome), buying tribal votes. Comprehensive corruption at EVERY stage of legal process.

ETHOS - Detective Work

"Detected and brought to light by my diligence": Positions himself as investigator who CAUGHT them. Not speculation or rumor - claims actual evidence of bribery attempts.

PATHOS - Persistence in Crime

"When these attempts were detected...they turned to another method": Even AFTER being caught, they continued! Shows desperation and confirms guilt. Innocent men don't keep trying new forms of corruption.

ASYNDETON - Rapid Accumulation

"Large sums were given out; men were sent around; the distribution took place": Three separate bribery actions with no connectors (no "and"). Creates rapid-fire effect showing multiple simultaneous corruption attempts.

DEVASTATING SUMMARY

"Everything could be accomplished and arranged through money": Reduces defense's entire strategy to one principle - throw money at problem. Simple statement hits hard after complex details.

üìå Understanding the Bribery System

"During my investigation in Sicily": Cicero spent 50 days in Sicily gathering evidence. Defense distributed money to witnesses/intimidate them during this period.

"Choice of prosecutor": Refers to the DIVINATIO - preliminary hearing where Q. Caecilius (Verres' former quaestor) tried to replace Cicero as prosecutor. Money was spent trying to get weaker prosecutor chosen.

"Certain tribes": Roman voting was organized by tribe. Money sent to tribal leaders to influence their members (potential jurors).

"House of a certain nobleman": Cicero DOESN'T name him (yet). Creates suspense - who is it? Also shows he has evidence but is being strategic about revealing it.

The Bribery Trail

Sicily (During Investigation):

  • Money distributed to Sicilian witnesses
  • Goal: silence them or change testimony
  • Cicero detected this during his 50 days there

Rome (Before Trial):

  • Cash for divinatio (choosing prosecutor)
  • Money to corrupt jury panel
  • Payments to tribal leaders
  • Distribution at nobleman's house

Why This Works

Shows Scale: "Huge amount" suggests millions of sesterces. Only possible with Verres' stolen wealth.

Multiple Independent Claims: Not one accusation but MANY separate bribery instances. Harder to dismiss all of them.

Implies Evidence Exists: Cicero claims detection - suggests he has witnesses/documentation to prove it.

Makes Jury Nervous: If bribery known publicly, jurors fear being caught. Makes accepting bribes riskier.

"In short, they thought that everything could be accomplished and arranged through money."
üîç The Devastating Summary

"Everything...through money": Total corruption. Not some things - EVERYTHING. Justice doesn't matter, only money.

"They thought": Past tense = they were WRONG. Cicero has defeated their plan. Creates sense he's already won.

Simplicity: After complex details, this simple statement hits hard. Reduces defense's entire strategy to: "throw money at the problem."

Challenge to Jury: Implicit question - "Will you prove them right?" If jury acquits, they confirm that money DOES buy everything. If they convict, they prove justice still matters.

Chapters 10-11 (Part 2 of 3)
Schemes: Chapter 13 - The False Prosecutor

The Divinatio Conspiracy

Before this trial could even BEGIN, there was a preliminary hearing called the DIVINATIO. The question: should Cicero or Q. Caecilius be the prosecutor? Cicero won - but he won't let the jury forget what this attempt reveals about Verres' guilt.

üìú Source C: In Verrem 1.13

"What sort of man did they seek to oppose to me? One who had been Verres' quaestor, who had been his friend and companion in all his crimes. They thought that if they could arrange for this man to be prosecutor, the case would appear to be underway while in reality nothing would be done. When that plan was overturned by my victory, and when they saw that this man had been rejected by the judges, they began to fear not only the prosecutor but also the judgement itself."

üìå What Was the Divinatio?

The System: If multiple people wanted to prosecute the same defendant, a preliminary hearing determined who would be prosecutor. Called "divinatio" because judges had to "divine" (guess) who would prosecute best.

Q. Caecilius: Had been Verres' QUAESTOR (junior financial officer) in Sicily 73-71 BC. Worked directly FOR Verres during his governorship. Now claiming HE should prosecute his former boss!

The Scheme: If Caecilius chosen as prosecutor, he'd deliberately lose the case. Verres would face trial but be acquitted through friendly prosecution.

Cicero's Victory: Court chose Cicero (January 70 BC). But attempt reveals Verres' desperation and guilt.

üîç How Cicero Makes This Convincing

The Absurdity: Verres' own QUAESTOR prosecuting him? "His friend and companion in all his crimes"? The illogic is so obvious it proves the conspiracy. Why would former employee prosecute boss unless it's fake?

"In all his crimes": Accusation that Caecilius was COMPLICIT. Not just employee - active participant. If true, Caecilius can't prosecute without incriminating himself. Perfect "prosecutor" for Verres.

Exposes the Strategy: "The case would appear to be underway while in reality nothing would be done." Brilliant summary of the scheme. Theatre of justice without actual justice.

"When that plan was overturned": Past tense = Cicero already defeated them once. Creates sense of momentum - he's been winning from the start.

"They began to fear...the judgement itself": After losing divinatio, they fear FAIR trial. Implies guilt - innocent men don't fear fair judgment.

The Caecilius Problem

Why He'd Be Perfect (for Verres):

  • Knows all the evidence (was there)
  • Could "accidentally" omit worst crimes
  • Wouldn't call damaging witnesses
  • Could deliver weak arguments
  • Verres "faces trial" but actually protected

Why He'd Be Terrible (for Justice):

  • Personal loyalty to Verres
  • Implicated in crimes himself
  • Financial ties to defendant
  • No motivation to win

Why This Strengthens Cicero's Case

Shows Premeditation: Defense planning BEFORE trial even authorized. Proves organized conspiracy, not spontaneous defense.

Demonstrates Guilt: Innocent men welcome capable prosecutors (confident in innocence). Guilty men need weak prosecutors.

Validates Cicero: They feared HIM specifically. Confirms he's the right person for this job.

Poisons Well: Jury now suspicious of ALL defense tactics. What else have they manipulated?

"They thought that if they could arrange for this man to be prosecutor, the case would appear to be underway while in reality nothing would be done."
üîç The Theatre of Justice

"Appear to be underway": Appearance vs. reality. Shows defense wanted PERFORMANCE of trial without substance. Fraud at most fundamental level.

"While in reality nothing would be done": Blunt statement. Exposes gap between public show (trial happening) and private reality (fix is in).

Implications: If defense tried to fake the prosecution, what ELSE are they faking? Defense speech? Witnesses? Evidence? Casts doubt on everything they do.

Meta-Message: "They think you're fools." Defense assumed jury wouldn't notice obvious manipulation. Insults jury's intelligence - makes them want to prove they're NOT fooled by convicting.

Chapter 13 (Part 3 of 3)
Crisis: Chapters 14-16 - The Republic on Trial

More Than One Man's Guilt

Having exposed the conspiracy, Cicero now ESCALATES dramatically. This isn't just about Verres anymore - it's about whether the Roman Republic can survive as a system of laws. He's putting the SENATE itself on trial.

"This is a trial in which you will be judging the defendant, and the Roman people will be judging you. In the case of this man, it will be established whether very guilty and very rich men are able to be condemned when Senators are acting as judges. Moreover, he is a criminal of such a sort, that there is nothing in his character except for the worst crimes and excessive riches. Thus, if he is acquitted, no other opinion of him will be held except that which is most shameful."
‚Äî In Verrem 1.14-15
üîç Rhetorical Analysis

PATHOS - The Double Trial

"You will be judging the defendant, and the Roman people will be judging you": Creates TWO trials simultaneously. Jurors aren't just deciding Verres' fate - they're deciding THEIR OWN fate. The people are watching and will judge them based on this verdict.

LOGOS - Binary Choice

"Whether very guilty and very rich men are ABLE to be condemned": Sets up test - can Senate do it? "Very guilty" = morally should be condemned. "Very rich" = practically hard to condemn (because of bribery). Senate must overcome BOTH moral failure AND systemic corruption.

ELIMINATION OF MITIGATION

"Nothing in his character except...worst crimes and excessive riches": Verres has NO redeeming qualities. Can't argue "yes, but he was brave in war" or "he had good family." ONLY crimes and money. Makes acquittal indefensible.

PATHOS - No Neutral Ground

"If he is acquitted, no other opinion...except that which is most shameful": Acquittal CAN'T be neutral. Won't be seen as "benefit of doubt" - will be seen as CORRUPTION. Removes any face-saving justification for acquittal.

üìå Why This Works - Historical Context

Political Reality: In 70 BC, there was ACTIVE political movement to remove judicial power from senators. The Lex Aurelia (passed same year) would create mixed juries. Senate's monopoly on judging was literally under threat.

The Stakes: Acquit Verres = prove Senate is corrupt = lose power to judge. Convict Verres = prove Senate can self-regulate = keep power. It's existential.

Public Pressure: Recent trials had resulted in obviously corrupt acquittals. Roman people were demanding reform. This trial determines whether Senate gets chance to prove itself.

What Cicero Claims

About Verres:

  • Completely guilty (no ambiguity)
  • Has only crimes + money (no virtues)

Chapters 19-21 (Part 2 of 2)
Strategy: Chapters 22-26 - The Revolutionary Plan

Breaking with Tradition

Now comes Cicero's masterstroke. He announces he's ABANDONING traditional trial procedure. Instead of delivering a long prosecution speech followed by defense speech, he'll call WITNESSES IMMEDIATELY. This is unprecedented - and brilliant.

"I will adopt this new style of prosecution: I will bring forward my witnesses immediately and then, when all the facts have been proven by testimony, evidence, documents, and private and public records, THEN - if they want - they can deploy all their prepared eloquence."
üîç Rhetorical Analysis

LOGOS - Strategic Innovation

"New style of prosecution": Openly admits this is unusual. But frames it as necessary adaptation, not rule-breaking. Shows flexibility and practical wisdom.

CLIMACTIC LISTING

"Testimony, evidence, documents, private and public records": Four types of proof accumulating. Creates overwhelming sense of comprehensive evidence. Every possible form of documentation will be presented.

SARCASM / IRONY

"THEN - if they want - they can deploy all their prepared eloquence": Mocking tone. Their "eloquence" (fancy speeches) won't matter against FACTS. Implies defense relies on rhetoric because they can't contest evidence.

ETHOS - Facts Over Words

Positions himself as truth-seeker who values EVIDENCE over PERFORMANCE. Contrasts with Hortensius (famous for flowery oratory). Cicero = substance, Defense = style.

üìå Why This Strategy Was Revolutionary

Normal Roman Procedure:

  1. Prosecution speech (several hours)
  2. Defense speech (several hours)
  3. Witnesses for prosecution
  4. Witnesses for defense
  5. Closing speeches from both sides

Cicero's Innovation: Skip straight to witnesses. Why revolutionary?

  • Hortensius was Rome's greatest orator - better than Cicero at long speeches
  • Defense wanted to use eloquence to confuse issues
  • By calling witnesses first, Cicero establishes FACTS before defense can spin them
  • Shortened trial meant less time for bribery/intimidation
  • Caught defense completely unprepared (they'd prepared speeches, not cross-examination)

What Cicero Claims

"Justice Requires Speed"

He presents his innovation as serving justice:

  • Sicilians have waited long enough for justice
  • Evidence is overwhelming, doesn't need long speeches
  • Quick verdict prevents more bribery/corruption
  • Facts should speak louder than eloquence
  • Traditional procedure would allow manipulation

What He's Really Doing

Neutralizing Hortensius

Strategic calculation:

  • Hortensius can't dazzle jury BEFORE they hear evidence
  • Once witnesses testify, eloquence becomes irrelevant
  • Speed prevents defense from organizing counterattack
  • Witnesses are harder to bribe than jurors (too many, too public)
  • Forces defense to react rather than control narrative

Cicero frames conviction as jurors' DUTY to multiple groups:

  • To Rome: Preserve Republic's credibility
  • To Sicily: Deliver justice to loyal allies
  • To Future: Set precedent that corruption won't be tolerated
  • To Themselves: Protect their own class's reputation
  • To Gods: Uphold sacred oaths as jurors

Appeal to Self-Interest (Ch. 52-54)

But also shows conviction serves jurors personally:

  • Reputation: Be remembered as reformers, not corrupt
  • Power: Retain judicial authority by using it well
  • Safety: Angry populace won't demand reform if Senate reforms itself
  • Legacy: Their children will inherit either honor or shame
"The immortal gods have placed this man in your power not so much to punish him as to show mercy to others - not so that this one man might be crushed, but so that all men like him might be deterred from similar crimes."
üîç Rhetorical Analysis - Divine Purpose

ETHOS - Religious Authority

"The immortal gods have placed this man": Trial is divinely ordained. Convicting Verres = fulfilling divine will. Makes verdict seem predetermined by gods.

PARADOX

"Not to punish...but to show mercy": Apparent contradiction. Punishing Verres = showing mercy to FUTURE victims. Reframes harsh verdict as compassionate act.

DETERRENCE ARGUMENT

"So that all men like him might be deterred": Utilitarian logic. Conviction serves greater good by preventing future corruption. Not revenge - prevention.

The Final Summation (Ch. 55-56)

Cicero closes by reviewing everything one last time:

Chapter 55 - What's Proven:

  • Verres stole 40 million sesterces
  • He corrupted every aspect of Sicilian governance
  • He violated sacred Roman citizenship rights
  • He attempted to rig this trial through bribery and delay

Chapter 56 - What's At Stake:

  • Senate's right to judge cases
  • Rome's reputation for justice
  • Future of provincial governance
  • Each juror's personal legacy

Effect: Leaves jury with complete picture. They can't claim ignorance. Everything necessary for conviction has been provided.

üìå Why Cicero Won

Perfect Timing: 70 BC was moment when Senate's judicial corruption was public scandal. Tribunes of the plebs (Pompey and Crassus were consuls) were pushing reform. Senate HAD to prove itself or lose power.

Overwhelming Evidence: Cicero brought 100+ Sicilian witnesses to Rome. The testimony lasted 9 days. Impossible to dismiss or explain away.

Strategic Innovation: Calling witnesses immediately caught Hortensius completely unprepared. He'd planned to dazzle jury with eloquence before evidence presented.

Public Pressure: By framing trial as test of Senate's integrity, Cicero made acquittal politically impossible. Too much attention, too much at stake.

Rhetorical Techniques: Summary for Exams

Cicero's Rhetorical Toolkit

This section summarizes the key techniques Cicero uses throughout In Verrem I. These are the tools you'll analyze in 10-mark questions.

The Three Persuasive Appeals

ETHOS - Character/Credibility

Establishing himself as trustworthy, moral, and authoritative

  • Shows connection to Sicily (served as quaestor there)
  • Presents himself as selfless servant of justice
  • Claims Sicilians CHOSE him (validates authority)
  • Positions as truth-teller against corrupt establishment

PATHOS - Emotion

Manipulating jury's feelings to create pressure for conviction

  • Horror at Gavius's crucifixion
  • Sympathy for victimized Sicilians
  • Fear of Republic's collapse
  • Shame about Senate's reputation
  • Anger at Verres' shameless corruption

LOGOS - Logic/Reason

Using rational arguments to prove guilt

  • Binary choice: convict or lose legitimacy
  • Schemes prove guilt (innocent men don't conspire)
  • Evidence is overwhelming (witnesses, documents)
  • Speed prevents corruption (practical necessity)

Structural Techniques

TRICOLON - Three-Part Lists

Three missions: domestic, civic, international (Ch. 3)

ASYNDETON - No Connectors

Five crimes listed rapidly without "and" (Ch. 2)

ANAPHORA - Repetition

"I demand...I demand...I demand" (Ch. 48+)

CLIMAX - Building Intensity

Each crime worse than last; stakes increase section by section

Language Techniques

RHETORICAL QUESTIONS

"What city...that Verres did not strip?" (Ch. 39+)

ANTITHESIS - Opposing Ideas

Convict vs. acquit; justice vs. corruption; facts vs. eloquence

HYPERBOLE - Exaggeration

"Laws were non-existent"; "unprecedented crimes"

METAPHOR

"Slip through the net" (criminals escaping); "eyes of all nations" (scrutiny)

Example Question: "How convincing is Cicero in presenting Verres' guilt in Chapter 2?"

Good Answer: "Cicero is highly convincing through his strategic combination of techniques. He uses asyndeton in his crime list - 'embezzler of public funds, abuser of Asia and Pamphylia, thief from the city, disgrace and ruin of Sicily' - where the lack of connectives creates overwhelming accumulation that makes guilt seem obvious and undeniable. This rapid-fire listing prevents the jury from questioning individual charges because they're buried in the sheer volume.

He also employs climactic structure, building from financial crimes (embezzlement) to moral corruption (disgrace) to complete destruction (ruin). This progression makes Verres seem progressively worse with each accusation, escalating the severity. The final phrase 'ruin of Sicily' is particularly effective because it personalises the crime - not just abstract theft but destruction of real people.

The alliterative pairing 'disgrace and ruin' creates memorable rhetoric whilst establishing a binary: Verres brought both shame AND material devastation. In Roman legal context, this works because it appeals to both practical concerns (economic damage) and moral ones (honour). Overall, very convincing because he presents guilt as self-evident through rhetorical accumulation rather than requiring proof at this stage."
