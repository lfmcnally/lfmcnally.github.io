3.2 In Verrem (Against Verres)

üìö A-Level Classical Civilisation ‚è±Ô∏è 60 min üìä Politics of the Late Republic

Why This Speech Matters

In 70 BC, Cicero delivered the FIRST ACTION of his prosecution against Gaius Verres. This wasn't just a criminal trial - it was a DEFINING MOMENT for Roman justice, Cicero's career, and the Republic itself.

The Stakes

For Verres: Accused of stealing 40 MILLION SESTERCES from Sicily. If convicted, exile and disgrace.

For Cicero: A NOVUS HOMO (new man - first in his family to reach high office) challenging the establishment. Victory would make his career.

For The Senate: Their entire judicial system was on trial. If Verres walked free despite overwhelming evidence, the people would demand reform.

Cicero's Advantages

  • 50 days of evidence gathering in Sicily
  • Hundreds of witnesses ready to testify
  • Public support - everyone knew Verres was guilty
  • Brilliant oratorical skill

Cicero's Problems

  • Verres' defender was Hortensius - ROME'S GREATEST ORATOR
  • All-senator jury might protect one of their own
  • Verres had massive wealth for bribes
  • Political conspiracy to delay the trial
üìå Who Was Verres?

Gaius Verres (120-43 BC) - A Roman governor who ruled Sicily from 73-71 BC. He systematically looted the province, stealing art, extorting money, and executing Roman citizens. Protected by Sulla during the civil war, he believed his wealth and connections made him untouchable.

üìå Who Was Hortensius?

Quintus Hortensius Hortalus - The leading orator of his generation, known for the dramatic "Asiatic" style. Recently elected consul for 69 BC. Defending Verres was meant to be an easy victory for him - instead it ended his dominance of the courts.

Opening Attack - Chapters 1-4

Cicero begins by framing the trial as a DIVINE OPPORTUNITY for the Senate to restore its reputation. He doesn't just attack Verres - he puts the ENTIRE JUDICIAL SYSTEM on trial.

"The rumour is spreading, not only among the Roman people, but also among foreign nations, that in these courts as they exist now, no wealthy man, however guilty he may be, can possibly be convicted."
üí° RHETORICAL ANALYSIS

PATHOS LOGOS

Cicero reports what "everyone says" without directly insulting the judges. This is BRILLIANT - it pressures them without attacking them. The phrase "no wealthy man" sets up the central conflict: WEALTH vs JUSTICE.
