3.2 In Verrem (Against Verres)

üìö A-Level Classical Civilisation ‚è±Ô∏è 90 min üìä Politics of the Late Republic

Understanding In Verrem: The Speech That Made Cicero

In 70 BC, Marcus Tullius Cicero stood up in a Roman courtroom to prosecute Gaius Verres for extortion. What happened next changed Roman politics forever. This wasn't just another trial - it was a DEFINING MOMENT for the Republic, exposing systemic corruption and establishing Cicero as the greatest orator of his age.

The speech we're studying is called IN VERREM I - the "First Action" against Verres. It's the opening speech of the prosecution, delivered BEFORE any witnesses were called. In normal Roman trials, this would be followed by a long defense speech, then witness testimony, then more speeches. But Cicero had other plans - plans that would revolutionise how trials were conducted.

What You Need to Know About This Trial

The Crime: Verres was accused of stealing 40 MILLION SESTERCES from Sicily during his governorship (73-71 BC). To put that in perspective, a Roman soldier earned about 900 sesterces per year. Verres stole enough to pay 44,000 soldiers for a full year.

Why It Mattered: This wasn't just about one corrupt governor. The entire Roman judicial system was under scrutiny. Senatorial juries had become notorious for letting wealthy criminals walk free. If Verres was acquitted despite overwhelming evidence, it would prove that justice was dead in Rome.

The Stakes for Cicero: He was a NOVUS HOMO - a "new man" without consular ancestors. He'd risen through talent alone. This trial was his chance to prove that MERIT could triumph over MONEY and aristocratic connections.

The Prosecution Team

Marcus Tullius Cicero (106-43 BC)

Age 36 at time of trial. Previously served as quaestor in Sicily (75 BC) where he gained reputation for fairness. The Sicilians specifically requested him as prosecutor because they trusted him. Currently running for aedile (a magistracy he'd win during the trial).

His Advantages:

  • 50 days of intensive evidence-gathering in Sicily
  • Hundreds of witnesses ready to testify
  • Public opinion strongly on his side
  • Brilliant rhetorical training and natural talent
  • Nothing to lose - this would make or break his career

The Defense Team

Quintus Hortensius Hortalus (114-50 BC)

Age 44, at the peak of his powers. Undisputed greatest orator in Rome. Known for the dramatic "Asiatic" style - emotional, theatrical, with grand gestures. Just elected consul for 69 BC, giving him enormous prestige and influence.

His Advantages:

  • Decades of courtroom experience and victories
  • Support of powerful aristocratic families (especially the Metelli)
  • All-senator jury potentially sympathetic to elite
  • Verres' massive wealth available for bribes
  • Political connections to delay and manipulate trial
üìå Who Was Gaius Verres?

Gaius Verres (c. 120-43 BC) came from an equestrian family (wealthy but not senatorial). His entire career was marked by opportunism and theft:

  • Quaestor under Carbo (84 BC): Stole army pay chest and defected to Sulla's side during civil war
  • Legate in Asia (80s BC): Notorious for extortion and art theft
  • Urban Praetor (74 BC): Fixed trials and plundered temples in Rome itself
  • Governor of Sicily (73-71 BC): Three years of systematic looting - stealing grain during famine, executing Roman citizens, despoiling temples, extorting money from every city

Verres wasn't just corrupt - he was SYSTEMATICALLY corrupt. He had a method, a plan, and the connections to think he'd get away with it.

üìå The Roman Judicial System in 70 BC

Understanding the trial requires understanding how Roman courts worked:

  • Permanent Courts (quaestiones perpetuae): Established for specific crimes. The quaestio de repetundis handled extortion cases.
  • Presiding Magistrate: A praetor oversaw proceedings but didn't decide guilt - that was the jury's job. In this case: Manius Acilius Glabrio.
  • The Jury: Since Sulla's reforms (81 BC), juries were ALL SENATORS. About 50-75 jurors selected by lot and then subject to challenges from both sides. They voted guilty/not guilty by secret ballot.
  • Penalties: For extortion, the penalty was repayment of stolen money plus exile. Not death, but political and social destruction.
  • The Problem: Senatorial juries repeatedly acquitted wealthy senators despite clear evidence. The system was widely seen as corrupt and broken.

Why This Speech Is Studied

For Its Rhetoric: Cicero displays virtually every rhetorical technique known to ancient oratory. His use of ethos (character), pathos (emotion), and logos (logic) is masterful. He flatters, threatens, reasons, and shames - often in the same paragraph.

For Its Historical Significance: The trial forced judicial reform. The Lex Aurelia (passed the same year) changed juries from all-senators to mixed panels of senators, equites, and treasury officials. This was a major constitutional change.

For Its Innovation: Cicero's decision to skip the traditional prosecution speech and call witnesses immediately was revolutionary. It showed that Roman legal procedure, while traditional, could be adapted when necessary.

For Understanding Cicero: This is where his career truly began. Understanding how he thought, argued, and positioned himself here helps explain his entire political career - his alliances, his values, and his ultimate fate.

What Happened After This Speech

After delivering In Verrem I, Cicero called witnesses. For NINE DAYS, Sicilians testified about Verres' crimes. The evidence was so overwhelming that Hortensius realized he couldn't win. He didn't even bother making a defense speech - traditionally the high point of any Roman trial.

Verres panicked. Before the jury could deliver a verdict, he fled Rome and went into voluntary exile in Massilia (modern Marseille). By leaving, he essentially admitted guilt. The jury didn't need to vote - Verres had condemned himself.

Cicero, however, wasn't finished. He had prepared FIVE more speeches for the second action (Actio Secunda) - though he never delivered them in court (Verres was already gone). But he PUBLISHED them anyway. These published speeches detailed every one of Verres' crimes in excruciating detail, cementing both Verres' infamy and Cicero's reputation.

The Result: Cicero became the most famous orator in Rome overnight. Hortensius' reputation never recovered. The Lex Aurelia reformed juries. And Verres lived in comfortable exile until 43 BC, when he was proscribed and killed during the Second Triumvirate - possibly because Mark Antony wanted his art collection.

Chapters 1-4: The Opening Attack - Framing the Trial

Understanding Cicero's Strategy

The first four chapters of In Verrem are a masterclass in rhetorical framing. Cicero doesn't start by attacking Verres directly - instead, he establishes the CONTEXT and STAKES of the trial. He's not just prosecuting a criminal; he's giving the Senate a chance to save itself. Let's break down exactly how he does this.

Chapter 1: A Divine Opportunity

Cicero opens with one of the boldest claims in ancient oratory: this trial has been arranged by DIVINE WILL. This isn't accident or coincidence - the gods themselves have given the Senate a chance to redeem itself.

"The thing which was desired most of all, O Judges, and which alone was thought to be the foremost factor in allaying the unpopularity of your order and the dishonour of the courts, seems, at this crucial time for the republic, to have been offered to and bestowed upon you; not by human counsel, but almost by divine influence."
üí° RHETORICAL ANALYSIS - OPENING GAMBIT

ETHOS - Establishing Authority PATHOS - Elevation

What Cicero Is Doing:

  • Elevating the Stakes: By calling this "divine influence," Cicero transforms a simple extortion trial into a COSMIC TEST. The judges aren't just deciding Verres' guilt - they're fulfilling a divine mission.
  • Flattery: Notice he doesn't say "you corrupt senators." He says this opportunity has been "bestowed upon you" - suggesting the judges are WORTHY of this divine gift.
  • Pressure: If this is divinely arranged, voting to acquit would be defying the gods themselves. Cicero is creating RELIGIOUS pressure alongside legal pressure.
  • Urgency: "At this crucial time for the republic" - he's saying NOW is the moment, THIS trial is the turning point.

Why It Works: Romans were deeply religious and believed in divine signs. Starting with this claim means everything that follows is part of a divine plan. It also makes the judges feel CHOSEN and IMPORTANT, which encourages them to rise to the occasion.

After establishing divine involvement, Cicero immediately pivots to the PROBLEM: public perception of the courts.

"For now, a belief has become established, which is both destructive for the republic, and dangerous for you. The rumour is spreading, not only among the Roman people, but also among foreign nations, that in these courts as they exist now, no wealthy man, however guilty he may be, can possibly be convicted."
üí° RHETORICAL ANALYSIS - THE CENTRAL PROBLEM

PATHOS - Shame & Fear LOGOS - Reported Evidence

Technique Breakdown:

  • Indirect Accusation: Cicero doesn't say "You judges are corrupt." He says "People SAY the courts are corrupt." This is brilliant because it creates pressure without giving offense. The judges can't argue against what people believe.
  • Expanding the Audience: "Not only among the Roman people but also among foreign nations" - this makes it WORSE. Rome's reputation internationally is at stake. The judges should feel ashamed that even foreigners are gossiping about Roman corruption.
  • The Absolute Statement: "NO wealthy man, HOWEVER GUILTY, can POSSIBLY be convicted" - this is hyperbole, but it's devastatingly effective. If the judges acquit Verres, they'll be PROVING this statement true. If they convict him, they'll be DISPROVING it.
  • Wealth vs Justice: This single sentence establishes the trial's central conflict. It's not about evidence or law - it's about whether MONEY is stronger than JUSTICE.

What This Does Psychologically: The judges are now trapped. Their reputation is already damaged (everyone thinks they're corrupt). This trial is their chance to prove everyone wrong. But if they acquit Verres, they'll confirm all the worst rumours. Cicero has made acquittal=shame and conviction=redemption.

üìå HISTORICAL CONTEXT: The Broken Court System

Why was this rumour so believable? Because it was largely TRUE. Here are some notorious cases from the previous decade:

  • Rutilius Rufus (92 BC): An honest governor convicted by corrupt equestrian jury on false charges - showing that ANY jury could be bought.
  • Multiple acquittals (80s BC): Under Sulla's all-senator juries, wealthy defendants were routinely acquitted despite overwhelming evidence.
  • The Stalemate: When equites controlled juries (before Sulla), they convicted senators unfairly. When senators controlled juries (under Sulla), they acquitted each other unfairly. There was NO trustworthy system.

Cicero is speaking to an audience that KNOWS the courts are broken. He's not revealing shocking news - he's acknowledging what everyone already believes.

Chapter 2: Positioning Verres as the Perfect Test Case

Having established the problem (corrupt courts), Cicero now presents Verres as the PERFECT solution - or rather, convicting Verres is the perfect solution. He's the ideal test case because he's SO OBVIOUSLY GUILTY that acquitting him would be indefensible.

"Now, in this time of crisis for your order and your judgements, when there are men prepared to try to kindle the unpopularity of the senate even further with speeches and the proposal of new laws, Gaius Verres has been brought to trial as a criminal. He is a man already condemned in everyone's opinion by his life and actions, yet acquitted by the magnitude of his wealth, according to his own hope and public boast."
üí° RHETORICAL ANALYSIS - ANTITHESIS & CHARACTERISATION

ANTITHESIS - Contrasting Ideas PATHOS - Outrage

The Power of Antithesis:

"Condemned in everyone's opinion...yet acquitted by his wealth" - this creates a SHOCKING CONTRAST. In a just world, public condemnation and wealth should have nothing to do with each other. But in Rome's broken system, wealth OVERRIDES evidence.

What Makes This Personal:

  • "According to his own hope": Verres HOPES his money will save him - suggesting he's not even trying to claim innocence.
  • "Public boast": WORSE - Verres is BRAGGING about it! He's not quietly confident; he's publicly mocking the system. This would enrage the judges because it shows contempt for their authority.
  • "Already condemned": Cicero asserts that public opinion has ALREADY found Verres guilty. The judges aren't discovering the truth - they're confirming what everyone knows.

The Trap: If Verres is "already condemned" by public opinion, then the judges have two choices: (1) Agree with the people and convict (2) Disagree with the people and confirm that courts don't care about justice. Either way, Cicero has framed the trial so that conviction is the only defensible outcome.

Cicero then presents himself not as an ambitious prosecutor, but as a PUBLIC SERVANT offering the Senate a chance to redeem itself:

"I have undertaken this case as prosecutor, O Judges, with the greatest good will and expectation of the Roman people; not so that I might increase the unpopularity of the senate, but so that I might relieve it from the dishonour which I share with it."
üí° RHETORICAL ANALYSIS - CICERO'S ETHOS

ETHOS - Character & Motivation

Positioning Himself:

  • Not an attacker: "Not so that I might increase unpopularity" - denying hostile intent. He's not attacking the Senate; he's trying to HELP it.
  • One of them: "The dishonour which I SHARE with it" - as a senator himself, Cicero shares in the Senate's shame. This makes him an insider reformer, not an outside critic.
  • Popular mandate: "With the greatest good will and expectation of the Roman people" - he's here because the PEOPLE want this. Opposing him means opposing the popular will.

Why This Matters: Cicero could easily be seen as a troublemaker or social climber using this case for personal glory. By positioning himself as a reluctant prosecutor doing his duty, he makes himself MORE sympathetic and his arguments MORE credible.

Then comes one of the most rhetorically powerful passages of the opening - a list of Verres' crimes using two key techniques: TRICOLON and ASYNDETON.

"For I have brought a man before you whose case will enable you to restore the lost reputation of your courts, return to favour with the people of Rome, and satisfy foreign nations: a man, the embezzler of public funds, the abuser of Asia and Pamphyliae, the thief of the city's rights, and the shame and ruin of the province of Sicily."
üí° RHETORICAL ANALYSIS - TRICOLON & ASYNDETON

TRICOLON - Rule of Three ASYNDETON - No Conjunctions

The Tricolon (Three Benefits):

Convicting Verres will accomplish THREE things:

  1. "Restore the lost reputation of your courts" - fixing internal credibility
  2. "Return to favour with the people of Rome" - fixing relationship with citizens
  3. "Satisfy foreign nations" - fixing international reputation

Three is rhetorically satisfying - it feels complete and comprehensive. Cicero is saying: this ONE trial fixes EVERYTHING.

The Asyndeton (Five Crimes):

Then Cicero lists FIVE damning descriptions of Verres with NO connecting words ("and", "or", etc.):

  1. "The embezzler of public funds"
  2. "The abuser of Asia and Pamphyliae"
  3. "The thief of the city's rights"
  4. "The shame...of Sicily"
  5. "The ruin of the province of Sicily"

Why No Connectors? Asyndeton creates a rapid-fire effect. The crimes pile up relentlessly without pause. It makes the list feel ENDLESS and OVERWHELMING. If Cicero had written "the embezzler AND the abuser AND the thief," it would slow down and lose impact. This way, each new crime HITS before you've processed the previous one.

Strategic Word Choice: Notice how Cicero varies his terms: "embezzler" (financial crime), "abuser" (personal violence), "thief" (property crime), "shame" (moral failing), "ruin" (complete destruction). He's showing Verres isn't just ONE kind of criminal - he's EVERY kind of criminal.

üìå WHO WERE ASIA AND PAMPHYLIA?

Asia: The Roman province covering western Anatolia (modern Turkey), one of Rome's wealthiest territories. Verres served there as legate in the 80s BC.

Pamphylia: Coastal region of southern Anatolia, also under Roman control.

Why Cicero Mentions Them: He's establishing that Verres has been a criminal for DECADES. This isn't a one-time lapse - it's a pattern. Sicily wasn't the beginning; it was just the most recent and egregious example. By mentioning Asia and Pamphylia, Cicero shows the jury that Verres has been getting away with this for 15+ years. If they let him off now, they're condoning a career criminal.

Chapters 3-4: Raising the Stakes and Establishing Motivation

In chapters 3 and 4, Cicero transitions from broad framing to personal stakes. He addresses potential criticisms of his own motivation and then introduces THE KEY QUOTE that will haunt the entire trial.

Chapter 3: Win-Win for Cicero

"If you come to a decision about this man rightly and conscientiously, then that authority which ought to remain within you, will still cling to you; but if that man's enormous riches shatter the sanctity and honesty of the courts, I would have at least achieved this: it would be clear that it was the administration of justice in the republic that was lacking, rather than a criminal for the judges, or a prosecutor for the criminal."
üí° RHETORICAL ANALYSIS - PROTECTING HIS REPUTATION

ETHOS - Self-Protection LOGOS - Logical Structure

Cicero's Brilliant Move:

He's creating a WIN-WIN scenario for HIMSELF:

  • If Verres is convicted: Cicero wins the case, proves his skill, makes his career.
  • If Verres is acquitted: Cicero has still "achieved" something - he's EXPOSED judicial corruption. His reputation is protected because he can say "I did everything right; the SYSTEM is broken."

Why This Is Smart: Cicero knows there's a real possibility Verres will be acquitted through bribery. By framing even acquittal as a victory ("I proved the courts are corrupt"), he ensures he doesn't look like a failure. More importantly, this framing creates MAXIMUM PRESSURE on the judges. If they acquit Verres, Cicero will publicly blame them, not himself.

The Subtle Threat: "It would be clear that it was the administration of justice that was lacking" - this is a polite way of saying "Everyone will know YOU FAILED, not me." Cicero is threatening to make the judges personally responsible for public outrage if they acquit.

Cicero then admits to feeling FEAR - but not the fear of losing the case. He's afraid of the CONSPIRACY against him:

"If I may indeed confess the truth about myself to you, O Judges, although many traps were laid for me by Gaius Verres, both by land and sea, which I avoided partly through my own diligence, and partly through the conscientiousness and service of my friends, I still never seemed to be in such danger, nor have I ever been so afraid, as I am now, here, in this very court of law."
üí° RHETORICAL ANALYSIS - BUILDING DRAMA

PATHOS - Suspense & Sympathy

Creating Narrative Tension:

  • "Traps...by land and sea": Verres tried to kill or stop Cicero during his investigation. This makes Verres seem DANGEROUS and DESPERATE.
  • "Never in such danger...as I am now": Cicero fears the COURTROOM more than assassination attempts! This seems paradoxical - why would a law court be scarier than armed assassins?
  • Answer: Because corruption is harder to fight than violence. You can dodge an assassin, but how do you fight a rigged jury?

Building Sympathy: By admitting fear, Cicero makes himself vulnerable and human. The judges might respect his courage for proceeding despite danger. It also makes Verres look even worse - he's so corrupt he makes brave men afraid.

Chapter 4: The Quote That Defines the Trial

Now Cicero introduces what might be the single most important quote of the entire speech - words he attributes to Verres himself:

"Neither the anticipation of my prosecution, nor an assembly of this great size...influences me so much as that man's wicked plans, which he endeavours to plot simultaneously against me, against you, Manius Glabrio, and against the Roman people; he plots against the allies, against foreign nations, against the senate and against the very name of senator; the man who frequently says that 'he who has only stolen enough for himself should be afraid, but this man has stolen enough to satisfy everyone; nothing is so holy that it cannot be corrupted, nor anything so fortified that it cannot be conquered by money.'"
üí° RHETORICAL ANALYSIS - THE DEFINING QUOTE

ANAPHORA - Repetition PATHOS - Outrage CHARACTERISATION

Expanding List of Victims:

Cicero uses ANAPHORA (repeating "against") to create a drumbeat effect, expanding the list of Verres' targets:

  • "Against me" (personal)
  • "Against you, Manius Glabrio" (the presiding magistrate)
  • "Against the Roman people" (all citizens)
  • "Against the allies" (Rome's foreign friends)
  • "Against foreign nations" (international community)
  • "Against the senate" (the institution)
  • "Against the very name of senator" (senatorial dignity itself)

Effect: Each repetition AMPLIFIES the threat. Verres isn't just attacking Cicero - he's attacking EVERYONE and EVERYTHING. He's not a man with a grudge; he's a UNIVERSAL MENACE.

The Quote Itself:

Whether Verres actually said this or Cicero invented it doesn't matter (though Cicero claims "he frequently says"). What matters is it becomes the THESIS STATEMENT of Verres' entire defense strategy:

  • "He who has only stolen enough for himself should be afraid": Modest criminals get caught because they can't afford protection.
  • "This man has stolen enough to satisfy everyone": Verres has stolen SO MUCH he can bribe everyone necessary - lawyers, patrons, judges.
  • "Nothing is so holy that it cannot be corrupted": Even religious sanctity can be bought.
  • "Nothing so fortified that it cannot be conquered by money": Any defense, any protection, can be purchased.

Why This Is Devastating:

This quote becomes a CHALLENGE to the jury. Verres (according to Cicero) believes that money is STRONGER than justice, stronger than honor, stronger than the law itself. The entire trial becomes a test: Is Verres right? Can money conquer everything?

If the judges acquit Verres, they're PROVING HIM RIGHT. If they convict him, they're proving that JUSTICE can defeat WEALTH. Cicero has framed the trial so that acquittal = confirming Verres' cynical worldview.

üìå WHY THIS QUOTE WORKS SO WELL

It's Memorable: The quote is concise, quotable, and captures a worldview in a few words. Even 2,000 years later, we remember it.

It's Universal: This isn't just about Verres - it's about whether the SYSTEM works. Every corrupt trial, every bribed jury, proves Verres right.

It's Personal: By saying Verres "frequently says" this, Cicero makes it seem like Verres is openly mocking Roman justice. This enrages people who believe in the system.

It's Testable: The entire trial becomes a test of this theory. Will Verres' money save him, or will justice prevail?

Summary: What Cicero Accomplished in Chapters 1-4

The Rhetorical Framework

1. Elevated the Stakes: Transformed a provincial extortion trial into a test of Roman civilization itself.

2. Created a Binary Choice: Convict = restore reputation; Acquit = confirm corruption.

3. Positioned Himself Perfectly: Not an ambitious prosecutor but a reluctant public servant.

4. Characterized Verres: Not just guilty, but OPENLY CONTEMPTUOUS of justice.

5. Established the Central Question: Is money stronger than justice?

6. Protected His Reputation: Even if he loses, he's exposed corruption.

7. Created Multiple Pressure Points: Divine will, public opinion, personal honor, international reputation, senatorial dignity.

And all of this is accomplished in just FOUR CHAPTERS before Cicero even begins discussing Verres' actual crimes! This opening establishes the psychological, political, and rhetorical framework for everything that follows.
