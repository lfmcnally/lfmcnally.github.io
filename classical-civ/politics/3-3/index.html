3.2 In Verrem (Against Verres)

üìö A-Level Classical Civilisation ‚è±Ô∏è 60 min üìä Politics of the Late Republic

Why This Speech Matters

In 70 BC, Cicero delivered the FIRST ACTION of his prosecution against Gaius Verres. This was a CAREER-DEFINING MOMENT that established him as Rome's greatest orator.

The Stakes

For Verres: Accused of stealing 40 MILLION SESTERCES from Sicily. If convicted: exile and disgrace.

For Cicero: A NOVUS HOMO challenging the establishment. Victory would launch his career.

For The Senate: The entire judicial system was on trial.

Chapters 1-4: The Opening Attack

Framing the Trial as a Divine Test

"The rumour is spreading, not only among the Roman people, but also among foreign nations, that in these courts as they exist now, no wealthy man, however guilty he may be, can possibly be convicted."
üí° RHETORICAL ANALYSIS

PATHOS - Shame LOGOS - Evidence

Cicero doesn't directly accuse the judges - he reports what "everyone says." This PRESSURES them without insulting them. The central conflict is established: WEALTH vs JUSTICE.

üìå Why Were Courts Unpopular?

In 81 BC, dictator Sulla made ONLY SENATORS eligible for jury service. This created a corrupt system where wealthy senators judged other wealthy senators - and repeatedly let them off.

Chapters 5-13: Verres' Criminal History

Exposing Decades of Corruption

"A remarkable stupidity has been joined to his incredible audacity."
üí° CHARACTER ASSASSINATION

Cicero presents Verres as both DANGEROUS (audacious) and PATHETIC (stupid). This makes him unworthy of protection - he's not even a clever criminal!

"Roman citizens were tortured and killed like slaves"
üí° MOST SHOCKING CRIME

PATHOS - Horror

Roman citizens had PROVOCATIO - the right to appeal rather than face execution. Verres treating citizens "like slaves" violated the core of Roman identity.

Chapters 14-21: The Political Conspiracy

The Metellus Family Plot

THE SMOKING GUN

Gaius Curio publicly congratulated VERRES (not Hortensius) after the consular elections, saying: "You have been acquitted by today's comitia."

The implication: Hortensius becoming consul means Verres will be saved through political manipulation.

üìå The Metellus Brothers

THREE Metelli coordinating to protect Verres:

  • Quintus Metellus - Consul-elect for 69 BC
  • Marcus Metellus - Would become extortion court judge Jan 1st
  • Lucius Metellus - New governor of Sicily (where witnesses live)
Chapters 22-37: Cicero's Tactical Masterstroke

The Revolutionary Procedure

"I reserve for another time that fruit of praise which will be learnt from a long uninterrupted speech; for now I prosecute this man with written records, witnesses, and the letters and authorities of public and private individuals."
üí° THE MASTERSTROKE

INNOVATIVE PROCEDURE

Normal trials: (1) Long prosecution speech (2) Long defense speech (3) Witnesses (4) More speeches

Cicero's innovation: (1) Brief opening (2) WITNESSES IMMEDIATELY

Effect: Defeats delay tactics, forces quick verdict before January 1st!

WHY THIS WORKS

If Verres can delay until January 1st, THREE things happen:

  • Marcus Metellus becomes judge (replacing honest Glabrio)
  • Hortensius & Quintus Metellus become consuls with massive power
  • Many current jurors replaced by new ones

By calling witnesses FIRST, Cicero forces a verdict in August before the conspiracy takes power!

Chapters 39-47: Systematic Judicial Corruption Exposed

Verres' Three-Year Plan

"He had distributed the proceeds of his three year Sicilian praetorship in such a way: he gained for himself the profits of just one year; the profits of the second year were given to his patrons and defenders, and he reserved the whole of the third year, the most fruitful and profitable, for the judges."
üí° THE SYSTEMATIC PLAN

Year 1: Steal enough for himself
Year 2: Steal enough for his lawyers/patrons
Year 3: Steal enough to bribe the jury

This makes Verres' corruption CALCULATED and PREMEDITATED - he PLANNED to buy acquittal!

"Foreign nations would send ambassadors to have the law of extortion repealed and trials stopped."
üí° THE BITTER IRONY

PARADOX

WITHOUT trials: governors steal what THEY need
WITH trials: governors steal TRIPLE (for themselves, lawyers, AND judges)

The system makes things WORSE, not better!

Chapters 48-56: The Final Challenge

The Ultimate Test

"This is a trial in which you will be judging the defendant, and the Roman people will be judging you."
üí° REVERSAL

PRESSURE

The judges aren't just deciding Verres' fate - THEY are being judged. This makes acquittal feel like admitting corruption.

THE FINAL LINE

"I have spoken." (DIXI)

Two words in Latin. No flourish, no drama - just the stark announcement that the evidence phase begins. The ancient mic drop.

What Happened Next?

Immediate Result

After 9 days of witness testimony, Hortensius GAVE UP. Verres fled into exile before verdict. Case won.

Long-term Impact

Made Cicero's career. Destroyed Hortensius' reputation. Forced judicial reform (Lex Aurelia).

Rhetorical Techniques Summary - For 10-Mark Questions

When answering exam questions, always: (1) Identify TECHNIQUE (2) Quote PASSAGE (3) Explain EFFECT

1. ETHOS (Authority & Character)

Examples: Positioning as public servant; emphasizing 50 days of evidence-gathering; sacrificing personal glory for justice

Effect: Makes Cicero seem principled and selfless vs Verres' corruption

2. PATHOS (Emotional Appeals)

Shame: "Everyone says no wealthy man can be convicted"

Outrage: "Roman citizens tortured like slaves"

Fear: "If you acquit, you'll lose all judicial power"

Effect: Emotionally pressures judges to convict

3. LOGOS (Logical Arguments)

Calendar calculations: Counting days to prove delay tactics

Three-year plan: Logical breakdown of systematic theft

Effect: Makes arguments seem rational, not just emotional

4. ANAPHORA (Repetition)

Example: "Against me, against you, against the Roman people..."

Effect: Creates rhythm and emphasis; makes Verres seem like universal threat

5. ANTITHESIS (Contrast)

Example: "Condemned in everyone's opinion...yet acquitted by his wealth"

Effect: Highlights contradictions; makes situations seem outrageous

How to Answer 10-Mark Questions

STRUCTURE

Question: "How does Cicero use rhetoric to pressure the jury?"

  1. Identify: "Cicero uses REVERSAL..."
  2. Quote: "...saying 'you will judge the defendant, and the people will judge YOU'"
  3. Effect: "This makes the judges aware they're being watched..."
  4. Link: "...forcing them to convict or admit corruption"
