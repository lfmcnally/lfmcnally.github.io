3.2 In Verrem (Against Verres)

How Cicero's REVOLUTIONARY prosecution strategy exposed Rome's most CORRUPT governor, the trial that made Cicero's career, and what it revealed about the ROTTEN STATE of senatorial justice in the Late Republic.

What You'll Learn

  • How Verres systematically plundered Sicily for three years
  • Why Cicero abandoned traditional courtroom rhetoric
  • How the trial exposed a conspiracy involving consuls, praetors, and senators
  • What the outcome revealed about Rome's judicial crisis

The Crime and Stakes

The Crime

Verres stole 40 MILLION SESTERCES from Sicily during his governorship (73-71 BC).

That's enough money to pay 40,000 soldiers for a YEAR.

The Stakes

This wasn't just about one corrupt governor.

It was about whether the senatorial class could police itself, or whether Rome's entire judicial system was rotten.

Chapter 1: Divine Opportunity

The thing which was desired most of all, O Judges, and which alone was thought to be the foremost factor in allaying the unpopularity of your order and the dishonour of the courts, seems, at this crucial time for the republic, to have been offered to and bestowed upon you; not by human counsel, but almost by divine influence.
- Cicero, In Verrem I.1

The Public Perception

"No wealthy man, however guilty he may be, can possibly be convicted."

This rumour was spreading not only among Romans, but among foreign nations. The courts were seen as corrupt, and senatorial justice as a joke.

Why Were Courts Unpopular?

The Sullan System

In 81 BC, dictator Sulla made ONLY SENATORS eligible for jury service.

This created a corrupt system where wealthy senators judged other wealthy senators - and repeatedly let them off.

The public was furious.

Chapter 2: The Embezzler Before You

I have brought a man before you whose case will enable you to restore the lost reputation of your courts, return to favour with the people of Rome, and satisfy foreign nations: a man, the embezzler of public funds, the abuser of Asia and Pamphyliae, the thief of the city's rights, and the shame and ruin of the province of Sicily.
- Cicero, In Verrem I.2

Chapter 3: The Binary Choice

Path A: Convict

"That authority which ought to remain within you, will still cling to you"

Keep your power, prove your worth as judges.

Path B: Acquit

"It would be clear that it was the administration of justice in the republic that was lacking"

Admit the system is broken, lose authority.

Cicero's Personal Stakes

Although many traps were laid for me by Gaius Verres, both by land and sea, which I avoided partly through my own diligence, and partly through the conscientiousness and service of my friends, I still never seemed to be in such danger, nor have I ever been so afraid, as I am now, here, in this very court of law.
- Cicero, In Verrem I.3

The Paradox: Cicero survived assassination attempts but fears the COURTROOM more. Why? Because you can fight assassins, but how do you fight a rigged jury? Corruption is more dangerous than violence.

Chapter 4: Verres' Own Philosophy

Nothing is so holy that it cannot be corrupted, nor anything so fortified that it cannot be conquered by money.
- Verres' own words, as reported by Cicero

Chapter 5: Audacious Stupidity

A remarkable stupidity has been joined to his incredible audacity. For, just as he was overt in his monetary thefts, so in his hope of corrupting the judges he has made his plans and endeavours clear to everyone.
- Cicero, In Verrem I.5

Chapters 6-7: The Sham Inquiry

The Fake Prosecutor

Verres tried to get his own man appointed as prosecutor who would deliberately lose.

  • The fake prosecutor claimed he needed "two days less" than Cicero to investigate
  • He didn't even LEAVE ITALY to investigate crimes in Achaia
  • Meanwhile, Cicero spent 50 DAYS gathering evidence across all of Sicily

The Divinatio

What Was It?

A preliminary hearing to determine who had the right to prosecute.

Verres tried to get his own man appointed (who would deliberately lose).

Cicero won this hearing, earning the right to prosecute.

Chapters 10-11: Trail of Betrayals

What else happened in his quaestorship (the first step of honour); except that Gnaeus Carbo was robbed of public money by his own quaestor? That the consul was plundered and betrayed? His army deserted? His province abandoned? The religious duties and rites assigned to him by lot were violated?
- Cicero, In Verrem I.10

The Career Ladder

Cursus Honorum - "Course of Honours"

  • Quaestor (age 30+) - Financial officer, entry-level. What Verres was under Carbo.
  • Aedile (age 36+) - Public buildings, games, grain supply. What Cicero was running for.
  • Praetor (age 39+) - Commands armies, governs provinces. What Verres became in Sicily.
  • Consul (age 42+) - Highest office, two elected annually.

Chapter 13: Citizens Killed Like Slaves

"Roman citizens were tortured and killed like slaves"

This was the MOST POWERFUL accusation. Roman citizenship guaranteed protection from arbitrary punishment. Treating citizens "like slaves" violated the core of Roman identity.

Provocatio - The Right to Appeal

What Was Provocatio?

Roman citizens had the right of PROVOCATIO - appeal to Rome rather than face execution in a province.

Governors could execute non-citizens, but killing a citizen without trial was MURDER.

Verres violated this sacred right repeatedly.

Chapters 16-17: Schemes of Bribery

But look! Suddenly, in these few days, with the consular comitia having taken place, he has returned to the same old plan with even more money, and the same plots are being prepared through those same people against your reputation and against everyone's fortunes.
- Cicero, In Verrem I.17

The Dramatic Reversal

Before Elections

Verres was "so low and downcast" after honest judges were selected.

He seemed "condemned, not only by the Roman people, but even by himself."

After Hortensius Elected

Suddenly confident again.

Returned to bribery "with even more money."

Hortensius as consul meant connections to save him.

Chapters 18-19: The Infamous Congratulation

He sees Verres in the crowd by the arch of Fabius itself; he calls to the man and with a loud voice, congratulates him. He does not say a single word to Hortensius himself, who had been made consul... 'I tell you,' he says, 'that you have been acquitted by today's comitia.'
- Cicero, In Verrem I.18

The Smoking Gun

Gaius Curio saw Hortensius' election and immediately congratulated VERRES instead of the actual consul-elect.

The implication: Hortensius becoming consul means Verres will be acquitted through political manipulation. This is the SMOKING GUN.

Chapter 21: The Metellus Celebration

On those very days when the praetors elect were dividing their duties by lot, and the role of holding trials regarding the extortion of money fell to Marcus Metellus, I was informed that he was receiving such congratulations that he also sent boys to his house to announce the news to his wife.
- Cicero, In Verrem I.21

Chapter 26: Strategic Manoeuvring

I found, O judges, that these plans were set up... so that they might protract matters, by whatever means possible, and end up pleading their case before Marcus Metellus as praetor.
- Cicero, In Verrem I.26

The January 1st Deadline

Why Delay Matters

  • Judge: Current praetor Glabrio (honest) becomes Marcus Metellus (corrupt)
  • Consuls: Current consuls (neutral) become Hortensius + Q. Metellus (both protect Verres)
  • Jury: Current jurors become magistrates, replaced by new senators (who can be bribed fresh)

Cicero has a tiny window to get conviction before the reset.

Chapters 27-29: Judicial Intimidation

The other consul elect sent for the Sicilians... To them he speaks in this manner: that he is the consul, that one of his brothers has obtained Sicily as his province; that the other is to be judge in all prosecutions for extortion; and that care had been taken in many ways to ensure that there was no way Verres would be harmed.
- Cicero, In Verrem I.27

Systematic Intimidation: Quintus Metellus SUMMONED Cicero's witnesses and threatened them. The three brothers control EVERYTHING: consul (highest office), extortion judge, Sicily governor.

Message: Testify against Verres and suffer when we take power.

Chapters 30-31: Counting the Days

Today is the Nones of August. You began your meeting at the eighth hour. This day they do not even count. There are ten days before the votive games... These games will take fifteen days, and then the Roman games will follow immediately. And so, when almost forty days have passed, only then will they think that they will have to respond.
- Cicero, In Verrem I.31

Why Trials Stopped for Games

Roman Ludi (Games)

Roman games were religious festivals when courts closed.

Major festivals in autumn created significant delays:

  • Votive games (Pompey's) - 15 days
  • Roman games - more days
  • Games of Victory - more days
  • Plebeian games - more days

Defense could easily push past January 1st using these breaks.

Chapter 33: The Revolutionary Procedure

I reserve for another time that fruit of praise which will be learnt from a long uninterrupted speech; for now I prosecute this man with written records, witnesses, and the letters and authorities of public and private individuals. This whole thing will be between you and me, Hortensius.
- Cicero, In Verrem I.33

The Masterstroke

What Cicero Did Differently

  • Normal: Long speech -> Defense speech -> Witnesses -> More speeches
  • Cicero: Skip speeches -> Call witnesses IMMEDIATELY -> Evidence speaks

This defeated the delay strategy, prevented Hortensius responding with eloquence, and forced a quick verdict before January 1st.

Chapter 40: Verres' Three-Year Plan

He had distributed the proceeds of his three year Sicilian praetorship in such a way: the profits of ONE year were for HIMSELF; the profits of the SECOND year were given to his patrons and defenders; and he reserved the WHOLE of the THIRD year, the most fruitful and profitable, for the JUDGES.
- Cicero, In Verrem I.40

Systematic Criminal Planning

Year 1

Steal for HIMSELF

Personal enrichment

Year 2

Steal for LAWYERS/PATRONS

Buy protection

Year 3

Steal for JURY BRIBERY

The "most fruitful" year

Chapter 41: The Bitter Irony

I thought that foreign nations would send ambassadors to the Roman people to have the law of extortion repealed and trials stopped. For if there were no trials, each man would only take as much as he thought enough for himself; currently because there are trials... everyone carries off as much as they need to satisfy themselves, their patrons, advocates, the praetor, and the judges.
- Cicero, In Verrem I.41

The Paradox: Extortion trials were supposed to PROTECT provinces.

But trials are so corrupt that provinces would be BETTER OFF without them!

Without trials: governors steal what THEY need.
WITH trials: governors steal TRIPLE (for themselves, lawyers, AND judges).

Chapters 43-45: Pompey's Support

When Pompeius had said in the assembly that the provinces were plundered and plagued; that the law courts had become disgraceful and wicked; and that he wished to look into and make provisions for this situation; then indeed the Roman people showed their assent not with a shout, but with the greatest uproar.
- Cicero, In Verrem I.45

Why Pompey's Opinion Matters

Pompey the Great

  • Rome's greatest living general
  • Just elected consul (despite not meeting age requirements)
  • Promised to restore tribune powers
  • Publicly demanded judicial reform
  • Has military might to back demands

If Senate doesn't clean up courts voluntarily, Pompey will FORCE reform.

Chapter 47: The Ultimate Test

This is a trial in which you will be judging the defendant, and the Roman people will be judging you.
- Cicero, In Verrem I.47

Chapter 50: Sacred Oath

I promise this to you, O Judges, and to the Roman people; by Hercules, my life shall fail before my strength and perseverance in prosecuting their wickedness.
- Cicero, In Verrem I.50

"By Hercules" - invoking the hero-god of impossible labors.

Cicero vows to pursue corruption until DEATH. This isn't just a trial - it's a CRUSADE. He's the tireless defender of justice, willing to sacrifice everything.

Chapters 55-56: The Innovative Approach

I will adopt this course... to call the witnesses immediately. What you will recognise as new from me is that I will arrange my witnesses so that the whole accusation is explained... so that there will be no difference between the customary method of prosecution and this new one, except that according to the usual method, when everything has been said, only then are the witnesses called. Here, they will be produced as each individual matter is reached.
- Cicero, In Verrem I.55

The Final Statement

We say that Gaius Verres has done many licentious deeds, many cruel ones against Roman citizens and allies, and many wicked acts against gods and men; but especially that he has stolen four hundred thousand sesterces from Sicily contrary to the laws. We shall make this clear to you with witnesses, with public records, and with private authorities... I have spoken.
- Cicero, In Verrem I.56

What Happened Next

The Outcome

  • Cicero called 100+ Sicilian witnesses over NINE DAYS
  • Hortensius delivered NO defense speech - silence = admission of defeat
  • Verres fled Rome into voluntary exile in Massilia before verdict
  • Property confiscated, permanently exiled
  • Lex Aurelia passed - reformed juries as Cicero predicted
  • Cicero's career launched - from novus homo to leading advocate

Key Points Summary

The Crime

40 million sesterces stolen from Sicily; Roman citizens killed "like slaves"; systematic three-year plunder

The Conspiracy

Metellus brothers + Hortensius coordinating; witnesses intimidated; delay strategy to push past January 1st

The Victory

Revolutionary procedure defeated delay; Verres fled; judicial reform forced; Cicero's career launched

Exit Question 1

Question 1 of 5
How did Cicero present the trial as a test of the entire senatorial order, not just Verres' individual guilt?
Cicero framed the trial as a "divine opportunity" for senators to restore their reputation. He told the jury: "You will be judging the defendant, and the Roman people will be judging YOU." He warned that if they acquitted Verres, it would confirm the public belief that "no wealthy man, however guilty, can possibly be convicted." The rumour was spreading not just among Romans but "among foreign nations" - making senatorial justice an international embarrassment. Conviction would prove senators could self-regulate; acquittal would prove they needed external reform (which indeed came with the Lex Aurelia).

Exit Question 2

Question 2 of 5
What was the conspiracy to delay the trial, and why was January 1st, 69 BC the crucial deadline?
The defense strategy was to delay past January 1st when everything would change in their favor: (1) The current honest judge Glabrio would be replaced by Marcus METELLUS as extortion court praetor - friendly to Verres. (2) The consuls would become HORTENSIUS (Verres' lawyer!) and Quintus METELLUS (brother of the judge). (3) Current jurors would become magistrates, replaced by new senators who could be bribed fresh. The Metellus family was a coordinated conspiracy - THREE brothers controlling consul, extortion judge, and Sicily governorship. Roman games and festivals would eat up 40+ days, making delay easy. Cicero's revolutionary decision to skip long speeches and call witnesses immediately defeated this strategy by forcing a quick verdict.

Exit Question 3

Question 3 of 5
What was revolutionary about Cicero's prosecution strategy, and why did it defeat Hortensius?
Traditional Roman trials followed a pattern: prosecution speech (could last DAYS) -> defense speech (equally long) -> witnesses -> more speeches -> verdict. This process took WEEKS or MONTHS - perfect for Verres' delay strategy. Cicero REVERSED this: he delivered only a short opening speech, then called 100+ witnesses IMMEDIATELY. He organized witnesses by topic so "the whole accusation is explained" through testimony rather than rhetoric. This (1) defeated the delay strategy - trial finished in NINE days not months, (2) neutralized Hortensius - Rome's greatest orator couldn't dazzle the jury before facts were established, (3) forced defense to react to overwhelming evidence rather than control the narrative. Hortensius was so outmaneuvered he delivered NO defense speech - his silence was an admission of defeat.

Exit Question 4

Question 4 of 5
How does Cicero use Verres' own words and reported boasts against him?
Cicero devastatingly quotes Verres' own philosophy: "Nothing is so holy that it cannot be corrupted, nor anything so fortified that it cannot be conquered by money." He reports Verres' THREE-YEAR PLAN: one year's profits for himself, second year for lawyers/patrons, third year (the "most fruitful") reserved for bribing judges. These weren't secret confessions - Verres said these things "while many people were listening" in Sicily, showing his contempt for law and confidence money would protect him. Similarly, when Curio congratulated Verres (not the newly-elected consul!) saying "you have been acquitted by today's comitia," Cicero uses this as a "smoking gun" proving the conspiracy. By quoting specific words with specific witnesses, Cicero makes his accusations feel documented and verifiable rather than rhetorical exaggeration.

Exit Question 5

Question 5 of 5
What did the Verres trial reveal about the Roman judicial system, and what reforms followed?
The trial exposed that Rome's judicial system was systemically corrupt, not occasionally flawed. Since Sulla's reforms (81 BC), ONLY senators could serve on juries, creating a closed system where wealthy senators judged other wealthy senators - and repeatedly let them off. Cicero's bitter irony: provinces might be BETTER OFF without extortion trials, because trials forced governors to steal TRIPLE (for themselves, lawyers, AND judges). The trial showed: (1) Witnesses could be intimidated by powerful families, (2) Judge selection could be manipulated, (3) Religious festivals weaponized for delay, (4) Entire conspiracies coordinated among elite families. The result was the LEX AURELIA (70 BC), which reformed juries to include senators (1/3), equestrians (1/3), and tribuni aerarii (1/3). Different classes would watch each other. Cicero's warning came true: prove you can self-regulate, or lose power entirely. The Senate couldn't fix itself voluntarily - external reform was required.
