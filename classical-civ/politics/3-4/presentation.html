3.4 Pompey and Crassus: Military Power in Politics

How MILITARY SUCCESS and VAST WEALTH could bypass traditional political institutions, creating the patterns that destroyed the Republic.

Learning Intention

To understand how Pompey and Crassus used military success and wealth to bypass traditional career paths, how their joint consulship violated Republican norms, and how their rivalry created political deadlock by 60 BC.

Success Criteria

  • Explain the different routes to power taken by Pompey (military glory) and Crassus (wealth and patronage)
  • Identify specific constitutional violations in their 70 BC consulship and evaluate whether these were justified
  • Analyse the significance of Pompey's extraordinary commands (Lex Gabinia and Lex Manilia) as constitutional precedents
  • Explain how the Pompey-Crassus inimicitia paralysed Republican politics and why neither could dominate alone by 60 BC

The New Model: Generals as Politicians

Pompey and Crassus represent a crucial turning point: military commanders using armies as springboards to political power, bypassing traditional career paths and constitutional norms. Their methods would become the blueprint for the Late Republic's collapse.

Two Routes to Power

Pompey's Route

Military glory through spectacular campaigns and popular support

Crassus's Route

Immense wealth used for patronage and political influence

Both men showed that armies + popular support could override senatorial authority. This established a dangerous precedent: military success trumps constitutional propriety.

Pompey the Great: The Teenage General

Gnaeus Pompeius Magnus (106-48 BC) earned his reputation early through ruthless military efficiency. Unlike Crassus, who bought influence, Pompey won it through spectacular victories.

The Unusual Start

Pompey raised a private army at age 23 to support Sulla during the civil war. This was ILLEGAL - only magistrates could command armies. But Sulla needed troops, so he accepted Pompey's help and gave him military commands despite his youth. Sulla's willingness to bend rules for effective commanders set a dangerous precedent.

Key Early Campaigns

Under Sulla (83-81 BC)

Campaigns in Sicily and Africa with such efficiency that Sulla greeted him as "Magnus" (the Great). Pompey was still in his early twenties.

Spain (77-71 BC)

Sent to suppress Sertorius's rebellion. Struggled initially but eventually succeeded after Sertorius's assassination. Claimed full credit.

Spartacus (71 BC)

Returning from Spain, killed fleeing remnants of Spartacus's army - then claimed he'd "finished" the revolt Crassus had already crushed.

For Your Essays: Pompey's early career demonstrates how military success could substitute for traditional qualifications. He never held the praetorship or followed the cursus honorum properly, yet his military achievements made him politically indispensable. This pattern would be crucial for understanding Caesar's later rise.

Marcus Licinius Crassus: The Richest Man in Rome

Whilst Pompey won glory through military campaigns, Crassus (c. 115-53 BC) accumulated power through vast wealth and careful political patronage. He represented a different but equally effective path to influence.

How Did He Get So Rich?

Property Speculation

Maintained Rome's only fire brigade - refused to deploy until property owners sold to him at rock-bottom prices. Morally questionable? Absolutely. Profitable? Extremely.

Sulla's Proscriptions

Bought confiscated properties from executed enemies at auction prices. Extraordinarily wealthy but morally compromised.

Silver Mines & Tax Farming

Controlled silver mines in Spain and invested in tax-collection contracts, earning massive profits from Rome's expanding empire.

How He Used His Wealth

Crassus weaponised patronage on an unprecedented scale:

  • Provided interest-free loans to indebted senators (creating obligated clients)
  • Funded electoral campaigns for political allies
  • Bought votes directly when necessary
  • Built vast networks of clients throughout Roman society

The Spartacus Campaign (73-71 BC)

Ruthless Efficiency: When one of his legions broke and ran, he used decimation - executing 1 in 10 soldiers chosen by lot. He trapped Spartacus's army, defeated them in battle, and then crucified 6,000 captured slaves along the Appian Way from Rome to Capua - a 120-mile line of crosses as a warning.

Then Pompey Arrived... Just as Crassus finished crushing the revolt, Pompey returned from Spain and killed a few thousand fleeing slaves. In his dispatch to Rome, Pompey claimed credit for "extirpating the war at its roots" - implying HE had finished what Crassus started. Crassus was FURIOUS. This insult created a personal rivalry that would define both their careers.

The Consulship of 70 BC: Breaking All the Rules

By late 71 BC, both Pompey and Crassus had armies camped outside Rome and demanded to be elected consuls for 70 BC. This was constitutionally outrageous - but the Senate couldn't refuse.

Constitutional Violations

Pompey skipped ALL offices

He'd skipped the entire cursus honorum - quaestor, aedile, praetor. He'd never even been a senator!

Both too young

Legal minimum age for consul was 42. Pompey was 36, Crassus about 45 but had skipped required offices.

Kept their armies

Commanders were supposed to dismiss armies before entering the city. Both maintained military forces as implicit threats.

Why Did the Senate Allow This?

They didn't really have a choice. Both Pompey and Crassus had their armies camped outside Rome. The Senate's options were:

  • Bend the rules and let them be consuls
  • Refuse and risk civil war
The Senate chose survival over principle.

What They Did as Consuls

Positive Actions

  • Restored tribunician powers that Sulla had stripped away
  • Reformed the jury system to reduce senatorial corruption
  • Appointed new censors who removed corrupt senators

The Paradox

Pompey broke constitutional rules to get power, but then used that power to strengthen Republican institutions. Was he defending or destroying the Republic? Both, simultaneously.

The Long-Term Damage: Even though Pompey and Crassus governed well, the precedent was disastrous. If you could become consul without following the proper career path, what was the point of the cursus honorum? The message was clear: military success could override any constitutional requirement.

Lex Gabinia (67 BC) - The Pirate Command

The Crisis

Mediterranean piracy had become a CRISIS. Pirates controlled shipping lanes, raided coastal cities, and even kidnapped prominent Romans. Traditional military responses had failed completely.

What the Lex Gabinia Gave Pompey

Imperium over ENTIRE Mediterranean

+ 50 miles inland from ALL coasts - unprecedented territorial scope

500 ships & 120,000 troops

Massive resources at his personal command

24 legates + 3 years

Could appoint subordinate commanders - building a personal power network

Spectacular Success

Cleared in THREE MONTHS: Pompey cleared the Mediterranean of pirates in just three months - a stunning achievement that proved his organisational genius. He divided the sea into sectors, coordinated simultaneous operations, and offered generous terms to pirates who surrendered. The people LOVED him for it.

Lex Manilia (66 BC) - The Eastern Command

The Problem

Mithridates VI of Pontus had been Rome's enemy for DECADES. The current commander, Lucullus, had achieved military success but lost his troops' loyalty through harsh discipline. His soldiers refused to fight, and Mithridates was regrouping.

What the Lex Manilia Gave Pompey

Supreme Command

Over Rome's ENTIRE eastern frontier, replacing Lucullus

Richest Provinces

Asia, Cilicia, Bithynia - enormous wealth and resources

Royal Powers

Authority to make war and peace, appoint client kings, reorganise territories

Cicero's Role

Virtue, fame, success and fortune all meet in one man - Pompey.
- Cicero, Pro Lege Manilia
By supporting Pompey's command, Cicero tied his political fate to Pompey's success. This alliance between the greatest orator and the greatest general shaped Roman politics throughout the 60s BC.

Pompey in the East (66-62 BC)

Military Victory

Defeated Mithridates VI, ending decades of conflict in the East

Provincial Reorganisation

Created new provinces and client kingdoms across the region

Financial Windfall

Brought enormous wealth back to Rome's treasury

The Most Powerful Roman

Pompey personally drew the boundaries of kingdoms and provinces. He decided who would be kings and who would be Rome's subjects. He brought back so much wealth that it transformed Rome's economy. He was, by 62 BC, the most powerful Roman alive.

But There's a Problem... Whilst Pompey was achieving military glory in the East (66-62 BC), his enemies at home were consolidating their positions. Success abroad meant vulnerability at home.

The Pompey-Crassus Rivalry

Their personal hatred created a political vacuum that the Republic couldn't resolve. By 60 BC, neither could dominate Rome alone, but they refused to work together permanently. This deadlock would require a solution - but that belongs to the next phase of the Late Republic.

The Nature of Roman Inimicitia

This wasn't just personal dislike - it was a formal state of enmity that required public displays of opposition. Pompey couldn't support Crassus's initiatives without losing face, and vice versa. Their inimicitia was so deep that even when forced to cooperate as co-consuls in 70 BC, they could barely speak to each other.

Pompey's Advantages vs Crassus's Advantages

Pompey

  • Military Glory: Spectacular victories, popular hero
  • Popular Support: The people loved him
  • Constitutional Powers: Extraordinary commands by popular vote

Crassus

  • Vast Wealth: Could buy support, fund campaigns, make loans
  • Political Networks: Extensive client relationships
  • Rome-Based Power: Stayed in Rome whilst Pompey was abroad

The Famous Reconciliation Scene (70 BC)

At the end of their consulship, Pompey and Crassus publicly reconciled in the Forum. According to Plutarch, they shook hands in front of the assembled people, who cheered at this display of amicitia restored.

But everyone knew it was theatre. They were performing amicitia for political necessity whilst their inimicitia remained intact. The moment they left office, they went back to competing for power and influence.

The Deadlock (62 BC)

Pompey's Problem

When he returned from the East in 62 BC, the Senate refused to ratify his eastern settlement or grant land to his veterans. His military glory meant nothing without political support.

Crassus's Problem

Despite his wealth and political networks, he lacked the military prestige that commanded popular support. The people didn't love him.

Result: Stalemate

Neither man could dominate Rome alone, but they refused to work together. The Republic was stuck.

Long-Term Impact: Constitutional Erosion

Every time Pompey broke a rule (skipping offices, keeping armies, accepting extraordinary commands), it became easier for the next commander to do the same. Constitutional norms only work when people believe in them.

Personal Power Networks: Crassus demonstrated that vast wealth could create political power independent of official position. Politics was increasingly about personal loyalty rather than factional alignment.

Army Loyalty

Both men exploited the consequences of Marius's military reforms. Soldiers were now landless volunteers dependent on their commanders for rewards. Armies followed generals who could give them land, wealth, and victory - not senators in Rome or abstract duty to the state.

Setting the Stage for What's Next

  • Constitutional Rules Can Be Broken: Pompey proved you could skip offices and still become consul
  • Popular Assemblies Trump the Senate: The Lex Gabinia and Lex Manilia showed how to bypass senatorial opposition
  • Military Success Overrides Everything: Spectacular victories gave commanders political capital to demand almost anything
  • Personal Rivalry Paralyses the State: By 60 BC, their mutual hatred had created political deadlock

The Crisis of 60 BC: By 60 BC, the Republic faced an impossible situation: Pompey couldn't get his settlements ratified, Crassus couldn't achieve military glory to match his wealth, and neither could dominate Rome alone. Their rivalry had created a political vacuum. How this deadlock was resolved belongs to Topic 4: The Collapse of Concordia.

Exit Question 2

Question 2 of 5
What specific constitutional violations occurred in the consulship of 70 BC, and why did the Senate allow them?
Three major violations: (1) Pompey had never held ANY lower office - skipped entire cursus honorum; (2) Both were too young (Pompey was 36, minimum was 42) and had skipped required offices; (3) Both kept their armies camped outside Rome as implicit threats when commanders were supposed to dismiss armies before entering. The Senate allowed this because they had no choice - with both armies threatening Rome, their options were: bend the rules and let them be consuls, or refuse and risk civil war. They chose survival over principle.

Exit Question 3

Question 3 of 5
Why were the Lex Gabinia and Lex Manilia so significant as constitutional precedents?
These laws established that popular assemblies could grant EXTRAORDINARY powers to favoured commanders, bypassing senatorial control. The Lex Gabinia gave Pompey imperium over the entire Mediterranean + 50 miles inland, 500 ships, 120,000 troops, 24 legates, and three years - unprecedented scope. The Lex Manilia just ONE YEAR later gave him supreme command over Rome's richest eastern provinces with authority to make war and peace and appoint client kings. The pattern became normalised: whenever there was a crisis, give unlimited power to one popular commander. This made traditional senatorial authority increasingly irrelevant and would be used repeatedly - most fatally by Caesar.

Exit Question 4

Question 4 of 5
How did the Pompey-Crassus inimicitia paralyse Republican politics by 60 BC?
Their rivalry began with the Spartacus incident (71 BC) when Pompey claimed credit for "finishing" the revolt Crassus had actually crushed. This created formal inimicitia - a public state of enmity requiring them to oppose each other. By 60 BC: Pompey couldn't get his eastern settlement ratified or land for his veterans because Crassus's allies in the Senate blocked him; Crassus couldn't achieve the military prestige that commanded popular support. Neither could dominate alone, but their hatred prevented cooperation. Their "reconciliation" in 70 BC was pure theatre - fake amicitia that dissolved the moment they left office. The result was political deadlock that made the Republic ungovernable.

Exit Question 5

Question 5 of 5
What dangerous precedents did Pompey and Crassus set for the Late Republic's collapse?
Key precedents: (1) Constitutional rules can be broken - Pompey proved you could skip offices and still become consul; (2) Popular assemblies can bypass the Senate - the extraordinary commands showed how to override senatorial opposition through direct popular vote; (3) Military success overrides everything - spectacular victories gave commanders political capital to demand almost anything; (4) Personal rivalry can paralyse the state - their inimicitia demonstrated how two powerful men refusing to cooperate could make the Republic ungovernable; (5) Armies follow generals, not Rome - soldiers dependent on commanders for rewards followed their generals' interests. Every precedent they set made the next ambitious commander's path easier - ultimately paving the way for Caesar.
