4.2 Caesar's Consulship (59 BC)

Topic 4: The First Triumvirate 25 min Political History

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how Caesar used his consulship to advance the Triumvirate's agenda, understand the constitutional methods and violations involved, and analyse Cicero's response to these events through his correspondence.

A Year That Changed the Republic

In 59 BC, Julius Caesar secured the consulship with the backing of the First Triumvirate. His year in office was transformative: it exposed the limits of senatorial authority, redefined what a consul could do, and gave Caesar the legal foundation for future military command. Though legal in form, the year was radical in effect.

The constitution has been shipwrecked.
— Cicero, Letters to Atticus 2.21
Key Context
Caesar's consulship represents a pivotal moment when the traditional checks and balances of the Roman Republic were systematically circumvented. The methods used in 59 BC would become the template for the Republic's final decades.

Caesar's Priorities and the Use of Power

Caesar had three main goals for his consulship, agreed upon with Pompey and Crassus:

  • Pass a land bill to provide for Pompey's veterans
  • Ratify Pompey's eastern settlement
  • Secure a long-term military command for himself

Once in office, Caesar encountered immediate resistance from his colleague Bibulus, an optimate who sought to block all legislation. When Bibulus attempted to veto the land bill, Caesar's supporters used violence and intimidation to drive him from the forum.

The "Consulship of Julius and Caesar"

Bibulus withdrew from public life, declaring he would "watch the skies" (obnuntiatio) — a religious excuse to suspend public business. This meant that technically all public assemblies were invalid.

Caesar effectively governed alone — a "consulship of Julius and Caesar," as some Roman wits remarked. This joke highlighted that both consuls were really just one man, since Bibulus had become politically invisible.

Constitutional Violation
The use of violence against a fellow consul and the deliberate ignoring of religious vetoes were serious violations of Republican norms. These actions set dangerous precedents for how ambitious politicians could bypass constitutional checks.

The Land Bill and Clodius' Adoption

Caesar's land bill was presented as moderate, but the Senate rejected it. He then took it to the popular assemblies, bypassing senatorial approval. With Pompey's public endorsement and the presence of armed veterans, the law was passed.

Bypassing the Senate

This set a crucial precedent for circumventing the Senate via popular support. The traditional process required senatorial approval before legislation went to the assemblies. Caesar demonstrated that a consul with sufficient backing could ignore this convention entirely.

To consolidate the Triumvirate's hold, Caesar also arranged for Publius Clodius Pulcher, a patrician, to be adopted into a plebeian family — a legal fiction that made him eligible for the tribunate of the plebs.

Clodius as a Political Weapon
Clodius would become a disruptive and violent political tool, primarily used to undermine Cicero. His transition to plebeian status through this adoption was legally questionable but served the Triumvirate's interests perfectly.

Control of the Provinces

Later in the year, Caesar was granted an extraordinary five-year command in Cisalpine Gaul, Illyricum, and later Transalpine Gaul (through the Lex Vatinia).

The Foundation for Future Power

This provincial command would ultimately lead to the Gallic Wars — and to Caesar controlling a veteran army loyal to him personally. The five-year term was unprecedented and gave Caesar time to build both military glory and personal wealth.

Unlike normal provincial assignments, which were typically for one year, this extended command allowed Caesar to develop long-term relationships with his troops and conduct sustained military campaigns.

Seeds of Civil War
The provincial command secured in 59 BC would eventually give Caesar the military resources to challenge the Senate directly. The army he built in Gaul would cross the Rubicon with him in 49 BC.

Cicero's Response: Letters to Atticus

Cicero was deeply troubled by the year's events. In his correspondence with Atticus, he expressed despair over Caesar's authoritarian conduct and the failure of the Senate to act. Though politically sidelined, Cicero remained convinced that the Republic had been irreparably weakened.

The constitution has been shipwrecked.
— Cicero, Letters to Atticus 2.21

A Dangerous Choice

Cicero refused to support Caesar or join the Triumvirate, a decision that made him a target for Clodius' hostility in the following year. This principled stand would cost Cicero dearly — Clodius would drive him into exile in 58 BC.

The letters to Atticus from this period reveal Cicero's growing anxiety and his sense that the traditional Republic was dying before his eyes.

Primary Source Value
Cicero's letters provide invaluable contemporary perspective on these events. They show how an educated Roman observer understood the constitutional crisis unfolding around him, even if he was powerless to stop it.

Historical Significance

Caesar's consulship of 59 BC marked a decisive break with traditional Republican practice. It demonstrated that:

  • A determined consul could rule without his colleague
  • Popular assemblies could be used to override the Senate
  • Violence and political manipulation were becoming the norm
  • Legislation could serve personal agendas rather than the common good

The Road to Civil War

This year laid the groundwork for the eventual civil war, giving Caesar both the legal authority and political confidence to challenge the Senate again. The methods pioneered in 59 BC — violence, constitutional manipulation, and appeals to the populace over the Senate — would become standard practice in the Republic's final years.

Examination Focus
When answering questions about the fall of the Republic, Caesar's consulship of 59 BC is essential evidence. It shows how the Triumvirate operated, how traditional checks were circumvented, and how the seeds of future conflict were planted through provincial commands.

Key Points Summary

Triumvirate Goals: Land bill, eastern settlement, provincial command
Bibulus: Driven from forum, "watched the skies"
Clodius: Adopted into plebeian family for tribunate
Lex Vatinia: Five-year Gallic command
Cicero: Refused Triumvirate, became target
Legacy: Precedents for bypassing Senate
