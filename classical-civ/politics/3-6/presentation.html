3.6 Early Caesar (to 60 BC)

The Man Who Learned From Everyone: How Caesar synthesised every path to power

The Complete Synthesis

By 60 BC, Rome had seen several figures try different approaches to power - and fail. Gaius Julius Caesar watched them all, learnt from their mistakes, and systematically built expertise in every area that mattered.

  • Military glory like Pompey
  • Popular support like the Gracchi
  • Rhetorical skill like Cicero
  • Elite connections like traditional aristocrats
  • Financial resources through Crassus's backing

What You Need to Know

This lesson covers Caesar's early life and career up to 60 BC only - before the First Triumvirate (Topic 4).

The Complete Politician

Mastered every skill: oratory, law, military command, popular politics, elite networking

The Synthesis

Combined strengths of Pompey, Crassus, Cicero whilst avoiding their weaknesses

Why Caesar Was Different

Caesar didn't invent new methods

He studied his contemporaries and avoided their mistakes:

  • Pompey alienated the Senate? Caesar built elite alliances
  • Crassus lacked glory? Caesar conquered Spain
  • Cicero had no army? Caesar built veteran loyalty
  • Cato was inflexible? Caesar compromised when useful

Family Background & Early Life

Gaius Julius Caesar was born on 12 or 13 July 100 BC into a patrician family - but not a particularly powerful one.

The Julii
Claimed descent from Iulus (Ascanius), son of Aeneas, making them theoretically descendants of the goddess Venus. This gave Caesar enormous symbolic capital - but his family had been politically inactive for generations. They had prestige without power.

The Marius Connection

Through His Aunt Julia

Caesar's aunt Julia married Marius (Topic 2.2), the great popularis general.

  • Connected Caesar to popularis politics from birth
  • Gave him military connections
  • Made him suspect to optimates who hated Marius's legacy

Marriage to Cornelia (84 BC)

Age 16

Caesar married Cornelia, daughter of Cinna - Marius's ally and a radical popularis who dominated Rome 87-84 BC.

This doubled down on Caesar's popularis identity during dangerous times.

Sulla's Proscriptions (82-81 BC)

When Sulla seized Rome and began executing populares supporters, Caesar was a marked man.

Sulla ordered him to divorce Cornelia - Caesar refused. He had to flee Rome and hide in the countryside.

Caesar learnt early that principle could be deadly - but also that backing down was worse.

I see many Mariuses in this young man.
- Sulla (reportedly), on pardoning Caesar

Early Career: Building Every Skill (80-60 BC)

After Sulla's death (78 BC), Caesar began his career properly. Unlike others who specialised, Caesar pursued every available avenue to prominence:

Military

Combat experience, leadership

Legal

Court cases, oratory

Popular

Games, monuments, reform

80-78 BC: Military Service

Asia & Cilicia: Caesar won the civic crown (corona civica) for saving a fellow soldier's life at the siege of Mytilene. This was Rome's second-highest military honour, giving him early credibility with soldiers.

77 BC: First Legal Cases

Prosecution of Dolabella
Prosecuted Gnaeus Cornelius Dolabella for provincial extortion. He lost, but gained attention for his clear, direct, forceful speaking style - different from Cicero's elaborate oratory.

75 BC: The Pirates Incident

Captured by Cilician pirates who demanded 20 talents ransom.

  • Caesar told them he was worth 50 talents
  • Whilst captive, he joked that he'd return and crucify them
  • Which he did, after raising a fleet and hunting them down
This story spread widely, showing his resourcefulness, confidence, and ruthlessness.

73-70 BC: Military Tribune

Tribunician Powers
As military tribune, Caesar publicly advocated for restoring tribunician powers (which Sulla had stripped). This aligned him with popularis causes whilst building military contacts.

69 BC: Quaestor in Spain

Serving as quaestor in Further Spain, Caesar reportedly saw a statue of Alexander the Great and wept - he was 30 and had achieved nothing, whilst Alexander had conquered the world by that age.
- This competitive drive would define his career

65 BC: Aedile - Spectacular Games

Put on the most lavish games Rome had ever seen, featuring 320 pairs of gladiators in silver armour.

Went massively into debt (borrowing from Crassus) but gained enormous popularity.

Also restored monuments to Marius, making a bold popularis statement.

63 BC: Pontifex Maximus

Won election as Pontifex Maximus (chief priest), defeating two senior ex-consuls.

This lifelong religious position gave him authority independent of annual magistracies.

He'd borrowed so much for bribes that defeat would have meant ruin.

62 BC: Praetor

The Bona Dea Scandal
As praetor, Caesar oversaw courts and gained legal expertise. When Clodius was caught infiltrating women-only rites, Caesar divorced his wife Pompeia, saying "Caesar's wife must be above suspicion" - protecting his reputation whilst staying neutral.

61-60 BC: Propraetor in Spain

As governor of Further Spain, conducted successful military campaigns, enriching himself and his soldiers.

Wrote directly to the Senate requesting a triumph and permission to stand for consul in absentia. The Senate (led by Cato) blocked both.

What Caesar Had Achieved by 60 BC

Military credibility

Civic crown, Spanish victories

Popular support

Games, popularis symbolism

Religious authority

Pontifex Maximus for life

Financial backing

Crassus's loans (3.4)

Spain & the Path to the Consulship

Caesar's governorship of Further Spain (61-60 BC) was crucial. It gave him everything he still lacked:

Wealth

Enough to pay off massive debts and fund future campaigns

Glory

Victories worthy of a triumph celebration

Loyalty

Soldiers who'd follow him based on generosity

The Spanish Campaign

Building Personal Loyalty
Caesar conducted aggressive campaigns against Lusitanian tribes, subduing resistance and capturing towns. He shared plunder generously with his soldiers (like Marius before him - Topic 2.2), creating personal loyalty. He also settled disputes between Spanish communities, showing administrative competence alongside military skill.

The Senate Blocks Him

Caesar requested permission to celebrate a triumph AND stand for consul in absentia (without entering Rome).

Led by Cato (3.5), the Senate refused. They forced Caesar to choose: triumph or consulship.

Caesar's choice: He gave up the triumph and entered Rome to stand for consul. This showed his priorities - power over glory.

The Solution: A Private Agreement

By 60 BC, three men wanted things the Senate wouldn't give:

Pompey (3.4)

Wanted land for his veterans and ratification of his Eastern settlement - Senate blocked him

Crassus (3.4)

Wanted tax relief for publicani who'd overpaid for Asian contracts - Senate blocked him

Caesar

Wanted the consulship but was outspent by optimate rivals - risked losing

Caesar's Masterstroke

The Private Agreement

Caesar proposed a private agreement: if all three worked together, they could force the Senate to comply.

  • Pompey had prestige
  • Crassus had money
  • Caesar had energy and political skill
This is where we stop for now. The formation and workings of the First Triumvirate (59 BC onwards) will be covered in Topic 4.

Popularis Methods - Learning from the Gracchi

Like the Gracchi before him (Topic 2.1), Caesar used popularis methods - appealing directly to the people and using tribunician power to bypass the Senate. But he learnt from their fatal mistakes.

RECALL: The Gracchi (133-121 BC) pioneered taking legislation directly to popular assemblies, bypassing senatorial opposition. Caesar watched how this worked - and how it got them killed.

Caesar's Popularis Actions (to 60 BC)

73-70 BC

Supporting Tribunician Power: As military tribune, publicly advocated for restoring tribunician powers that Sulla had stripped away.

65 BC

Restoring Marius's Monuments: Secretly had monuments to Marius restored overnight. When optimates protested, crowds defended them.

Caesar's Popularis Actions (continued)

63 BC

Opposing Excessive Punishment: During the Catilinarian conspiracy debate, argued against executing citizens without trial - positioning himself as defender of citizen rights.

62 BC

Supporting Land Reform: As praetor, quietly supported tribunician land reform proposals. He learnt to work through proxies rather than making himself the target.

What Caesar Learnt

From the Gracchi

  • Use popular support as leverage
  • Champion citizen rights and benefits
  • Bypass Senate when necessary
  • Build a loyal constituency

Caesar's Innovation

  • DON'T fight the Senate alone
  • Build alliances with powerful men
  • DON'T make yourself the target
  • Use proxies and coalitions

The Key Difference

The Gracchi were idealists who genuinely wanted to reform the Republic.

Caesar was a pragmatist who used popularis methods as tools for personal advancement.

Did Caesar believe in popular sovereignty? Maybe. But more importantly, he understood that popular support gave him power the Senate couldn't take away - as long as he also had military force (which the Gracchi lacked).

The Complete Politician: Comparing Caesar to Others

By 60 BC, Caesar had systematically built competence in every area that mattered in Roman politics. He studied his contemporaries (all from Topic 3) and avoided their weaknesses.

Caesar vs Pompey (3.4)

Military Genius, Political Novice

Pompey's Strengths: Brilliant general, massive military prestige, loyal veterans

Pompey's Weaknesses: Politically clumsy, alienated Senate with constitutional violations, couldn't navigate civilian politics

Caesar learnt: Military success alone isn't enough. You need political sophistication and elite alliances.

Caesar vs Crassus (3.4)

Wealth Without Glory

Crassus's Strengths: Enormous wealth, vast patronage network, shrewd businessman

Crassus's Weaknesses: No military prestige, not respected, seen as purely transactional

Caesar learnt: Money buys influence but not respect. You need military glory for real authority.

Caesar vs Cicero (3.1-3.2)

Words Without Swords

Cicero's Strengths: Brilliant orator, legal expertise, intellectual authority

Cicero's Weaknesses: No army, dependent on goodwill, vulnerable to those with military power

Caesar learnt: Rhetoric is powerful but words need backing by force. Combine oratory with legions.

Caesar vs Cato (3.5)

Principle Without Pragmatism

Cato's Strengths: Moral authority, incorruptible reputation, represented Republican ideals

Cato's Weaknesses: Inflexible, created deadlock, refused all compromise, made enemies unnecessarily

Caesar learnt: Principle is admirable but obstruction creates enemies. Be flexible enough to achieve goals.

Caesar's Synthesis

By 60 BC, Caesar had combined:

Military credibility

Spain, civic crown, loyal soldiers

Financial resources

Spanish plunder + Crassus's backing

Oratorical skill

Clear, forceful speaking style

Popular support

Popularis methods, generous games

Religious authority

Pontifex Maximus for life

Elite connections

Alliances with Pompey & Crassus

Why This Mattered for the Republic

The Gracchi failed because they fought alone. Pompey struggled because he was politically naive. Crassus lacked military prestige. Cicero lacked force. Cato lacked flexibility.

The Complete Power Base
Caesar studied all of them and built a complete power base that couldn't be attacked from any single direction. By 60 BC, blocking him would require the Senate to unite completely - which Cato's inflexibility had made impossible.

The Danger

Republican institutions were designed to prevent any one person from accumulating too much power.

Caesar's genius was to master every form of power simultaneously.

By 60 BC, the Republic had no mechanism to contain him - because he'd systematically learnt how to circumvent every check and balance.

Question 1 of 5
How did Caesar's family connections shape his early political identity?
Caesar's family connections fundamentally shaped his political identity as a POPULARIS from birth. His aunt Julia married Marius, the great popularis general who reformed the army and dominated Rome. At age 16, Caesar married Cornelia, daughter of Cinna - another radical popularis. These connections meant Caesar was automatically suspect to optimates but gave him credibility with the popular faction. When Sulla ordered him to divorce Cornelia during the proscriptions, Caesar's REFUSAL showed he would not abandon his popularis identity even under threat of death - establishing a pattern of principled defiance that would define his career.

Question 2 of 5
What made Caesar's approach to building power different from his contemporaries (Pompey, Crassus, Cicero, Cato)?
Unlike his contemporaries who each relied on ONE primary source of power, Caesar systematically built competence in EVERY area. Pompey had military glory but was politically clumsy - Caesar combined military success with political skill. Crassus had wealth but no prestige - Caesar gained both Spanish plunder AND military glory. Cicero had oratory but no army - Caesar was an accomplished speaker backed by veteran soldiers. Cato had moral authority but created enemies through inflexibility - Caesar was flexible enough to form alliances. Caesar's genius was understanding that these elements REINFORCED each other: military success funded politics, popularity intimidated opponents, elite alliances provided protection, religious authority gave independence from annual elections.

Exit Question 3

Question 3 of 5
How did Caesar learn from the Gracchi's mistakes in using popularis methods?
The Gracchi pioneered popularis methods - taking legislation directly to popular assemblies to bypass the Senate - but both were killed. Caesar learnt TWO crucial lessons from their failure. First, DON'T FIGHT ALONE: the Gracchi faced the Senate without powerful allies and were isolated and killed. Caesar built coalitions with Pompey and Crassus, ensuring he wasn't an easy target. Second, DON'T MAKE YOURSELF THE TARGET: the Gracchi put themselves at the centre of conflict. Caesar worked through proxies, supported tribunician proposals rather than proposing them himself, and used popular support as LEVERAGE rather than confrontation. The key difference was pragmatism over idealism - Caesar used popularis methods as TOOLS for advancement, not out of genuine reform conviction.

Exit Question 4

Question 4 of 5
Why was Caesar's position as Pontifex Maximus politically significant?
The position of Pontifex Maximus was politically significant for several reasons. First, it was a LIFELONG position - unlike annual magistracies that ended after a year, this gave Caesar permanent authority independent of elections. Second, it gave him control over Roman RELIGIOUS LIFE, including the calendar, festivals, and religious law - all of which had political implications. Third, winning the election in 63 BC against two senior ex-consuls demonstrated his popularity and political skill; he borrowed so much for bribes that defeat would have meant financial ruin, showing his willingness to take enormous risks. Finally, the position gave him LEGITIMACY and PRESTIGE that transcended factional politics - even optimates had to respect the chief priest.

Exit Question 5

Question 5 of 5
Why did Caesar give up his triumph in 60 BC, and what does this reveal about his priorities?
After his Spanish victories, Caesar was entitled to a triumph - the ultimate public celebration of military glory. However, a general waiting for a triumph had to remain outside Rome, while candidates for consul had to be present in the city. Caesar asked the Senate to let him stand for consul IN ABSENTIA. Led by Cato, the Senate refused, forcing him to choose. Caesar chose the CONSULSHIP over the triumph, which reveals his fundamental priority: POWER over GLORY. A triumph was a single day of celebration; the consulship was a year of executive power that could reshape Roman politics. This decision shows Caesar understood that real power came from OFFICE and ACTION, not symbolic honours. It also showed his willingness to sacrifice prestige for strategic advantage - a pragmatism that distinguished him from glory-seeking figures like Pompey.
