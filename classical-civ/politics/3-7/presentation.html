Topic 2: The Precedents (133-78 BC)

The Gracchi (133-121 BC)

Showed tribunes could bypass Senate. Used popular assemblies for reform. Both killed ‚Äî violence became acceptable.

Marius (107-86 BC)

Army reform made soldiers loyal to generals, not state. Showed military success = political power. Broke norms on multiple terms.

Sulla (82-78 BC)

Proved armies could march on Rome. Showed dictatorship could "fix" Republic. Proscriptions normalised political murder.

What Topic 2 Taught Everyone: Constitutional limits can be broken. Military force works. Popular support works. Violence is an option. The Senate can't stop you if you're strong enough.

Topic 3: The New Generation (78-60 BC)

Five ambitious men learn from Topic 2's lessons. Each finds their own path to power:

  • Cicero ‚Äî Oratory & Law: Proves talent can overcome birth. No army = vulnerable.
  • Pompey ‚Äî Extraordinary Commands: Bypasses normal rules through military glory. Unprecedented power.
  • Crassus ‚Äî Wealth & Patronage: Buys influence. Funds others' careers. Still wants glory.
  • Cato ‚Äî Moral Authority: Refuses all compromise. Blocks everything. Creates deadlock.
  • Caesar ‚Äî Everything: Learns from everyone. Combines all methods. Complete package.

The Problem by 60 BC

Senate can't accommodate them. Pompey blocked from rewards. Crassus wants respect. Caesar needs advancement. Cato refuses compromise. Constitutional deadlock ‚Äî no legal solution.

If the Senate won't accommodate them individually... they work together. The First Triumvirate (60 BC): Pompey (prestige) + Crassus (money) + Caesar (political skill) = private agreement to bypass Senate entirely.

Why This Matters

The Republic was designed to prevent exactly this. But Topic 2 showed it was possible. Topic 3 created five people capable of it. Topic 4 is when they actually do it ‚Äî and the Republic can't survive.

Key Events to Remember

The critical moments from 78-60 BC that shaped the end of the Republic.

The 70s BC

  • 78 BC ‚Äî Pompey Crushes Lepidus: First extraordinary command to someone outside normal offices.
  • 73-71 BC ‚Äî Spartacus Rebellion: Crassus defeated it but Pompey stole credit, creating rivalry.
  • 70 BC ‚Äî Joint Consulship: Pompey and Crassus elected despite not meeting requirements. Had armies.
  • 70 BC ‚Äî Verres Trial: Cicero prosecuted corrupt governor. Made him Rome's top orator.

The 60s BC (Part 1)

  • 67 BC ‚Äî Lex Gabinia (Pirate Command): Pompey given unlimited power over Mediterranean. Precedent-setting.
  • 66 BC ‚Äî Lex Manilia (Eastern Command): Pompey now controlled Rome's East. Most powerful man alive.
  • 65 BC ‚Äî Caesar's Games: 320 gladiator pairs. Massive debt. Built popular support.

The 60s BC (Part 2)

  • 63 BC ‚Äî Cicero's Consulship: Consul as novus homo. Stopped Catiline. Illegal executions.
  • 63 BC ‚Äî Caesar = Pontifex Maximus: Lifelong religious authority. Gambled everything on bribes.
  • 62 BC ‚Äî Cato Blocks Pompey: Refused veteran land. Created deadlock that set up Topic 4.
  • 61-60 BC ‚Äî Caesar in Spain: Military success. Gained wealth, glory, veteran loyalty.

Four Big Themes

The patterns that explain why the Republic was failing by 60 BC.

Theme 1: Extraordinary Commands

Lex Gabinia and Lex Manilia gave Pompey unprecedented power over entire regions. Once granted, these created precedent for bypassing normal constitutional limits.

These commands concentrated power in ways the Republic was designed to prevent. They became the template for future extra-legal arrangements.

Theme 2: Tribunician Power

Restored in 70 BC, tribunes could bypass the Senate through popular assemblies. Caesar (following the Gracchi) used this extensively ‚Äî but with elite backing unlike his predecessors.

The tribunate became a tool for ambitious individuals rather than a protection for the common people.

Theme 3: Senate Lost Control

The Senate couldn't accommodate ambitious men. Cato's inflexible opposition (blocking Pompey in 62 BC) forced enemies to work together. Constitutional limits became irrelevant.

The Senate's inability to compromise pushed powerful individuals into private alliances that operated outside the constitution entirely.

Theme 4: Military Force in Politics

Pompey and Crassus won consulships (70 BC) because they had armies outside Rome. Force could override rules. Made military success essential for real power.

When soldiers were loyal to their generals rather than the state, the Republic's civilian politics became subordinate to military power.

Why 78-60 BC Matters

The Republic's institutions couldn't contain ambitious individuals. Each of the five figures found ways to circumvent traditional limits:

  • Pompey through extraordinary commands
  • Crassus through wealth and patronage
  • Cicero through oratory and law
  • Cato through moral authority (ineffectively)
  • Caesar through systematic mastery of everything

What Happens Next

By 60 BC, the Senate couldn't accommodate these ambitions.

Cato's obstruction had created political deadlock with no constitutional solution.
