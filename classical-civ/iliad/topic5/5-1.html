5.1 Book 16 in Detail: Patroclus's Aristeia and Death

üìö A-Level Classical Civilisation ‚è±Ô∏è 75 min üìñ Homer's Iliad

Why Book 16 Is the Turning Point

Book 16 is where EVERYTHING changes. Patroclus‚ÄîAchilles's closest companion‚Äîcan't bear watching the Greeks suffer anymore. He persuades Achilles to let him fight wearing Achilles's armour. He drives the Trojans back in a spectacular aristeia, kills Zeus's son Sarpedon, and then‚Äîignoring Achilles's warnings‚Äîpushes too far. Apollo strips him of his armour, Euphorbus wounds him, and Hector delivers the killing blow.

Patroclus's death is the PIVOT of the entire Iliad. It ends Achilles's withdrawal, redirects his wrath from Agamemnon to Hector, and sets in motion the final tragedy. Without Patroclus's death, Achilles never returns. Without Achilles's return, Hector never dies. This is the book that makes everything that follows inevitable.

Book 16's Central Tragedy
Patroclus dies BECAUSE he succeeds. His aristeia is so spectacular that he forgets Achilles's warning to "come back when you have driven them from the ships." He pushes towards Troy itself‚Äîand gods and Hector kill him for his presumption. Success breeds overconfidence, overconfidence breeds death.

What Book 16 Establishes

  • Patroclus's character: Compassionate, brave, but ultimately overconfident
  • Achilles's love: He agrees to let Patroclus fight‚Äîhis first concession since Book 1
  • Divine intervention's limits: Even Zeus can't (or won't) save his own son Sarpedon
  • The cost of kleos: Patroclus achieves glory but dies for it‚Äîexactly what Achilles fears
  • Achilles's responsibility: He sends Patroclus to his death by lending him the armour

Book 16 has a CLEAR dramatic arc: persuasion ‚Üí preparation ‚Üí triumph ‚Üí overconfidence ‚Üí death. Homer builds Patroclus up systematically, then tears him down with equal care.

The Dramatic Arc
Notice the RISE and FALL: Patroclus starts weeping and helpless, achieves spectacular success (killing Zeus's son!), then overreaches and dies. Homer gives us a complete heroic trajectory in one book‚Äîfrom compassion to triumph to tragic death.

Patroclus Weeping

Book 16 opens with an IMAGE: Patroclus weeping. Achilles compares him to a little girl following her mother. It's tender, slightly mocking, and establishes Patroclus's compassion.

'Patroclus, why are you in tears, like a little girl running along beside her mother and begging to be carried, tugging at her skirt and looking up at her till at last she picks her up? That, Patroclus, is how you look, with the soft tears rolling down your cheeks.'
‚Äî Achilles to Patroclus, Rieu Book 16

üí° The Simile's Implications

Achilles compares Patroclus to a CHILD‚Äîhelpless, emotional, needy. It's affectionate but also reveals power dynamics. Achilles is the strong one; Patroclus is the vulnerable one. This makes what happens later even MORE tragic‚Äîthe "child" goes to war and dies.

Patroclus's Plea

'Achilles son of Peleus, by far the greatest of the Greeks, don't be angry at what I say. It's the Greeks ‚Äì they are in terrible distress. All our best men are lying by their ships, hit or stabbed. Mighty Diomedes son of Tydeus has been hit; the great spearman Odysseus has been stabbed; so has Agamemnon; and Eurypylus has had an arrow in his thigh.'
‚Äî Patroclus to Achilles, Rieu Book 16

Patroclus lists the wounded: Diomedes, Odysseus, Agamemnon, Eurypylus. These are the BEST warriors. All incapacitated. The Greeks are DESPERATE.

'But you, Achilles, are quite pitiless. Peleus was not your father, or Thetis your mother. No, the grey sea and the sheer cliffs produced you and your unfeeling heart. But if you are privately deterred by some prophecy, some word from Zeus that your lady mother has told you, at least allow me to take the field with the Myrmidon contingent at my back, if perhaps I might bring salvation to the Greeks. Give me your own armour to fight in, so that the Trojans take me for you and break off the battle.'
‚Äî Patroclus continues, Rieu Book 16

Patroclus's Argument

  • "You are quite pitiless": You've become INHUMAN
  • "The grey sea and sheer cliffs produced you": You're hard, cold, unfeeling
  • "If you won't fight, let ME fight": Practical compromise
  • "Give me your armour": The Trojans will think Achilles returned
  • "I might bring salvation": I can SAVE them, even if you won't

This is BRILLIANT persuasion. Patroclus doesn't argue about honour or heroic code. He appeals to COMPASSION (people are dying) and offers a PRACTICAL solution (I'll go instead). He's found Achilles's weak point: guilt.

Achilles Agrees‚ÄîWith Conditions

Achilles agrees, but notice: he's STILL angry at Agamemnon. His concession isn't reconciliation‚Äîit's pragmatic.

'Nevertheless, Patroclus, you must save the ships. Attack with all your force before the Trojans send them up in flames and cut us off from home. But listen carefully and do exactly as I say if you want to win me great honour and glory in the eyes of all the Greeks, so that they give me back the lovely woman as well as splendid gifts to boot. When you have driven them from the ships, come back.'
‚Äî Achilles to Patroclus, Rieu Book 16
'Do not let success go to your head and lead the Trojans-loving Myrmidons further towards Troy without me, or you will diminish my honour. And do not let any dreams of fighting take you as far as Troy. One of the everlasting gods from Olympus may intervene. Apollo loves the Trojans dearly.'
‚Äî Achilles continues, Rieu Book 16

‚ö†Ô∏è Achilles's Warning

"Come back when you have driven them from the ships." This is EXPLICIT. Don't pursue. Don't overreach. Save the ships, then STOP. Achilles knows the danger‚Äîsuccess breeds overconfidence. Apollo might intervene. But Patroclus, caught up in the glory of battle, will forget.

The Arming of Patroclus

Homer gives us a FULL arming scene‚Äîone of the most detailed in the Iliad. Every piece of armour is described. This ritualistic preparation heightens the drama: we're watching Patroclus become a warrior, knowing he'll die.

So he spoke, and Patroclus armed himself in the gleaming bronze. First he placed fine leg-guards on his shins, fitted with silver ankle-clips. Then he put on Achilles's body-armour, glittering and starry. Over his shoulder he slung his bronze, silver-riveted sword, then his mighty shield. On his mighty head he placed his well-built helmet with a horsehair crest, the plume nodding frighteningly from the top.
‚Äî Rieu Book 16, lines approx. 130-140

Leg-guards, body-armour, sword, shield, helmet. Every detail emphasised. This is ACHILLES'S armour‚Äîthe best in the Greek camp. Patroclus is being transformed into a simulacrum of Achilles.

Then he took up two powerful spears that fitted his grip. The only weapon of matchless Achilles he did not take was Achilles's long, thick, heavy spear. No Greek could wield this but Achilles, who alone knew how to handle it. It was made from an ash-tree on the top of Mount Pelion and had been given to Achilles's father Peleus, to bring death to warriors.
‚Äî Rieu Book 16, lines approx. 140-145
The Spear He Cannot Take
Patroclus takes EVERYTHING except Achilles's spear. Why? Because "no Greek could wield this but Achilles." This is SYMBOLIC: Patroclus can LOOK like Achilles, but he can never BE Achilles. The spear represents the unbridgeable gap between imitation and reality.

üí° Dramatic Irony

We KNOW Patroclus will die. So this arming scene is TRAGIC‚Äîwe're watching him prepare for death, not victory. Every piece of armour is a step closer to disaster. Homer makes us complicit: we want him to succeed but know he won't.

Achilles Prays to Zeus

After Patroclus leaves, Achilles prays to Zeus. This prayer is CRUCIAL‚Äîit shows what Achilles wants and what Zeus will grant.

'Lord Zeus, god of Dodona, you listened when I prayed to you before and you honoured me by striking a mighty blow at the Greek army. Now grant me another wish. I myself am going to stay here by the ships, but I am sending my comrade with many of my Myrmidons into battle. Grant him victory, far-thundering Zeus, and fill his heart with daring. And directly he has driven the tumult and the fighting back from the ships, let him come back to me here at my own ships sound with all my armour.'
‚Äî Achilles praying, Rieu Book 16

What Achilles Asks For

  • "Grant him victory": Let Patroclus succeed
  • "Fill his heart with daring": Make him brave
  • "Let him come back to me": Bring him home ALIVE
So Achilles spoke in prayer, and Zeus wise in counsel heard him. One half the Father granted, but not the other. The Father agreed that Patroclus should drive the tumult and the fighting back from the ships, but he did not agree that Patroclus should come back safely from the battle.
‚Äî Rieu Book 16

‚ö†Ô∏è Zeus's Partial Grant

Zeus grants HALF the prayer. Patroclus will succeed (drive Trojans back) but NOT return alive. This is Homer telling us EXPLICITLY: Patroclus is doomed. Everything that follows happens under the shadow of this divine decree.

The Myrmidons Attack

When the Myrmidons see Patroclus in Achilles's armour, they SURGE forward. Homer compares them to wasps‚Äîpredators unleashed after long confinement.

Meanwhile the armed contingents under great-hearted Patroclus advanced and fearlessly attacked the Trojans. They came swarming on like roadside wasps that boys always like to tease, stirring them up in their nests by the road, the young fools. With their courage and spirit the Myrmidons swarmed out in a mass from the ships, and the tumult of battle filled the air.
‚Äî Rieu Book 16

The wasp simile is PERFECT. Wasps are aggressive, protective, swarm as a unit. They've been "stirred up" (by watching Greeks suffer). Now they attack with coordinated fury.

'Myrmidons, companions of Achilles, be men, my comrades, call up that fighting spirit of yours and win glory for the son of Peleus, the best man in the Greek camp, with the best warriors under him. Make wide-ruling Agamemnon son of Atreus realize the delusion he is under in giving no respect to the best of all the Greeks.'
‚Äî Patroclus rallying the Myrmidons, Rieu Book 16

Patroclus Kills Pyraechmes

Patroclus was the first to throw a glittering spear. He hurled it straight into the mass of men where the fighting was at its most confused round the stern of Protesilaus's ship and struck Pyraechmes, who had brought his Paeonians from Amydon. He hit him in the right shoulder. With a groan Pyraechmes fell on his back in the dust, and his Paeonian troops ran for it.
‚Äî Rieu Book 16

Patroclus's FIRST kill turns the tide. By killing Pyraechmes (their leader), he causes panic. The Paeonians flee. One targeted kill creates cascading effects‚Äîthis is SMART warfare.

The Immediate Effect

  • Trojans panic: Think Achilles has returned
  • Fire extinguished: The burning ship is saved
  • Greeks rally: Suddenly they have hope
  • Trojans retreat: Pull back from the ships

This is what Achilles WANTED: drive them from the ships. Mission accomplished. If Patroclus stopped HERE, he'd live. But success is intoxicating.

Patroclus's Killing Spree

Homer now gives us a CATALOGUE of Patroclus's kills. The Greeks are "picking off Trojans one by one." This establishes Patroclus as formidable‚Äîwhich makes his death more shocking.

So each of these Greek chieftains killed his man. Just as predatory wolves harry lambs or kids and snatch them away from their mothers when they have become separated on the mountain-tops through the carelessness of the shepherd, and the wolves seize their chance to pick off the timid creatures ‚Äì so the Greeks harried the Trojans. The Trojans could think only of tumultuous retreat, and all the fight went out of them.
‚Äî Rieu Book 16

"Predatory wolves... pick off the timid creatures" ‚Äî the Trojans are PREY now. Complete reversal from earlier books when Hector was unstoppable.

Sarpedon Steps Forward

Someone has to stop Patroclus. That someone is Sarpedon‚Äîson of ZEUS, leader of the Lycians, one of Troy's best allies.

When Sarpedon saw how his beltless Lycians were falling to Patroclus son of Menoetius, he turned on his godlike warriors: 'Shame on you, Lycians! Where are you off to? Come on, now! I'm going to take on this man who is out there. I intend to find out who it is that's carrying all before him and has done the Trojans so much harm already, bringing down so many of our best men.'
‚Äî Sarpedon, Rieu Book 16

Sarpedon recognises the crisis. His men are FLEEING. Someone must stand. As a leader and Zeus's son, that someone is him. This is heroic responsibility‚Äîfacing danger because no one else will.

Zeus Debates Saving Sarpedon

Before Sarpedon fights Patroclus, Homer takes us to OLYMPUS. Zeus watches his son about to die and considers saving him. This is one of the most important divine scenes in the Iliad.

'This is an unhappy business! My son Sarpedon, dearest of men, is destined to be killed by Patroclus son of Menoetius. I wonder now ‚Äì I am in two minds. Shall I snatch him up and set him down alive on Lycia's rich soil, far from the war with all its tears? Or shall I now let him fall at Patroclus's hands?'
‚Äî Zeus, Rieu Book 16
Ox-eyed lady Hera replied: 'Dread son of Cronus, what are you suggesting now? Are you proposing to reprieve from the pains of death a mortal man whose destiny has long been settled? Do what you like, then; but not all the rest of us gods will approve.'
‚Äî Hera to Zeus, Rieu Book 16
Fate vs Divine Will
Can Zeus save his own son? PHYSICALLY, yes. But Hera warns: if you break fate for Sarpedon, every god will want to save THEIR favourites. The system collapses. Zeus chooses to honour fate over paternal love. Even the king of gods is bound by destiny.
'But if Sarpedon is dear to you and your heart grieves for him, let him fall in the thick of the fight at Patroclus's hands, but when breath and life have left him, send Death and sweet Sleep to carry him back to the broad land of Lycia, where his relatives and retainers will give him burial with a grave-mound and monument ‚Äì the honour that is due to the dead.'
‚Äî Hera continues, Rieu Book 16

Hera's argument: let him die HONOURABLY, then give him a proper burial. Zeus agrees. He weeps blood (a sign of grief) but lets Sarpedon die.

Sarpedon Dies

Sarpedon then missed with a glittering spear; the point passed harmlessly over Patroclus' left shoulder and failed to make its mark. But Patroclus threw his spear, and the weapon did not leave his hand for nothing. It struck Sarpedon where the lungs enclosed his dense heart, and he crashed down. As a lion's will to fight overpowers an indomitable wild boar when the fearless pair battle it out in the mountains over a little stream ‚Äì so Sarpedon lay stretched in front of his chariot and horses, gurgling and clutching at the bloodstained dust.
‚Äî Rieu Book 16

Sarpedon's death is described with a SIMILE: he falls like a tree cut down. This is SIGNIFICANT‚Äîeven Zeus's son, when his time comes, crashes down. The lion and boar simile emphasizes that both fought bravely, but one must die.

Sarpedon, leader of the shield-bearing Lycians, struggled defiantly to speak as he yielded up his life to Patroclus, and called on his dear companion: 'Glaucus, old friend, champion among men, now's the time to show your bravery and ability as a fighter. Fight over me with your own spear. Then fight over me with your own spear, with all your strength.'
‚Äî Sarpedon's dying words, Rieu Book 16

Patroclus Pushes Too Far

After killing Sarpedon and fighting over his body, Patroclus should STOP. Achilles told him: save the ships, then come back. But he doesn't. Success has made him reckless.

Patroclus, with murder in his heart, leapt on the Trojans relentlessly. Three times he charged with an impetuous yell, and three times he killed nine men. But when he leapt in like something superhuman for the fourth time, then, Patroclus, the end was in sight. In the heat of the battle, Phoebus encountered Patroclus most terrible.
‚Äî Rieu Book 16
Three Times, Then the Fourth
The "three plus one" pattern signals disaster. Patroclus charges THREE times successfully. On the FOURTH attempt, Apollo intervenes. It's a formulaic way of saying: you've pushed your luck too far, and now the gods will stop you.

Apollo Strips the Armour

This is the CRUCIAL moment. Apollo himself appears and disarms Patroclus, leaving him vulnerable.

Patroclus had not seen him coming through the mayhem; the god had wrapped himself in a thick mist for this meeting. He stood behind Patroclus now and, striking his back and broad shoulders with the flat of his hand, he made Patroclus' eyes spin and knocked the helmet off his head. With its heavy bronze, it rolled clattering under the horses' hooves, and its plume was defiled with blood and dust. The long-shadowed spear, huge, thick and heavy with its head of bronze, was shattered in Patroclus' hands. The fringed shield with its strap fell from his shoulder to the ground; and lord Apollo son of Zeus undid the body-armour on his chest.
‚Äî Rieu Book 16

What Apollo Does

  • Strikes Patroclus from behind (makes eyes spin‚Äîdazed)
  • Knocks helmet off (exposed head)
  • Shatters his spear (weaponless)
  • Shield falls from shoulder (defenseless)
  • Undoes his body-armour (unprotected)

In seconds, Apollo transforms Patroclus from terrifying warrior to helpless victim. The divine intervention is SYSTEMATIC‚Äîevery layer of protection stripped away.

The Three-Stage Death

Homer makes Patroclus's death a THREE-STAGE process: Apollo disarms, Euphorbus wounds, Hector kills. This emphasizes that it takes divine intervention PLUS two warriors to bring him down.

A fatal blindness overtook Patroclus. His shining limbs were paralyzed; and as he stood there in a daze, a Dardanian called Euphorbus, son of Panthous, came up behind him at close range, drew a sharp spear and hit him in the middle of the back between the shoulders. This Euphorbus was the best spearman, racer and horseman of his years. He ran back and mingled with the crowd. He did not stay to fight Patroclus, defenceless though he was.
‚Äî Rieu Book 16

Euphorbus stabs Patroclus in the back, then RUNS AWAY. He's scared. Patroclus, even wounded and disarmed, is terrifying. But the damage is done.

When Hector saw great-hearted Patroclus wounded and in retreat, he made his way towards him through the ranks and, coming up, stabbed him with his spear in the gut belly, driving the bronze clean through. Patroclus thudded to the ground, throwing the whole Greek army into consternation.
‚Äî Rieu Book 16

Hector delivers the FATAL blow‚Äîspear through the belly. Patroclus falls. The Greeks are shattered.

Hector's Taunt and Patroclus's Prophecy

'Patroclus, you probably thought you'd sack our town, make Trojan women slaves and ship them off to the land of your fathers. You innocent! Hector taunts Patroclus: "You thought you'd sack Troy"‚Äîaccusing him of overreaching, which is EXACTLY what happened. "The vultures are going to eat you"‚Äîthreatening desecration.

Patroclus's Final Prophecy
With his last breath, Patroclus makes a PROPHECY: Achilles will kill you, Hector. This is dramatically powerful‚Äîthe dying man cursing his killer. And WE know it's true. Hector dismisses it ("Who knows?"), but Book 22 will prove Patroclus right.

"Bewailing... the youth and the manhood it had left behind" ‚Äì Patroclus dies YOUNG, unfulfilled. His psyche (life-force/soul) mourns what might have been. This is the COST of kleos: you win glory but lose everything else.

Why Patroclus Had to Die

The Multiple Causes

  • Zeus willed it: Granted only HALF of Achilles's prayer
  • Apollo intervened: Stripped armour, leaving him vulnerable
  • Euphorbus wounded him: First blow from behind
  • Hector killed him: Delivered the fatal spear-thrust
  • Patroclus overreached: Ignored Achilles's warning, pushed too far
The Tragic Irony
Patroclus dies BECAUSE he's successful. If he'd been mediocre, he'd have stopped at the ships. But his aristeia is SO spectacular that he gets drunk on glory and forgets the warning. Success ‚Üí overconfidence ‚Üí death. This is Homer showing that victory contains the seeds of defeat.

  • Patroclus's compassion: Can't watch Greeks suffer; weeps "like a little girl"
  • Achilles's warning: "Drive them from ships, then COME BACK" ‚Äì explicit limit
  • Zeus's partial prayer: Grants victory but NOT safe return ‚Äì Patroclus doomed from the start
  • The spear Patroclus cannot take: Symbolic‚Äîcan look like Achilles but never be him
  • The wasp simile: Myrmidons swarm out aggressive and protective
  • Sarpedon's death: Even Zeus's son must die when fate decrees it
  • Three times successful, fourth time fatal: Traditional pattern showing hubris punished
  • Apollo strips the armour: Divine intervention makes Patroclus vulnerable‚Äîsystematic disarming
  • Three-stage death: Apollo (disarms), Euphorbus (wounds), Hector (kills)‚Äîtakes god + 2 men
  • Patroclus's prophecy: "Achilles will kill you" ‚Äì dying curse that comes true
  • Success leads to overconfidence: Spectacular aristeia makes him forget the warning
  • The pivot of the epic: This death ends Achilles's withdrawal and dooms Hector
