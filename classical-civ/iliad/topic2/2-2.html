üìö Topic 2: Books 1 & 3 ‚è±Ô∏è 45 min üíî The Duel for Helen

Why Book 3 Matters

Book 3 is fascinating because it takes us BACK to the beginning of the war. After Books 1-2 showed us the Greek camp in crisis, Book 3 finally introduces the people the war is actually about: Helen, Paris, and the duel that could have ended everything.

The War Should End Here
Paris and Menelaus agree to a duel: winner gets Helen, war over. This is literally what the war was fought for. But (spoiler alert) the gods won't let it end. Book 3 shows us the war that COULD have been avoided‚Äîif the gods didn't have their own agendas.

Quick summary: The armies face each other. Paris challenges Menelaus to single combat. Helen is brought to the walls to watch (the Teichoscopia). They duel. Menelaus is winning when Aphrodite rescues Paris and returns him to Helen's bedroom. Aphrodite forces Helen to go to him. The war continues.

The Core Theme
Book 3 explores the contrast between heroic ideals and messy reality. Paris SHOULD be ashamed. Helen SHOULD leave him. Menelaus SHOULD win. But human weakness, divine intervention, and sexual power complicate everything.

Key Context: What You Need to Know

The Judgement of Paris

Before the war, Paris was asked to judge which goddess was most beautiful: Hera, Athena, or Aphrodite. Each goddess offered a bribe:

  • Hera: Political power
  • Athena: Military glory
  • Aphrodite: The most beautiful woman in the world (Helen)

Paris chose Aphrodite. This is why Aphrodite protects him and why Hera and Athena hate Troy.

Helen's Marriage to Menelaus

Helen was the most beautiful woman in Greece. So many men wanted to marry her that her father made all suitors swear an oath: they would defend whoever married her.

Menelaus won. When Paris "abducted" Helen (or she ran away with him‚Äîsources disagree), all of Greece was oath-bound to help Menelaus get her back. That's why the entire Greek army came to Troy.

The Structure of Book 3

Book 3 has a clear five-part structure that builds to the duel, then shows us the aftermath when divine intervention ruins everything.

Scene 1: Paris Challenged
Lines 1-120
Armies advance. Menelaus spots Paris and charges. Paris panics and hides. Hector shames him. Paris proposes single combat.
Scene 2: The Oaths
Lines 121-244
Both sides agree to the duel. Elaborate oaths sworn. Winner gets Helen and all her treasure. The war will end.
Scene 3: Teichoscopia
Lines 161-244
Helen brought to the walls. Priam asks her to identify Greek heroes. She points out Agamemnon, Odysseus, Ajax. Emotional scene.
Scene 4: The Duel
Lines 314-382
Paris and Menelaus fight. Menelaus dominates. He's about to kill Paris when Aphrodite intervenes and saves him.
Scene 5: Helen & Paris
Lines 383-461
Aphrodite brings Paris back to his bedroom. Forces Helen to join him. Tense, bitter scene between them. War continues.

The Pattern: Promise ‚Üí Betrayal

Book 3 sets up a perfect resolution‚Äîsingle combat, winner takes all, war ends. But the gods (especially Aphrodite) won't allow it. The pattern is: Humans try to solve things rationally ‚Üí Gods intervene for selfish reasons ‚Üí Chaos continues.

This is Homer showing us that the war ISN'T really about Helen or justice‚Äîit's about divine politics and human weakness.

Paris: The Anti-Hero

Paris is the opposite of what a Homeric hero should be. He's beautiful, cowardly, and prioritizes pleasure over honour. Homer makes this VERY clear from his first appearance.

Paris's Cowardice
When Menelaus spots Paris in the front ranks, Paris literally runs away and hides behind the Trojan lines. This is humiliating. A hero should seek out his enemy, not flee from him.
But when Paris the handsome saw Menelaus come out in front, his heart failed him and he shrank back into the Trojan ranks to save his life, like a man who has come on a snake in a mountain glen. He recoils, a tremor seizes his limbs, he backs away, and the colour drains from his cheeks.
‚Äî Lines 30-37 (Rieu)

Hector's Shame Speech

Hector (Paris's brother) sees this and DESTROYS him with insults. This is one of the most savage speeches in the Iliad:

'Paris, you handsome, woman-crazy deceiver, I wish you had never been born, or had died unmarried. Yes, and I wish it too, rather than see you make a mockery and a disgrace of us like this. Listen to the long-haired Greeks jeering! They thought you were a champion because you have good looks. But you have no spirit in you, no courage. Were you like this when you got your gang together, sailed oversea in your ships and carried off a beautiful woman from a foreign land, a woman who was attached by marriage to a family of warriors? You are a curse to your father, your city and your people, a source of rejoicing to our enemies and an object of contempt to yourself.'
‚Äî Lines 39-57 (Rieu)
Why This Matters
Hector's speech establishes the heroic values Paris violates: courage, honour, responsibility to family and city. Paris is "woman-crazy" and lacks "spirit." He's caused massive suffering for personal pleasure. Even his own brother is disgusted.

Paris's Defense

Paris doesn't deny he's a coward. Instead, he basically says: "Yeah, but I'm good-looking and Aphrodite likes me!"

'Hector, your rebuke is not unjustified. But do not cast in my teeth the delectable gifts of golden Aphrodite. The glorious gifts of the gods are not to be rejected: they are bestowed by the gods themselves; no man can have them for the asking.'
‚Äî Lines 58-63 (Rieu)

This is hilariously weak. Paris basically admits he's chosen beauty and sex over honour‚Äîand he's fine with that. He then proposes the duel, probably to save face rather than from genuine courage.

The Teichoscopia: "View from the Walls"

The Teichoscopia ("view from the walls") is one of the most famous scenes in the Iliad. Priam asks Helen to identify the Greek heroes for him. This is emotionally charged‚ÄîHelen describing the men fighting to get her back.

Why This Is Odd
It's the TENTH YEAR of the war. Priam should already know who these people are! Homer puts this scene here to introduce us (the audience) to the main Greek heroes‚Äîand to show us Helen's complicated feelings about the war.

Priam's Kindness to Helen

'Come here, dear child, and sit by me to see your former husband, your kinsmen and your friends. I do not hold you to blame; as far as I am concerned it is the gods who are to blame. They brought on me this calamitous war with the Greeks.'
‚Äî Lines 162-165 (Rieu)

Priam is remarkably kind. He blames the gods, not Helen. This makes Helen's guilt even more painful‚Äîthe Trojans are dying for her, and they're NICE to her.

Helen on Agamemnon

'That is Agamemnon son of Atreus, lord of men, a good king and a powerful spearman. He was my brother-in-law once‚Äîslut that I am‚Äîif I did not dream it all.'
‚Äî Lines 178-180 (Rieu)

Notice: "slut that I am." Helen's self-hatred is devastating. She calls herself names and questions if her old life was even real.

Helen on Odysseus

'That is the resourceful Odysseus, son of Laertes, who was brought up in the rocky land of Ithaca. There is nothing he cannot think of, no stratagem too subtle for his cunning mind.'
‚Äî Lines 200-202 (Rieu)

Helen knows these men WELL. She's describing her former life, the world she abandoned. The pain is implicit in every identification.

Helen Searches for Her Brothers

The most heartbreaking moment: Helen looks for her brothers Castor and Pollux among the Greeks. She can't find them.

Thus spoke Helen; but the life-giving earth already held them fast in Lacedaemon, in their own dear native land.
‚Äî Lines 243-244 (Rieu)

They're DEAD. They died before the war even started. Helen doesn't know. This is tragic‚Äîshe's lost everything, including the brothers she doesn't even realize are gone.

The Duel: Menelaus Dominates

The duel is brutally one-sided. Menelaus is winning from the start. Paris is outmatched in every way.

Paris Strikes First‚ÄîAnd Fails

Paris cast first. His long javelin struck the shield of Menelaus but did not pierce it: the point was turned back by the tough hide.
‚Äî Lines 346-349 (Rieu)

Paris's spear is useless. It doesn't even penetrate Menelaus's shield. This establishes the pattern: Paris is ineffective.

Menelaus Nearly Kills Him

The bronze spear struck Paris on the plate of his breastplate and bent on the strong metal. Then Menelaus drew his silver-studded sword, swung it high and brought it down on the ridge of Paris's helmet. But the sword broke up into three or four pieces and fell from his hand.
‚Äî Lines 355-360 (Rieu)

Menelaus's spear hits Paris. His sword shatters on Paris's helmet. Menelaus is DESTROYING him‚Äîbut the gods protect Paris with divine armor.

Menelaus Drags Paris by the Helmet

This is the most brutal moment: Menelaus grabs Paris by his helmet and starts dragging him back to the Greek lines like a prize.

Menelaus seized him by the horse-hair crest of his helmet, swung him round and began to drag him back towards the Greeks. The embroidered strap that passed under Paris's soft throat to hold his helmet fast was throttling him, and Menelaus would have won immeasurable glory by dragging him off, if Aphrodite had not been quick to see what was happening.
‚Äî Lines 369-375 (Rieu)
Menelaus Should Win
Menelaus is about to win "immeasurable glory" by killing/capturing Paris. Justice would be served. Helen would return to her rightful husband. The war would end. But Aphrodite won't allow it.

Aphrodite Intervenes

She broke the strap, which was made from the hide of a slaughtered ox, and the empty helmet came away in Menelaus's powerful hand. He swung the helmet round and flung it across to the Greeks. Then he leapt back at Paris, determined to kill him with his bronze spear. But Aphrodite snatched Paris away, wrapped him in a thick mist and set him down in his own perfumed bedroom.
‚Äî Lines 375-382 (Rieu)

Aphrodite literally teleports Paris from the battlefield to his bedroom. This is divine intervention at its most blatant. The war SHOULD be over‚Äîbut the gods have other plans.

Aphrodite's Power

After saving Paris, Aphrodite goes to fetch Helen. But Helen doesn't WANT to go to Paris‚Äîso Aphrodite has to force her. This scene reveals how divine power works.

Aphrodite in Disguise
Aphrodite appears to Helen disguised as an old woman. But Helen recognizes her anyway‚Äîshe sees through the disguise. This shows Helen's perceptiveness and the intimate connection between Helen and Aphrodite.
'Come! Paris is asking for you to come home. There he is in his bedroom, on the carved bed, gleaming with beauty and fine clothes. You would never think he had just come from fighting. He looks as though he were going to a dance, or resting after one.'
‚Äî Lines 390-394 (Rieu)

Notice Aphrodite's sales pitch: Paris looks beautiful and well-rested, "as though he were going to a dance." She's emphasizing his attractiveness‚Äîbecause that's all Paris has to offer.

Helen Refuses

Helen is ANGRY. She sees through Aphrodite's manipulation and refuses to go:

'Monstrous goddess! Why do you insist on persecuting me? Where are you going to take me next? I will not go to him. It would be shameful. I will not make his bed. The Trojan women would all despise me; and I have sorrow enough as it is.'
‚Äî Lines 399-402 (Rieu)
Why Helen Resists
Helen knows Paris just lost the duel. To go back to him NOW, after he's been humiliated, would be shameful. Helen has some pride left‚Äîbut Aphrodite won't let her keep it.

Aphrodite's Threat

'Don't provoke me, you obstinate girl, or I may lose my temper and desert you, and come to hate you as immoderately as I love you now. I could make you detested by both sides, Trojans and Greeks alike. Then you would come to a miserable end.'
‚Äî Lines 414-417 (Rieu)

Aphrodite THREATENS Helen. If Helen doesn't obey, Aphrodite will make everyone hate her. Helen is trapped‚Äîshe has no choice but to comply.

What This Reveals About Divine Power

Gods don't respect free will. Aphrodite saved Paris not because he deserved it, but because she chose him in the Judgement of Paris. Now she forces Helen to return to him‚Äîeven though Helen knows it's shameful. Divine power = compulsion, not justice.

Helen's Complexity

Book 3 gives us a nuanced portrait of Helen. She's not simply a victim or a villain‚Äîshe's trapped, self-aware, and deeply unhappy.

Understanding Helen
Helen's character revealed through her own words and actions
Self-Hatred

Helen repeatedly calls herself "slut," "bitch," "shameless." She's internalized everyone's judgment. She knows what she's cost both sides and hates herself for it.

Trapped by Aphrodite

Helen wants to resist Paris, but Aphrodite forces her. She's caught between divine compulsion and her own shame. She has no real agency.

Nostalgia for Greece

During the Teichoscopia, Helen describes her former life with longing. She misses Greece, her family, her old life. Troy is exile, not home.

Contempt for Paris

Helen despises Paris for his cowardice. She wishes he'd died in the duel. But she's bound to him by Aphrodite's power and her own reputation.

Helen's Final Speech to Paris

When Helen finally goes to Paris's bedroom (forced by Aphrodite), she doesn't hide her contempt:

'So you have come back from the war! I wish you had died there, beaten by that great soldier who was once my husband. You used to boast that you were a better man than Menelaus, with your strength, your hands, your spear. Go now and challenge Menelaus, who beat you just now, to fight you again. But no‚ÄîI advise you to keep well away from the fair-haired Menelaus and never fight a duel with him. It would be rash. His spear might soon bring you down.'
‚Äî Lines 428-436 (Rieu)
Helen's Sarcasm
This is SAVAGE. "I wish you had died there." "You used to boast." "I advise you to keep well away." Helen is openly mocking Paris for his cowardice. But she's still stuck with him.

Paris's Response: Sex Over Honour

'Wife, do not taunt me with my bitter defeat. This time Menelaus has won, with Athene's help. Another time I shall beat him: we too have gods on our side. Come now, let us go to bed and make love. Never has such desire overwhelmed me, not even when I first took you away from lovely Lacedaemon.'
‚Äî Lines 438-444 (Rieu)

Paris literally ignores everything Helen said and just wants sex. He admits he lost but doesn't care. This is who Paris is: pleasure above all else.

Why Book 3 Matters for Understanding Helen

Book 3 shows us Helen is neither willing participant nor innocent victim‚Äîshe's trapped in a situation she helped create but can't escape. She's self-aware enough to recognize her shame, but powerless against divine compulsion and social constraints. She despises Paris but can't leave him. This complexity makes her one of Homer's most psychologically realistic characters.

Key Themes in Book 3

Theme How Book 3 Explores It
Heroic Values vs Reality Paris violates every heroic ideal (courage, honour, responsibility) yet survives because Aphrodite protects him. Heroism doesn't guarantee success.
Divine Intervention Gods don't care about justice or human suffering. Aphrodite saves Paris because she chose him, not because he deserves it. Humans are playthings.
Female Agency Helen has awareness and opinions but no real power. She's forced by Aphrodite, constrained by reputation, trapped by circumstances. Her intelligence can't free her.
War's Futility The war COULD end here‚ÄîMenelaus beat Paris fairly. But divine politics and human weakness ensure it continues. All the suffering is pointless.
Beauty vs Virtue Paris chose beauty (Aphrodite's gift) over power or glory. He prioritizes pleasure over honour. Helen is the most beautiful woman but the most miserable. Beauty ‚â† happiness.
Why Book 3 Matters for Essays
Book 3 is ESSENTIAL for discussing: Helen's character and agency, Paris as anti-hero, divine intervention's role, the war's causes and futility, and the contrast between heroic ideals and messy reality. You can't write well about Helen or the war's origins without Book 3.
