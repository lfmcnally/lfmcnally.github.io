2.1 Book 1 in Detail

📚 A-Level Classics - The Iliad ⏱️ 50 min 📖 E.V. Rieu Translation

Why Book 1 Matters

Book 1 is the foundation for EVERYTHING in the Iliad. The quarrel between Achilles and Agamemnon triggers the entire plot. Their conflict establishes the poem's central themes: honour, pride, heroic identity, and divine intervention.

The Opening Lines
"The wrath of Achilles—sing, goddess, the anger of Peleus' son Achilles, that accursed anger which brought untold grief to the Greeks..."

Notice what Homer does here: he doesn't start with Achilles' heroism. He starts with his ANGER. This is a poem about the destructive consequences of wounded pride.

What Happens in Book 1?

Quick summary: Apollo sends a plague. Achilles calls an assembly. Agamemnon takes Achilles' prize-woman Briseis. Achilles withdraws from battle and asks his mother Thetis to persuade Zeus to let the Greeks lose. Hera and Zeus quarrel about it on Olympus.

The Core Question
What's more important: the collective good of the army, or an individual warrior's honour? Achilles and Agamemnon give opposite answers—and thousands die as a result.

Book 1's Seven Scenes

Book 1 has a clear dramatic structure. Each scene builds tension until the explosive quarrel, then shows the consequences rippling through both mortal and divine worlds.

The structure mirrors the theme: a quarrel between mortals (Achilles & Agamemnon) is paralleled by a quarrel between gods (Zeus & Hera). The personal becomes cosmic.

Analysing Achilles' Sceptre Oath

This is THE most important speech in Book 1. Achilles swears by his sceptre that the Greeks will regret dishonoring him. Let's break down HOW Homer makes this speech so powerful.

"But I will tell you this, and I swear a solemn oath upon it. By this sceptre, which will never put out leaves or twigs again, once cut from its stem in the hills, it can never sprout again. The bronze axe stripped it of its bark and foliage: it will sprout no more. The Greek judges hold it when they give judgement. By this I solemnly swear that the day is coming when the Greeks one and all will miss Achilles badly, and when in your despair you will be powerless to help them as they fall in their multitudes to man-slaying Hector. Then you will tear your heart out in remorse for giving no respect to the best of the Greeks."
🎯 Extended Metaphor: The Dead Sceptre
Achilles doesn't just swear an oath—he uses the sceptre as a metaphor for his own withdrawal. Like the sceptre cut from its tree, Achilles will be cut from the Greek army. Just as the sceptre can never grow again, Achilles will never fight for Agamemnon again. This transforms a simple oath into a symbolic death sentence for the Greek army.
🌳 Vivid Natural Imagery: Living vs Dead
"Leaves or twigs" evokes living growth, making the dead sceptre even more stark. Achilles is showing what Agamemnon has killed: the living, growing relationship between commander and greatest warrior. The contrast between natural life (leaves, stems, sprouting) and violent cutting creates emotional impact.
⚔️ Violent Imagery: The Bronze Axe
The "bronze axe" that stripped the sceptre is Agamemnon's insult. This isn't gentle—it's violent destruction. The word "stripped" suggests complete removal of everything that gave life. Achilles is saying: you've destroyed me, so I'm dead to you. The imagery of stripping bark and foliage makes the violation feel brutal and permanent.
🔮 Prophecy: "The Day Is Coming"
This is terrifying because it's absolutely true. We (the audience) know Achilles is right—the Greeks WILL desperately need him. The certainty in "the day is coming" isn't bluster; it's prophecy. "One and all" emphasises the totality of the disaster. Everyone will suffer, not just Agamemnon.
💀 Prophecy: Mass Death
"Fall in their multitudes" is chilling. Not "some will die" but MULTITUDES. "Man-slaying Hector" is a compound epithet that makes Hector sound unstoppable. Without Achilles, Hector is a killing machine. This prophecy will come brutally true in Books 8-17 when Hector nearly destroys the Greek army.
😰 Emotive Language: "In Your Despair"
Achilles isn't just predicting defeat—he's predicting Agamemnon's emotional breakdown. "Despair" is the loss of all hope. This is personal: Achilles wants Agamemnon to feel exactly what he (Achilles) feels now—complete helplessness in the face of disaster.
😱 Emotive Language: "Powerless"
The word "powerless" is devastating for a commander. Agamemnon, who thinks he has all the power now (taking Briseis), will have NONE when it matters. The reversal is complete: the powerful becomes powerless. This is revenge framed as inevitable justice.
💔 Emotive Language: "Tear Your Heart Out"
This physical imagery of self-destruction is incredibly powerful. Agamemnon won't just feel bad—he'll want to destroy himself with guilt. "Tear your heart out" is violent, desperate, irreversible. Achilles is promising that Agamemnon's remorse will be as destructive as his pride was.
🔁 Repetition: The Solemn Oath
Achilles uses formal oath language to give his words binding power. The repetition of "swear" and "oath" makes this ceremonial and irrevocable. This isn't just anger talking—it's a sacred vow. Breaking such an oath would bring divine punishment, so the audience knows Achilles MEANS this.
🔁 Repetition: "The Best of the Greeks"
Achilles ends by reasserting his identity. This phrase "the best of the Greeks" appears throughout Book 1. It's his claim to supreme honour—the very thing Agamemnon has violated. By ending with this, Achilles reminds everyone what's at stake: not just Briseis, but his entire heroic identity and the respect that comes with it.
Why This Speech Works
This isn't just a threat—it's a complete dramatic performance. The metaphor, the prophecy, the emotional intensity, and the formal oath structure all combine to make this speech unforgettable. When this prophecy comes true in later books, we remember THIS moment.

The Great Quarrel: Insults & Accusations

Before the sceptre oath, Achilles and Agamemnon trade increasingly vicious insults. Both men attack the other's claim to leadership and honour. Here's what they actually say:

Achilles Says... Agamemnon Says...
"We joined your expedition, you shameless swine, to please you, to get satisfaction from the Trojans for Menelaus and yourself, dog-face—a fact you utterly ignore." (158-160) "You drunkard, you, with your eyes of a dog and heart of a doe! You never have the courage to arm yourself and go into battle with the men." (225-228)
"You of all people, to rob me of my prize, in person, my hard-earned prize which was a tribute from the army." (161-164) "It suits you better to remain in camp, walking off with the prizes of anyone who contradicts you—a leader who grows fat on his own people!" (231-234)
What They're Really Fighting About
Not Briseis. They're fighting about WHO DESERVES HONOUR. Achilles says: I do the fighting, you take the prizes. Agamemnon says: I'm the commander, I decide who gets what. Both think their honour (timē) gives them the right to Briseis.

Achilles Withdraws From Battle

After the quarrel, Achilles makes his fateful decision: he will no longer fight for Agamemnon. This is devastating because Achilles IS the Greek army's greatest weapon.

"I swear to you that when the day comes that the Greeks one and all are desperate for Achilles, you will be powerless to help them, however much you grieve, as they fall in their multitudes to man-slaying Hector."
— Achilles to Agamemnon, Lines 240-244 (Rieu)
Why Nestor Fails
Nestor, the wise old counsellor, tries to mediate. He appeals to past examples of heroic cooperation. But it doesn't work. Why? Because this quarrel isn't rational—it's about wounded pride. Neither man can back down without losing face.

Athene also intervenes—she stops Achilles from killing Agamemnon by promising him "three times the compensation" later. But the damage is done. Achilles returns to his tent, and the Greek army loses its greatest warrior.

The Quarrel Goes Cosmic

Achilles asks his mother Thetis (a sea-goddess) to persuade Zeus to let the Trojans win battles—so the Greeks will realise they need Achilles. Zeus agrees, but this creates a NEW quarrel: with his wife Hera.

"Here's trouble! You'll provoke a quarrel between me and Hera. She's always at me, saying that I support the Trojans in the war."
— Zeus to Thetis, Lines 518-520 (Rieu)
Divine Quarrel Mirrors Mortal Quarrel
Zeus and Hera fight using the same tactics as Achilles and Agamemnon: insults, threats, wounded pride. Even Hephaestus has to play peacemaker (like Nestor did). Homer is showing us that these conflicts—pride, honour, power—are UNIVERSAL, even among gods.

Book 1 ends with uneasy peace on Olympus. But Zeus has nodded his agreement to Thetis, which means the Trojans WILL gain the upper hand. The consequences of Achilles' withdrawal are about to become catastrophic.
