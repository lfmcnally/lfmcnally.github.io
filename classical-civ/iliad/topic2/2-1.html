Olympian Politics: The Quarrel Goes Cosmic

The book's final movement shifts to Olympus. Achilles appeals to his mother Thetis, who supplicates Zeus. The mortal conflict becomes divine politics.

Why Zeus Owes Achilles
Achilles argues: "Since you bore me to live the briefest of lives, surely Zeus owes me some measure of respect." (352-356) If his life will be short, his honour matters MORE, not less.

Thetis Supplicates Zeus

'Father Zeus, if ever I have served you well by word or deed, grant me this wish: give honour to my son. He is singled out for an early death, and now Agamemnon has dishonoured him. He took his prize in person and now he has her for himself.'
— Lines 503-510 (Rieu)

Thetis appeals to past favours (she once saved Zeus from divine conspiracy). Gods, like heroes, must honour obligations.

Zeus's Dilemma

Zeus is silent for a long time. Why? Political difficulty:

'This is going to mean trouble! You will make me fall foul of my wife Hera. Even as things are, she slanders me constantly and accuses me of helping the Trojans. However, I will see to this matter. When I seal a promise with a nod, there can be no failure to fulfil it, no going back, no deception.'
— Zeus, lines 518-527 (Rieu)

Divine Politics Mirrors Mortal Politics

Zeus's agreement to honour Achilles by helping Trojans creates NEW conflict—with Hera. Just as Agamemnon's refusal to honour Achilles caused crisis among Greeks, Zeus's decision will cause crisis among gods.

Hera's Quarrel with Zeus

Book 1 ends with a divine quarrel mirroring the mortal one. Hera immediately suspects Zeus's meeting with Thetis:

'Dread son of Cronus, what are you suggesting now? I have a horrible fear you have been talked round by silver-footed Thetis. She sat with you this morning and took you by the knees. This makes me suppose you have given your word to honour Achilles and let the Greeks be slaughtered in their multitudes!'
— Hera, lines 551-559 (Rieu)

Zeus responds with threats, asserting supreme authority. But Hera DARES challenge him—showing even divine marriages involve power struggles.

Hephaestus Restores Peace

Like Nestor tried (and failed) for mortals, Hephaestus successfully reconciles Zeus and Hera—but only temporarily:

'Mother, be patient and swallow your resentment, or I may see you thrashed here in front of me. A distressing sight for me, but I will be unable to help you. The Olympian is a hard god to resist.'
— Hephaestus, lines 586-589 (Rieu)

Divine Comedy Ends Book 1

Oddly comic scene: Hephaestus bustling about serving wine. "Helpless laughter seized the blessed gods" watching him.

Why Comedy?
Tonal shift provides relief after intense conflict. Reminds us: for immortal gods, these quarrels don't have the same stakes as mortal conflicts. They can afford to laugh; mortals can't.

Book 1: What Have We Learned?

By the end of Book 1, Homer has established EVERYTHING the epic will explore:

Theme How Book 1 Establishes It
Wrath (Menis) The opening word. Both Achilles' anger at Agamemnon and Apollo's anger at Greeks drive action. Anger (justified or not) brings devastating consequences.
Honour (Timē) The entire quarrel = competing claims to honour. Agamemnon's seizure of Briseis isn't just theft—it's public declaration that Achilles' worth is subordinate. The heroic code is both essential and problematic.
Leadership Agamemnon has institutional authority but questionable judgement; Achilles has martial excellence but subordinate status. Neither can effectively lead alone.
Divine Politics Gods actively interfere based on personal relationships. Zeus's agreement with Thetis will shape the entire war. Divine quarrels mirror mortal ones.
Collective Consequences Individual actions affect entire communities. Agamemnon's insult to Chryses → plague on all Greeks. Achilles' withdrawal → mass Greek deaths. Zeus's favour → affects entire war.
Why Book 1 Matters for Essays
Book 1 provides essential context for EVERY other book. Analysing Achilles' character development? Must understand where he starts. Discussing honour or leadership? Book 1 establishes baseline values. Examining divine intervention? Book 1 shows how gods' personal relationships drive involvement. You CANNOT write sophisticated essays about the Iliad without thorough knowledge of Book 1.

2.1 Book 1 in Detail

📚 A-Level Classics - The Iliad ⏱️ 50 min 📖 E.V. Rieu Translation

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will have a detailed understanding of Book 1's narrative, recognise how Homer establishes all the epic's major themes in the opening book, analyse the characterisation of Achilles and Agamemnon, and understand how the quarrel sets the entire plot in motion.

Book 1: The Foundation

Book 1 is the foundation upon which the entire Iliad is built. In just 611 lines, Homer introduces his central themes, establishes the characters of his two main heroes, sets the plot in motion, and reveals the complex relationships between mortals and gods that will drive the epic forward.

Anger—sing, goddess, the anger of Achilles son of Peleus, that accursed anger, which brought the Greeks endless sufferings and sent the mighty souls of many warriors to Hades, leaving their bodies as carrion for the dogs and a feast for the birds; and Zeus' purpose was fulfilled. It all began when Agamemnon lord of men and godlike Achilles quarrelled and parted.
— Iliad 1.1-7 (Rieu translation)

Notice what Homer tells us immediately: this is a poem about anger. Not the war, not Troy, not even really about Achilles himself—but about his anger and its devastating consequences. Everything that follows stems from this opening.

Why Book 1 Matters
Book 1 is essential for understanding the entire epic. It establishes the conflict that drives the plot (Achilles' withdrawal), introduces the major themes (honour, wrath, mortality), presents the key characters, and shows us how the human and divine worlds interact. You cannot properly understand any later book without understanding Book 1.

The Structure of Book 1

Book 1 divides into seven distinct scenes. Understanding this structure helps you see how carefully organized the book is—and how human conflict leads to divine intervention, which creates more consequences.

Scene 1: Chryses Rejected
Lines 8-52
Chryses (priest of Apollo) begs Agamemnon to return his daughter. Agamemnon brutally refuses. Chryses prays to Apollo for revenge.
Scene 2: The Plague
Lines 43-100
Apollo sends plague for nine days. On day ten, Achilles (not Agamemnon!) calls an assembly to find out why.
Scene 3: Calchas Reveals
Lines 68-120
Prophet Calchas explains Apollo's wrath. Agamemnon must return Chryseis—but demands compensation.
Scene 4: The Quarrel
Lines 121-307
Agamemnon seizes Briseis from Achilles. They exchange vicious insults. Athene stops Achilles from murder. Nestor fails to reconcile them.
Scene 5: Returns
Lines 308-430
Chryseis returned to her father. Briseis taken from Achilles. Achilles withdraws to his hut, refusing to fight.
Scene 6: Thetis & Achilles
Lines 348-430
Achilles' mother Thetis comforts him. Achilles asks her to persuade Zeus to help the Trojans win—so Greeks realize they need him.
Scene 7: Divine Politics
Lines 493-611
Thetis supplicates Zeus, who agrees. Hera quarrels with Zeus about favouring Trojans. Hephaestus restores divine peace.

The Pattern: Human → Divine → Consequences

Notice the structure: Human action → Divine response → Human consequences → Divine politics. This pattern repeats throughout the Iliad.

Also notice ring composition: Book 1 opens with a father (Chryses) supplicating for his child and closes with a mother (Thetis) supplicating for her child. Both supplications succeed, framing the book's action.

Analysing Achilles' Sceptre Oath

This is THE most important speech in Book 1. After Athene stops him from murdering Agamemnon, Achilles swears a devastating oath. Let's break down exactly HOW Homer makes this speech so powerful and memorable.

"But I tell you this, and I swear a solemn oath upon it. By this sceptre, which will never put out leaves or twigs again, once cut from its stem in the hills, it can never sprout again. The bronze axe stripped it of its bark and foliage: it will sprout no more. The Greek judges hold it when they give judgement. By this I solemnly swear that the day is coming when the Greeks one and all will miss Achilles badly, and when in your despair you will be powerless to help them as they fall in their multitudes to man-slaying Hector. Then you will tear your heart out in remorse for giving no respect to the best of the Greeks."
🎯 Extended Metaphor: The Dead Sceptre
Achilles doesn't just swear an oath—he uses the sceptre as a metaphor for his own withdrawal. Like the sceptre cut from its tree, Achilles will be cut from the Greek army. Just as the sceptre can never grow again, Achilles will never fight for Agamemnon again. This transforms a simple oath into a symbolic death sentence for the Greek army.
🌳 Vivid Natural Imagery: Living vs Dead
"Leaves or twigs" evokes living growth, making the dead sceptre even more stark. Achilles is showing what Agamemnon has killed: the living, growing relationship between commander and greatest warrior. The contrast between natural life (leaves, stems, sprouting) and violent cutting creates emotional impact.
⚔️ Violent Imagery: The Bronze Axe
The "bronze axe" that stripped the sceptre is Agamemnon's insult. This isn't gentle—it's violent destruction. "Stripped" suggests complete removal of everything that gave life. Achilles is saying: you've destroyed me, so I'm dead to you. The imagery makes the violation feel brutal and permanent.
🔮 Prophecy: "The Day Is Coming"
This is terrifying because it's absolutely true. We (the audience) know Achilles is right—the Greeks WILL desperately need him. The certainty in "the day is coming" isn't bluster; it's prophecy. "One and all" emphasises totality—everyone will suffer, not just Agamemnon.
💀 Prophecy: Mass Death
"Fall in their multitudes" is chilling. Not "some will die" but MULTITUDES. "Man-slaying Hector" makes Hector sound unstoppable. Without Achilles, Hector is a killing machine. This prophecy will come brutally true in Books 8-17 when Hector nearly destroys the Greek army.
😰 Emotive Language: "In Your Despair"
Achilles predicts Agamemnon's emotional breakdown. "Despair" = loss of all hope. This is personal: Achilles wants Agamemnon to feel exactly what he feels now—complete helplessness in the face of disaster.
😱 Emotive Language: "Powerless"
Devastating for a commander. Agamemnon, who has all the power now (taking Briseis), will have NONE when it matters. The reversal is complete: powerful becomes powerless. Revenge framed as inevitable justice.
💔 Emotive Language: "Tear Your Heart Out"
Physical imagery of self-destruction. Agamemnon won't just feel bad—he'll want to destroy himself with guilt. "Tear your heart out" is violent, desperate, irreversible. Achilles promises remorse will be as destructive as pride was.
🔁 Repetition: The Solemn Oath
Formal oath language gives words binding power. Repetition of "swear" and "oath" makes this ceremonial and irrevocable. This isn't just anger—it's a sacred vow. Breaking such an oath brings divine punishment, so the audience knows Achilles MEANS this.
🔁 Repetition: "The Best of the Greeks"
Achilles ends by reasserting his identity. This phrase appears throughout Book 1. It's his claim to supreme honour—the very thing Agamemnon violated. By ending with this, Achilles reminds everyone what's at stake: not just Briseis, but his entire heroic identity.
Why This Speech Works
This isn't just a threat—it's a complete dramatic performance. The metaphor, the prophecy, the emotional intensity, and the formal oath structure combine to make this speech unforgettable. When this prophecy comes true in later books, we remember THIS moment. That's the power of Homer's technique.

The Great Quarrel: Insults & Accusations

Before the sceptre oath, Achilles and Agamemnon trade increasingly vicious insults. Both men attack the other's claim to leadership and honour. Here's what they actually say:

Achilles Says... Agamemnon Says...
"We joined your expedition, you shameless swine, to please you, to get satisfaction from the Trojans for Menelaus and yourself, dog-face—a fact you utterly ignore." (158-160) "You drunkard, you, with your eyes of a dog and heart of a doe! You never have the courage to arm yourself and go into battle with the men." (225-228)
"You of all people, to rob me of my prize, in person, my hard-earned prize which was a tribute from the army." (161-164) "It suits you better to remain in camp, walking off with the prizes of anyone who contradicts you—a leader who grows fat on his own people!" (231-234)
What They're Really Fighting About
Not Briseis. They're fighting about WHO DESERVES HONOUR. Achilles says: I do the fighting, you take the prizes. Agamemnon says: I'm the commander, I decide who gets what. Both think their honour (timē) gives them the right to Briseis.

Achilles Withdraws From Battle

After the quarrel, Achilles makes his fateful decision: he will no longer fight for Agamemnon. This is devastating because Achilles IS the Greek army's greatest weapon.

"I swear to you that when the day comes that the Greeks one and all are desperate for Achilles, you will be powerless to help them, however much you grieve, as they fall in their multitudes to man-slaying Hector."
— Achilles to Agamemnon, Lines 240-244 (Rieu)
Why Nestor Fails
Nestor, the wise old counsellor, tries to mediate. He appeals to past examples of heroic cooperation. But it doesn't work. Why? Because this quarrel isn't rational—it's about wounded pride. Neither man can back down without losing face.

Athene also intervenes—she stops Achilles from killing Agamemnon by promising him "three times the compensation" later. But the damage is done. Achilles returns to his tent, and the Greek army loses its greatest warrior.

The Quarrel Goes Cosmic

Achilles asks his mother Thetis (a sea-goddess) to persuade Zeus to let the Trojans win battles—so the Greeks will realise they need Achilles. Zeus agrees, but this creates a NEW quarrel: with his wife Hera.

"Here's trouble! You'll provoke a quarrel between me and Hera. She's always at me, saying that I support the Trojans in the war."
— Zeus to Thetis, Lines 518-520 (Rieu)
Divine Quarrel Mirrors Mortal Quarrel
Zeus and Hera fight using the same tactics as Achilles and Agamemnon: insults, threats, wounded pride. Even Hephaestus has to play peacemaker (like Nestor did). Homer is showing us that these conflicts—pride, honour, power—are UNIVERSAL, even among gods.

Book 1 ends with uneasy peace on Olympus. But Zeus has nodded his agreement to Thetis, which means the Trojans WILL gain the upper hand. The consequences of Achilles' withdrawal are about to become catastrophic.
