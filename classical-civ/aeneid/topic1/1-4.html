1.4 Epic Structure and Composition

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Aeneid's overall structure, recognise how Virgil uses techniques like in medias res and parallelism, and appreciate the careful architectural design of the epic.

Written, Not Oral

Unlike Homer's epics, which emerged from oral tradition and show signs of improvisation and variation, the Aeneid is a carefully composed literary text. Virgil worked on it for eleven years, crafting intricate verbal and structural patterns. This allows for a more architectural approach to structure.

Beginning in the Middle

In medias res ("into the middle of things") is the epic convention of beginning not at the chronological start of the story but in the midst of the action. Horace, Virgil's contemporary, codified this as essential to epic.

Where the Aeneid Begins

The poem opens seven years after the fall of Troy, with Aeneas's fleet caught in Juno's storm near Carthage. The destruction of Troy (chronologically first) is narrated only in Book 2, through Aeneas's flashback story to Dido.

Effects of This Technique

Immediate drama: The opening storm creates urgency and action from the first lines.

Characterisation: We meet Aeneas already tested by years of suffering; his past is revealed gradually.

Audience engagement: Roman readers knew the story of Troy's fall; starting in medias res assumes this knowledge.

Narrative variation: The flashback in Books 2-3 allows Virgil to tell Troy's fall in Aeneas's own voice, creating intimacy and pathos.

The Two-Part Division

Books 1-6: The "Odyssean" Half

Focus: Wandering, journey, personal development

Homeric model: Odyssey

Key scenes: Storm, Carthage, Troy flashback, Underworld

Aeneas's role: Passive sufferer, man of sorrows

Climax: Book 6 - Underworld descent and prophecy of Rome

Books 7-12: The "Iliadic" Half

Focus: War, conquest, national destiny

Homeric model: Iliad

Key scenes: Catalogue of warriors, Shield, battles, final duel

Aeneas's role: Active warrior, agent of destiny

Climax: Book 12 - Duel with Turnus

Book 6 as Pivot
The Underworld book stands at the exact centre of the epic and transforms Aeneas. Before it, he is reactive and mourning the past. After seeing Rome's future, he becomes purposeful. The two halves are connected through this turning point.

Parallel Structures

Virgil creates symmetries between books in the two halves. These parallels invite comparison and reveal thematic connections.

Key Parallels

Book 1 / Book 7: Both open new movements with storm/conflict; both introduce a welcoming ruler (Dido/Latinus) whose hospitality is disrupted.

Book 4 / Book 12: Both end with a death caused by Aeneas's mission; Dido's suicide and Turnus's killing parallel each other as victims of Roman destiny.

Book 2 / Book 8: Both are narrated/shown (Troy's fall, Shield of Aeneas); both contrast destruction and future glory.

Book 6 / Book 6: The central book has no parallel‚Äîit stands alone as the pivot of the entire structure.

Unified Design

Opening and Closing

The poem opens with Aeneas nearly destroyed by a storm and ends with Aeneas destroying Turnus. This framing raises the central question: has Aeneas's journey transformed violence into order, or does the ending mirror the opening chaos?

Recurring Images

Fire: Troy burning, Dido's pyre, the fires of war in Italy

Storm: Juno's storm (Book 1), the storm during Dido's "marriage" (Book 4), metaphorical storms of passion

Snakes: The serpents killing Laocoon (Book 2), Allecto's Fury form (Book 7), imagery of civil war

Gold: The golden bough, the shield, wealth as both glory and corruption

Thematic Unity

Despite the two-part structure, consistent themes unify the epic:

Pietas vs Furor: Present from Book 1 to Book 12

Cost of empire: Evident in Creusa, Dido, Pallas, Lausus, Turnus

Divine will vs human suffering: Jupiter's prophecies vs characters' pain

The "Unfinished" Poem
Virgil left some incomplete lines (hemistichoi)‚Äîlines lacking full metrical feet. There are about 58 of these. Some scholars see these as evidence the poem is unfinished; others argue they are deliberate artistic choices creating pauses or emphasis.

Summary: Virgil's Architectural Art

The Aeneid's structure is not merely organisational but meaningful. The two halves create a journey from suffering to action, from past to future, from Odyssean wandering to Iliadic warfare. The parallels between halves invite comparison; the central Book 6 provides the cosmic justification. Every element is positioned for maximum effect.

For the exam, you should be able to discuss how structural choices‚Äîwhere scenes are placed, how books parallel each other, how the opening relates to the ending‚Äîcontribute to the poem's meaning.
