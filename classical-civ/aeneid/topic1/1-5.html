Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and analyse the key literary techniques Virgil uses in the Aeneid, including similes, speeches, ekphrasis, and various narrative devices.

Why Study Literary Techniques?

The OCR specification requires understanding of "language of the epic including the use of speeches, themes, flashback, similes and other narrative and descriptive techniques and their effects." Identifying these techniques in specific passages is essential for exam success.

Exam Approach
When analysing passages, look for: (1) What technique is used? (2) How does it work? (3) What effect does it create? (4) How does it connect to broader themes? Always link technique to meaning.

Epic Similes

Epic similes are extended comparisons, often running several lines, that compare events in the narrative to scenes from nature, everyday life, or myth. Virgil inherits this technique from Homer but uses it with distinctive Roman applications.

Example: Neptune Calming the Storm (Book 1)

"Just as when sedition often arises in a great people, and the common crowd rages with passion, and now firebrands and stones fly (fury supplies weapons), then if by chance they see a man respected for his piety and services, they fall silent and stand with ears alert; he rules their spirits with his words and soothes their hearts..."

Analysis: The calming of the sea is compared to a statesman calming a riot. This reverses the expected direction (nature ‚Üí human), making the natural world reflect Roman political order. It also introduces the theme of pietas as a force for order against furor.

Example: Dido's Passion as a Wound (Book 4)

"She feeds the wound with her life-blood and is consumed by hidden fire... like a deer struck by an arrow, which a shepherd hunting through Cretan woods has pierced from afar, unaware, leaving the winged steel in her side; she flees through forests and groves of Dicte‚Äîbut the deadly shaft clings to her flank."

Analysis: This simile appears early in Book 4, foreshadowing Dido's death. The deer is innocent; the shepherd is "unaware"‚Äîsuggesting Aeneas is not deliberately cruel. Yet the wound is fatal. The simile creates sympathy for Dido while complicating blame.

Example: Turnus as a Trapped Lion (Book 9)

Turnus, surrounded in the Trojan camp, is compared to a lion hemmed by hunters‚Äîmagnificent, dangerous, but doomed. This simile elevates Turnus while also suggesting his ultimate fate.

Functions of Similes
Virgilian similes: (1) create visual imagery, (2) pause intense action for reflection, (3) connect epic events to everyday Roman experience, (4) foreshadow outcomes, (5) guide emotional response, (6) introduce thematic commentary.

Speeches and Direct Discourse

Like Homer, Virgil gives his characters extensive speeches. About 40% of the Aeneid is direct speech. Speeches reveal character, advance plot, and allow rhetorical display.

Types of Speech

Deliberative: Speeches urging a course of action (e.g., Ilioneus's plea to Dido, Book 1)

Forensic: Speeches of accusation or defence (e.g., Dido's accusations against Aeneas, Book 4)

Epideictic: Speeches of praise or blame (e.g., Anchises's praise of future Romans, Book 6)

Soliloquy: Characters speaking their inner thoughts aloud (e.g., Dido's final speech, Book 4)

Key Speeches to Know

Aeneas to his men (1.198-207): "Forsan et haec olim meminisse iuvabit" ("Perhaps one day it will please us to remember even these things"). Shows Aeneas as leader concealing his own despair.

Dido's curse (4.607-629): Prophesies eternal enmity between Carthage and Rome, invoking Hannibal. Links myth to Roman history.

Anchises's mission statement (6.851-853): "Tu regere imperio populos, Romane, memento..." ("You, Roman, remember to rule peoples with empire...")‚Äîdefines Roman identity.

Analysing Speeches
Consider: Who speaks? To whom? What is the rhetorical purpose? What emotions are evoked? How does the speech reveal character? Does the speaker's rhetoric match their actions?

Other Key Techniques

Ekphrasis (Description of Art Objects)

Extended descriptions of artworks within the narrative. Key examples:

Temple of Juno (Book 1): Aeneas sees murals depicting the Trojan War, prompting his famous cry "sunt lacrimae rerum" ("there are tears for things").

Shield of Aeneas (Book 8): Depicts Roman history from Romulus to Augustus. Unlike the timeless Shield of Achilles, Virgil's is teleological‚Äîhistory moving toward a destined end.

Doors of Apollo's Temple (Book 6): Daedalus's carvings, including his failed attempt to depict Icarus‚Äîart's limits when facing grief.

Flashback (Analepsis)

Books 2-3 are one extended flashback as Aeneas narrates Troy's fall to Dido. This technique allows first-person emotional immediacy for events that occurred before the poem's "present."

Prophecy (Prolepsis)

Multiple prophecies point forward: Jupiter's to Venus (Book 1), the Parade of Heroes (Book 6), the Shield (Book 8). These create dramatic irony‚Äîreaders know what characters cannot‚Äîand reinforce the theme of fate.

Apostrophe

The narrator directly addresses characters, breaking the fourth wall. "Infelix Dido" ("unhappy Dido"), "miserande puer" ("pitiable boy," to Lausus). These interventions guide reader sympathy.

Pathetic Fallacy

Nature reflects emotional states. The storm mirrors Juno's rage; the cave during the "marriage" of Dido and Aeneas is accompanied by thunder and lightning (ill omens); Turnus's death is accompanied by shadows.

Epithets

Repeated descriptive phrases attached to characters: pius Aeneas (pious/dutiful Aeneas), pater Aeneas (father Aeneas), infelix Dido (unhappy Dido). These guide interpretation and create formula-like effects echoing Homer.

Summary: Technique and Meaning

Virgil's literary techniques are never merely decorative. Similes interpret action; speeches reveal character and advance themes; ekphrasis connects narrative to cosmic or historical meaning; apostrophe guides emotional response. Always ask: what does this technique do?
