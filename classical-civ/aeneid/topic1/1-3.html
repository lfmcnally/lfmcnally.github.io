1.3 Homeric Influence

üìö Topic 1: Introduction to the Aeneid ‚è±Ô∏è 35 min üìä Literary Analysis

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify specific parallels between the Aeneid and Homer's Iliad and Odyssey, understand how Virgil uses these models, and analyse how he transforms Homeric material to serve his own purposes.

Why Homer Matters

The OCR specification explicitly requires study of "Homeric influence" on the Aeneid. This is not incidental‚ÄîVirgil deliberately constructed his epic as a response to and rivalry with Homer. Every educated Roman would have known the Iliad and Odyssey, and Virgil expected readers to recognize his allusions.

The Aeneid engages with Homer through imitation (using similar scenes, epithets, and structures), emulation (attempting to surpass Homer), and transformation (changing Homeric material to express Roman values).

The "Two Halves" Structure
The most fundamental Homeric influence is structural: Books 1-6 (wandering, storms, Underworld) echo the Odyssey, while Books 7-12 (war in Italy) echo the Iliad. Virgil combines both epics into one, implicitly claiming to surpass both.

Parallels with the Odyssey (Books 1-6)

Odyssey Aeneid Significance
Poseidon's storm (Book 5) Juno's storm (Book 1) Both heroes are shipwrecked by hostile deities; establishes divine opposition
Calypso's island Dido's Carthage A queen/goddess delays the hero; but Dido is far more developed than Calypso
Odysseus's tales to Phaeacians (Books 9-12) Aeneas's tale to Dido (Books 2-3) Flashback narration by the hero; but Aeneas narrates tragedy, not adventure
Odysseus visits the dead (Book 11) Aeneas descends to Underworld (Book 6) Meeting deceased companions and prophecy; Virgil's is far more elaborate
Athena helps Odysseus Venus helps Aeneas Divine mother/patron protects the hero

Key Transformation: Dido vs Calypso/Circe

In the Odyssey, Calypso and Circe are obstacles Odysseus must overcome on his way home to Penelope. They are powerful but essentially static figures.

Virgil's Dido is radically different: a fully realized tragic heroine with her own history, kingdom, and moral complexity. Her story draws on Greek tragedy (especially Euripides' Medea) as much as Homer. When Aeneas leaves, the emotional weight falls on Dido, not on Aeneas.

Key Transformation: The Underworld

Odysseus's nekyia (Book 11) is brief and focused on personal ghosts. Aeneas's katabasis (full descent, Book 6) is comprehensive: geography of the afterlife, philosophical teachings on reincarnation, and the Parade of Heroes showing Rome's future.

Virgil expands Homer's personal journey into a cosmic and political vision.

Iliad Aeneid Significance
Catalogue of Ships (Book 2) Catalogue of Italian forces (Book 7) Epic convention; establishes scope of conflict
Shield of Achilles (Book 18) Shield of Aeneas (Book 8) Divine armour depicting cosmic/historical scenes
Patroclus's death Pallas's death (Book 10) Beloved companion killed by enemy leader; triggers hero's rage
Doloneia night raid (Book 10) Nisus and Euryalus (Book 9) Night expedition ending in death; Virgil's is more tragic

Priam begging for Hector's body (Book 24) Turnus begging for mercy (Book 12) Enemy at hero's mercy; crucially different outcomes

Key Transformation: The Shield

Homer's Shield of Achilles shows a timeless vision of human life: peace and war, city and country, marriage and harvest. It is universal and philosophical.

Virgil's Shield of Aeneas shows specific Roman history: Romulus and Remus, the Gauls, Catiline, and (climactically) Augustus at Actium. It is teleological‚Äîhistory moving toward a destined end.

Key Transformation: The Final Duel

The Iliad ends with Achilles returning Hector's body to Priam‚Äîa moment of shared humanity transcending enmity. The poem closes with funeral rites and a sense of tragic reconciliation.

The Aeneid ends with Aeneas killing the suppliant Turnus in a "flame of rage" (furiis accensus). There is no reconciliation, no funeral, only violence. This transformation of the Homeric model is deeply significant for interpreting the poem.

Exam Focus
When discussing Homeric influence, always note HOW Virgil changes his model. The comparison between Iliad 24 (mercy) and Aeneid 12 (killing) is one of the most discussed parallels in classical scholarship.

What Virgil Adds to Homer

Roman Values

Homer's heroes seek personal glory (kleos) and honour (time). Virgil's Aeneas must subordinate personal desire to duty (pietas) and fate (fatum). This reflects Roman rather than Greek heroic ideals.

Historical Consciousness

Homer's epics exist in mythological time with minimal reference to the poet's present. Virgil constantly connects mythological past to Roman present: prophecies of Rome, the site of future Rome, the Shield's Roman history. The Aeneid is a foundation myth for a specific historical entity.

Psychological Depth

Virgil gives his characters more complex inner lives. Dido's passion, Aeneas's conflict between love and duty, Turnus's mixture of heroism and fatalism‚Äîthese receive extended psychological treatment that goes beyond Homeric characterization.

Philosophical Content

The Aeneid incorporates Stoic and Platonic philosophy, particularly in Book 6's teachings on the soul and reincarnation. Homer's afterlife is vague and cheerless; Virgil's is a structured moral cosmos.

Sympathy for the Defeated

While Homer shows sympathy for Trojans (especially Hector), Virgil makes them his protagonists. More remarkably, he extends profound sympathy to those the Trojans/Romans defeat: Dido, Camilla, Turnus. This creates the poem's characteristic ambivalence.

Summary: Imitation and Originality
Virgil's use of Homer is neither slavish copying nor rejection. He assumes readers know Homer, quotes and adapts Homer, but transforms Homeric material to create something distinctively Roman. The Aeneid is a creative dialogue with Homer, not a translation.
