Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will understand Virgil's biography and literary career, recognise how the Aeneid fits within the Roman literary tradition, and appreciate Virgil's relationship with Augustus and his cultural programme.

Why Study the Author?

Unlike Homer, whose identity remains mysterious, we know a great deal about Publius Vergilius Maro (70-19 BC). Understanding Virgil's life, earlier works, and the literary environment of Augustan Rome helps us interpret the Aeneid's political dimensions and artistic choices.

Exam Relevance
The OCR specification requires understanding of "Virgil's relationship to Augustus and his regime and the extent to which they are promoted within the epic." Knowing Virgil's career and patronage network is essential for addressing this.

Virgil's Life (70-19 BC)

Early Life

Virgil was born near Mantua in Cisalpine Gaul (northern Italy), not in Rome itself. His family were modest landowners, possibly of equestrian status. This provincial background gave Virgil an outsider's perspective on Roman politics and culture.

He studied in Cremona, Milan, and Rome, receiving an excellent education in rhetoric and philosophy, particularly Epicureanism. Unlike many Roman poets, Virgil avoided public life and never held political office.

The Civil Wars

Virgil came of age during Rome's bloodiest era. He was 21 when Julius Caesar crossed the Rubicon (49 BC), 26 at Caesar's assassination (44 BC), and 31 at the Battle of Actium (31 BC). The land confiscations following Philippi (42 BC) may have affected his family's property.

This experience of civil war profoundly shaped the Aeneid's meditations on violence, suffering, and the cost of political order.

Patronage and Maecenas

Gaius Maecenas, Augustus's unofficial minister of culture, became Virgil's patron around 38 BC. Through Maecenas, Virgil entered Rome's literary elite and gained financial security. This patronage system meant poets like Virgil could dedicate themselves to art‚Äîbut also created expectations of supporting Augustus's regime.

Death and the Aeneid's Fate

Virgil worked on the Aeneid for the last eleven years of his life (29-19 BC). He died in 19 BC at Brundisium, returning from a trip to Greece where he had planned to revise the poem. On his deathbed, he reportedly asked for the manuscript to be burned, considering it unfinished.

Augustus overruled this wish and had the poem published by Virgil's literary executors, Varius and Tucca. The Aeneid became Rome's national epic almost immediately.

Virgil's Earlier Works

Before the Aeneid, Virgil established his reputation with two other major works, each in a different genre. These show his development as a poet and his growing engagement with Roman politics.

Eclogues (c. 42-39 BC)

Ten pastoral poems modelled on the Greek poet Theocritus. These depict idealised shepherds singing of love and loss in an Arcadian landscape. However, the civil war intrudes: Eclogue 1 features a shepherd dispossessed of his land, while Eclogue 4 (the "Messianic" Eclogue) prophesies a golden age ushered in by a mysterious child.

The Eclogues established Virgil's reputation and brought him to Maecenas's attention. Their blend of Greek form with Roman content set the pattern for his career.

Georgics (c. 36-29 BC)

A didactic poem in four books about farming: crops (Book 1), trees and vines (Book 2), livestock (Book 3), and bees (Book 4). Modelled on Hesiod's Works and Days, the Georgics celebrate Italian rural life and honest labour while exploring themes of suffering, death, and renewal.

The Georgics were completed as Augustus consolidated power. They promote the values Augustus claimed to restore: traditional piety, hard work, and connection to the land. The famous ending, the story of Orpheus and Eurydice, foreshadows the Aeneid's tragic exploration of loss.

Progression of Genres
Virgil's career follows a "wheel" (rota Vergilii) from low to high style: pastoral (humble shepherds) ‚Üí didactic (farmers) ‚Üí epic (heroes and nations). This deliberate ascent through genres, culminating in the Aeneid, shows Virgil positioning himself as Rome's answer to Homer.

Augustan Literary Culture

The Augustan Age

Augustus's reign (27 BC - AD 14) is considered a "Golden Age" of Latin literature. Major poets included Virgil, Horace, Propertius, Tibullus, and Ovid; prose writers included Livy. This flowering was not accidental‚ÄîAugustus and Maecenas actively cultivated literature that would support the new regime.

Patronage and Politics

The patron-client relationship was central to Roman society. Poets depended on wealthy patrons for financial support and social access. In return, they were expected to produce works that reflected well on their patrons. Maecenas's circle specifically promoted Augustus's cultural agenda: celebrating peace, traditional values, and Rome's divine destiny.

However, patronage did not mean simple propaganda. Virgil and Horace maintained artistic independence, and their works contain ambiguities that resist easy political readings.

The Need for a National Epic

Rome had conquered Greece militarily but felt culturally inferior. Greeks had Homer; Romans had adapted Greek literature but lacked an original epic. Earlier attempts (Naevius's Bellum Poenicum, Ennius's Annales) were historical epics, lacking Homer's mythological scope.

The Aeneid solved this by linking Rome to Troy and thus to Greek mythology, while surpassing Homer in literary polish and philosophical depth.

Virgil and Augustus

The Aeneid clearly supports Augustan ideology: Rome's destiny is divinely ordained, pietas and duty are supreme values, and Augustus (mentioned in the Parade of Heroes and depicted on the Shield) is the culmination of Roman history. Virgil reportedly recited portions to Augustus, who wept at the reference to his nephew Marcellus's death.

Yet the poem's sympathy for losers‚ÄîDido, Turnus‚Äîand its ambiguous ending have led many modern readers to detect a "pessimistic" or "subversive" voice beneath the Augustan surface.

Critical Debate
Scholars divide into "Augustan" readers (the Aeneid celebrates Rome and Augustus) and "anti-Augustan" or "ambivalent" readers (the Aeneid questions imperial violence). For the exam, you should be able to discuss both views with evidence from the text.
