Book 7: The Second Beginning

Book 7 opens the second half of the Aeneid‚Äîthe "Iliadic" half focused on war rather than wandering. Virgil invokes the Muse again, signalling a new beginning. The Trojans arrive in Italy, receive a peaceful welcome, then are plunged into war by divine intervention.

Lines 1-36: Death of Caieta; second invocation of the Muse

Lines 37-106: Arrival in Latium; the fulfilled prophecy (eating tables)

Lines 107-285: King Latinus; the oracle about Lavinia

Lines 406-571: Juno's intervention; Allecto summoned

Lines 641-817: The deer incident; war breaks out

Arrival in Latium

The Prophecy Fulfilled

The Trojans land at the Tiber's mouth. When they eat bread "tables" with food on top, Ascanius jokes "we're eating our tables too!" Aeneas recognises the prophecy fulfilled (Celaeno in Book 3, Anchises in Book 6): they have found their destined home. This transforms a curse into a blessing.

King Latinus

Latinus rules a peaceful, prosperous Latium. He has one daughter, Lavinia, sought by many suitors, especially Turnus of the Rutulians. But omens and oracles warn Latinus: his daughter must marry a foreigner, and their descendants will rule the world.

The Embassy

Aeneas sends ambassadors bearing gifts. Latinus, recognising the prophecy's fulfilment, offers Lavinia to Aeneas and alliance with the Trojans. Peace seems assured‚Äîbut Juno intervenes.

Allecto's Intervention

Juno's Rage

Juno, seeing the Trojans about to achieve their destiny, rages: "If I cannot change the will of heaven, I will move Hell." She summons Allecto, a Fury from the Underworld, to destroy the peace. This parallels Book 1's storm‚ÄîJuno works through supernatural agents to obstruct fate.

Queen Amata

Allecto first attacks Queen Amata, Latinus's wife, who favours Turnus. The Fury throws a snake into her breast, kindling furor. Amata rages through the city, then leads Latin women in Bacchic frenzy to the mountains, hiding Lavinia.

Turnus Inflamed

Allecto visits Turnus in disguise, urging him to fight. When he dismisses her, she reveals her true form and throws a torch into his heart. Turnus wakes burning with war-madness: "Arms! I demand arms!" His furor will drive the war.

The Deer Incident

Allecto engineers the casus belli: Ascanius, hunting, wounds a pet deer belonging to Silvia, daughter of Latinus's herdsman. The Latins arm themselves; Trojans defend Ascanius; blood is shed. From this trivial incident, war erupts‚Äîfate and Fury working through chance.

Furor vs Fate
Book 7 establishes the pattern of the war books: fate destines Aeneas's success, but furor (divine and human) creates the suffering along the way. Juno cannot prevent the outcome but can ensure the cost is terrible. The innocent (Silvia's deer, the Latin farmers) suffer first.

The Catalogue of Italian Forces

Epic Convention

The Catalogue of Italian warriors (7.641-817) echoes the Catalogue of Ships in Iliad 2. This formal list establishes the epic scale of the coming war and honours the Italian peoples who will eventually become Romans.

Key Warriors

Mezentius: Exiled Etruscan king, "contemptor divum" (scorner of gods). His impiety will prove fatal.

Lausus: Mezentius's noble son, devoted despite his father's cruelty‚Äîa tragic figure.

Turnus: Prince of the Rutulians, Lavinia's spurned suitor. Magnificent in arms, driven by wounded pride.

Camilla: Virgin warrior queen of the Volscians, raised in the wild, devoted to Diana. A Roman Amazon.

The Italian Landscape

The Catalogue maps ancient Italy: Etruria, the Sabine hills, Praeneste, Tibur, Ardea, Aricia. Virgil's Roman readers would recognise their own countryside, now populated with heroic ancestors. The war is fought on ground they know.

Sympathy for the Enemy

Unlike Homer's Catalogue, Virgil's treats the "enemy" sympathetically. These Italians are not villains but future Romans. Their defeat is also their incorporation into the Roman story. This double perspective shapes the war's tragedy.

Rome's Double Heritage
The Catalogue establishes that Rome descends from both Trojans and Italians. The war is not foreign conquest but civil war between future countrymen. This makes the violence more tragic‚Äîand the eventual peace more meaningful.
