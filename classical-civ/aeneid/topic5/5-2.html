Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will understand Book 8's structure, recognise the significance of the Roman site at Pallanteum, and analyse the Shield of Aeneas as Augustan propaganda.

Book 8: Rome Before Rome

Book 8 takes Aeneas to the future site of Rome to seek alliance with Evander, a Greek king living on the Palatine Hill. This allows Virgil to show Roman readers their city in its primitive origins‚Äîand on the Shield of Aeneas, its glorious future.

Key Sections

Lines 1-101: Turnus's preparations; the river god Tiberinus appears to Aeneas

Lines 102-183: Journey up the Tiber; arrival at Pallanteum

Lines 184-305: Evander welcomes Aeneas; the feast of Hercules

Lines 306-369: The story of Hercules and Cacus

Lines 370-453: Tour of the Roman site; Evander's simple dwelling

Lines 454-607: Venus obtains armour from Vulcan

Evander and Pallanteum

The River God's Command

Tiberinus, the Tiber's god, appears to Aeneas in a dream: seek alliance with Evander, a Greek settled on the future site of Rome. As confirmation, Aeneas will see a white sow with thirty piglets‚Äîsignalling Alba Longa (to be founded thirty years hence).

Arcadian Greeks in Italy

Evander is an Arcadian Greek who settled on the Palatine Hill, founding Pallanteum (named for his ancestor Pallas). His son Pallas will join Aeneas's forces‚Äîand die tragically in Book 10. Greek-Trojan alliance against the Italians complicates ethnic categories.

The Tour of Rome

Evander shows Aeneas the future Roman sites: the Asylum, the Lupercal (where Romulus and Remus were suckled), the Tarpeian Rock, the Capitol (now overgrown, later to hold Jupiter's temple). Virgil's readers would recognise every site, now transformed by Augustus's building programme.

Humble Hospitality

Evander's dwelling is modest: "Dare to scorn wealth... make yourself worthy of the god." Aeneas sleeps on leaves under a bearskin. This primitive virtue contrasts with (and legitimates) Augustan Rome. True Roman values precede luxury.

Aude, hospes, contemnere opes et te quoque dignum finge deo...

Dare, guest, to scorn wealth and make yourself too worthy of the god...

Hercules and Cacus

The Story

Evander tells how Hercules killed the monster Cacus who terrorised the site. Cacus, half-human son of Vulcan, lived in a cave on the Aventine, stole Hercules's cattle (dragging them backwards to hide the tracks), and was destroyed when Hercules tore open the cave and strangled him.

Mythological Layering

Cacus represents primal Italian chaos‚Äîhalf-beast, fire-breathing, dwelling in darkness. Hercules is the civilising hero who makes the land habitable. The story establishes Pallanteum/Rome as a site purified by heroic action.

Augustan Parallels

Romans would recognise parallels: Augustus as Hercules, defeating monstrous opponents (Antony?). The Altar of Hercules at the Forum Boarium, where the poem's Evander worships, was an ancient Roman site. Myth and contemporary politics interweave.

Aeneas as Hercules

The story also parallels Aeneas's task: he must defeat monstrous furor (Turnus, inflamed by the Fury) to make Italy safe for Roman civilisation. Hercules prefigures Aeneas's role as civilising hero.

The Shield of Aeneas

Divine Armour

Venus persuades her husband Vulcan to forge armour for Aeneas. The scene parallels the Iliad (Thetis obtaining armour for Achilles), but Virgil's shield depicts not the cosmos but Roman history.

Scenes on the Shield

Romulus and Remus: The she-wolf suckling the twins; Rome's foundation

The Rape of the Sabines: The wars and eventual peace with the Sabines

Horatius and the Bridge: Republican heroism against Lars Porsena

The Gauls at the Capitol: The geese warning, Manlius's defence

Religious Ceremonies: Salii dancing, Roman ritual

Tartarus and Elysium: Catiline punished, Cato giving laws to the virtuous

Battle of Actium: Augustus versus Antony and Cleopatra

Augustus's Triple Triumph: Victory celebration, nations submitting

Actium as Climax

The shield's centre shows Actium (31 BC): Augustus's fleet versus Antony with "Egyptian wife" Cleopatra and their monstrous gods. Apollo draws his bow; the enemy flees. This recent history is presented as cosmic battle between Roman order and Eastern chaos.

The Shield's Meaning
Aeneas lifts onto his shoulders "the fame and fate of his descendants" (famamque et fata nepotum). He doesn't understand the images but carries them. This captures the poem's theology: the hero serves a destiny beyond his knowledge. Roman history is his burden and glory.
