How Virgil Tells His Story

Virgil doesn't just TELL the story‚Äîhe shapes HOW and WHEN we learn information. The Aeneid uses sophisticated narrative techniques: beginning in the middle of the action (in medias res), extended flashbacks, descriptive pauses (ekphrasis), prophecies that create dramatic irony, and constant foreshadowing. These aren't just stylistic choices‚Äîthey shape our emotional response to the epic.

Why Structure Matters
The ORDER in which we learn information shapes our emotional response. We meet Aeneas suffering BEFORE we learn why. We know Rome's destiny BEFORE watching Aeneas's painful journey to create it. This creates sympathy and makes his sacrifices meaningful‚Äîwe understand the cosmic stakes even when characters don't.

Chronological Order

  • Trojan War ends, Troy falls
  • Aeneas escapes with refugees
  • Seven years wandering Mediterranean
  • Storm wrecks fleet near Carthage
  • Meets Dido, tells Troy story

Narrative Order (How Virgil Tells It)

  • Book 1: Storm (7 years AFTER Troy)
  • Book 1: Aeneas meets Dido
  • Book 2: FLASHBACK to Troy's fall
  • Book 3: FLASHBACK to wandering
  • Book 4: RETURNS to present

In Medias Res: Beginning in the Middle

"In medias res" means "into the middle of things"‚Äîthe epic starts NOT at the chronological beginning but SEVEN YEARS into Aeneas's journey.

I sing of arms and the man who first from Troy's shores, exiled by fate, came to Italy‚Äîmuch buffeted on land and sea by the gods' violence, because of cruel Juno's unforgetting anger.
‚Äî Aeneid 1.1-7

Why Start in the Middle?

Immediate drama: We jump straight into crisis (storm, shipwreck, despair) rather than slow buildup.

Mystery: We wonder WHY Aeneas is suffering. The flashback becomes revelation.

Emphasizes suffering over action: We meet Aeneas at his low point‚Äîbroken, wishing for death.

Homer's Influence vs Virgil's Innovation

Homer's Odyssey

  • 10 years after Troy
  • Odysseus on Calypso's island
  • Flashback: Adventures
  • Tone: Adventurous, episodic

Virgil's Aeneid

  • 7 years after Troy
  • Aeneas shipwrecked
  • Flashback: Trauma narrative
  • Tone: Tragic, unified

The Flashback Structure: Books 2-3

Books 2-3 are entirely Aeneas's first-person narrative‚Äîa massive flashback revealing Troy's fall and his wandering. This isn't just backstory‚Äîit's CHARACTER REVELATION through storytelling.

What First-Person Narration Achieves

  • Subjective emotion: "I saw," "I felt"‚Äîwe access Aeneas's inner experience
  • Controlled revelation: Aeneas chooses WHAT to tell‚Äîhis selectivity reveals character
  • Unreliable perspective: This is HIS version‚Äîpotentially biased, definitely traumatized
  • Sympathy manipulation: Presents himself as dutiful, pious, tragic

Aeneas as Narrator

Notice what he emphasizes: (1) Obeyed gods, (2) Saved father and son (pietas), (3) Fought bravely, (4) Losses were fated. This is SELF-JUSTIFICATION disguised as storytelling‚Äîproving he's worthy despite failing to save Troy.

Ekphrasis: Descriptive Pause

Ekphrasis is extended description of visual art‚Äîthe narrative PAUSES to describe something in detail. The main example in Books 1-2 is the Temple murals depicting the Trojan War.

Aeneas scans the whole temple‚Äîeverywhere the battles fought at Troy. He stops and weeps: "Look‚ÄîPriam! Here too there are tears for suffering, and mortal sorrows touch the heart."
‚Äî Aeneid 1.450-463

Functions of the Temple Ekphrasis

  • Characterization: Aeneas weeps‚Äîshows unhealed trauma
  • Theme: Art preserves memory‚Äîstories survive
  • Connection: Dido honors HIS suffering in HER temple
  • Irony: In Juno's temple‚Äîgoddess who caused this suffering
  • Foreshadowing: Troy's fall became art; so will Dido's tragedy

"Sunt Lacrimae Rerum"

"There are tears for things"‚Äîone of the Aeneid's most famous lines. Expresses UNIVERSAL EMPATHY. Even Carthaginians (future enemies) mourn Trojan suffering. This gives Aeneas hope that kindness exists in the world.

Foreshadowing and Prophecy

Virgil constantly tells us what WILL happen before it occurs. This creates dramatic irony‚Äîwe know outcomes characters don't.

BOOK 1
Jupiter's Prophecy
"Empire without end"‚Äîwe know Rome will succeed. Everything else is HOW, not IF.
BOOK 1
"Infelix Dido"
Narrator calls Dido "doomed" BEFORE her love develops. We know she's fated for tragedy.
BOOK 4

"The shaft of death clings to her side"‚Äîlove is already a fatal wound.
BOOK 2
Flame on Ascanius
Harmless divine fire‚Äîprophecy of future kingship. Contrasts Troy's destructive flames.

Why Tell Us the Ending?

Modern stories hide outcomes for suspense. Virgil does the OPPOSITE‚Äîtells us what will happen, then makes us watch it unfold. This shifts focus from "what?" to "how and why?" We're not surprised by Dido's death; we're moved by watching the inevitable tragedy develop.

Exam Strategy

Analyzing Narrative Techniques

1. WHEN do we learn information? (Order creates effects)
2. HOW do we learn it? (Prophecy, flashback, narrator)
3. WHO tells us? (First-person vs third-person)
4. WHAT is the effect? (Sympathy, irony, suspense)
5. WHY this structure? (What does ORDER achieve?)

Connect Technique to Theme
Don't just identify‚ÄîANALYZE. "Virgil uses in medias res" = identification. "Virgil uses in medias res to emphasize suffering over heroism, making us meet Aeneas at his lowest point before understanding his past, which creates sympathy" = ANALYSIS.
