Book 2: The Fall of Troy

Book 2 is Aeneas's first-person flashback narration to Dido, recounting Troy's final night. It is one of the most celebrated books of the epic, combining horror, pathos, and heroism.

Key Sections

Lines 1-56: The Wooden Horse and Greek stratagem

Lines 57-198: Sinon's deception; Laocoon's warning and death

Lines 199-267: The horse enters Troy; night falls

Lines 268-369: Greeks emerge; Hector's ghost appears to Aeneas

Lines 370-558: Street fighting; Priam's death

Lines 559-633: Helen scene; Venus's revelation

Lines 634-804: Escape with Anchises, Ascanius, and Creusa's loss

The Wooden Horse

Equo ne credite, Teucri! Quidquid id est, timeo Danaos et dona ferentes.

Do not trust the horse, Trojans! Whatever it is, I fear Greeks even bearing gifts.
‚Äî Laocoon, 2.48-49

Sinon's Deception

Sinon, a Greek left behind, pretends to be a deserter. He tells a convincing story: Odysseus persecuted him; the horse is an offering to Athena; if brought inside Troy, the Trojans will conquer Greece.

This scene shows Greek cunning (dolus) versus Trojan trust‚Äîand the fatal consequences of misplaced hospitality.

Laocoon and the Serpents

The Warning Ignored

Laocoon, priest of Neptune, hurls his spear at the horse and warns against Greek treachery. But his warning is ignored, and fate intervenes terribly.

The Serpents

Two monstrous serpents emerge from the sea and kill Laocoon and his two sons at the altar. This is one of the epic's most horrific scenes‚Äîinnocent children destroyed, a prophet punished for speaking truth.

The Trojans interpret this as divine punishment for attacking the sacred horse. In reality, it ensures Troy's destruction.

Divine Cruelty
The Laocoon episode raises questions about divine justice. Why is the truth-teller punished? Virgil shows gods ensuring fate even through apparent injustice‚Äîa troubling theology.

The Fall of Troy

Hector's Ghost

As the Greeks pour from the horse, Hector's ghost appears to Aeneas in a dream. Hector is disfigured‚Äîas he was when Achilles dragged him‚Äîand weeps. He commands Aeneas to flee and save Troy's gods (sacra) and Penates.

This scene transfers Hector's role to Aeneas: the dead champion passes the mission to the survivor.

Priam's Death

The aged Priam arms himself and goes to fight. His son Polites is killed before his eyes by Pyrrhus (Neoptolemus), Achilles' son. Priam throws his spear feebly and is butchered at his own altar.

Virgil emphasises the sacrilege: a king killed at a sacred altar, the Penates spattered with blood. This is the ultimate violation of pietas.

Haec finis Priami fatorum... hoc illum finis habuit...

This was the end of Priam's fate... this end took him...

Aeneas's Escape

Venus's Revelation

Aeneas, maddened by rage, nearly kills Helen (in a famous textually disputed passage). His mother Venus appears and reveals that the gods themselves‚ÄîNeptune, Juno, Athena‚Äîare destroying Troy. Mortal resistance is futile.

Anchises' Refusal and Signs

Anchises initially refuses to leave, preferring to die in Troy. Only divine signs convince him: a flame plays harmlessly on Ascanius's head, and a shooting star indicates the way. Anchises finally agrees to flee.

The Loss of Creusa

Aeneas carries Anchises on his shoulders and leads Ascanius by hand. His wife Creusa follows behind. In the chaos, she is lost. Aeneas searches frantically until her ghost appears. She tells him not to grieve‚Äîfate takes her, but he must go to Italy and a new kingdom.

Creusa's loss establishes the pattern: Aeneas's destiny requires loss, and the women who love him pay the price.

Book 2's Significance
Book 2 shows what Aeneas has lost, justifying his sorrow throughout the epic. It also establishes him as pius‚Äîsaving father, son, and gods‚Äîwhile showing the terrible cost of Troy's fall and foreshadowing future losses.
