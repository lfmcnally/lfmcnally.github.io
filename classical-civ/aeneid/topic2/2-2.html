2.2 Speeches and Direct Discourse in the Aeneid

üìö A-Level Classical Civilisation ‚è±Ô∏è 50 min üìñ Virgil's Aeneid

Why Speeches Matter in Epic

Speeches reveal character, advance plot, express emotion, and demonstrate rhetoric‚Äîthe art of persuasion Romans valued above almost everything else. Virgil's characters speak in ways that reveal their education, status, emotional state, and moral character. A good speech shows pietas and ratio (reason); a bad speech shows furor and self-interest.

Direct Discourse vs Narrative Summary
When Virgil gives someone a SPEECH (direct quotation), he's signaling: THIS MATTERS. Pay attention to what's said, how it's said, and whether it works. When he just summarizes ("Aeneas explained the situation"), the content matters less than the fact that speaking occurred.

What Speeches DO

  • Reveal inner thoughts/emotions
  • Attempt to persuade/manipulate
  • Display rhetoric and education
  • Advance plot through action (supplication, command, prophecy)
  • Create dramatic irony (we know what speaker doesn't)

What to Analyze

  • Who speaks? (status/authority matters)
  • To whom? (audience shapes rhetoric)
  • What persuasive techniques used?
  • Does it work? (success/failure)
  • What does it reveal about speaker?

Dido's First Speech: Hospitality (Book 1)

Not ignorant of suffering, I learn to help the unfortunate. My own story mirrors yours‚Äîtossed by fortune, I finally found rest in this land. Through many perils, fortune has taught me to bring aid to those in distress.
‚Äî Dido, Aeneid 1.630-631

Rhetorical Analysis

  • "Non ignara mali": Famous phrase‚Äîestablishes empathy through shared suffering
  • "miseris succurrere disco": "I learn to help"‚Äîactive, present tense, ongoing commitment
  • "My own story mirrors yours": Creates bond‚Äîwe're the same, fellow exiles
  • "tossed by fortune": Passive victim, like Aeneas‚Äîfate brought us both here
  • "finally found rest": Carthage as refuge‚Äîoffers same to Trojans

Why This Speech Succeeds

Dido doesn't command or threaten. She offers understanding through shared experience. This is perfect hospitality rhetoric‚Äîmakes the guest feel safe by emphasizing similarity, not power imbalance. Aeneas responds with gratitude and trust.

Dido's Fury: The Confrontation Speech (Book 4)

Traitor! Did you think you could hide such treachery and slip away from my land in silence? Does our love mean nothing? Nor the pledge we made? Nor Dido doomed to a cruel death? Even in winter you labor at your ships, hurrying to cross the sea under northern storms‚Äîcruel man! If you weren't seeking foreign fields and unknown homes, if ancient Troy still stood, would you sail for Troy through these wild waves? From me you flee? By these tears, by your right hand‚Äîsince I've left myself nothing else‚Äîby our union, by the marriage we began, if ever I deserved well of you, if anything of mine was sweet to you, pity my falling house and put aside this plan‚Äîif any prayers can still be heard.
‚Äî Dido, Aeneid 4.305-319 (adapted)

Emotional and Rhetorical Escalation

  • "Traitor!" (Dissimulare): Opens with accusation‚Äîyou're a deceiver, liar
  • "slip away in silence": Cowardly, dishonorable‚Äîappeals to shame
  • "Does our love mean nothing?": Series of questions‚Äîrhetorical, demanding answers he can't give
  • "Nor the pledge... Nor Dido doomed": Repetition (anaphora)‚Äîbuilding emotional pressure
  • "cruel man" (crudelis): Direct accusation of character flaw
  • "By these tears, by your right hand": Supplication formula‚Äîhumbles herself
  • "since I've left myself nothing else": Self-pity and guilt‚Äîyou've destroyed me
  • "if anything of mine was sweet": Conditional‚Äîappeals to any memory of love
  • "pity my falling house": Political appeal‚Äîyou're ruining my kingdom

Why This Speech FAILS

Dido uses every rhetorical tool‚Äîaccusation, questions, supplication, appeal to past, guilt, pity‚Äîbut it backfires. The speech shows furor (uncontrolled passion), not ratio. By moving through too many contradictory emotions (rage, then pleading, then accusation again), she undermines her own credibility. Aeneas can dismiss this as hysteria, not reasoned argument.

Dido's Final Speech: The Curse (Book 4)

I hope‚Äîif the gods have any power‚Äîyou'll drink down punishment on the rocks mid-sea, calling Dido's name again and again. Though absent, I'll hound you with dark fires, and when cold death has severed soul from body, my shade will stalk you everywhere. You'll pay, you traitor! And I'll hear of it‚Äîthe news will reach me deep among the dead.
‚Äî Dido, Aeneid 4.382-387 (adapted)

From Persuasion to Curse

  • "if the gods have any power": Conditional‚Äîquestions divine justice itself
  • "drink down punishment on the rocks": Wishes death by shipwreck‚Äîreverses hospitality (safe harbor ‚Üí dangerous rocks)
  • "calling Dido's name": Wants him to die thinking of her‚Äîobsessive
  • "Though absent, I'll hound you": Promises haunting‚Äîcan't let go even in death
  • "my shade will stalk you everywhere": Becomes vengeful ghost‚Äîtransformation from lover to fury
  • "the news will reach me deep among the dead": Even in underworld, she wants revenge satisfied

The Curse Comes True

  • Book 6: Aeneas DOES meet Dido's shade in the underworld‚Äîshe turns away, refusing to speak
  • Book 6: She returns to Sychaeus (her first husband), rejecting Aeneas forever
  • Historical: Carthage and Rome fought the Punic Wars‚ÄîDido's curse becomes historical enmity
  • The speech transforms from failed persuasion to successful prophecy

Aeneas's Response to Dido (Book 4)

I'll never deny what you deserve, what you can claim‚Äîall you care to say of what you've done for me, and I'll never regret remembering Dido, not while I remember myself, while breath still rules this body. But hear my side: I never hoped to hide my going (don't claim that), nor did I ever offer marriage vows or enter such a pact. If the Fates allowed me to live my own life, guide my own cares, I'd first rebuild Troy, restore our fallen community‚ÄîPriam's high halls would rise again. But now Italy‚Äîgreat Italy‚ÄîApollo's oracle and the Lycian lots command me to seize. There lies my love, there my homeland. If Carthage and these Libyan towers can hold a Phoenician woman, why begrudge the Trojans settling Italian soil? We too can seek a foreign kingdom. As often as night covers earth with dewy shadows, as often as the stars rise in flame, my father Anchises's troubled ghost warns me in dreams and fills me with fear. I wrong my dear son Ascanius, cheating him of his destined Italian kingdom. And now Jove's messenger himself‚ÄîI swear by both our lives‚Äîcame down through the swift air and brought commands. I saw the god myself in clear daylight entering our walls, and these ears drank in his words. Stop inflaming both of us with complaints. I don't seek Italy by choice.
‚Äî Aeneas, Aeneid 4.333-361 (adapted)

Rhetorical Defense Strategy

  • "I'll never deny what you deserve": Acknowledges her service‚Äîgives ground before defending
  • "I'll never regret remembering Dido": Diplomatic‚Äîhonors her without promising to stay
  • "But hear my side": Shifts to rebuttal‚Äî"BUT" is the pivot word
  • "I never hoped to hide my going": Denies deception‚Äîcorrects her accusation
  • "nor did I ever offer marriage vows": CRUCIAL claim‚Äîdenies the marriage she assumes
  • "If the Fates allowed me": Conditional contrary to fact‚ÄîI WOULD stay if I could, but Fate won't let me
  • "Apollo's oracle... Lycian lots command": Multiple divine sources‚Äînot my choice, gods' command
  • "Why begrudge the Trojans settling Italian soil?": Turns her logic against her‚Äîyou got Carthage, why not let us have Italy?
  • "my father's ghost warns me in dreams": Filial duty‚Äîancestors demand obedience
  • "I wrong my dear son": Paternal duty‚ÄîAscanius's future at stake
  • "Jove's messenger... I swear": Appeals to highest authority‚ÄîJupiter himself commanded this
  • "I don't seek Italy by choice": Final statement‚Äîremoves personal agency entirely

Does This Speech Work?

Logically, YES‚ÄîAeneas provides multiple valid reasons (divine command, duty to father/son/people, never promised marriage). Emotionally, NO‚Äîit sounds like legalistic excuse-making. He prioritizes ratio (reason) over pathos (emotion), which is virtuous by Roman standards but feels cold. The speech succeeds in justifying his departure but fails to comfort Dido.

Aeneas's Narrative: The Fall of Troy (Book 2)

Book 2 is entirely Aeneas's speech to Dido's court‚Äîa first-person flashback. This extended narrative reveals his character through storytelling choices.

What Aeneas's Narration Reveals

  • Opens with reluctance: "You ask me to recall unspeakable grief"‚Äîestablishes this is painful to tell
  • Emphasizes Trojan virtue: His people were brave, pious, trusting‚ÄîGreeks were cunning deceivers
  • Shows his errors: Admits he wanted to die fighting, not flee‚ÄîVenus had to redirect him
  • Highlights pietas: Carries father, leads son, saves household gods‚Äîproper priorities
  • Frames loss as tragic necessity: Creusa's death was fated, not his fault
  • Positions himself as survivor with mission: Not just escaping‚Äîobeying divine command

Why Dido Falls for This Speech

Aeneas tells a story that makes him sympathetic‚Äîsuffering hero, devoted son, pious leader, tragic widower. He's EVERYTHING a Roman hero should be. The speech isn't trying to seduce Dido, but it does: she hears noble suffering, sees a man worthy of her queenship.

Ironically, the qualities that make his Book 2 narrative attractive (devotion to duty, prioritizing mission over emotion) are the same qualities that will destroy their relationship in Book 4.

Jupiter's Prophecy to Venus (Book 1)

Cast aside your fear, Cytherea. Your people's fate stands unmoved‚Äîyou'll see Lavinium's walls, your promised city, and raise great-hearted Aeneas to the stars. I have not changed my mind. Your son will wage a great war in Italy, crush fierce nations, and establish city walls and laws for his people, until a third summer has seen him ruling in Latium, and three winters have passed. But young Ascanius‚Äînow called Iulus‚Äîwill transfer power to Alba Longa and rule for thirty great years. After three hundred years, a priestess of royal blood, pregnant by Mars, will bear twin sons. One, Romulus, raised by a wolf's tawny hide, will found Mars's city and give his name to Romans. For them I set no limits in space or time: I have granted empire without end.
‚Äî Jupiter, Aeneid 1.257-279 (adapted)

Prophecy as Rhetorical Authority

  • "Cast aside your fear": Command, not request‚ÄîJupiter's authority is absolute
  • "Your people's fate stands unmoved": Fate is fixed‚Äînothing can change it
  • "I have not changed my mind": Emphasizes consistency‚ÄîJupiter's will is eternal
  • Specific details (three summers, thirty years, three hundred years): Precision creates credibility‚Äîthis isn't vague prophecy but detailed foreknowledge
  • "Romulus... Mars's city... Romans": Etymology‚Äîexplains Rome's name through divine plan
  • "imperium sine fine": The KEY phrase‚Äî"empire without end," Rome's eternal destiny

Why This Speech Matters

  • Establishes the ENTIRE epic's framework‚Äîeverything leads to Rome
  • Makes Aeneas's suffering meaningful‚Äîpersonal pain serves cosmic purpose
  • Justifies imperialism‚ÄîRome's empire is divinely ordained, not just conquest
  • Creates dramatic irony‚Äîreaders know Rome's greatness; characters don't

Mercury's Command (Book 4)

Is THIS what you're doing? Building Carthage's towers, laying foundations for someone else's city? Forgetting your own kingdom and destiny? The ruler of gods himself, who turns heaven and earth with his power, sends me down to you with this message through the swift winds: What are you planning? What do you hope for, wasting time in Libya? If the glory of such great deeds doesn't move you, if you won't labor for your own honor, at least think of growing Ascanius, the hopes of Iulus your heir‚Äîowed the kingdom of Italy and Roman land.
‚Äî Mercury, Aeneid 4.265-276 (adapted)

Divine Rhetoric: Shame and Command

  • "Is THIS what you're doing?": Scornful question‚Äîimplies he should be ashamed
  • "Building someone else's city": Emasculating‚Äîhelping a woman build HER kingdom, not your own
  • "Forgetting your own kingdom": Accuses negligence of duty
  • "The ruler of gods himself": Appeals to ultimate authority‚Äînot my command, Jupiter's
  • "What are you planning?": Implies he has no plan‚Äîjust drifting
  • "wasting time": Direct accusation‚Äîyou're failing your mission
  • "If glory doesn't move you": Questions his manhood‚Äîaren't you ambitious?
  • "at least think of... Ascanius": Final appeal‚Äîif not for yourself, for your son

Why This Works Immediately

Mercury doesn't ask or persuade‚Äîhe COMMANDS and SHAMES. The speech strips away Aeneas's excuses and forces him to confront that he's violated his duty. Unlike Dido's emotional appeals, Mercury uses shame (failing manhood), duty (son's inheritance), and divine authority (Jupiter's command). Aeneas can't argue with this‚Äîhe immediately begins preparations to leave.

Rhetorical Techniques in the Aeneid

Virgil was trained in rhetoric‚Äîthe art of persuasion. His characters use formal techniques that Romans would recognize and judge. Understanding these patterns helps you analyze WHY speeches succeed or fail.

ETHOS
Appeal to Character/Authority
Speaker establishes credibility. Jupiter says "I'm king of gods," Dido says "I know suffering too," Aeneas says "I'm pious." Success depends on audience accepting the authority claimed.
PATHOS
Appeal to Emotion
Dido's tears, descriptions of suffering, appeals to pity or love. Effective for sympathy but can backfire as "furor" if excessive. Romans valued ratio over pathos.
LOGOS
Appeal to Logic/Reason
Aeneas's logical defense (gods commanded, duties require, never promised marriage). Builds argument step-by-step. Most Roman form of persuasion‚Äîratio controlling emotion.
SUPPLICATION
Formal Ritual Appeal
Thetis touching Jupiter's chin, Dido using supplication formula ("by these tears, by your right hand"). Creates obligation‚Äîrefusing supplication is impious. Works unless speaker has no status.

Common Rhetorical Devices

Sound & Structure

  • Anaphora: Repetition at start of phrases ("Nor the pledge... Nor Dido doomed")
  • Tricolon: Three-part structure ("By tears, by hand, by marriage")
  • Rhetorical questions: Don't expect answer, make point ("Is THIS what you're doing?")
  • Apostrophe: Direct address ("Traitor! Cruel man!")

Logical Structure

  • Conditional arguments: "If X, then Y" logic
  • Appeal to precedent: "You did X, so I can do Y"
  • Comparison: "If you can have Carthage, I can have Italy"
  • Appeal to higher authority: "The gods command it"

Exam Strategy: Analyzing Speeches

The Speech Analysis Framework

1. CONTEXT: Who speaks to whom? What's at stake?
2. PURPOSE: What does speaker want? (persuade, command, explain, curse)
3. TECHNIQUE: What rhetorical devices used? (ethos/pathos/logos, repetition, questions)
4. EFFECT: Does it work? Why or why not?
5. CHARACTER: What does speech reveal about speaker?

Success vs Failure
Successful speeches show pietas (duty) and ratio (reason). Failed speeches show furor (excessive emotion) or lack of authority. When analyzing, note: Dido's early speech (calm, hospitable) succeeds. Her later speeches (emotional, accusatory) fail. Aeneas's logical defense succeeds in justifying his actions but fails in comforting Dido. Jupiter's prophecy succeeds absolutely‚Äîno one can argue with fate.
