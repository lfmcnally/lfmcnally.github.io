Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and analyse specific literary techniques used in Books 1 and 2, with examples suitable for exam discussion.

Techniques in Context

Books 1-2 showcase Virgil's full range: epic similes, ekphrasis (the temple murals), first-person narrative (Aeneas's flashback), prophecy, apostrophe, and vivid description. These techniques serve thematic and emotional purposes.

Key Similes in Books 1-2

The Political Simile (1.148-156)

Neptune calming the storm is compared to a respected statesman calming a riot. This reverses the typical direction (natural ‚Üí human) and introduces Roman political order as a cosmic principle. It's the first appearance of Augustus's ideal of order over chaos.

Dido as Diana (1.498-504)

Dido entering Carthage is compared to Diana leading her nymphs on Mount Cynthus. This elevates Dido to semi-divine status, emphasising her beauty and authority. The comparison to a virgin goddess is ironic given what will happen.

The Serpents (2.203-227)

The serpents killing Laocoon are described with vivid horror: their crests raised, eyes burning. The detailed physical description makes the scene cinematically real‚Äîa technique Virgil uses for moments of violence throughout.

Aeneas as a Shepherd (2.304-308)

Aeneas waking to discover Troy burning is compared to a shepherd realising his fields are ablaze. The pastoral simile humanises the epic moment and emphasises Aeneas's helplessness‚Äîhe is a caretaker whose charge is being destroyed.

Ekphrasis: The Temple Murals

The temple of Juno in Carthage features murals depicting the Trojan War. This ekphrasis (extended description of art) serves multiple functions.

What Aeneas Sees

The murals depict: the battles around Troy, Priam, Achilles, Hector's body dragged, the Trojan women supplicating, Memnon and Penthesilea, and Aeneas himself fighting the Greeks.

Functions of the Ekphrasis

Emotional: Aeneas weeps, showing his unhealed grief. "Sunt lacrimae rerum" expresses the universal sorrow of the human condition.

Thematic: Art preserves memory and creates sympathy across cultures‚Äîthe Carthaginians (future enemies) mourn Trojan suffering.

Proleptic: The murals look back, but the Shield of Aeneas (Book 8) will look forward. Together they frame the epic's engagement with history.

Sunt lacrimae rerum et mentem mortalia tangunt.

There are tears for things and mortal matters touch the mind.

Narrative Techniques

First-Person Flashback

Book 2 is narrated by Aeneas himself, giving subjective, emotional access to Troy's fall. This differs from Virgil's third-person narrator: Aeneas says "I saw," "I felt," "I wept." The intimacy heightens pathos.

In Medias Res

The epic begins seven years into Aeneas's journey. The flashback to Troy (chronologically first) comes in Book 2. This creates dramatic structure: we meet the suffering hero before learning what broke him.

Aposiopesis (Breaking Off)

Neptune's "Quos ego‚Äî!" (Whom I‚Äî!) breaks off his threat against the winds. This rhetorical figure conveys anger beyond words and became famous as an example of the technique.

Apostrophe

The narrator addresses Dido directly: "infelix Dido" (unhappy Dido). This breaks narrative distance to guide reader sympathy, signalling that Virgil mourns what will happen even as he narrates it.
