2.1 Similes and Imagery in the Aeneid

üìö A-Level Classical Civilisation ‚è±Ô∏è 55 min üìñ Virgil's Aeneid

Why Similes Matter in Epic Poetry

Virgil's similes aren't decoration‚Äîthey're MEANING. Every extended comparison does multiple jobs: creates vivid visual imagery, guides your emotional response, connects human action to the natural world, and reveals character psychology. The Aeneid's similes are more sophisticated than Homer's, often inverting traditional patterns or layering multiple meanings.

What Makes a Simile "Epic"?
Epic similes are EXTENDED comparisons‚Äînot just "he fought like a lion" but detailed, multi-line descriptions that develop the comparison. Virgil inherited this technique from Homer but refined it: his similes are shorter, more concentrated, and often surprising in their comparisons.

The basic pattern: Virgil compares characters, emotions, or actions to natural phenomena‚Äîstorms, animals, fire, rivers, celestial bodies. But he constantly subverts expectations. A queen is compared to Diana (goddess imagery), a statesman calming riot appears in a description of Neptune calming the sea (reversing natural‚Üíhuman to human‚Üínatural), and Dido's love-wound is compared to a deer shot with an arrow she doesn't even know is there.

What Virgil's Similes Achieve

  • Emotional amplification: Similes slow down narrative time, forcing readers to FEEL moments intensely
  • Universal resonance: Comparing epic events to everyday phenomena (shepherds, bees, rivers) makes grand actions relatable
  • Thematic reinforcement: Repeated imagery patterns (fire, storms, wounds) create thematic threads through the epic
  • Character insight: HOW someone is compared reveals their inner state‚ÄîAeneas as a storm-tossed oak shows both strength and vulnerability

Imagery vs Simile: What's the Difference?

Similes (Explicit Comparison)

  • Uses "like" or "as" to compare
  • "Dido ranged through Carthage LIKE a wounded deer"
  • Extended, multi-line descriptions
  • Creates pause in narrative
  • Reader consciously notices comparison

Imagery (Descriptive Language)

  • Vivid sensory description without explicit comparison
  • "Troy blazed" (fire imagery embedded in narrative)
  • Woven seamlessly into storytelling
  • Creates atmosphere and mood
  • Works subconsciously on reader

Why Both Matter for Exams

The OCR specification requires analysis of "literary, stylistic and linguistic features." Virgil uses BOTH similes and imagery to create meaning. When analyzing a passage, identify whether he's using explicit comparison (simile) or embedded description (imagery), and explain the EFFECT of each choice.

The Political Simile: Neptune as Statesman (Book 1)

This is the FIRST major simile in the Aeneid, and it's revolutionary. After Juno unleashes a storm, Neptune calms the sea‚Äîand Virgil compares this to a statesman calming a riot.

And just as, often, when a crowd of people is rocked by a rebellion, and the common mob goes mad with rage, and firebrands and rocks start flying (hysteria finds its weapons), if they then catch sight of a man remarkable for leadership and service to the state, they fall silent and stand close with attentive ears. He controls their passion with his words and calms their hearts. Just so, all the crashing of the sea fell silent.
‚Äî Aeneid 1.148-154 (Fagles translation)

Why This Simile Is Groundbreaking

  • Reverses the pattern: Normally epic similes compare human‚Üínatural ("he fought like a lion"). Virgil compares natural‚Üíhuman (sea like a riot)
  • Elevates Roman politics: Political order becomes a COSMIC principle, as fundamental as Neptune's control of the sea
  • Introduces Augustus: The "man remarkable for leadership" subtly evokes Augustus bringing order after civil war
  • Sets the epic's theme: Control of furor (madness) through pietas and ratio (reason) defines Romanness

Breaking Down the Technique

  • "often, when a crowd... is rocked by a rebellion": Makes civil disorder sound commonplace‚ÄîRome's recent history
  • "common mob goes mad with rage": "Furor" imagery‚Äîirrational passion that must be controlled
  • "firebrands and rocks start flying": Vivid, specific detail makes the comparison real
  • "hysteria finds its weapons": Personification‚Äîrage itself becomes an active force
  • "a man remarkable for leadership and service": Pietas identified as the quality that gives authority
  • "they fall silent... with attentive ears": Willing obedience, not forced submission
  • "He controls their passion with his words": Ratio (reason) overcomes furor through speech, not violence

Connections Across the Aeneid

  • Book 1: Jupiter's prophecy describes Augustus closing the gates of War, binding Furor in chains (1.291-296)
  • Book 6: Anchises tells Aeneas that Rome's mission is "to impose the habit of peace, to spare the conquered and war down the proud" (6.851-853)
  • Book 12: Jupiter finally restrains Juno, ending her furor and allowing fate to complete itself
  • The pattern: Pietas and political order control destructive passion

Dido as Diana: The Virgin Goddess (Book 1)

Like Diana training her dancers along the banks of the Eurotas or over the heights of Cynthus, with a thousand mountain-nymphs massed in her wake‚Äîshe shoulders her quiver, and as she goes she outshines all other goddesses and Latona's heart thrills with silent joy‚Äîso Dido moved among her people, radiant, urging on the work of her kingdom's rising glory.
‚Äî Aeneid 1.498-504 (Fagles)

What This Comparison Does

  • Elevates Dido to divine status: First impression is of near-goddess beauty and authority
  • Emphasizes her virginity: Diana is the virgin huntress‚ÄîDido has vowed fidelity to dead Sychaeus
  • Shows her active leadership: Like Diana "training her dancers," Dido actively directs her city's construction
  • Creates tragic irony: Comparing her to a virgin goddess makes her coming violation (by love) even more painful
  • Parental pride imagery: "Latona's heart thrills with joy"‚ÄîDido's people love her like a parent loves a child

The Tragic Dimension

Readers familiar with the myth KNOW Dido's story ends in suicide. Comparing her to Diana‚Äîgoddess who punished those who violated her virginity (remember Actaeon?)‚Äîmakes the coming tragedy even more powerful. Virgil shows us Dido at her peak so we can measure how far she falls.

Dido as Wounded Deer: Love as Injury (Book 4)

And now the queen, stricken by a deadly wound, tends the fire in her veins, coursing through her flesh. Unlucky Dido, burning, wandering through the city like a doe struck by an arrow, hit from a distance when a shepherd hunting in the Cretan woods had ambushed her and left his flying steel in her flesh, all unknowing. She flees through the Cretan glades and groves, but the shaft of death clings to her side.
‚Äî Aeneid 4.68-73 (adapted)

Unpacking the Deer Simile

  • "stricken by a deadly wound": Love literally described as vulnus (wound)‚ÄîCupid's arrow is REAL injury
  • "tends the fire in her veins": Fire and wound imagery combined‚Äîlove as both burning and bleeding
  • "like a doe struck by an arrow": Innocent, vulnerable animal‚ÄîDido is prey, not hunter
  • "hit from a distance... all unknowing": She doesn't know Cupid (disguised as Ascanius) caused this‚Äîvictim of divine plot
  • "the shaft of death clings to her side": The wound is FATAL‚Äîthis love will kill her
  • "flees through glades and groves": Constant motion, seeking relief, finding none

Why This Simile Is Psychologically Brilliant

The deer doesn't know WHY it's wounded‚Äîit just runs in pain. Similarly, Dido doesn't understand that Venus and Cupid caused her love. She thinks it's natural, voluntary emotion. The simile reveals what she doesn't know: she's a victim of divine manipulation, and the wound is already fatal.

The hunter is "all unknowing" too‚Äîhe doesn't know he killed the deer. Aeneas doesn't intend to destroy Dido; he's focused on his mission. But intention doesn't matter‚Äîthe wound is mortal regardless.

Connecting to Diana Simile

  • Book 1: Dido compared to Diana, virgin huntress‚Äîshe's the hunter, powerful and independent
  • Book 4: Dido compared to wounded deer‚Äîshe's the prey, vulnerable and dying
  • The transformation is complete: from goddess-like queen to hunted animal
  • Diana's own mythology: she turned Actaeon into a deer for seeing her naked. Dido becomes the deer when love "violates" her vow to Sychaeus

Storm and Sea Imagery Throughout the Aeneid

Virgil uses storms as his primary metaphor for FUROR‚Äîungoverned passion that threatens order. Real storms (Book 1, Book 5) and storm similes appear whenever characters or situations spiral out of control.

BOOK 1
Juno's Storm
Juno unleashes winds to scatter the Trojan fleet. Represents divine furor opposing fate. Neptune restores order with political simile‚Äîreason controlling chaos.
BOOK 2
Aeneas as Oak Tree
Aeneas withstanding attack compared to oak battered by winds but with deep roots. Shows his pietas‚Äîfirmly rooted in duty despite emotional storms.
BOOK 4
Dido's Emotional Tempest
Her rage and grief described with storm imagery. She becomes the storm‚Äîuncontrolled furor destroying everything around her.
BOOK 7
War as Storm
Allecto (Fury sent by Juno) whips up Italian opposition. War described as storm‚Äîanother manifestation of furor disrupting peace.

Aeneas as Storm-Tossed Oak (Book 4)

But Aeneas, though he longed to ease her sorrow and soften her grief with words of comfort, though he groaned deeply, shaken in heart by the force of his love, yet he carried out the gods' commands and returned to his ships. Just as when northern winds from the Alps compete to uproot an oak tree grown mighty through the years, blasting it now from this side, now from that‚Äîthe trunk creaks, and leaves scatter the ground though the tree grips the crag‚Äîas deep as its crown soars to heaven, so deep its roots drive down to hell. Just so the hero, battered this way and that by appeals, feels the pain deep in his great heart, but his mind stays firm; the tears fall uselessly.
‚Äî Aeneid 4.437-449 (adapted)

Breaking Down the Oak Simile

  • "though he longed... though he groaned": Virgil emphasizes Aeneas FEELS the pain‚Äîhe's not heartless
  • "shaken in heart by the force of his love": Emotional storm INSIDE him mirrors the tree being battered
  • "yet he carried out the gods' commands": "Yet"‚Äîpietas overcomes personal desire
  • "oak tree grown mighty through the years": Suggests Aeneas's maturity, strength, longevity of commitment
  • "blasting it now from this side, now from that": Dido's appeals from all directions‚Äîemotional, logical, moral
  • "the trunk creaks": He's not immune‚Äîthe strain is real, audible
  • "leaves scatter": Minor damage occurs (tears fall), but structure holds
  • "grips the crag": Rooted in duty/fate‚Äîunmovable foundation
  • "as deep as its crown soars... so deep its roots drive down": His greatness (reaching heaven) requires equally deep commitment (roots to underworld/duty)
  • "his mind stays firm; the tears fall uselessly": Emotions and duty separated‚ÄîRoman ideal of ratio controlling pathos

Is Aeneas Heartless or Heroic?

This simile is Virgil's answer to critics who call Aeneas cold. The tree DOES get battered‚Äîleaves scatter, trunk creaks. Aeneas weeps and groans. But the oak doesn't uproot itself just because the wind hurts. His suffering is real; his duty is stronger. Whether you admire or despise this depends on your values‚Äîwhich is exactly Virgil's point.

The Rocks in the Storm (Book 1)

When describing the storm, Virgil uses vivid imagery of waves smashing ships against rocks:

Three ships the East Wind spins into hidden rocks‚Äîrocks the Italians call "The Altars," a massive spine lurking under the surge‚Äîthree the South Wind rams from the open sea onto shoals and sandbars, wedging them in, a painful sight, walling them round with a mountain of sand.
‚Äî Aeneid 1.108-112 (Fagles)

The Violence of Nature

  • "spins into hidden rocks": Helplessness‚Äîships have no control over their direction
  • "The Altars": Ominous name‚Äîsuggests sacrifice, death on sacred ground
  • "massive spine lurking under the surge": Monster imagery‚Äînature as predator
  • "rams... wedging them in": Violent, aggressive verbs‚Äînature attacks
  • "a painful sight": Narrator's empathy‚Äîwe're positioned to feel for the victims
  • "walling them round with a mountain of sand": Burial imagery‚Äîtrapped, suffocating, dying

Why Storms Matter Symbolically

Every storm in the Aeneid represents the same thing: FUROR (irrational passion) disrupting FATUM (ordered destiny). Juno causes storms because she embodies furor. Neptune/Jupiter end storms because they represent cosmic order.

When you see storm imagery, ask: What passion is out of control here? Whose furor is being expressed? How is order (pietas, fate, Roman values) being threatened?

Animal Similes in Battle Scenes

Virgil inherited battle similes from Homer‚Äîcomparing warriors to lions, wolves, boars. But he uses them more sparingly and often with subversive twists that question the glory of warfare.

BOOK 9
Nisus & Euryalus as Lions
"Like ravening lions driven mad by gnawing hunger" slaughtering sleeping Rutulians. Traditional heroic imagery BUT the victims are asleep‚Äîundermines glory.
BOOK 10
Turnus as Lion
"Like a lion spotting a bull" stalking Pallas. Emphasizes Turnus's predatory power but also foreshadows his death‚ÄîAeneas will hunt HIM.
BOOK 12
Aeneas as Wolf
"Like a wolf that's harried a sheepfold" pursuing Turnus. Predator imagery shows Aeneas's rage‚Äîhe's become the killer, not the dutiful hero.
BOOK 2
Greeks as Wolves
"Like wolves in dark fog, driven blind with hunger" sacking Troy. Greeks are predators, Trojans are prey‚Äîshifts sympathy.

Pyrrhus as Serpent (Book 2)

And Pyrrhus bursts in, reveling in slaughter, and brings his Myrmidons through the threshold. Like a snake that's fed on poisonous plants, swollen huge, emerging into the light after a cold winter underground, sloughing its skin, made new, glistening‚Äîits triple tongue flickering, back coiled and rearing its chest to the sun. Just so Pyrrhus in full armor towers before the gates.
‚Äî Aeneid 2.469-475 (adapted)

Why Serpent Imagery?

  • "fed on poisonous plants": Pyrrhus is toxic, dangerous‚Äîhis very nature is poison
  • "swollen huge": Bloated with violence‚Äîgrotesque, not heroic
  • "emerging into the light after... winter underground": Coming from darkness/death realm‚Äîunderworld associations
  • "sloughing its skin, made new": Renewal through shedding‚Äîbut this is ominous, not positive
  • "triple tongue flickering": Forked tongue = deception; triple = excessive, monstrous
  • "back coiled and rearing": Poised to strike‚Äîpredator about to attack

Connecting to Laocoon's Serpents

  • Earlier in Book 2: Twin serpents kill Laocoon and his sons‚Äîsent by gods to ensure Troy's fall
  • Now: Pyrrhus (son of Achilles) compared to serpent attacking Priam and his family
  • Both serpents are divine instruments of Troy's destruction
  • Pattern: Greeks = serpents (cunning, poisonous, sent by hostile gods)
  • Snakes aren't just dangerous‚Äîthey're associated with treachery and divine punishment

Virgil's Critique of Achilles

Pyrrhus is Achilles' son, acting like his father (violent, honorless killing of aged king). By comparing him to a poisonous snake‚Äînot a noble lion‚ÄîVirgil questions Homeric heroism. Is this aristeia (glorious warrior excellence) or just murder? The serpent simile guides us to see it as monstrous.

Fire Imagery in Warfare

Fire appears constantly in battle scenes‚Äîboth literal (Troy burning) and metaphorical (warriors "blazing" with rage). Virgil uses fire to show war's consuming, destructive nature.

Turnus blazes with rage and hurls himself into the fight. Like fire roaring through a wheat field or a torrent boiling down a mountain, smashing everything in its path‚Äîthe farmer watches in horror from a distance as his year's work is destroyed.
‚Äî Aeneid 12.521-525 (adapted)

Fire as Destruction

  • "blazes with rage": Emotion literally described as fire‚Äîfuror as flame
  • "fire roaring through a wheat field": Consumes productive agriculture‚Äîwar destroys civilization
  • "torrent boiling down a mountain": Unstoppable natural force‚Äîno human can control it
  • "smashing everything in its path": Indiscriminate destruction‚Äîfire doesn't choose targets
  • "farmer watches in horror": Innocent victim‚Äîperspective shifts to casualty, not warrior
  • "his year's work is destroyed": One moment undoes months of labor‚Äîfutility of civilian life in wartime

Fire = Furor = War

Virgil consistently links fire imagery to furor (destructive passion). War is fire: it consumes everything, spreads uncontrollably, destroys what took years to build in moments. The farmer watching his fields burn = civilians watching their cities destroyed = readers watching the cost of empire.

Bees and Social Order (Book 1)

When Aeneas first sees Carthage being built, Virgil compares the workers to bees‚Äîone of the most famous similes in the epic.

Aeneas gazes in wonder at the vast construction work, once just shepherds' huts. He marvels at the gates, the uproar, the paved roads. The Tyrians press on eagerly‚Äîsome build walls, haul stones and wheel up boulders, others choose sites for houses, plow out their boundaries. They elect laws and magistrates and a sacred senate. Here some dredge harbors, there others lay foundations deep for theaters, hewing enormous columns from the rock, high fitting adornments for stages still to come. Like bees in early summer through flowering fields, busy in sunlight, leading out the young of their race now ripe, or packing cells with honey, sweet clear nectar, swelling the combs, or gathering loads from workers coming in, or closing ranks, driving the drones, that lazy crew, from the hive. The work glows, and the sweet smell of honey drifts through the air.
‚Äî Aeneid 1.421-436 (adapted)

What the Bee Simile Reveals

  • "press on eagerly": Voluntary, enthusiastic labor‚Äînot slavery but civic pride
  • "some... others... here... there": Division of labor‚Äîorganized, efficient society
  • "elect laws and magistrates": Political organization‚Äîcivilization emerging from chaos
  • "bees in early summer... busy in sunlight": Natural, productive activity‚Äîparallels human city-building
  • "leading out the young now ripe": Intergenerational‚Äîbuilding for the future
  • "packing cells with honey": Storing resources‚Äîplanning, providence
  • "driving the drones... from the hive": Eliminating non-contributors‚Äîharsh but necessary for collective survival
  • "The work glows": Labor itself becomes beautiful‚ÄîRoman value of industry
  • "sweet smell of honey drifts": Sensory reward‚Äîcivilization produces sweetness from toil

Why Bees Matter to Romans

Romans LOVED bee imagery. Virgil wrote a whole book of the Georgics about beekeeping, treating it as a metaphor for ideal society. Bees represent: (1) Selfless service to the collective, (2) Natural hierarchy (queen/workers/drones), (3) Productive labor creating sweetness from toil, (4) Orderly cooperation producing beauty.

When Aeneas sees Carthage-as-beehive, he sees what he WANTS to build in Italy‚Äîorganized, productive, beautiful civilization. The tragedy is that he must destroy Dido's hive to build his own.

Trees and Roots: Stability vs Uprooting

Virgil repeatedly uses tree imagery‚Äîespecially the contrast between rooted stability and violent uprooting‚Äîto explore themes of home, exile, and resistance.

Rooted = Stable/Dutiful

  • Aeneas as oak with deep roots (Book 4)‚Äîpietas keeps him grounded
  • The Golden Bough rooted in underworld (Book 6)‚Äîdifficult to pluck, requires fate's permission
  • Latinus's kingdom like ancient tree (Book 7)‚Äîold, established, legitimate
  • Italy described with rich forests‚Äîfertile, ready for civilization

Uprooted = Chaos/Destruction

  • Trojans torn from homeland‚Äîroots severed by Greeks
  • Storm trying to uproot oak = emotional appeals trying to break Aeneas's resolve
  • Forests cut down for funeral pyres (Books 6, 11)‚Äîdeath uproots even nature
  • War described as fire through forest‚Äîconsuming what grew for generations

Exile as Perpetual Uprooting

The Trojans are "uprooted" people searching for where to "plant" themselves. The entire epic is about finding the right soil (Italy) where their roots can grow deep again. Aeneas's pietas is the taproot‚Äîit keeps him stable even when storms batter him. When he finally reaches Italy, he can put down roots and grow Rome.

Rivers and Floods: Unstoppable Forces

Like a river in winter flood, swollen with rain from the mountains, hurling down, smashing dams, bursting embankments, sweeping forests and bridges in its path‚Äîthe shepherd on his hilltop hears the roar and stands helpless. So Aeneas drove through the Rutulian lines.
‚Äî Aeneid 2.304-308 (adapted)

Rivers as Metaphor for Unstoppable Events

  • Winter floods: Natural, seasonal‚Äîinevitable, not malicious
  • "swollen with rain from the mountains": Accumulated force‚Äîmany causes creating one overwhelming effect
  • "smashing dams, bursting embankments": Human efforts to control fail‚Äîfate overwhelms human will
  • "sweeping forests and bridges": Destroys both nature and civilization‚Äîindiscriminate
  • "shepherd... hears the roar... stands helpless": Can only witness, not prevent‚Äîfate's inevitability

River Imagery Patterns

  • Book 1: Storm-swollen seas = Juno's furor disrupting order
  • Book 2: Flood of Greeks into Troy = unstoppable conquest
  • Book 4: Dido's tears "flow like rivers" = uncontrolled grief
  • Book 6: Rivers in the underworld (Lethe, Styx, Acheron) = death as ultimate unstoppable flow
  • Book 10: Aeneas bursting through battle lines = hero as elemental force

Light and Darkness: Knowledge, Hope, and Despair

Virgil uses light/dark imagery to represent knowledge/ignorance, hope/despair, divine favor/abandonment, and life/death. This isn't just atmosphere‚Äîit's meaning.

BOOK 1
Storm as Darkness
"Darkness snatches sky and day from the Trojans' eyes." Storm literally removes light‚Äîconfusion, terror, loss of orientation. Neptune restores light when he restores order.
BOOK 2
Troy Burns at Night
Troy falls at night but is illuminated by its own burning. Perverted light‚Äîshould provide safety but reveals destruction. Fire provides visibility but destroys what it illuminates.
BOOK 4
Dido's Sleepless Nights
"All others were at rest, but the unhappy queen..." Night should bring sleep/peace; instead brings torment. Darkness = her psychological state‚Äîno rest, no light, no hope.
BOOK 6
Underworld Darkness
"Obscure they went through darkness." Death realm has no sun‚Äîonly occasional torchlight. Virgil makes the ultimate darkness (death) navigable but oppressive.

Apollo's Coming Like Dawn (Book 4)

But all-seeing Sun had lifted up his lamp, bringing daylight to the earth, when Aurora, leaving Tithonus's saffron bed, began to scatter fresh light over the world. The queen saw from her tower the whitening dawn, the fleet sailing off under set canvas, knew the harbor and beaches were empty of rowers. Three and four times she beat her lovely breast, tore her golden hair, and cried: "Jupiter! Will this stranger sail away mocking me? ... Is this my reward for welcoming him, for sharing my throne? Fool! Fool!"
‚Äî Aeneid 4.584-595 (adapted)

Dawn as Revelation and Betrayal

  • "all-seeing Sun... bringing daylight": Light reveals truth‚Äîcan't hide in darkness anymore
  • "Aurora... scatter fresh light over the world": Dawn goddess imagery‚Äîshould be hopeful (new day) but brings horror
  • "saffron bed": Beautiful, luxurious imagery contrasts with Dido's anguish
  • "whitening dawn": Color draining‚Äîdeath imagery embedded in sunrise
  • "saw... knew... empty": Vision brings knowledge brings pain
  • "Three and four times": Epic repetition formula‚Äîexcessive grief

Dawn as Cruel Witness

Dawn SHOULD symbolize hope, renewal, fresh start. But for Dido, it reveals abandonment. The "all-seeing Sun" becomes an all-seeing witness to her humiliation. Light‚Äînormally positive‚Äîhere illuminates her worst moment. Virgil subverts the expected symbolism to create tragedy.

Fire and Light: The Dual Nature

Fire can be light (illuminating, warming, civilizing) OR destruction (burning, consuming, chaotic). Virgil plays with this duality throughout.

Positive Fire/Light

  • Ascanius's head blazing harmlessly (Book 2)‚Äîdivine favor
  • Household fires/Penates‚Äîcivilization, family, continuity
  • Torches at ceremonies‚Äîsacred, controlled fire
  • Stars guiding ships‚Äîcelestial light as divine guidance
  • Venus appearing in brightness‚Äîdivine epiphany

Destructive Fire

  • Troy burning‚Äîcivilization destroyed
  • Dido's pyre‚Äîlove turned to death
  • Warriors "blazing with rage"‚Äîfuror as flame
  • Fire through wheat fields‚Äîwar consuming peace
  • Funeral pyres‚Äîdeath consuming life

Context Determines Meaning

Fire isn't inherently good or bad‚Äîit depends on control. CONTROLLED fire (hearth, altar, torch) = civilization, pietas, order. UNCONTROLLED fire (Troy burning, warriors raging, Dido's pyre) = furor, chaos, destruction.

The same is true of light: Controlled, revealing light = knowledge and hope. Harsh, exposing light = cruelty and despair (like dawn revealing Aeneas's departure).

Exam Strategy: Analyzing Similes and Imagery

The Five-Step Analysis Method

1. IDENTIFY: Quote the simile/imagery precisely
2. EXPLAIN: What is being compared to what? Or what sensory description is used?
3. ANALYZE: Break down the details‚Äîwhat specific words/images matter?
4. CONNECT: How does this relate to themes (pietas, furor, fate)?
5. EVALUATE: What is the EFFECT? How does it guide reader response?

Common Exam Mistake
Students identify similes but don't ANALYZE them. Don't just say "Virgil compares Dido to a wounded deer." Explain WHY: shows her as innocent victim, emphasizes she doesn't know Cupid caused her love, reveals the wound is fatal, transforms her from hunter (Diana simile) to prey. That's the analysis that gets high marks.

Cross-Reference Patterns

  • Track repeated imagery: storms, fire, wounds, animals, trees, light/dark
  • Notice when Virgil REVERSES expected patterns (political simile, dawn as cruel)
  • Compare how different characters are described with similar imagery (both Dido and Aeneas compared to deer at different points)
  • Connect similes to themes: furor = storms/fire; pietas = roots/steadfastness; fate = rivers/floods
