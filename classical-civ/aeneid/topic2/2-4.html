Aeneas: The New Kind of Hero

Introduction in Book 1

Aeneas first appears during the storm, despairing and wishing he had died at Troy. This is startlingly unheroic by Homeric standards‚ÄîOdysseus never surrenders to despair. Yet Virgil shows us Aeneas's humanity before his heroism.

Immediately after, Aeneas conceals his fear to encourage his men: "perhaps one day it will please us to remember even these things." This split between inner turmoil and outward leadership defines him.

Pius Aeneas

The epithet pius appears repeatedly. In Book 2, Aeneas demonstrates pietas by saving his father, son, and household gods. He is the ideal Roman paterfamilias‚Äîresponsible for family across generations.

A Hero of Suffering

Unlike Achilles (defined by rage) or Odysseus (defined by cunning), Aeneas is defined by endurance. He suffers, grieves, and continues. The temple murals move him to tears; his losses (Creusa, Troy) are not forgotten but carried forward.

Critical View
Some critics find Aeneas "dull" compared to Homeric heroes. Others argue this misses the point: Virgil creates a hero suited to Roman values of duty over individual glory. His restraint is precisely his heroism.

Dido: Queen and Future Tragedy

Introduction

Before we meet Dido, Venus tells her story: she fled Tyre after her husband Sychaeus was murdered by her brother Pygmalion. She now leads exiles building Carthage. Like Aeneas, she is an exile and a founder.

First Appearance

Dido enters "most beautiful" (forma pulcherrima), compared to Diana among her nymphs. She sits in judgment at Juno's temple, dispensing laws and organizing her people. She is competent, authoritative, admirable.

Hospitality and Connection

Dido welcomes the Trojans with understanding: "Not ignorant of suffering, I learn to help the unfortunate" (non ignara mali miseris succurrere disco). Her empathy with Aeneas's loss creates their bond‚Äîand sets up her tragedy.

Foreshadowing

Cupid, substituted for Ascanius, kindles love while Dido holds him. She is described as infelix (unhappy/doomed) even before her love develops. Virgil's narrator already mourns what will come.

Juno: The Divine Antagonist

Juno's Anger

Juno is established immediately as the Trojans' implacable enemy. Her anger has multiple causes: Paris's judgment against her, Jupiter's love for the Trojan Ganymede, the prophecy that Rome will destroy Carthage. Personal grudge and cosmic stakes combine.

Juno as Furor

Juno embodies furor‚Äîirrational, destructive passion opposed to the order represented by Jupiter and fate. Her storm disrupts cosmic order; Neptune must restore it. Yet Juno is also sympathetic: she fights for her favourites (Carthage, Turnus) just as Venus fights for hers.

Relationship with Fate

Juno cannot change fate, only delay it. She knows this: "flectere si nequeo superos, Acheronta movebo" ("If I cannot bend the heavens, I shall move the Underworld," 7.312). Her resistance is tragic‚Äîpassionate but doomed.

Juno's Function
Juno provides the epic with its primary conflict: if fate simply unfolded, there would be no drama. Her opposition creates obstacles that test Aeneas and delays that stretch the narrative. She is necessary for the plot to exist.
