4.3 Role of Fate and Destiny

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Book 6 presents fate and destiny, recognise the philosophical dimensions of Anchises's teaching, and analyse implications for the epic's meaning.

Fate as Theme

Book 6 is the epic's most concentrated meditation on fate. The Parade of Heroes presents Roman history as predestined; Anchises's philosophy explains the cosmic machinery behind destiny. Understanding fate here is essential for the whole poem.

Fate in Book 6

The Golden Bough

The Golden Bough comes away easily only if fate allows it. This makes entry to the Underworld itself fate-dependent. Aeneas's ability to find the bough and descend confirms his destiny.

Palinurus and Individual Fate

Palinurus died because he trusted calm seas‚Äîfate determined his death. Yet within fate, there is agency: he can be honoured with a tomb and cape named after him. Individual fate operates within cosmic fate.

Dido and Resisting Fate

Dido appears in the Underworld, silent and grieving. Her fate was to build Carthage, but love destroyed her. The encounter shows that resistance to fate brings suffering, even beyond death.

The Parade as Fate Revealed

Anchises's revelation makes Roman history visible as fate. The souls waiting to be born will become great Romans‚Äînot because of chance but because of cosmic design. History becomes teleology.

Philosophy of Fate

Anchises's Cosmology

Anchises teaches that a divine mind (mens) pervades the universe: "Spirit nourishes within, and mind, diffused through all the parts, moves the whole mass and mingles with the great body." This draws on Stoic and Platonic philosophy.

Soul and Body

Souls are divine fire trapped in mortal bodies. Death separates them; punishment or purification follows; eventually souls drink from Lethe and are reborn. This cycle explains how future Romans can be shown: their souls already exist, awaiting incarnation.

Jupiter and Fate

Elsewhere in the epic, Jupiter is fate's agent. But is Jupiter bound by fate or identical with it? The Aeneid suggests something like Stoic providence: divine reason governing all things toward cosmic order. Roman destiny is part of this order.

Fate and Human Agency

If the future is fated, do humans choose? The epic maintains both: fated outcomes occur, but humans still act meaningfully. Aeneas chooses to accept his fate; Dido chooses to resist; both choices matter even if results are determined.

Implications

Justifying the Present

If Rome's rise is fated, then Augustus's rule is cosmically justified. The Parade of Heroes argues that current power is the culmination of divine plan. This is potent ideological support for the regime.

The Problem of Suffering

But if fate is just, why does it destroy the innocent? Dido, Creusa, Palinurus‚Äîthey suffer for others' destinies. Book 6 does not resolve this. The lament for Marcellus shows that even fate's beneficiaries lose what they love.

The Gate of Ivory

Aeneas exits through the Gate of Ivory, which sends forth false dreams. Does this undercut everything? Scholars debate: perhaps it's conventional, or perhaps Virgil hints that the glorious vision should not be trusted uncritically.

Critical Balance
Book 6's fate is both triumphant and troubling. Rome is destined for glory‚Äîbut the glory costs suffering. The vision is magnificent‚Äîbut exit through falsehood's gate. Virgil celebrates and questions simultaneously. This complexity is what makes the epic literature rather than propaganda.
