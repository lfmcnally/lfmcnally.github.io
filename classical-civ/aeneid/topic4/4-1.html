4.1 Pietas: Duty and Roman Virtue

üìö A-Level Classical Civilisation ‚è±Ô∏è 60 min üìñ Virgil's Aeneid

Why Pietas Is THE Central Theme

Pietas isn't just "duty"‚Äîit's the DEFINING Roman virtue. Aeneas is called "pius Aeneas" more than any other epithet. Understanding pietas means understanding what Virgil thought made someone truly Roman, and what that costs.

I sing of warfare and a man at war. From the seacoast of Troy in early days he came to Italy by destiny, to our Lavinian western shore, a fugitive, this captain, buffeted cruelly on land as on the sea by blows from powers of the air‚Äîbehind them baleful Juno in her sleepless rage.
‚Äî Opening lines, Fitzgerald translation (1.1-4)
The First Description
Notice Virgil doesn't call Aeneas "brave" or "mighty" first‚Äîhe calls him a man driven "by destiny." Pietas means accepting your fate even when it destroys what you love. This is the poem in miniature.

What we'll cover: Pietas has THREE dimensions‚Äîduty to the gods, duty to family, and duty to your people/mission. Throughout the Aeneid, these come into CONFLICT. When Aeneas chooses duty over desire (leaving Dido), when he carries his father from burning Troy, when he descends to the underworld‚Äîhe's demonstrating pietas at terrible personal cost.

Three Aspects of Pietas

  • Pietas toward the gods: Obedience to divine will, proper worship, accepting fate even when it hurts
  • Pietas toward family: Duty to father, son, dead wife‚Äîfamily comes before personal safety
  • Pietas toward community: Leading the Trojan remnant to found Rome‚Äîcollective good over individual happiness

Why This Matters for Exams

The specification explicitly asks about "Roman values including pietas" and "the character of Aeneas." You CANNOT write sophisticated essays about the Aeneid without understanding how pietas functions‚Äîand how it's questioned.

Key Exam Questions Pietas Answers

"How does Virgil present Aeneas as a model Roman?" ‚Üí Through pietas demonstrations in Books 1, 2, 4, 6

"To what extent is the Aeneid propaganda for Augustus?" ‚Üí Augustus claimed to restore pietas; Aeneas models it

"Does Virgil celebrate or question Roman values?" ‚Üí Pietas brings both glory AND suffering‚Äîthis ambiguity is crucial

What Exactly IS Pietas?

Pietas is HARD to translate. "Duty" is too cold. "Devotion" is too emotional. It's closer to "fulfilling your proper role in the cosmic order"‚Äîdoing what you OWE to gods, family, and community, regardless of personal cost.

Roman Context

For Romans, pietas was the virtue that held society together. Sons owed pietas to fathers, citizens to the state, everyone to the gods. It was about maintaining right relationships and proper hierarchy.

Augustus himself claimed the title "Pater Patriae" (Father of the Fatherland) and built the Ara Pacis (Altar of Peace) with imagery emphasizing pietas. When Virgil makes Aeneas THE model of pietas, he's showing what an ideal Roman‚Äîand by extension, what Augustus‚Äîshould be.

What Pietas IS

  • Putting duty before desire
  • Obeying divine commands
  • Honoring ancestors and family
  • Sacrificing personal happiness for community
  • Accepting fate even when unjust
  • Maintaining proper religious observance

What Pietas Is NOT

  • Following your heart/passion
  • Questioning divine will
  • Choosing romantic love over duty
  • Prioritizing individual fulfillment
  • Rebelling against fate
  • Acting without religious sanction

Pietas vs Greek Heroism

  • Achilles (Iliad): Chooses personal honor over collective good; withdraws from battle when insulted; glory matters most
  • Odysseus (Odyssey): Wants to GET HOME; cleverness and survival drive him; individual goal-oriented
  • Aeneas (Aeneid): Puts OTHERS first; carries father from Troy; leaves Dido despite love; obeys gods even when it destroys him personally

üí° Critical Question

Is pietas wholly admirable, or does Virgil show its costs? When Aeneas leaves Dido and she kills herself‚Äîis that proof of his virtue or a tragedy caused by rigid duty? The poem gives you evidence for BOTH readings. This complexity is what makes it literature, not propaganda.

The Epithet: "Pius Aeneas"

Virgil calls Aeneas "pius" (dutiful, pious) repeatedly throughout the epic. This is his DEFINING characteristic‚Äîmore than "brave" or "strong." Let's look at when this epithet appears and what it reveals:

BOOK 1
"Pius Aeneas"
First appearance: 1.378
Aeneas is called "pius" when he's suffering from the storm, worried about his men. Even in crisis, he thinks of others first.
BOOK 2
"Pius Aeneas"
Falls of Troy: 2.707
Called "pius" when he carries his father Anchises from burning Troy‚Äîthe iconic image of filial pietas.
BOOK 4
"Pius Aeneas"
Dido episode: 4.393
Called "pius" even as he abandons Dido‚ÄîVirgil insists this IS pietas, choosing divine duty over human love.
BOOK 6
"Pius Aeneas"
Underworld: 6.176
Called "pius" when he descends to meet his father‚Äîultimate act of filial devotion crossing the boundary of death.

For Essays: Track the Epithet

When analyzing character, note WHEN Virgil calls Aeneas "pius"‚Äîit's often at moments of terrible choice or suffering. The epithet insists we see duty even when it looks like cruelty (leaving Dido) or madness (entering the underworld alive).

Book 1: The Storm and Pietas Under Pressure

Book 1 opens with Aeneas already SEVEN YEARS into his journey. Juno sends a storm to destroy the Trojan fleet. Watch how Aeneas responds‚Äîthis immediately establishes what kind of hero he is.

A sudden squall from the north struck full on the sail and lifted the waves to heaven. The oars snapped, the bow slewed round and lay beam on to the sea, and a steep mountain of water, a precipice, loomed up and broke upon them. Some of the ships hung on the crest of a wave, and as it opened up, the trough between its walls showed them the bottom of the sea, with the sand boiling in the surge.
‚Äî Book 1.102-107, Day Lewis translation

Aeneas's Response to Crisis

  • "A groan came from the depths of Aeneas's chest": He feels despair‚Äîhe's human, not superhuman
  • "Lucky the men who died before their fathers' eyes": Envies those who died honorably at Troy, not in anonymous storm
  • But he doesn't give up: Despite despair, he KEEPS TRYING to save his ships and men
  • No rage at the gods: Unlike Achilles who rages at dishonor, Aeneas accepts divine hostility as part of fate

üí° Pietas in Despair

Aeneas's first speech is him wishing he'd died at Troy. But pietas doesn't mean never feeling despair‚Äîit means continuing your duty DESPITE despair. He envies the dead but still fights to save his men. That's the difference between feeling and action.

Book 1: Speech to His Men

After the storm, the Trojans make landfall in North Africa. Aeneas is devastated‚Äîbut watch what he does when addressing his men:

Aeneas, though he was sick at heart with all the troubles he had suffered, put on a brave face and hid the sorrow deep within him. "My comrades," he said, "we are not unacquainted with suffering. You have endured worse than this. The gods will put an end to this also. Some day, perhaps, remembering even this will be a pleasure. Through so many different crises and calamities we travel to Latium... Endure, and keep yourselves for better times."
‚Äî Book 1.208-212, Day Lewis translation

Breaking Down Leadership Pietas

  • "sick at heart... put on a brave face": Private suffering vs public duty‚Äîthe leader's burden
  • "hid the sorrow deep within him": Doesn't share his despair with those who depend on him
  • "we are not unacquainted with suffering": Reminds them of shared past, building solidarity
  • "Some day... even this will be a pleasure": Future perspective‚Äîcurrent pain will become proud memory
  • "keep yourselves for better times": Duty to survive for the mission, not just for themselves
Public vs Private Self
The NEXT line after this speech? "So spoke Aeneas, but his heart was sick with terrible anxiety." He inspires them while dying inside. THIS is pietas‚Äîdoing your duty to others even when you're breaking. It's not about being emotionless; it's about functioning despite emotion.

Compare: Achilles in Iliad Book 1

  • Achilles: Publicly rages at Agamemnon, withdraws from battle, lets his men die to prove his point
  • Aeneas: Privately despairs but publicly encourages, puts collective morale before personal feelings
  • Both are heroes, but COMPLETELY different models of leadership
  • Greek hero: Individual honor first; Roman hero: Community duty first

Book 1: Meeting Venus Disguised

Aeneas's mother Venus appears to him disguised as a huntress. He doesn't recognize her. His response reveals his priorities:

"I am Aeneas, the dutiful (pius). I carry with me in my ships the household gods I rescued from the Greeks. My fame is known in the heavens above. I am looking for Italy, my homeland, and my descent is from highest Jupiter."
‚Äî Book 1.378-380, Day Lewis translation

How Aeneas Defines Himself

  • "I am Aeneas, the dutiful": FIRST thing he says‚Äîidentity = pietas, not military glory
  • "I carry... the household gods I rescued": Defines himself by religious duty, protecting sacred objects
  • "My fame is known in the heavens": Fame matters because gods know it‚Äîdivine approval, not human celebrity
  • "Looking for Italy, my homeland": Mission-driven‚Äîhe has a destination given by fate
  • "Descent from highest Jupiter": Divine lineage = divine obligation to fulfill destiny

For Essays: Self-Definition

When asked to introduce himself, Aeneas says "I am dutiful" BEFORE his name. Compare Achilles: "I am Achilles, best of the Achaeans"‚Äîidentity through combat excellence. This one speech encapsulates the difference between Greek and Roman heroism.

Book 2: The Fall of Troy‚ÄîUltimate Test

Book 2 is Aeneas's flashback narration of Troy's destruction. This is where we see pietas at its most powerful‚Äîand most costly. The iconic image of Aeneas carrying his father from burning Troy defines his character forever.

What Happens

Troy falls to the Greeks' wooden horse trick. Aeneas tries to fight but realizes it's hopeless. He runs home to save his family. His father Anchises refuses to leave (too old, wants to die with Troy). Omens from the gods‚Äîflames around his son Ascanius's head, a falling star‚Äîconvince Anchises that the gods want them to flee. Aeneas carries his father, leads his son, and tells his wife Creusa to follow. In the chaos, Creusa is lost and dies. Her ghost tells him to go‚Äîhis destiny is Italy and a new wife.

Come then, dear father, climb up onto my shoulders. I shall carry you on my back. This labour of love will never weary me. Whatever falls to us now, we shall both face one and the same danger and find one and the same salvation. Let little Ascanius walk beside me, and let my wife follow at a distance in my footsteps.
‚Äî Book 2.707-711, Day Lewis translation

The Iconic Image Analyzed

  • "Come then, dear father": Tender address despite crisis‚Äîfamily love drives him
  • "climb up onto my shoulders": Physical burden = metaphor for duty's weight
  • "This labour of love will never weary me": Willing acceptance of difficulty‚Äîpietas as chosen burden
  • "one and the same danger... salvation": Solidarity‚Äîwon't abandon father even if it costs his life
  • "Let my wife follow at a distance": Hierarchy of duty‚Äîfather comes first, then son, then wife (Roman patriarchal order)
Why This Image Matters
Aeneas carrying Anchises became THE defining image of pietas in Roman culture. Statues, coins, artwork‚Äîeverywhere. It shows physical strength used not for combat but for family duty. A warrior carrying his elderly father (who carries the household gods) while leading his son‚Äîthree generations, past-present-future, all protected by pietas.

The Loss of Creusa: Pietas's First Cost

In saving his father and son, Aeneas loses his wife. This isn't random‚ÄîVirgil is showing that pietas DEMANDS sacrifice. You can't save everyone; choices must be made.

Creusa alone, by some mischance, was torn from her husband and her friends. Did she halt or lose her way or sink down exhausted? We never knew. We never set eyes on her again. I did not even look back for her or give her a thought until we reached the sacred mound and ancient shrine of Ceres. There at last, when all the rest had assembled, she alone was missing.
‚Äî Book 2.738-744, Day Lewis translation

What This Reveals

  • "Did she halt or lose her way...? We never knew": The chaos of flight‚Äîno time to ensure everyone's safety
  • "I did not even look back for her": Harsh truth‚Äîhe was focused on father/son, not checking on wife
  • "We never set eyes on her again": Finality‚Äîdeath comes without goodbyes or closure
  • "She alone was missing": One life lost to save the others‚Äîthe mathematics of survival

üí° The Moral Complexity

Is Aeneas RIGHT to prioritize father over wife? Roman culture said yes‚Äîpaternal lineage mattered most. But Virgil makes us FEEL the cost. Creusa's ghost releases him from guilt, tells him to go, even blesses his future marriage. But her death still haunts the poem. Pietas saves some by sacrificing others.

Creusa's Ghost Speech

  • She appears as a ghost to release him from guilt: "Do not mourn me. The gods will it."
  • Tells him Italy awaits, and a royal bride (Lavinia‚Äîhis Roman future)
  • Makes her death MEANINGFUL by connecting it to fate‚Äîshe died so Rome could be founded
  • Aeneas tries to embrace her three times; she dissolves like air‚Äîcan't even mourn properly

Book 4: Dido‚ÄîWhen Duty Destroys Love

Book 4 is the HARDEST test of pietas in the entire epic. Aeneas falls in love with Dido, Queen of Carthage. They become lovers. He starts building her city instead of seeking Italy. Then Jupiter sends Mercury to remind him of his duty. He must choose: love and happiness with Dido, or duty and Italy's future.

The Crisis

Aeneas and Dido shelter in a cave during a storm and become lovers. Dido considers this marriage; Aeneas seems content to stay in Carthage. Jupiter sends Mercury with a harsh message: "You're wasting time building CARTHAGE when you should be founding ROME." Aeneas decides to leave immediately. Dido pleads, rages, curses him. He remains firm. She kills herself on a funeral pyre. As he sails away, he sees the flames but doesn't know she's dead.

But the devoted Aeneas, though he longed to soothe her sorrow with consolation and dispel her grief with words, still, moaning much and shaken in his mind by the great love he bore her, nevertheless carried out the gods' commands and went back to his ships.
‚Äî Book 4.393-396, Day Lewis translation

Breaking Down This Crucial Passage

  • "the devoted Aeneas": Virgil calls him PIUS even as he abandons Dido‚Äîinsisting this IS virtue
  • "though he longed to soothe her sorrow": He WANTS to comfort her‚Äîhe's not heartless
  • "moaning much and shaken... by the great love": He genuinely loves her‚Äîthis isn't easy for him
  • "nevertheless carried out the gods' commands": DESPITE love and pain, duty wins
  • "went back to his ships": Action follows duty, not desire‚Äîthe definition of pietas
The Central Ambiguity
Is Aeneas a model of virtue for obeying the gods, or is he cold and cruel for abandoning a woman who loves him? Virgil gives BOTH readings. He's "pius" for leaving‚Äîbut Dido dies by suicide, cursing Rome with eternal enmity (explaining the Punic Wars). Pietas brings both Rome's foundation AND Carthaginian hatred. Glory AND tragedy.

Dido's Accusation vs Aeneas's Defense

When Dido confronts Aeneas about leaving, their exchange crystallizes the conflict between personal love and cosmic duty:

Dido's Arguments

  • "I saved you from shipwreck"
  • "I gave you shelter and resources"
  • "We're married in all but name"
  • "You're abandoning me to enemies"
  • "At least leave me a child to remember you"
  • "You're heartless, born from rocks"

Aeneas's Defenses

  • "I never claimed marriage vows"
  • "My fate is Italy, not Carthage"
  • "Apollo commands me to go"
  • "My father's ghost told me in dreams"
  • "I'm defrauding my son of his Italian kingdom"
  • "I don't WANT to go‚Äîgods compel me"
I sail for Italy not of my own free will.
‚Äî Aeneas's ultimate defense, Book 4.361

üí° Who's Right?

Dido has justice on her side‚Äîhe DID enter a relationship knowing he'd leave. Aeneas has DUTY on his side‚Äîthe gods command him, and he MUST found Rome for future generations. Virgil doesn't tell you who to side with. That's the POINT. Pietas sometimes means doing terrible things for cosmic reasons beyond individual happiness.

Dido's Curse

  • As she dies, Dido curses Aeneas and all his descendants
  • She prays for eternal enmity between Carthage and Rome
  • She calls for an "avenger" to rise from her bones‚Äîthis is HANNIBAL
  • Pietas founding Rome CREATES the Punic Wars that nearly destroyed Rome
  • Divine duty brings both glory and catastrophic suffering across centuries

Book 6: Meeting Dido's Ghost

In the Underworld, Aeneas sees Dido among those who died of love. He tries to explain and justify himself one more time:

"Was I the cause of your death? I swear by the stars, by the gods above, by whatever there is to swear by in the depths of the earth, that it was against my will, O queen, that I left your shores... Believe me, I did not know that my going would cause you such terrible grief. Stay. Do not leave my sight. Who are you flying from? This is the last time fate will allow me to speak to you."
‚Äî Book 6.458-466, Day Lewis translation

Dido's Response

  • She says NOTHING‚Äîturns away without speaking
  • Her face is "as unyielding as if she were carved from flint or marble"
  • She returns to the shade of her first husband Sychaeus, who "answers her love and gives her love for love"
  • Aeneas is left weeping, "stirred by her unjust fate"

For Essays: The Silence

Dido's silence is MORE devastating than any speech. She denies him forgiveness, denies him absolution, denies him the comfort of thinking he did the right thing. Even DEATH doesn't resolve the moral complexity of pietas. He followed duty; she's still destroyed; neither is simply right or wrong.

When Pietas Conflicts: The Impossible Choices

Pietas would be simple if duty to gods, family, and community always aligned. They DON'T. Throughout the Aeneid, Aeneas must choose WHICH duty takes priority when they conflict.

CONFLICT 1
Duty to Troy vs Duty to Future
Book 2: Should Aeneas die defending Troy (honoring past/ancestors) or flee to found Rome (honoring destiny/future)? He chooses future over past‚Äîpainful but necessary.
CONFLICT 2
Duty to Love vs Duty to Mission
Book 4: Should Aeneas stay with Dido (personal happiness/gratitude) or leave for Italy (divine command/community future)? He chooses mission over love‚Äîdevastating but dutiful.
CONFLICT 3
Duty to Dead vs Duty to Living
Book 6: Palinurus (helmsman who died) begs for burial. Sibyl says fate forbids it now, but promises eventual honor. Immediate comfort vs larger mission.
CONFLICT 4
Duty to Peace vs Duty to Victory
Books 10-12: Aeneas must wage brutal war in Italy‚Äîkilling indigenous people to found Rome. Peace would be easier; conquest serves destiny. Violence becomes dutiful.

Why These Conflicts Matter

If pietas always felt good and right, it would be easy moralism. Virgil makes it HARD. The right choice brings suffering. Duty conflicts with desire, with mercy, with personal happiness, even with other duties.

This is what separates the Aeneid from simple propaganda. Yes, Aeneas demonstrates perfect pietas‚Äîbut Virgil SHOWS what that costs. The poem both celebrates Roman virtue AND mourns what's sacrificed for it.

The Cost of Pietas: What's Sacrificed

What Aeneas Loses to Duty

  • His city: Troy burns; he becomes a refugee
  • His wife: Creusa dies in the flight from Troy
  • His father: Anchises dies in Sicily before reaching Italy
  • His lover: Dido kills herself when he leaves
  • His helmsman: Palinurus falls overboard and drowns
  • His friend: Pallas dies in battle under his protection
  • His peace: Must wage war in Italy against Turnus
  • His mercy: Kills Turnus despite plea for life (Book 12)

The Pattern

  • Every book involves LOSS‚Äîpietas is defined by what you give up, not what you gain
  • Aeneas never gets what HE wants‚Äîhe gets what ROME needs
  • Individual happiness is sacrificed to collective destiny
  • The hero becomes a vehicle for history, not a person living for himself

üí° Is This Heroic or Tragic?

Roman audiences were meant to see Aeneas as THE ideal‚Äîproof that Rome's greatness came from moral virtue, from men willing to sacrifice everything for duty. But MODERN readers often see tragedy‚Äîa man who never gets to live for himself, whose every relationship ends in death or abandonment, who becomes almost robotic in his obedience to fate.

Pietas and Augustan Ideology

Augustus claimed to be RESTORING traditional Roman values after the chaos of civil wars. Pietas was central to this propaganda. By making Aeneas the model of pietas, Virgil supports this‚Äîbut with crucial complexity.

Historical Context

The Roman civil wars (49-31 BC) saw Romans killing Romans for personal power. Augustus presented himself as the man who ENDED this by restoring peace, traditional religion, and family values. He built temples, reformed marriage laws, promoted pietas as civic virtue.

The Aeneid was commissioned during this "moral revival." Aeneas embodies what Augustus claimed to represent: duty over ambition, community over individual, divine sanction over personal will.

How It Supports Augustus

  • Aeneas = ideal Roman = Augustus
  • Pietas brings peace and empire
  • Obedience to fate = obedience to Augustus
  • Individual desires subordinate to state
  • War justified when fate demands it

How It Questions Augustus

  • Pietas causes immense suffering
  • Dido's death shows cost of duty
  • Violence in Italy seems brutal
  • Marcellus's death shows dynasty fragility
  • Exits through gate of false dreams
For Essays: The Ambiguity
Strong essays recognize BOTH aspects. Virgil celebrates pietas AND shows its costs. This isn't contradiction‚Äîit's sophistication. He's not writing propaganda OR subversion, but literature that holds both glory and grief in tension. The best essays argue for one reading while acknowledging evidence for the other.

Exam Preparation: Using Pietas in Essays

Key Points for Any Pietas Question

1. DEFINE pietas specifically‚Äîduty to gods, family, community
2. Give SPECIFIC examples from prescribed books (Books 1, 2, 4, 6, 12)
3. Discuss BOTH celebration and cost‚Äîshow the complexity
4. Connect to Augustan context where relevant
5. Use Latin terms: "pius Aeneas," "fatum" (fate), "furor" vs "pietas"
