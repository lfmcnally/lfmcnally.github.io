Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will have detailed knowledge of Book 6's narrative structure, the Underworld's geography, and the climactic meeting with Anchises.

Book 6: The Pivot of the Epic

Book 6 stands at the exact centre of the Aeneid and marks the transition from the "Odyssean" half (wandering) to the "Iliadic" half (war). Aeneas descends to the Underworld to find his father, learns Rome's future, and emerges transformed and purposeful.

The Descent (Lines 1-263)

The Sibyl of Cumae

At Cumae in Italy, Aeneas consults the Sibyl, Apollo's prophetess. She prophesies more wars and suffering but promises eventual success. When Aeneas requests to visit his father in the Underworld, she describes the difficulty: "Easy is the descent to Avernus... but to retrace your steps, to escape to the upper air‚Äîthis is the task, this is the labour."

The Golden Bough

The Sibyl instructs Aeneas to find a golden bough sacred to Proserpina as a gift for entry. If fate favours him, the bough will come away easily. He finds it with divine guidance from two doves sent by Venus‚Äîthe bough yields to his touch, confirming his destiny.

Misenus's Funeral

Before descending, Aeneas must bury his trumpeter Misenus, who has died. The proper funeral rites underscore the theme of pietas and the importance of proper burial‚Äîa theme that will reappear with Palinurus below.

Facilis descensus Averno... sed revocare gradum superasque evadere ad auras, hoc opus, hic labor est.

Easy is the descent to Avernus... but to retrace your steps and escape to the upper air‚Äîthis is the task, this is the labour.
‚Äî Sibyl, 6.126-129

Geography of the Underworld

The Entrance

The descent begins at Lake Avernus. Aeneas and the Sibyl encounter personified horrors: Grief, Care, Disease, Old Age, Fear, Hunger, War, and Discord. These allegorical figures represent the terrors of mortal existence.

The River Styx and Charon

Charon ferries souls across the Styx. Aeneas sees crowds of unburied dead who cannot cross‚Äîincluding his helmsman Palinurus, who begs for burial. The Sibyl promises he will be honoured. The Golden Bough secures their passage.

Meeting Dido

In the Fields of Mourning, where those who died of love wander, Aeneas sees Dido. He weeps and tries to explain: "I did not leave you willingly..." She turns away without speaking, rejoining her husband Sychaeus. Her silence is more powerful than any words.

Tartarus

They pass Tartarus, where the wicked are punished, but do not enter. The Sibyl describes its torments: Tityos, Ixion, Tantalus, Sisyphus. The catalogue of sinners includes those who betrayed family, country, or gods‚Äîviolations of pietas.

Elysium

The blessed dwell in Elysium: poets, heroes, benefactors. The landscape is bright, with its own sun and stars. Here Aeneas finds his father Anchises.

Meeting Anchises

The Reunion

Aeneas finds Anchises reviewing souls waiting to be reborn. Father and son try three times to embrace, but Anchises is a shade‚Äîtheir arms pass through. This poignant failure emphasises death's finality even within the afterlife.

Anchises's Teaching

Anchises explains the nature of the soul: divine fire enclosed in mortal bodies, purified after death, eventually reincarnated. This philosophical section draws on Platonic and Stoic ideas, giving the epic intellectual depth.

The Parade of Heroes

Anchises shows Aeneas souls waiting to be born as future Romans: Silvius (Aeneas's son), the Alban kings, Romulus, the Republic's heroes (Brutus, the Fabii, Scipios), and finally Augustus himself. This vision of Roman destiny is the book's climax.

Rome's Mission

Anchises defines what it means to be Roman: others may excel in art and science, but "you, Roman, remember to rule peoples with empire... to spare the conquered and war down the proud."

Tu regere imperio populos, Romane, memento... parcere subiectis et debellare superbos.

You, Roman, remember to rule peoples with empire... to spare the conquered and war down the proud.
‚Äî Anchises, 6.851-853
The Gate of Ivory
Aeneas exits through the Gate of Ivory, through which false dreams pass (true dreams exit through the Gate of Horn). Scholars debate this: Does Virgil suggest the vision is false? Or is it a conventional exit from the Underworld? The ambiguity is probably deliberate.
