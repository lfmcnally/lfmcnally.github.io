4.4 The Cost of Empire

üìö A-Level Classical Civilisation ‚è±Ô∏è 50 min üìñ Virgil's Aeneid

Empire's Dark Side

The Aeneid celebrates Rome's imperial destiny‚Äî"empire without end," Anchises says. But Virgil also shows what that empire COSTS. Every victory requires death. Every step toward Rome means someone else's destruction. This isn't just military history; it's moral tragedy.

Why This Matters for OCR
Essay questions often ask whether the Aeneid is "propaganda for Augustus" or "critique of imperialism." The answer is BOTH. Virgil shows Rome's glory AND its cost. Sophisticated answers demonstrate this tension‚Äîdon't simplify into pure celebration or pure condemnation.

The basic paradox: Rome's foundation is divinely ordained, cosmically just, historically necessary. And it requires the destruction of Carthage (Dido), the conquest of Italy (Turnus), the death of innocents (Pallas, Lausus, Marcellus). Fate justifies the outcome but doesn't erase the suffering.

What This Lesson Examines

  • Loss and Sacrifice: What individuals give up for collective destiny‚ÄîAeneas loses Creusa, Dido, his identity as a Trojan
  • Deaths of Innocents: Young warriors (Pallas, Lausus, Euryalus) who die for others' causes
  • Moral Ambiguity: Are Aeneas's actions justified? Is imperial conquest heroic or brutal?
  • Sympathy for Victims: Virgil makes us care about Rome's enemies‚ÄîDido, Turnus, the Italians

Context: Augustan Ideology vs Virgilian Pessimism

What Augustus Wanted

Augustus commissioned the Aeneid to legitimise his rule. He wanted a foundation myth showing: (1) Rome's destiny is divinely ordained, (2) Empire brings peace and order (Pax Romana), (3) The Julian family (Augustus's) descends from Aeneas/Venus‚Äîmaking his rule cosmically justified.

What Virgil Gave Him

Virgil delivered all that‚ÄîBUT he also showed the COST. The epic is full of grief, loss, moral compromise, and sympathy for the defeated. It's as if Virgil said: "Yes, Rome was fated. Yes, Augustus is legitimate. But look what it took. Look who suffered."

This creates the epic's profound ambivalence. It's simultaneously pro-imperial (Rome is destined) and anti-war (war destroys the innocent). This tension is why scholars still argue about Virgil's "real" position‚Äîthe ambiguity is INTENTIONAL.

Is This Subversion or Complexity?

Some scholars think Virgil is subtly critiquing Augustus‚Äîshowing empire's brutality while pretending to celebrate it. Others think he's genuinely torn between patriotic pride and moral horror. Either way, the result is an epic that refuses simple triumphalism. Empire is glorious AND terrible.

What Aeneas Loses

Aeneas reaches Italy and fulfills his destiny. But count what he sacrifices: his city (Troy), his wife (Creusa), his father (Anchises), his lover (Dido), his Trojan identity, his personal happiness. He becomes the founder of Rome by ceasing to be fully himself.

"I, Aeneas, who carry with me the Penates we rescued from the enemy, famous beyond the stars... I seek Italy as my homeland, and my race is from highest Jove."
‚Äî Aeneas to Dido, Book 1.378-380 (Kline)

Identity as Sacrifice

  • "I, Aeneas": Introduces himself through his mission, not his personality
  • "who carry the Penates": Defined by what he bears (gods, duty), not who he is
  • "famous beyond the stars": His fame is cosmic, but is he happy?
  • "I seek Italy": Always seeking, never arriving emotionally
  • "my race is from highest Jove": Divine ancestry = burden more than blessing

The Founder Who Loses Everything

Aeneas is the anti-Odysseus. Odysseus fights to GET HOME, to RETURN to wife and kingdom. Aeneas loses his home, his wife, his father‚Äîand must create something new from that loss. He doesn't restore the past; he sacrifices it for the future.

This makes him profoundly ROMAN. Rome wasn't about preserving tradition intact‚Äîit was about transforming loss into power, grief into empire. Aeneas embodies that transformation, and Virgil shows how much it HURTS.

Creusa: The First Sacrifice

"Three times I tried to put my arms around her neck, three times the form I grasped in vain escaped my hands, like the light breeze, most like a winged dream."
‚Äî Aeneas on Creusa's ghost, Book 2.792-794 (Kline)

The Failed Embrace

  • "Three times I tried": Homeric formula (from Odyssey)‚Äîtradition of failed reunions with the dead
  • "escaped my hands": She's already beyond reach‚Äîphysically AND symbolically
  • "like the light breeze": Insubstantial, untouchable‚Äîdeath is absolute
  • "most like a winged dream": She's becoming memory, not person

Why Creusa Must Die

Creusa tells Aeneas he'll find "a kingdom and a royal wife" in Italy. For that future to happen, SHE must die. Her death isn't random tragedy‚Äîit's fated necessity. Rome requires Aeneas to marry Lavinia (Italian, not Trojan), so Creusa is sacrificed to destiny. This is empire's cost: individuals die to serve collective future.

Dido: Love Sacrificed to Duty

"I do not go to Italy of my own free will."
‚Äî Aeneas to Dido, Book 4.361 (Kline)

What This Short Sentence Reveals

  • "I do not go": Distinguishes choice from destiny‚Äîhe's CHOOSING to obey
  • "of my own free will": Implies external compulsion (fate, gods, duty)
  • But notice what he DOESN'T say: "I don't love you" or "I don't want to stay"
  • He admits desire conflicts with duty‚Äîand duty wins
"I would have preferred to have remained near Troy and cared for my people's relics. Priam's tall palace would still stand, and I would have refounded a new Pergamum for the defeated."
‚Äî Aeneas to Dido, Book 4.342-344 (Kline)

Aeneas's Alternative Future

This is the most revealing moment in the epic. Aeneas admits he WANTED something else‚Äîto stay at Troy, rebuild it, live there. But fate demanded Italy, Rome, empire. So he sacrifices his preference.

This makes pietas deeply TRAGIC. It's not joyful acceptance of destiny; it's painful submission to it. Aeneas obeys not because he wants to, but because he must. And Dido dies because of that obedience. Empire costs love.

Dido's Death as Imperial Critique

  • Dido built Carthage from nothing‚Äîa model of female leadership and refugee success
  • Aeneas's arrival DESTROYS her‚Äîshe abandons building projects, loses political authority, dies
  • Her final curse initiates the Punic Wars‚ÄîRome vs Carthage, centuries of bloodshed
  • Virgil makes us SYMPATHISE with her, not just Aeneas‚Äîcomplicating Rome's "heroic" origin

Anchises: Wisdom Lost

"Father, you alone were comfort in every sorrow, comfort taken from me."
‚Äî Aeneas mourning Anchises, Book 5.724 (Kline)

What Anchises Represented

  • Connection to the past: He remembered Troy's glory, carried its memory
  • Paternal guidance: "comfort in every sorrow"‚Äîemotional anchor
  • Divine intermediary: In death, he reveals Rome's future in the Underworld
  • But his death means: Aeneas now leads ALONE, without paternal wisdom to guide him

Pattern of Loss

Creusa (wife), Anchises (father), Dido (lover)‚ÄîAeneas loses every personal relationship that could compete with his public mission. By the time he reaches Italy, he's been stripped of private life entirely. All that's left is DUTY. This is what founding Rome demands.

Pallas: Youth Sacrificed

Pallas is young, brave, idealistic‚Äîand he dies horribly. His death is the emotional climax of the second half, and it directly causes the epic's controversial ending (Aeneas kills Turnus for wearing Pallas's belt).

"Turnus, closing with the youth, hurled his massive spear first. He stopped, and called out: 'See now if my weapon has a sharper point than yours.' He spoke: the quivering spear struck the shield full centre, piecing all the iron plates, all the bronze layers, and the tough hide of bull that covered it round about, and passed through the corselet's barrier, and through the mighty chest. Pallas tore the warm weapon from the wound in vain: together, the same way, his life blood and his life go."
‚Äî Pallas's death, Book 10.479-489 (Kline)

The Brutality of the Description

  • "See now if my weapon has a sharper point": Turnus TAUNTS him first‚Äîmakes it cruel, not just military
  • "quivering spear": The weapon is alive with violence
  • "piercing all... all... all": Enumeration emphasises thorough destruction
  • "through the mighty chest": Ironic‚ÄîPallas's chest is described as "mighty" as it's pierced
  • "tore the warm weapon": Grotesque detail‚Äî"warm" with his own blood
  • "in vain": Everything he does is futile; death is certain
  • "his life blood and his life go": Physical and spiritual death simultaneous

Why Virgil Dwells on This Death

Virgil could have killed Pallas off-screen or quickly. Instead, he gives us DETAIL: the taunt, the spear's path through layers of armor, the warm blood, the futile attempt to pull the weapon out. Why?

Because he wants us to FEEL the cost of war. Pallas isn't a statistic; he's a young man with a father who trusted Aeneas to protect him. His death isn't heroic glory‚Äîit's waste. This is what empire requires: the deaths of promising youth.

"Pallas, I will send you home dressed in the armour you deserve. I will not deny Evander that solace: the burial his son deserves."
‚Äî Aeneas over Pallas's body, Book 11.26-28 (Kline)

The Burden of Failure

Aeneas PROMISED Evander he'd protect Pallas. He failed. This death haunts him‚Äîand when he sees Pallas's belt on Turnus at the epic's end, all that grief and guilt explode into rage. Pallas's death proves that even fated heroes can't save everyone. Empire demands sacrifice even from its champions.

Lausus: Filial Piety Punished

"But when Lausus saw his dear father hard pressed by the cruel wound, he groaned deeply, and tears rolled down his cheeks. [...] Rushing in, the youth raised his sword, holding up the combat, while Aeneas's friends took Mezentius away. [...] Aeneas, incensed, threatened the youth with his sword: 'Why do you rush to death, daring more than your strength allows? Your devotion to your father tricks you into rashness.' [...] The sword passed right through the shield, and through the tunic his mother had woven with pliant gold, and blood filled his chest."
‚Äî Lausus's death, Book 10.789-832 (Kline, condensed)

The Tragic Irony

  • "groaned deeply, tears rolled": Emotional response‚Äîhe loves his father
  • "daring more than your strength allows": Aeneas KNOWS Lausus will die, warns him
  • "Your devotion tricks you": Pietas (devotion to father) leads to death‚Äîthe virtue becomes fatal
  • "his mother had woven": Domestic detail‚Äîmakes him human, not just enemy
  • "blood filled his chest": Grotesque but immediate‚Äîdeath is visceral
"Then indeed, pious Aeneas, groaning deeply, stretched out his hand, and a vision of his own love for his father touched his heart: 'What can poor Aeneas give you now, so worthy of praise, what reward worthy of such a nature? Keep the armour you delighted in, and I will restore you to your father's ashes and ghosts, if that matters to you. Yet be consoled in your sad death by this: you fall to great Aeneas's right hand.'"
‚Äî Aeneas over Lausus's body, Book 10.821-832 (Kline)

Aeneas Recognises Himself in the Enemy

  • "pious Aeneas": The epithet appears precisely when Aeneas sees Lausus's pietas
  • "a vision of his own love for his father": Aeneas carried Anchises from Troy‚ÄîLausus tried to save Mezentius. Same devotion
  • "What can I give you... worthy": Acknowledges debt‚ÄîLausus deserved better
  • "your sad death": Admits the death is tragic, not glorious
  • "you fall to great Aeneas": Offers consolation‚Äîbut is dying to a famous enemy really comfort?

The Enemy as Mirror

Lausus displays the SAME virtue (pietas) that defines Aeneas. He dies protecting his father, just as Aeneas risked death carrying Anchises. Yet Aeneas kills him anyway‚Äînot from cruelty, but from necessity.

This is empire's deepest cost: it forces you to destroy people who are JUST LIKE YOU. Lausus isn't evil; he's devoted. But Rome's destiny requires his death. Virgil makes sure we see the tragedy of that requirement.

Nisus and Euryalus: Love Destroyed

"Nisus rushed into the thick of the enemy, searching everywhere for Euryalus, calling his name through the hostile throng. [...] He found him at last: the unlucky youth, overwhelmed by numbers, surrounded by enemies in the dark confusion, struggling vainly against them all. What should he do? By what force, what weapons dare rescue the youth? Should he rush to certain death among the enemy swords, and find a quick and glorious end among so many wounds?"
‚Äî Nisus searching for Euryalus, Book 9.390-401 (Kline)

Devotion to the Point of Death

  • "searching everywhere": Desperate, frantic‚Äîlove drives him
  • "calling his name through the hostile throng": Gives away position to find his friend‚Äîreckless devotion
  • "struggling vainly against them all": Euryalus is doomed; Nisus knows it
  • "Should he rush to certain death": Considers dying WITH him‚Äîcompanion more important than life
  • "quick and glorious end": Death together would be glorious, but still END
"Euryalus, in dying, rolled over in blood, death's sleep settled on his lovely limbs, and his neck sank conquered on his shoulder: as when a bright flower, cut by the plough, withers and dies, or poppies droop their heads with weary necks when weighed down by a random shower."
‚Äî Euryalus's death, Book 9.433-437 (Kline)

The Flower Simile

  • "lovely limbs": Beauty emphasised even in death‚Äîhe's young, beautiful, destroyed
  • "death's sleep settled": Euphemism makes death gentle‚Äîironic given the violence
  • "as when a bright flower, cut by the plough": Most famous simile‚Äîflower = youth, plough = war's machinery
  • "withers and dies": Natural process applied to unnatural death
  • "poppies droop... weary necks": Exhaustion imagery‚Äîwar tires even the young
  • "random shower": Arbitrary violence‚Äî"random" suggests meaninglessness

Why This Death Matters

Nisus and Euryalus aren't major characters. They're young Trojan soldiers who volunteer for a night raid. They succeed brilliantly‚Äîthen get caught and killed. Their story could be omitted without changing the plot.

But Virgil gives them 200+ lines, intimate detail, the flower simile. Why? Because they represent all the anonymous youth destroyed by war. Every empire is built on thousands of Nisus-and-Euryalus pairs: brave, devoted, dead. The flower simile universalises their fate‚Äîthis is what war DOES to young men.

"Fortunate pair! If my poetry has any power, no day will ever erase you from the memory of time, while the house of Aeneas dwells by the Capitol's immovable rock, and while a Roman father holds power."
‚Äî Virgil on Nisus and Euryalus, Book 9.446-449 (Kline)

Virgil's Promise

Virgil breaks the narrative to address them directly. He promises immortal fame‚ÄîPOETRY will preserve them while Rome stands. But notice the irony: they're immortalised in a poem celebrating the empire that required their deaths. They're remembered BECAUSE Rome succeeded, but Rome succeeded BECAUSE they died. Imperial glory depends on endless young deaths.

Is Aeneas a Hero or a Destroyer?

The Aeneid refuses to give simple answers. Aeneas is BOTH the pious founder and the brutal conqueror. He's BOTH victim of fate and agent of destruction. This moral ambiguity is central to Virgil's vision.

Hero

  • Rescues his father and son from burning Troy
  • Obeys the gods despite personal cost
  • Shows mercy (spares some enemies)
  • Grieves for those he kills (Lausus)
  • Accepts burden of founding Rome

Destroyer

  • Abandons Dido, causing her suicide
  • Invades Italy, killing native peoples
  • Fails to protect Pallas despite promise
  • Kills Turnus in rage, not justice
  • Enables empire through violence

Why Both Are True

Virgil doesn't present these as contradictions to resolve. They COEXIST. Aeneas IS pious AND destructive. He fulfills divine will AND causes suffering. This is the nature of imperial foundation‚Äîit requires both virtue and violence.

The sophistication of Virgil's vision is refusing to choose. He won't say "empire is good, so violence is justified" OR "empire is bad, so Aeneas is evil." He shows empire's NECESSITY (Rome must exist) alongside its COST (look at the bodies). The tension is unresolved‚Äîdeliberately.

The Killing of Turnus: Justice or Murder?

The epic's final scene is its most morally ambiguous. Aeneas kills Turnus‚Äîbut was he right to do so?

"I beg you, by your father Anchises, spare my life. You have won. The Italians have seen me stretch out my hands in defeat. Lavinia is your wife. Do not extend your hatred further."
‚Äî Turnus begs for mercy, Book 12.931-936 (Kline)

Turnus's Plea

  • "by your father Anchises": Appeals to Aeneas's pietas‚Äîuses the virtue that defines him
  • "You have won": Acknowledges defeat‚Äîfighting is over
  • "seen me stretch out my hands": Public submission‚Äîhonour is satisfied
  • "Lavinia is your wife": You got what you came for‚Äîno need for more
  • "Do not extend your hatred": Implies Aeneas is acting from passion (hatred), not justice
"Aeneas stood, terrible in his armour, his eyes rolling, and he restrained his right hand: and now, now the speech had begun to sway him more and more, when the luckless belt was seen high on Turnus's shoulder, gleaming with familiar studs, the belt of the boy Pallas."
‚Äî Aeneas hesitates, sees Pallas's belt, Book 12.938-942 (Kline)

The Moment of Choice

  • "restrained his right hand": Active restraint‚Äîhe's CHOOSING not to kill (initially)
  • "the speech had begun to sway him": Mercy was winning‚Äîhe was listening
  • "more and more": Emphasises the process‚Äîreason gaining ground
  • "when the luckless belt was seen": Visual trigger changes everything
  • "gleaming with familiar studs": Physical detail evokes Pallas vividly
"When his eyes drank in the sight of the spoils, the memorial to his own fierce grief, terrible in his wrath, and filled with fury, he cried: 'Are you to escape me, wearing the spoils of my people? Pallas sacrifices you with this stroke, Pallas, and exacts penalty from your guilty blood.' Saying this, he buried his sword fiercely in Turnus's breast."
‚Äî Aeneas kills Turnus, Book 12.945-952 (Kline)

The Language of Fury

  • "eyes drank in": Consuming imagery‚Äîvisual input becomes emotional fuel
  • "memorial to his own fierce grief": Belt = reminder of failure, guilt, loss
  • "terrible in his wrath, filled with fury": FUROR‚Äîthe force Aeneid consistently condemns
  • "Pallas sacrifices you": Frames killing as ritual, not justice‚Äîrevenge masquerading as piety
  • "buried his sword fiercely": Violence emphasized‚Äîthis is brutal, not reluctant

Arguments FOR Killing

  • Turnus killed Pallas (revenge is just)
  • He's wearing Pallas's belt (desecration)
  • He started the war (aggressor guilt)
  • Killing him ends the war permanently
  • Mercy might look like weakness

Arguments AGAINST Killing

  • Turnus is defeated and begging (supplicant right)
  • Aeneas acts from FUROR, not justice
  • Anchises said "spare the conquered"
  • Killing violates Roman mercy ideal
  • Epic ends in rage, not peace

No Clear Answer

Virgil gives evidence for BOTH positions. Scholars have argued for 2000 years about whether Aeneas was right. This isn't authorial confusion‚Äîit's deliberate ambiguity. Empire's foundation involves violence that can be justified AND condemned. Virgil forces you to grapple with that tension, not resolve it neatly.

Pietas vs Furor: The Final Contradiction

The Epic's Central Opposition

Throughout the Aeneid, PIETAS (duty, devotion, rational obedience) is good. FUROR (rage, passion, irrational violence) is bad. Juno operates through furor. Dido dies in furor. Turnus fights in furor. Aeneas is defined by pietas.

Except at the very end, when AENEAS kills in FUROR. "Terrible in his wrath, filled with fury"‚Äîthat's furor language. The pious hero becomes the furious killer.

What This Means

Option 1: Aeneas FAILS at the end‚Äîgives in to rage, betraying his own virtue. Rome is founded on moral compromise.

Option 2: Pietas and furor can't be cleanly separated‚Äîeven pious action requires passionate violence sometimes.

Option 3: Empire REQUIRES both‚ÄîRome needs rational planning (pietas) AND passionate violence (furor) to succeed.

Virgil doesn't tell us which interpretation is right. The epic ENDS with this unresolved tension, forcing readers to decide.

Virgil Makes Us Care About Rome's Enemies

If the Aeneid were pure propaganda, Dido would be a temptress, Turnus a villain, the Italians barbarians. Instead, Virgil humanises them‚Äîgives them dignity, sympathy, even tragic nobility. This complicates Rome's triumph.

Why This Is Radical

Virgil is writing for ROMANS about Rome's enemies. He could have demonised Carthage (historical enemy) and the Italians (conquered peoples). Instead, he makes Dido MORE sympathetic than Aeneas in Book 4, and Turnus MORE heroic in his final stand.

This suggests profound moral ambivalence about empire. Yes, Rome was destined. But the people it destroyed weren't evil‚Äîthey were human, admirable, tragic. Empire's cost includes the destruction of people worth preserving.

Dido: The Enemy Who Loves

"Is this the loyalty, the justice of the pious? Is this how he obeys the gods? Him, who took me when I was shipwrecked, and madly made him king and partner of my throne, who saved his lost fleet and drew his comrades from death? I burn with furies. Now prophetic Apollo, now the Lycian oracles, now the messenger of the gods sent from Jove himself through the air, brings these dread commands. As if that were work for the gods, as if that anxiety disturbs their calm!"
‚Äî Dido to Aeneas, Book 4.305-313 (Kline)

Dido's Devastating Critique

  • "Is this the loyalty... of the pious?": Attacks his identity‚Äîif THIS is pietas, it's hollow
  • "Is this how he obeys the gods?": Suggests gods are excuse, not genuine command
  • "made him king and partner": She GAVE him everything‚Äîhe returns abandonment
  • "saved his lost fleet": Material help‚Äîhe'd have died without her
  • "I burn with furies": Furor language‚Äîbut her rage seems JUSTIFIED
  • "as if that were work for the gods": Sarcasm‚Äîgods wouldn't care about one relationship
  • "as if that anxiety disturbs their calm": Gods are serene; Aeneas uses them as excuse

Who's Right?

From Aeneas's perspective, he MUST obey the gods‚ÄîItaly is fated, Dido isn't. From Dido's perspective, he's abandoning someone who saved his life for an abstract destiny. Both positions have validity. Virgil doesn't adjudicate‚Äîhe presents both, lets sympathy complicate judgment.

"There are tears for things, and mortal matters touch the heart."
‚Äî Aeneas viewing Trojan War murals in Carthage, Book 1.462 (Kline: "sunt lacrimae rerum")

Sunt Lacrimae Rerum

  • Most famous phrase in the Aeneid‚Äîphilosophically profound
  • "tears for things": The world itself evokes tears‚Äîreality is tragic
  • "mortal matters touch the heart": Human suffering is universal, recognisable
  • Aeneas says this seeing TROJAN suffering depicted in CARTHAGE
  • Dido's people understand and commemorate Troy's tragedy‚Äîcreates empathy
  • But Aeneas will DESTROY Dido's Carthage‚Äîthe tears flow both ways

The Irony of Sunt Lacrimae Rerum

Aeneas feels comforted that Carthaginians understand Trojan suffering. They share tears‚Äîuniversal human sympathy. But he'll cause CARTHAGE to weep (Dido's death, future Punic Wars). The phrase becomes tragically ironic: yes, there are tears for all things‚Äîincluding the suffering your destiny causes others.

Turnus: The Sympathetic Antagonist

"Turnus, alone, undaunted, remained, calling on the gods and his pledge: 'What have I to fear? I will not run, Trojans. My compact was with the gods. It is yours to start your war with me. Either I will send this Asian, this deserter from the East, down to Tartarus with this right hand, and the Latins will watch one man wipe out the nation's shame, or he will triumph and Lavinia will be his bride.'"
‚Äî Turnus challenges Aeneas, Book 12.894-899 (Kline)

Turnus's Courage

  • "alone, undaunted": Isolated but brave‚Äîheroic stance
  • "What have I to fear?": Rhetorical‚Äîhe fears, but fights anyway
  • "My compact was with the gods": Claims divine sanction too (not just Aeneas)
  • "this Asian, this deserter from the East": Xenophobia‚Äîbut from HIS perspective, Aeneas IS foreign invader
  • "one man wipe out the nation's shame": Heroic ideal‚Äîindividual excellence redeems collective
  • "or he will triumph": Accepts possibility of defeat‚Äîfatalistic courage
"Fortune favoursthe brave."
‚Äî Turnus rallying troops, Book 10.284 (Kline: "audentis Fortuna iuvat")

Turnus as Homeric Hero

  • Turnus fights for glory, personal honour, martial excellence (aristeia)
  • He's more like Achilles/Hector than Aeneas is‚Äîpassionate, individualistic, brave
  • His values are HEROIC in the Homeric sense‚Äîcourage, strength, fame
  • But those values are doomed in Virgil's world‚Äîpietas (collective duty) triumphs over virtus (individual excellence)

Why Turnus Is Sympathetic

From Turnus's perspective, HE is the defender. Aeneas is the invader. Lavinia was promised to him first. He's fighting for his homeland against foreign aggression. Everything we'd call "heroic" if it were the protagonist.

Virgil makes us feel this. Turnus isn't a monster‚Äîhe's brave, passionate, devoted to his people. His defeat is necessary for Rome, but it's TRAGIC, not triumphant. Empire requires destroying people who don't deserve destruction.

"His limbs collapsed in chill, and his life fled, with a moan, indignantly, to the Shades."
‚Äî Turnus dies, Book 12.951-952 (Kline)

"Indignantly"

The final word about Turnus is "indignantly" (indignata in Latin). He dies ANGRY, protesting, refusing to accept fate. This isn't peaceful submission‚Äîit's defiant rejection. The epic ends with the defeated enemy's rage, not the victor's peace. Virgil's final sympathy is with the LOSER, not the winner.

For Essays: Empire's Tragic Necessity

Key Arguments

1. Virgil celebrates AND mourns empire. Rome's destiny is glorious (Jupiter's prophecy), but its cost is terrible (Dido, Pallas, Turnus die). Both are true simultaneously.

2. Sympathy for victims complicates triumphalism. By humanising Rome's enemies (Dido's love, Turnus's courage), Virgil prevents simple celebration of conquest.

3. Pietas requires sacrifice of the self and others. Aeneas loses personal happiness (Dido), fails to protect dependents (Pallas), and kills admirable enemies (Lausus, Turnus). Duty is COSTLY.

4. The ending refuses resolution. Aeneas wins, but through FUROR. Rome is founded, but on rage and death. Virgil won't let us feel simply triumphant‚Äîthe cost shadows the victory.

5. "Sunt lacrimae rerum" summarises Virgil's vision. There are tears for all things‚Äîfor Troy's fall, for Dido's death, for Turnus's defeat, for empire's necessity. Tragedy is universal, inescapable, built into the structure of history itself.

Ultimate Exam Point
The Aeneid's greatness is its REFUSAL to simplify. It's simultaneously: pro-Augustus propaganda AND critique of imperial violence; celebration of Roman destiny AND lament for its victims; assertion that empire is necessary AND acknowledgment that necessity is tragic. Show examiners you understand this complexity‚Äîdon't reduce the epic to one position.
