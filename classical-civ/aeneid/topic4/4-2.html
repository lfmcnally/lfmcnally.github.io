4.2 The Parade of Heroes and Rome's Destiny

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Parade of Heroes as both prophecy and propaganda, recognise key figures mentioned, and analyse how Virgil connects mythological past to Augustan present.

The Parade as Propaganda

The Parade of Heroes (6.756-886) is the most explicitly political section of the Aeneid. Anchises shows Aeneas souls waiting to be born as great Romans, culminating in Augustus. This makes Roman history look inevitable‚Äîdestined from the mythological age.

Exam Focus
The specification requires understanding of "Augustan context" and "Virgil's relationship to Augustus." The Parade of Heroes is essential evidence for discussing how Virgil promotes (and perhaps complicates) the Augustan regime.

The Heroes

Early Kings and Founders

Silvius: Aeneas's future son by Lavinia, ancestor of the Alban kings. Procas, Capys, Numitor: Alban kings linking Aeneas to Romulus. Romulus: Son of Mars, founder of Rome itself. Anchises points to him preparing to found the city.

Republican Heroes

Brutus: Who expelled the Tarquin kings and founded the Republic‚Äîand executed his own sons for conspiracy. The Decii and Drusi: Families who sacrificed themselves for Rome. Torquatus: Who executed his son for disobeying orders. Camillus: Who saved Rome from the Gauls. The Scipios: Who defeated Carthage.

Cato and Others

Cato: Model of old Roman virtue. The Gracchi: Reformers (controversial, but included). Fabius Maximus: Who saved Rome by delay against Hannibal. Pompey and Caesar: Anchises grieves that they will fight civil war.

Pattern and Purpose

The selection emphasises military glory, self-sacrifice, and pietas (even severe pietas like Brutus executing his sons). These are values the Augustan regime claimed to restore. The Parade creates a usable past for the new Rome.

Augustus in the Parade

The Climax

Augustus receives the longest and most elevated description. Anchises points him out: "This is the man, this is he whom you so often hear promised to you, Augustus Caesar, son of a god, who will establish again a golden age in Latium."

Divine Connections

Augustus is "divi genus" (offspring of a god)‚Äîhis adoptive father Julius Caesar was deified. He is connected to Hercules and Dionysus, culture-heroes who civilised the world. His rule will extend to Indians and Garamantes‚Äîcosmic scope.

The Golden Age

Augustus will restore the "golden age" (aurea saecula)‚Äîthe mythic era of Saturn before civilization brought corruption. This was central Augustan propaganda: the civil wars had ended, peace returned, traditional values restored.

Hic vir, hic est, tibi quem promitti saepius audis, Augustus Caesar, divi genus, aurea condet saecula...

This is the man, this is he whom you so often hear promised to you, Augustus Caesar, son of a god, who will establish golden ages...

Marcellus: Glory and Grief

Two Marcelli

Anchises sees two Marcelli: the elder, Republican hero who fought Hannibal, and a young man walking beside him in shadow. This is Marcus Claudius Marcellus, Augustus's nephew and intended heir, who died at 19 in 23 BC.

The Lament

Aeneas asks about the youth; Anchises bursts into lamentation. Marcellus will be shown briefly to the world then snatched away. "If only you could break through harsh fate! You will be Marcellus."

Contemporary Impact

Ancient sources report that when Virgil recited this passage to Augustus and his sister Octavia (Marcellus's mother), Octavia fainted from grief. Whether literally true, the story captures how personally the poem engaged its original audience.

Thematic Function

The Marcellus passage complicates the triumphalism. Even as the Parade celebrates Rome's destiny, it ends with loss. Glory and grief intertwine; the cost of empire includes even Augustus's family. This is characteristic Virgilian ambivalence.

Critical Balance
The Parade both celebrates and mourns. Augustus's golden age is real‚Äîbut Marcellus died young. The poem glorifies Rome's destiny while acknowledging what that destiny costs. This complexity is essential to Virgil's achievement.
