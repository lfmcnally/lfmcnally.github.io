7.3 Aeneas's Development as Hero

üìö Topic 7: Books 11 & 12‚è±Ô∏è 35 minüìä Character Analysis

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace Aeneas's development throughout the epic, identify key moments of transformation, and evaluate whether he becomes a complete hero.

The Problem of Aeneas

Many readers find Aeneas less compelling than Achilles or Odysseus. He seems passive, dutiful, lacking personality. But Virgil is doing something different: creating a hero who subordinates self to mission. Understanding Aeneas requires understanding this new heroic ideal‚Äîand its costs.

Early Aeneas (Books 1-4)

The Storm (Book 1)

Our first view of Aeneas: despairing in the storm, wishing he had died at Troy. This is not heroic self-confidence. Aeneas is introduced as suffering, vulnerable, uncertain. He will grow, but he starts in crisis.

The Fall of Troy (Book 2)

Aeneas narrates Troy's destruction. He fought until fighting was futile; he wanted to die gloriously. His mother Venus stopped him: "Your duty is not to die here but to save your family and found a new Troy." This divine intervention establishes his mission‚Äîand his subordination to it.

Carrying Anchises

The iconic image: Aeneas carrying his father on his shoulders, leading his son by the hand, the household gods in his arms. This is pietas visualised‚Äîduty to parents, children, and gods. But Creusa is lost. Devotion to mission costs personal relationship.

Dido (Book 4)

Aeneas loves Dido‚Äîor at least enjoys her love. But when Mercury reminds him of his duty, he prepares to leave. His famous defence: "I do not seek Italy willingly" (Italiam non sponte sequor). He obeys fate, not his heart. This obedience destroys Dido and haunts him forever.

Early Pattern

In Books 1-4, Aeneas is learning to subordinate personal desire to divine mission. He is not naturally dutiful‚Äîhe has to become so. The cost is emotional suppression: he cannot have what he wants.

The Middle Journey (Books 5-8)

Funeral Games (Book 5)

Aeneas presides over games for Anchises‚Äîshowing leadership, fairness, and piety. He also navigates crisis when the women burn the ships. His prayer to Jupiter shows faith; his decision to leave the weak behind shows pragmatism. He is becoming a leader.

The Underworld (Book 6)

The katabasis is Aeneas's definitive transformation. He sees Dido (silent, unforgiving), meets Anchises, learns Rome's destiny. He emerges with knowledge of what he serves‚Äîno longer following blindly but understanding the stakes. The vision motivates his subsequent actions.

Evander and Pallanteum (Book 8)

Aeneas makes alliance with Evander, accepts responsibility for young Pallas. He visits the site of future Rome, hears of Hercules's civilising labour. He receives the Shield showing Roman destiny. He is now prepared for war‚Äîarmed with weapons and purpose.

Transformation Complete?

By Book 8's end, Aeneas seems complete: he has knowledge, purpose, divine armour. He lifts the Shield's burden onto his shoulders, accepting destiny. But the war books will test whether this acceptance holds under the pressure of killing and loss.

The War Books (Books 9-12)

Absence and Return (Books 9-10)

Aeneas is absent from Book 9‚Äîhis followers survive without him. When he returns in Book 10, he is a warrior of supreme power. But Pallas's death transforms him: he rages, slaughters, takes prisoners for sacrifice. His pietas becomes fury.

Grief and Anger (Book 10)

Aeneas's rampage after Pallas's death is disturbing. He refuses mercy, mocks the dying, acts like Achilles at his most savage. The pious hero becomes a killer. Is this failure of virtue‚Äîor proof that virtue cannot survive war untainted?

The Final Duel (Book 12)

Aeneas in Book 12 seems more controlled. He wants the treaty honoured; he tries to stop the renewed battle; he attacks the city only when Turnus cannot be found. But the ending shows he has not transcended anger.

The Killing of Turnus

Aeneas hesitates‚Äîmoved by Turnus's plea, perhaps ready to spare him. Then he sees Pallas's belt. Memory of his friend's death overcomes mercy. He kills "burning with fury" (furiis accensus). The very word‚Äîfuror‚Äîthat characterises his enemies now describes him.

Incomplete Hero?
Aeneas's development is complex. He learns duty, accepts mission, becomes leader and warrior. But the ending shows pietas and furor are not fully separable. He kills for Pallas‚Äîis that loyal devotion or uncontrolled rage? The poem doesn't say. Aeneas may represent not the perfection of virtue but the impossibility of remaining virtuous in a violent world.
