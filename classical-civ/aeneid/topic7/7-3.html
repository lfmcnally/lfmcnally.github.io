7.3 Book 12 Overview

üìö OCR A-Level Classical Civilisation ‚è±Ô∏è 50 min üìñ Virgil's Aeneid

The Final Book: Single Combat Proposal

Book 12 brings the epic to its controversial close. Turnus accepts single combat; the treaty is broken; battle resumes; finally the duel happens. Turnus falls, begs for mercy, and Aeneas - seeing Pallas's belt - kills him in fury. The poem ends abruptly.

Why Book 12 Matters
This book resolves EVERYTHING: divine conflict (Jupiter/Juno), ethnic tension (Trojan/Latin), personal vendetta (Aeneas/Turnus), and the question of mercy. The ending is deliberately ambiguous - does Aeneas succeed or fail? You MUST know this book for any essay on the Aeneid.

The Complete Narrative

Turnus accepts single combat. Latinus and Amata plead with him to reconsider - he refuses. A treaty is sworn: if Turnus wins, Trojans leave; if Aeneas wins, Trojans and Latins unite as equals. Juno sends Juturna (Turnus's divine sister) to break the truce. Battle resumes. Aeneas is wounded, healed by Venus, returns. He attacks the Latin city. Amata hangs herself. Jupiter forces Juno to accept defeat. A Fury terrifies Turnus. The final duel: Turnus wounded, begs mercy, Aeneas hesitates, sees Pallas's belt, kills Turnus. END.

Why This Matters for Exams

The controversial ending: Is Aeneas right to kill Turnus? Or does he fail pietas by refusing mercy?

Divine resolution: Jupiter and Juno finally reconcile - but only AFTER more humans die

Roman identity: The treaty terms establish what "Roman" means - Trojan-Latin fusion, not pure Trojan

Turnus Accepts Single Combat

After Camilla's death and Latin defeats, Turnus finally accepts what Drances demanded in Book 11: single combat with Aeneas to settle the war.

"Now my mind is made up - let no one try to shake my resolve with fear. I will go to meet the Trojan in single combat. I will face my destiny or win glory. Let the war end with me alone."
‚Äî Turnus, Aeneid 12.48-53 (adapted)

Why Turnus Finally Agrees

  • Camilla is dead - no more cavalry
  • Latins are routed - military defeat imminent
  • Drances's challenge (Book 11) still echoes - his honor demands response
  • Amata begs him not to fight - which actually strengthens his resolve (can't appear cowardly)
  • He sees no other honorable way out

Lavinia's Blush

When Turnus announces his decision, Virgil describes Lavinia: "She wept, her face flaming with blush, deep crimson spreading like stained ivory." This is the ONLY time Virgil shows her emotional response.

Why? Some read it as love for Turnus (she doesn't want him to die). Others read it as shame (she's the prize being fought over, objectified). The ambiguity is deliberate - Lavinia remains silent throughout, her thoughts unknown.

The Treaty Terms

Aeneas and Latinus swear a formal treaty before witnesses. The terms are surprisingly generous - this isn't conquest, it's fusion.

"If Turnus wins, the Trojans will depart for Evander's city and never return to war. But if Victory favors Aeneas, the Trojans will not command the Italians or seek kingship. Let both nations, equal in laws, join in eternal treaty. I will give them my sacred rites and gods. Latinus will rule the land; the Trojans will build walls bearing Lavinia's name."
‚Äî Aeneas, Aeneid 12.189-194 (adapted)

What These Terms Mean

"Equal in laws": Not conquest - partnership. Latins keep autonomy

"I will give them my sacred rites": Trojans contribute religion (this becomes Roman pietas)

"Latinus will rule": Political power stays Latin

"Lavinia's name": The city will be called Lavinium - honoring the Latin princess, not Troy

Fusion, not replacement: This is what Rome claims to be - Trojan divine ancestry + Italian practical governance

Juno Breaks the Truce

Just when peace seems possible, Juno intervenes one last time. She sends Juturna (Turnus's immortal sister, a river nymph) to break the treaty.

Juturna's Mission

  • Juno knows she's losing - Jupiter has decreed Trojan victory
  • But she can delay, cause more suffering before accepting defeat
  • Sends Juturna to stir up Latins: "Will you let one man decide your fate?"
  • Disguised as warrior, she incites crowd against treaty
  • A Latin soldier throws a spear - kills a Trojan
  • Chaos erupts. Battle resumes. Treaty shattered

Why This Matters

The truce is broken NOT because humans are inherently warlike but because a goddess intervenes. This is Virgil's critique: even when humans choose peace (Latinus, Aeneas both want the treaty), divine forces (Juno's hatred) prevent it.

Later, when Jupiter FINALLY restrains Juno, peace becomes possible. The message: human rationality can't overcome divine irrationality. Only when gods reconcile can humans live in peace.

"Aeneas, unarmed, shouted across the battlefield: 'Where are you rushing? What madness rises? The treaty is made! I alone have the right to fight!' But even as he spoke, a flying arrow struck him."
‚Äî Aeneid 12.311-323 (adapted)

Aeneas Wounded & Healed

Aeneas is wounded by an arrow (unknown source - possibly divine). He's carried from battle. Iapyx, a healer, cannot extract the arrow. Venus intervenes with magical dittany herb from Crete. Aeneas is healed instantly and returns to battle with renewed fury.

The Wound's Symbolic Meaning

Vulnerability: Even Aeneas can be hurt - he's human, not invincible

Divine dependence: He needs Venus to heal him - can't succeed without divine help

Restored fury: When healed, he returns MORE violent than before - wound doesn't teach mercy, it increases rage

Prefigures Turnus: Aeneas wounded‚Üíhealed‚Üívictorious. Turnus wounded‚ÜíNOT healed‚Üídies. Divine favor makes the difference

Jupiter's Ultimatum to Juno

Finally, Jupiter confronts Juno directly. The divine conflict that has driven the entire epic must end.

"Wife, why this endless opposition? What limit is there to your wrath? You know the outcome is decreed by fate. Aeneas will be made a god; the Trojans will found Rome. What more do you seek? I allow you to prolong suffering - but enough. Make peace."
‚Äî Jupiter, Aeneid 12.793-806 (adapted)

Juno's Terms for Surrender

  • "Let the Latins keep their ancestral name": No "New Troy" - Rome must be Italian-named
  • "Let them keep their language": Latin, not Trojan, becomes Rome's tongue
  • "Let them keep their customs": Italian traditions, not imported Trojan culture
  • "Let Troy fall and stay fallen": No revival of the defeated city
  • "The Romans shall arise mixed from both peoples": Fusion creates something new

What This Divine Bargain Establishes

Roman identity = Trojan + Latin: Not purely Trojan ancestry. Mixed heritage

Juno becomes Roman goddess: She accepts Rome once it's NOT Troy reborn

Language/culture = Italian: Explains why Romans speak Latin, not Greek/Trojan

Divine reconciliation enables human peace: Only when gods stop fighting can mortals stop

The Dira (Fury)

After Juno yields, Jupiter sends a Dira - one of the Furies/monsters - to terrify Turnus and drive away Juturna. It appears as an owl of death, shrieking around Turnus's head, beating against his shield. His strength drains. Juturna recognizes the sign and abandons her brother to his fate.

This is Jupiter enforcing the divine settlement. Turnus must die. Even his divine sister can't save him now.
