Book 11: Lament and Last Battles

Book 11 opens with mourning for the dead, proceeds to political debate among the Latins (should they continue the war?), and climaxes with Camilla's brilliant but doomed cavalry battle. The book balances grief, politics, and warfare.

Pallas's Funeral

Trophy to Mars

The book opens with Aeneas dedicating a trophy to Mars from Mezentius's armour. He addresses his troops: their work is nearly done; now they must bury the dead. The pious act precedes the personal grief to follow.

Preparing the Body

Pallas's body is dressed, his wounds covered. Aeneas weeps over him: "Poor boy... I promised your father I would protect you." The failure of that promise haunts him. He sends the body home with Pallas's thousand companions.

The Funeral Procession

The cort√®ge includes Pallas's horse Aethon, weeping like a human, and captive enemies for the funeral pyre‚Äîthe same prisoners Aeneas took in his fury after Pallas's death. The grim detail recalls Achilles's sacrifice of Trojans at Patroclus's funeral.

Evander's Lament

The scene cuts to Evander receiving the news, then the body. His speech is devastating: "This is not what I prayed for... I should have died, not you." He blames Turnus and calls for vengeance. His grief legitimates Aeneas's later refusal of mercy.

The Latin Council

Embassy from Diomedes

Latinus had sent to Diomedes, the Greek hero who now rules in Italy, seeking alliance against the Trojans. Diomedes refuses: he has fought Trojans before and wants no more of it. "Let them keep their land; make peace."

Latinus Proposes Peace

Latinus argues for peace: Diomedes won't help; the war goes badly; perhaps the Trojans should be given land and allowed to settle. He is old, weary, and has always doubted the war. He represents moderation.

Drances Attacks Turnus

Drances is an orator who hates Turnus. He argues that Turnus alone is responsible for the war, and Turnus alone should fight Aeneas in single combat: "Let one life, deservedly lost, save thousands."

Turnus's Response

Turnus responds with contempt: Drances is brave only in speeches. The war isn't lost. He, Turnus, will fight Aeneas in single combat if that's what it takes‚Äîhe doesn't fear death. His speech is proud, defiant, but also defensive.

News of Attack

Before the debate concludes, news arrives: the Trojans are advancing. The council breaks up in chaos. Turnus arms for battle, his question of single combat left unresolved until Book 12.

Political Drama
The council debate shows war's political dimension: disagreement among allies, personal rivalries, the gap between those who fight and those who talk. Virgil gives sympathetic hearing to peace arguments‚Äîthe Latins aren't simply villains‚Äîwhile showing how honour and anger prevent resolution.

Camilla's Aristeia and Death

Diana's Background

Diana tells her nymph Opis about Camilla: daughter of an exiled king, raised in the wild, dedicated to the goddess, a virgin warrior who rejected marriage for hunting and war. Diana loves her but cannot save her‚ÄîCamilla is fated to die today.

Camilla in Battle

Camilla leads the Latin cavalry with spectacular success. She kills enemy after enemy‚Äîthe poem dwells on her speed, skill, and beauty. She is a Roman Amazon, embodying fierce Italian martial virtue.

Fatal Attraction

Camilla sees a Trojan priest in gilded armour and pursues him through the battle‚Äîdesire for his spoils blinds her to danger. The Etruscan Arruns stalks her, praying to Apollo for the kill. His spear strikes her breast.

Death and Vengeance

Camilla dies with her women around her, sending a last message to Turnus: he must take her place. Diana's Opis kills Arruns in revenge‚Äîbut Camilla remains dead. The Latins rout; Turnus abandons his ambush and rushes back.

Symbolic Meaning

Camilla represents Italian virtue at its finest‚Äîand shows its destruction. Her fatal pursuit of spoils mirrors Turnus's fatal taking of Pallas's belt. She is magnificent but finally human, killed by the greed for glory that drives all warriors.
