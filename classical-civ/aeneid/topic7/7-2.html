7.2 Camilla's Aristeia

üìö OCR A-Level Classical Civilisation ‚è±Ô∏è 45 min üìñ Virgil's Aeneid

The Warrior Maiden

Camilla is the last great Italian hero. A virgin warrior devoted to Diana, she dominates the battlefield before dying from a fatal error. She represents Italian excellence destroyed by the same flaw that kills Turnus: greed for glory.

What Is An Aristeia?
From Greek "aristos" (best) - the moment when a hero displays supreme excellence, killing multiple enemies spectacularly. Homer gives Diomedes, Patroclus, and Achilles aristeias. Virgil gives Camilla hers - then immediately kills her. Epic glory followed by tragic death.

Why Camilla Matters for Exams

Gender: How does Virgil portray a female warrior? Admirably? Critically? This lets you discuss women in epic.

Tragic pattern: Her fatal mistake (pursuing spoils) mirrors Turnus's (taking Pallas's belt). One moment of greed destroys a lifetime of virtue.

Quick Overview

Who She Is

Daughter of exiled King Metabus. Raised in wilderness. Dedicated to Diana as infant. Trained in hunting/warfare. Rejected marriage for virginity/battle. Leads Volscian cavalry. Dies pursuing a priest's golden armor.

What Makes Her Remarkable

  • Only major female warrior in Aeneid
  • Commands male warriors' respect
  • Gets full aristeia sequence
  • Dies heroically, not shamefully
  • Associated with virgin goddess Diana

Literary Models

  • Amazons (Greek warrior women)
  • Penthesilea (Amazon at Troy)
  • Atalanta (virgin huntress)
  • Diana/Artemis herself
  • Virgil invents her (not in earlier sources)

Diana's Flashback: Origin Story

Before Camilla fights, Diana tells her nymph Opis the backstory. This humanizes Camilla and makes her death more tragic.

"Her father Metabus, fleeing his kingdom, carried infant Camilla through the forest. At a raging river, unable to cross, he bound her to his spear and hurled her across, praying to me. I accepted. She grew wild on mare's milk, trained in the hunt, spurning marriage to keep her virginity for me and warfare."
‚Äî Diana, Aeneid 11.539-566 (adapted)

The Key Details

  • Metabus = tyrant, exiled by his people
  • Fled with baby Camilla (chose her over safety)
  • At river Amasenus: tied her to spear, threw her across - dedicated her to Diana
  • Diana accepted - Camilla is divinely chosen
  • Raised in wilderness - no civilization, pure nature
  • Trained in hunting/warfare from childhood
  • Rejected all marriage - devoted to Diana/battle

What This Tells Us

Camilla's origin is WILD - literally. Not a city warrior but a creature of nature. Raised by a fugitive father with no mother, no female socialization. Learned hunting/fighting before domestic arts.

Her dedication to Diana isn't metaphorical - her father literally threw her across a river as an offering. The goddess accepted. Camilla owes her life to divine intervention.

She chose warfare over marriage. In Roman culture, a woman's role was wife/mother. Camilla rejects this explicitly. Virgil presents this as admirable, not deviant.

Why Diana Can't Save Her

Diana loves Camilla - calls her "dearest" of followers. But even gods can't override fate. Diana knows Camilla will die today. All she can do is promise vengeance. Like Thetis and Achilles: divine parent, mortal beloved, inevitable death.

The Aristeia: Combat Dominance

Camilla's battle sequence is spectacular. Virgil catalogs her kills with the same detail given male heroes. She's not a token female - she's a legitimate epic warrior.

Her Major Kills (11.597-835)

  • Eunaeus: Spear through chest - first kill, combat begins
  • Liris & Pagasus: Double kill - one thrown, one stabbed helping
  • Hippotas, Tereus, Harpalycus, Demophoon, Chromis: Rapid sequence showing speed
  • Orsilochus, Butes, Amastrus: Virgil lingers on details - armor, wounds, deaths
  • Ornytus: Killed while wearing his armor - hunter becomes hunted
"She moves like a whirlwind through Trojan ranks. One bare breast exposed for battle, quiver rattling, missiles flying. Her strong right hand wields the battle-axe; her shoulder bears Diana's golden bow. Even retreating, she bends her bow backward and shoots while fleeing."
‚Äî Aeneid 11.648-654 (adapted)

Combat Techniques

  • Speed: "whirlwind" - faster than men can track
  • Amazon image: "one bare breast" - Greek Amazon iconography
  • Multiple weapons: Spear, axe, bow - versatile fighter
  • Parthian shot: Shoots backward while retreating - advanced cavalry technique
  • Diana's weapons: Golden bow (divine gift)
  • Leads from front: Surrounded by women companions (Larina, Tulla, Tarpeia)

Fatal Error: Greed for Spoils

At peak success, Camilla sees Chloreus - a Trojan priest in spectacular golden armor. She pursues him alone through the entire battlefield, abandoning strategy. This obsession kills her.

"Camilla, with a woman's desire for plunder and spoils, pursued him alone through the battle line, reckless, burning with passion to strip his armor and wear his golden weapons."
‚Äî Aeneid 11.778-782

The Controversial Line

"femineo praedae amore" = "with a woman's love of plunder"

Does Virgil blame her gender? Debated. Some say yes (women = naturally greedy). Others say no ("woman's" just describes the type of desire - for beautiful objects vs. glory).

Male warriors do this too: Achilles (Briseis), Turnus (Pallas's belt). But they usually survive it. Does Camilla die for what men get away with? That's the question.

Arruns: The Cowardly Killer

  • While Camilla pursues Chloreus, Arruns (Etruscan) stalks HER
  • Prays to Apollo: "Let me kill her, I'll seek no glory"
  • Throws spear - strikes exposed breast beneath armor
  • Camilla dies. Arruns flees, terrified of vengeance
  • Diana's Opis hunts and kills Arruns - but Camilla stays dead

The Irony

Arruns doesn't want glory - unusual for a warrior. He just wants to survive. Yet he kills Italy's greatest hero. Unheroic cowardice defeats heroic excellence. War doesn't reward virtue - it's random, cruel, indifferent.

Death Scene

Camilla dies surrounded by her women companions. Tender, personal - different from male deaths.

"Her companions caught her falling. Acca, most faithful, stayed beside her. Camilla spoke with failing breath: 'I fought as long as I could. Now death overwhelms me. Go to Turnus - tell him to take my place and drive the Trojans from the city. Farewell.' She released the reins, slipping unwillingly to earth. Cold, conquered by death, her life fled groaning to the shades below."
‚Äî Aeneid 11.820-835 (adapted)

Why This Death Matters

Female companionship: Dies among women - her chosen family

Last words purely tactical: Not personal (no father/Diana/glory) - just "tell Turnus to defend." Duty to the end

"Unwillingly to earth": She doesn't want to die - tragic loss, not noble sacrifice

"Conquered by death": Death is the only thing that defeats her. No man did

Prefigures Turnus: Both die from ONE fatal error (spoils/belt). Both represent Italian excellence. Both losses are tragic waste.
