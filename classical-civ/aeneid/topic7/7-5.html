7.5 Literary Techniques in Books 11 & 12

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and analyse key literary techniques in Books 11 & 12, including Iliadic parallels, significant speeches, and closure techniques.

The Final Movement

Books 11-12 bring the epic to its close. Virgil deploys his full technical resources: Homeric allusion reaches its climax (the duel echoes Hector vs. Achilles), speeches carry thematic weight, and the abrupt ending itself becomes a technique for generating meaning.

Iliadic Parallels

The Final Duel

Aeneas pursuing Turnus around the walls mirrors Achilles pursuing Hector (Iliad 22). Turnus's sword breaking, his failed boulder throw, his final plea‚Äîall echo Hector's doom. This parallel elevates the scene: readers recognise epic's greatest duel replayed.

Differences Matter

But Virgil changes key elements: Hector was deceived by Athena; Turnus knows his sister has left him. Achilles refused ransom but knew he'd be punished; Aeneas hesitates at mercy then kills in fury. The differences highlight what Virgil adds: greater moral complexity.

Priam's Appeal

Turnus's plea‚Äîmentioning his aged father‚Äîechoes Priam's appeal to Achilles in Iliad 24. Achilles was moved, wept, returned Hector's body. Aeneas is also moved‚Äîbut then kills. The failed echo of mercy is devastating: Rome's founder does what Achilles, supposedly less civilised, did not.

Functional Allusion

These Homeric parallels are not mere decoration. They create expectation (we expect mercy after Priam-like appeals), then violate it. The gap between model and copy generates meaning. Virgil uses Homer to say something Homer didn't.

Key Speeches

Drances vs. Turnus (Book 11)

The debate speech is a Roman political technique: contio speeches, senatorial rhetoric. Drances attacks Turnus with insinuation and sarcasm; Turnus responds with proud defiance. The contrast characterises both: Drances the coward with words, Turnus the warrior with pride. But Drances's arguments have force‚Äîthe war is costly and Turnus bears responsibility.

Evander's Lament

Evander's speech over Pallas (11.152-81) combines grief and revenge. He wishes he had died instead; he blames Turnus; he calls on Aeneas to kill Turnus. This speech provides moral justification for the ending‚Äîa father's charge demands fulfillment.

Jupiter and Juno (Book 12)

The divine negotiation resolves cosmic conflict through bargain. Jupiter's terms are blunt; Juno's acceptance is grudging. The scene shows how divine will operates through compromise‚Äîeven gods cannot have everything. The outcome (Trojan name lost, Latin customs preserved) determines Roman identity.

Turnus's Final Plea

Turnus's last words are measured and moving: he acknowledges defeat; he appeals to Aeneas as one who also has a father; he asks only for his body to be returned. The speech creates maximum sympathy at the moment of death‚Äîtechnique ensuring the killing is felt as tragedy.

Closure Techniques

The Dira

The Dira (Fury/death-bird) sent by Jupiter is a closure device: it marks Turnus as doomed, drives off Juturna (removing divine protection), and drains Turnus's strength. The supernatural signal indicates the fight is now to the death‚Äîno escape possible.

The Belt

Pallas's belt reappears as catalyst. Throughout the poem, objects carry meaning across time: the belt taken in Book 10 becomes the trigger in Book 12. This token connects the deaths, makes Aeneas's action retrospective (avenging the past), and provides the ending's emotional logic.

Aeneas's Silence

After Turnus's plea, Aeneas speaks once‚Äîexplaining why he kills‚Äîthen acts. The speech names Pallas; the action is silent. This economy intensifies the moment: no extended debate, no drawn-out death. Violence is quick and final.

The Final Lines

"His limbs went slack with cold, and his life fled groaning to the shades below" (ast illi solvuntur frigore membra / vitaque cum gemitu fugit indignata sub umbras). The lines echo Patroclus's death in the Iliad‚Äîbut there the poem continued. Here it ends. The echo without continuation creates interpretive space.

The Abrupt Ending
The ending's abruptness is itself a technique. We expect resolution‚Äîwedding, founding, peace. We get none. The absence generates meaning: perhaps peace cannot be shown because it hasn't yet been achieved; perhaps violence is what actually founds states; perhaps Virgil wants us disturbed. The technique of non-closure forces interpretation.
