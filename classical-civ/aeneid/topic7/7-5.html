7.5 The Controversial Ending

The Final Lines: Aeneas's Choice

Turnus has begged for mercy. Aeneas hesitates, moved by the plea. Then he sees Pallas's belt on Turnus's shoulder. Rage overwhelms him. He drives his sword into Turnus. The poem ends on Turnus's death. No celebration. No resolution. Just death and the abrupt word "END."

Why This Ending Is Controversial
For 2,000 years, readers have debated: Is Aeneas RIGHT to kill Turnus? Does he fulfill pietas (duty to Evander, to Pallas) or violate it (refusing mercy, giving in to furor)? Virgil doesn't tell you. The ambiguity is DELIBERATE. This makes it perfect for exam essays.
"Aeneas stood, fierce in arms, rolling his eyes, and checked his hand. And now, more and more, the speech was beginning to sway him - when high on Turnus's shoulder appeared the accursed belt of the boy Pallas, shining with its familiar studs, whom Turnus had struck down and laid low. Now he wore the spoils, a memorial of cruel grief. Aeneas, drinking deep of the sight of this plunder, this memorial of savage pain, terrible in his fury, blazing with rage, cried out: 'Are you, wearing the spoils of one of mine, to be snatched from me? Pallas sacrifices you with this wound, Pallas!' And saying this, he buried his sword deep in that breast, burning with rage. His limbs went slack and cold; his life fled groaning with resentment to the shades below."

Why These Lines Matter

"checked his hand": Aeneas WAS going to spare him - mercy was winning

"more and more... beginning to sway him": Turnus's speech was WORKING. Pietas (pity for Daunus) was overcoming anger

"when... appeared the belt": ONE visual detail reverses everything

"terrible in his fury, blazing with rage": This is FUROR - the irrational rage Aeneas has fought against the whole epic

"Pallas sacrifices you": Aeneas becomes the instrument of vengeance, acting for the dead

"burning with rage": Repeated emphasis on furor - the final act is ANGER, not reason

"life fled... with resentment": Turnus dies angry, not reconciled - no closure

Pallas's Belt: The Symbol That Destroys Mercy

The belt is NOT incidental. Virgil has been building toward this moment since Book 10, when Turnus killed Pallas and took the belt as spoils. Now that belt becomes the reason Turnus dies.

What We Know About the Belt

  • Described in Book 10: "A richly worked belt with designs of murder - fifty young sons killed on their wedding night" (mythological scene: the Danaids)
  • Taken as spoils: Turnus stripped it from Pallas's corpse after killing him
  • Worn as trophy: Turnus wears it into the final duel - visible proof of his victory over Pallas
  • Visual trigger: Aeneas sees it and remembers Pallas's death, Evander's grief
  • Symbol of fatal error: Like Camilla pursuing spoils, Turnus's greed for trophies kills him

Why the Belt Matters Symbolically

Ironic decoration: The belt shows murder (Danaids killing bridegrooms). Turnus wears a symbol of killing to his own death

Reminder of duty: Aeneas promised Evander he'd protect Pallas. Belt = broken promise, failed pietas

Proof of Turnus's character: He took spoils from a dead boy - dishonorable, cruel

Visual vs verbal: Turnus's WORDS appeal to mercy. Belt's visual presence overwhelms words with memory

Fate's instrument: If Turnus hadn't taken the belt, he might have lived. One act of greed seals his doom

The Two Main Interpretations

Scholars have debated this ending for two millennia. There are TWO main readings, both well-supported by the text.

OPTIMISTIC: Aeneas Is Right

  • Pietas fulfilled: He avenges Pallas, honors his promise to Evander
  • Justice served: Turnus killed Pallas; he deserves death
  • Roman virtue: Vengeance for allies is duty, not vice
  • Pallas demands it: "Pallas sacrifices you" - acting for the dead is pious
  • Mercy would dishonor Pallas: Letting his killer live insults the dead
  • Turnus wore the belt: Flaunting spoils = no remorse, doesn't deserve mercy
  • Establishes Roman power: Shows Rome will punish its enemies

PESSIMISTIC: Aeneas Fails

  • Furor defeats pietas: "blazing with rage" - succumbs to the passion he's fought all epic
  • Supplication violated: Refusing a suppliant's plea violates Roman custom
  • Mercy is Roman virtue: Clementia (mercy to defeated) defines great leaders
  • Turnus was right: Appealing to Anchises/Daunus parallel SHOULD have worked
  • No future shown: Epic ends on death, not foundation/marriage - implies failure
  • Abrupt ending: No resolution, celebration, or peace - just killing
  • Becomes Achilles: Aeneas acts like Greek rage-hero, not Roman pius founder
The Third Option: Tragic Ambiguity
Some scholars argue BOTH readings are true simultaneously. Aeneas is RIGHT (fulfills duty to Pallas) AND WRONG (violates mercy). This is tragic: there's no good choice. Kill Turnus = betray clementia. Spare Turnus = betray Pallas/Evander. Either way, Aeneas loses something. This is Virgil's vision: human life requires impossible choices, and even "right" actions have costs.

How to Write About the Ending in Essays

The ending is exam GOLD because it's genuinely debatable. Here's how to approach it:

Essay Strategy

  • Acknowledge both sides: "Some scholars argue... while others contend..." (shows awareness)
  • Pick ONE interpretation: Don't fence-sit. Argue for optimistic OR pessimistic (or tragic ambiguity)
  • Support with evidence: Quote the final lines, cite earlier moments (Evander's lament, Aeneas's furor in Book 10)
  • Address counterarguments: "While it's true that... nevertheless..." (sophisticated analysis)
  • Connect to broader themes: Pietas vs furor, fate vs free will, individual vs collective good

Key Evidence to Use

For "Aeneas is right": Book 11 (Evander's grief), Book 10 (promise to protect Pallas), Roman values of vengeance for allies, Jupiter's decree that Turnus must die

For "Aeneas fails": Book 1 (Jupiter's prophecy of Rome bringing peace), Book 6 (Anchises: "spare the conquered"), repeated emphasis on clementia as Roman virtue, supplication customs

For "tragic ambiguity": Virgil's use of furor language (negative) combined with Pallas language (justified), "checked his hand" (shows hesitation = internal conflict), abrupt ending (no authorial judgment)

The Ultimate Exam Point
Whichever interpretation you choose, emphasize this: Virgil DELIBERATELY leaves it ambiguous. The lack of authorial judgment IS the point. He's forcing readers to grapple with the moral complexity of founding Rome. There's no easy answer - and that's what makes the Aeneid a masterpiece, not propaganda.
