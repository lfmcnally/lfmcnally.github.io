Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will have detailed knowledge of Book 9's narrative, understand its major episodes (the night raid, the ship transformation, the wall breach), and recognise its focus on young warriors.

Book 9: War Without Aeneas

Turnus Attacks

Divine Intervention

Juno sends Iris to tell Turnus: Aeneas is away; the camp is vulnerable; attack now. Turnus needs no urging‚Äîhe advances with his full force. The absence of Aeneas creates crisis and allows Turnus to shine as warrior.

The Ships Transformed

Turnus orders the Trojan fleet burned. But these ships were built from Cybele's sacred pines on Mt Ida. Jupiter, honouring Cybele's request, transforms the ships into sea-nymphs‚Äîthey dive beneath the waves and swim away. This miracle preserves divine favour while denying Trojans easy escape.

Siege Established

Unable to burn the ships or storm the walls, Turnus besieges the camp. The Trojans, following Aeneas's orders, refuse to fight in the open. This defensive posture frustrates Turnus‚Äîhe wants decisive battle‚Äîbut Trojan discipline holds.

Turnus's Aristeia

Though the siege frustrates him, Turnus dominates Book 9. He is magnificent, fearless, irresistible. Virgil portrays him at his heroic best‚Äîmaking his eventual defeat more tragic. Even the enemy is shown glory before fall.

Nisus and Euryalus

The Pair

Nisus is older, experienced. Euryalus is young, beautiful, beloved. Their relationship echoes Achilles and Patroclus, though the nature of their bond is more explicit: Nisus loves Euryalus with passionate devotion.

The Plan

The Trojans need to reach Aeneas with news of the siege. Nisus proposes a night raid through enemy lines. Euryalus insists on joining. Despite misgivings, Nisus cannot refuse. The commanders bless them; Ascanius promises rewards; they set out into darkness.

The Raid

They find the enemy camp asleep after celebrating. Nisus and Euryalus slaughter sleeping men‚Äîthe violence is described graphically. But Euryalus lingers to take spoils, including a glittering helmet. This cupido laudis (desire for glory) proves fatal.

Discovery and Death

Enemy cavalry spots the helmet's glint. Euryalus is captured. Nisus, who had escaped, returns to rescue him. He kills two enemies but cannot save Euryalus‚Äîthe Rutulians kill the youth. Nisus throws himself on the killers and dies on his friend's body.

Fortunati ambo! si quid mea carmina possunt, nulla dies umquam memori vos eximet aevo...

The Poet's Intrusion
Virgil breaks his narrative to address Nisus and Euryalus directly, promising them immortal fame. This rare authorial intrusion signals the episode's emotional importance. The young warriors' death embodies war's cost to youthful beauty and love.

Ascanius and the Battle

Euryalus's Mother

Euryalus's mother learns of her son's death. Her lament is the poem's most concentrated expression of maternal grief: "Could you leave me, cruel one?" Her collapse halts the battle‚Äîeven warriors are moved. She is carried away, broken.

Ascanius's First Kill

Young Ascanius (Iulus) makes his first kill, shooting the arrogant Numanus who had mocked the Trojans as "twice-conquered Phrygians." Apollo approves but tells the boy to withdraw: "Henceforth refrain from war, boy." Ascanius's future is peace, not combat.

Turnus Breaches the Wall

Turnus fires a gate tower and storms through the breach. He rampages inside the camp, killing Trojans. But in his battle-fury he forgets to open the gates for his army. He fights alone, eventually escaping by diving into the Tiber.

Turnus's Heroic Flaw

Turnus's failure to open the gates shows his fatal limitation: he fights for personal glory, not strategic victory. His furor makes him magnificent but self-defeating. Had he thought collectively, the war might have ended here.

Book 9's Themes
Book 9 focuses on young warriors: Nisus and Euryalus, Ascanius, the many who die. It shows war's cost to youth, the limits of individual heroism (Turnus), and the importance of collective discipline. Aeneas's absence proves the Trojans can survive‚Äîbut at great price.
