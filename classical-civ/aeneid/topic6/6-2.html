Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will have detailed knowledge of Book 10, including the council of gods, Pallas's death, Aeneas's revenge, and the Mezentius-Lausus episode.

Book 10: Return and Revenge

Aeneas returns with Etruscan allies as the Trojans face destruction. The book's central tragedy is Pallas's death at Turnus's hands‚Äîthe event that seals Turnus's doom. Aeneas's grief-driven slaughter and the Mezentius-Lausus deaths complete the book's exploration of war's human cost.

The Council of Gods

Divine Assembly

Jupiter summons the gods: why are they warring through mortals? He wanted peace between Trojans and Italians. Venus complains of Juno's persecution. Juno counters: why do Trojans deserve Italy? Each goddess blames the other.

Jupiter's Decree

Jupiter refuses to arbitrate. He declares divine neutrality: let fate find its way without divine interference. "The Fates will find their path" (fata viam invenient). Both goddesses must cease intervention‚Äîthough neither fully obeys.

Theological Implications

This council raises questions about fate and divine will. Is fate Jupiter's will or something even he cannot control? Are the gods responsible for mortal suffering? The poem doesn't resolve these questions‚Äîthe theodicy remains troubling.

Narrative Function

The council provides epic dignity (gods debating mortal affairs) while explaining why neither side receives decisive divine help. The war must be won by human effort‚Äîthough divine agents continue to intervene at crisis moments.

The Death of Pallas

Aeneas's Return

Aeneas arrives with thirty ships of Etruscan allies. His armour gleams; his shield blazes. The sea-nymphs (transformed ships) tell him of the siege. He lands, and battle joins. The Trojans are saved‚Äîbut at terrible cost.

Pallas in Battle

Young Pallas, Evander's son, fights brilliantly. Aeneas had promised to protect him; Evander had entrusted his only son. But war separates them. Turnus, divinely prompted, seeks out Pallas for single combat.

The Duel

Pallas faces Turnus knowing he will likely die‚Äîbut he will not flee. His spear grazes Turnus; Turnus's spear pierces Pallas's chest. The youth falls. Turnus stands over him, mocking: "Carry this message to Evander: I return Pallas as he deserved."

The Belt

Turnus strips Pallas's sword-belt, depicting the Danaids' crime (murdering husbands). This spoliation seals Turnus's fate: in Book 12, seeing this belt on Turnus, Aeneas will refuse mercy. The belt becomes the token of vengeance.

Nescia mens hominum fati sortisque futurae et servare modum rebus sublata secundis!

The mind of men, ignorant of fate and future fortune, and unable to keep measure when lifted by success!
‚Äî Virgil on Turnus, 10.501-502
Turnus's Fatal Error
Virgil comments directly: Turnus doesn't know what he does. Taking the belt brings nemesis. This narratorial intervention ensures readers understand: Turnus's triumphant spoliation will destroy him. His present glory contains future doom.

Lausus and Mezentius

Aeneas's Fury

Learning of Pallas's death, Aeneas explodes in grief and rage. He slaughters enemies without mercy, taking prisoners for sacrifice (a dark act, recalling Achilles' treatment of Trojans after Patroclus's death). His pietas warps into furious vengeance.

Juno's Rescue

Juno, seeing Turnus about to face the raging Aeneas, creates a phantom Aeneas. Turnus chases it onto a ship, which carries him away from battle. He survives‚Äîbut dishonoured, tricked by divine intervention into apparent flight.

Mezentius's Aristeia

Mezentius, the exiled Etruscan tyrant, takes Turnus's place as champion. Despite his impiety and cruelty, he fights magnificently. Aeneas wounds him; he retreats, leaning on his son Lausus.

Lausus's Sacrifice

Lausus interposes himself between Aeneas and his wounded father. He fights bravely but cannot match Aeneas, who kills him. Immediately, Aeneas feels pity: "What can I give you worthy of such deeds? Your devotion to your father‚ÄîI can understand that."

Mezentius's Death

Mezentius, learning of his son's death, returns to fight. He knows he will die; he welcomes it. Aeneas kills him. Mezentius's last words ask only to be buried with his son. The monster becomes human in death.

Pietas and Violence
The Mezentius-Lausus episode explores pietas in extreme form: Lausus dies protecting an unworthy father; Mezentius dies of grief for his son. Even villains love and are loved. Aeneas, who killed Lausus for fighting his father, understands: he too is a son who loved a father. Violence and humanity intertwine.
