6.3 Turnus and Pallas: Characterisation

üìö Topic 6: Books 9 & 10‚è±Ô∏è 35 minüìä Character Analysis

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Virgil characterises Turnus and Pallas, recognise the thematic significance of their duel, and analyse how their fates illuminate the epic's central concerns.

Two Tragic Figures

Turnus and Pallas are both young warriors whose fates intertwine tragically. Pallas, the promising son of Evander, dies at Turnus's hands. Turnus, the magnificent Italian champion, seals his doom by killing Pallas and taking his belt. Both are sympathetic; both are destroyed.

Turnus: The Tragic Antagonist

Homeric Model

Turnus combines elements of Achilles and Hector. Like Achilles, he is the greatest warrior on his side, driven by anger and pride. Like Hector, he defends his homeland against invaders. This dual model creates sympathy: Turnus is not simply villain.

Legitimate Grievance

Turnus has real cause for anger: Lavinia was promised to him; the Trojans are invaders; his people face foreign domination. Unlike Juno's cosmic spite, Turnus's opposition stems from understandable human motives. He fights for his country and his honour.

Furor

But Turnus is marked by furor‚Äîthe maddened rage that contrasts with Aeneas's pietas. Allecto inflames him; his own nature responds to inflammation. His anger blinds judgment: in Book 9, he fails to open the gates; in Book 10, he takes Pallas's belt without thinking of consequences.

Magnificence and Excess

Turnus is magnificent: "like Mars himself" crossing the battlefield. His aristeia in Book 9 shows epic heroism at its finest. But his excellence exceeds proper limits. He boasts over Pallas's corpse; he mocks the dying. His greatness lacks measure.

Tragic Awareness

By Book 12, Turnus recognises his doom. His conversation with Juturna shows a man who knows he will die but cannot choose otherwise. He is trapped by his nature, his honour, and fate. This awareness elevates him from villain to tragic hero.

Pallas: Youth Destroyed

Introduction in Book 8

We first meet Pallas when Aeneas arrives at Pallanteum. He is young, brave, eager‚Äîriding out to challenge the strangers before knowing they are friends. His father Evander entrusts him to Aeneas: "teach him to endure hardship and war."

Evander's Farewell

Evander's farewell is poignant: he is old, Pallas is his only son, he may never see him again. "If you, Fortune, are preparing some terrible blow, let me die now." This speech foreshadows Pallas's death and marks its human cost.

Courage in Battle

Pallas fights brilliantly in Book 10, rallying his forces, killing enemies. He shows the martial excellence his father hoped for. But excellence attracts attention‚ÄîTurnus seeks him out for the glory of defeating Aeneas's prot√©g√©.

The Duel with Turnus

Pallas knows Turnus is his superior. He prays to Hercules for help; Hercules weeps but cannot intervene‚ÄîJupiter forbids it. Pallas attacks anyway. He wounds Turnus but Turnus's spear is fatal. Courage cannot overcome divine destiny.

Symbol of War's Cost

Pallas represents young promise destroyed by war. His death devastates Aeneas (who had pledged to protect him) and Evander (who loses his only son). The beauty and potential destroyed by warfare is concentrated in his death.

Comparison: Two Fates Intertwined

Victor and Victim

Turnus kills Pallas in Book 10; Aeneas kills Turnus in Book 12. The poem's arc connects these deaths. Pallas's death demands vengeance; Turnus's death provides it. But both deaths are tragic‚Äîboth young men die before their time.

The Belt

Pallas's sword-belt, depicting the Danaids' crime, passes from Pallas to Turnus. When Aeneas sees it on Turnus at the end, it reignites his fury. The belt is a physical token of connection: Turnus carries Pallas's death with him.

Different Heroisms

Pallas embodies brave submission to fate‚Äîhe knows Turnus is stronger but fights anyway, trusting the gods. Turnus embodies defiant resistance‚Äîhe fights against destiny, magnificent but doomed. Both are heroic; the poem does not privilege one over the other.

Fathers and Sons

Both are beloved sons. Evander loses Pallas; Daunus loses Turnus. The fathers' grief frames the young men's deaths. War destroys families‚Äîthe poem's sympathy extends to all bereaved parents, Italian and Trojan.

The Tragedy of Heroism
Neither Turnus nor Pallas is simply hero or villain. Pallas is brave but doomed; Turnus is magnificent but excessive. Their linked deaths show war's cost to both sides. Virgil's sympathy for the defeated (Turnus, Pallas, Mezentius, Lausus) complicates any triumphalist reading. Victory comes with grief.
