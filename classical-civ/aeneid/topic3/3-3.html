3.3 Turnus - The Antagonist

üìö Topic 3: Characterisation ‚è±Ô∏è 40 min üìä Character Analysis

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will understand Turnus's role as the antagonist of the Aeneid, recognise his heroic qualities, analyse his parallels with Achilles, and evaluate the tragedy of his death.

Introduction: The Sympathetic Antagonist

Turnus is not a simple villain. He is the Rutulian prince betrothed to Lavinia before Aeneas's arrival. His opposition to the Trojans is legitimate‚Äîhe fights for his bride, his kingdom, and his honour. Yet he must be defeated for Rome to exist. This creates one of the epic's most profound tensions.

Who is Turnus?
Background: Prince of the Rutulians, promised Lavinia by King Latinus before an oracle demanded she marry a foreign prince (Aeneas).

Character: Young, handsome, fierce warrior with aristeia (individual excellence) comparable to Homeric heroes.

Divine opponent: Persecuted by Juno (who ultimately stirs him to war but later abandons him) and opposed by Jupiter's fate.
Why Turnus Matters
Turnus embodies the cost of Roman destiny. He is brave, passionate, and fighting for what he believes is rightfully his. His defeat shows that even justified resistance must be crushed for fate to unfold. The moral complexity of his death‚Äîand the manner of it‚Äîis central to interpreting the Aeneid.

Turnus's Heroic Qualities

Despite being the antagonist, Turnus displays many qualities that would make him the hero in a different epic. Virgil deliberately presents him as admirable to complicate our response.

Martial Excellence (Virtus)

Turnus is repeatedly described as the greatest warrior among the Italian forces:

  • Book 7: "Handsome beyond all others... outstanding in arms"‚Äîpraised even by the narrator
  • Book 9: His night raid and single-handed slaughter in the Trojan camp demonstrate terrifying prowess
  • Book 10: His aristeia‚Äîkilling Pallas‚Äîis presented with the same epic grandeur as Achilles's victories
  • Book 12: He faces Aeneas in single combat without hesitation, showing courage even when abandoned by Juno
Turnus, praecipuus forma, praecipuus armis.

Turnus, outstanding in appearance, outstanding in arms.
‚Äî Aeneid 7.783

Personal Courage

Facing overwhelming odds: In Book 9.126-158, Turnus fights alone inside the Trojan camp after his men fail to support him. He could flee but chooses to cut his way out through enemies.

Accepting the duel: Book 12 shows Turnus willing to settle the war through single combat, sparing his people further bloodshed.

Leadership and Loyalty

Inspires his forces: Turnus rallies the Italian tribes and leads from the front, never asking men to take risks he won't.

Grief for his men: When Pallas dies, Turnus honours him; when his own men fall, he feels genuine loss (contrast with Achilles's indifference to Greek casualties).

Connection to Roman Values
Ironically, Turnus displays virtus (manly courage) that Rome celebrated. He fights for honour, shows personal bravery, and accepts defeat with dignity (eventually). Yet he opposes fate and must be destroyed. This suggests Roman values alone aren't enough‚Äîsubmission to destiny is required.

Turnus and Achilles: Parallels and Contrasts

Virgil deliberately models Turnus on Achilles from Homer's Iliad, inviting comparison between the two warriors. These parallels highlight both Turnus's heroic stature and the differences between Homeric and Virgilian heroism.

Explicit Parallels

  • Aristeia (9-10): Like Achilles in Iliad 20-22, Turnus has extended battle sequences where he dominates the field
  • Killing a young warrior (10.491-509): Turnus kills Pallas just as Achilles kills Hector; both strip armour from their victims
  • Facing the protagonist (12): The Aeneas-Turnus duel mirrors the Achilles-Hector pursuit and combat
  • Withdrawal from battle (11-12): Turnus's temporary absence (wound, divine intervention) echoes Achilles's withdrawal in Iliad 1-9

The Fatal Baldric: A Homeric Echo

The most explicit Achilles parallel is Turnus wearing Pallas's baldric (shoulder belt) as spoils. This directly mirrors Achilles stripping Hector's armour‚Äîbut with a crucial difference:

Achilles: His desecration of Hector's body is excessive but doesn't cause his downfall.

Turnus: The baldric becomes the immediate cause of his death. When Aeneas sees it (12.941-952), his hesitation vanishes and he kills Turnus in fury.

The echo emphasizes how Virgil transforms Homeric motifs. The baldric makes Turnus's death seem like poetic justice‚Äîbut also more morally troubling, since Aeneas acts from passion, not justice.

Achilles: The Homeric Ideal

  • Fights for personal honour and glory (kleos)
  • Chooses fame over long life‚Äîindividual heroism is supreme
  • His wrath is central to the plot
  • Divine mother (Thetis) protects him
  • Dies young but achieves immortal fame
  • Acts from personal motives throughout

Turnus: The Virgilian Version

  • Fights for honour but also for legitimate claims (bride, kingdom)
  • His aristeia is impressive but doomed by fate
  • His furor (rage) is both strength and fatal flaw
  • Divine patron (Juno) abandons him
  • Dies to enable someone else's destiny
  • His personal excellence is irrelevant to fate's design
For Your Essays: Interpreting the Parallels
The Achilles-Turnus parallel invites us to ask: Is Turnus being presented as the "real hero" and Aeneas as less admirable? Or is Virgil showing that Homeric-style individual heroism is obsolete‚Äîthat fate and duty matter more than personal glory? Different critics read this differently, and the exam rewards thoughtful engagement with this ambiguity.

Turnus as Antagonist: Furor and Fate

Despite his heroic qualities, Turnus functions as the antagonist because he opposes fate. His defining characteristic is furor‚Äîthe uncontrolled passion that drives him to resist destiny.

How Turnus Becomes the Antagonist
Book 7 (170-285): Juno sends Allecto, a Fury, to inflame Turnus with madness and jealousy. The war is thus divinely caused‚Äîbut Turnus embraces it.

Book 7 (435-470): Turnus explicitly rejects peace and the oracle's guidance. He chooses war over accepting fate.

Books 9-12: Turnus's rage intensifies. He repeatedly refuses compromise, driven by honour, pride, and fury.

Furor: Turnus's Fatal Flaw

Furor (rage, madness, uncontrolled passion) is the opposite of pietas. Throughout Books 7-12, Turnus is associated with furor:

  • 7.460-466: Allecto's poison "burns in his chest"‚Äîfuror as physical contamination
  • 9.760: Turnus "burns with unspeakable fury" after nearly being trapped
  • 12.894-952: Even when wounded and defeated, Turnus's eyes blaze with rage

This furor makes him a formidable warrior but prevents him from accepting reality. Like Dido's furor, Turnus's rage is both divinely caused and embraced‚Äîmaking his moral status ambiguous.

Narrative Function
Structurally, Turnus must be defeated for the plot to reach its destined conclusion. But Virgil complicates this by making him sympathetic. His death feels necessary (fate demands it) and tragic (he didn't deserve it) simultaneously. This tension is intentional and central to the epic's meaning.

Legitimate Claims vs. Divine Will

From a human perspective, Turnus's position is entirely justified:

  • He was promised Lavinia before Aeneas arrived
  • The Trojans are foreign invaders of Italian land
  • He fights to defend his people and his honour
  • He offers single combat to spare both armies (12.11-17)

Yet divine will (fate) decrees he must lose. This creates the ethical problem: Is resistance to fate heroic defiance or hubristic folly? The Aeneid leaves this question disturbingly open.

Non me tua fervida terrent
dicta, ferox: di me terrent et Iuppiter hostis.

Your fiery words do not frighten me, fierce one: the gods frighten me, and Jupiter as my enemy.
‚Äî Aeneid 12.894-895 (Turnus's final words)

Key Moments in Turnus's Story

These five scenes are essential for understanding Turnus's character and should be prepared with specific textual evidence for exam questions.

1. Allecto's Infection (7.445-470)

The Fury Allecto throws a torch into Turnus's chest, filling him with rage. This scene establishes that his furor is divinely caused‚Äîbut the text also shows Turnus welcoming war: "Let them ask peace from me!" His pride cooperates with the divine madness.

2. Slaughter in the Camp (9.726-761)

Turnus breaks into the Trojan camp at night and kills dozens single-handedly. This aristeia showcases his martial prowess‚Äîbut also his bloodlust. When surrounded, he leaps into the Tiber rather than surrender, escaping miraculously.

3. Killing Pallas (10.439-509)

Turnus defeats and kills Pallas, young son of Evander and Aeneas's prot√©g√©. He strips Pallas's baldric as spoils. The scene is presented with tragic grandeur‚ÄîPallas's bravery, Turnus's mercy (quick death), but also Turnus's fatal pride in taking the trophy.

4. Juturna's Deception (12.614-696)

Turnus's sister Juturna, a nymph, disguises herself to break the duel's truce, reigniting general battle. Turnus doesn't recognize the deception, but he eagerly rejoins the fight‚Äîshowing how easily his furor overrides reason.

5. The Final Duel (12.887-952) ‚Äî The Most Important Scene

This is the climax of the entire epic and requires detailed analysis:

The Pursuit: Turnus flees Aeneas (echoing Hector fleeing Achilles), but not from cowardice‚Äîhis strength fails (divine intervention by Jupiter).

The Stone: Turnus tries to throw a huge boundary stone but can't‚Äîhis body betrays him. This symbolizes fate's physical power over human will.

The Plea: Wounded, Turnus makes a dignified speech acknowledging defeat and asking for mercy: "You have won; the Italians have seen me stretch out my hands in defeat; Lavinia is your bride. Do not press your hatred further."

Aeneas's Hesitation: For a moment, Aeneas considers mercy‚Äîthen sees Pallas's baldric on Turnus's shoulder.

The Killing: "Pallas sacrifices you with this blow!" Aeneas drives his sword into Turnus's chest. Turnus's life flees to the shades below.

‚ö†Ô∏è Exam Essential
The final scene is the single most discussed passage in the Aeneid. You must be able to quote from it and discuss multiple interpretations: Is Aeneas justified (avenging Pallas, serving fate)? Is he morally compromised (killing in furor, ignoring mercy)? Does Turnus deserve death (for his own furor) or pity (as fate's victim)? All positions are defensible with evidence.

The Tragic Dimension: Turnus's Death and Meaning

Turnus's death is tragic in the classical sense: a character with greatness (heroic qualities) is destroyed by a combination of fate, divine will, his own flaw (furor), and circumstances beyond his control. Understanding this tragedy is essential for sophisticated analysis.

Pro-Augustan Reading

Turnus must die: He represents furor, disorder, resistance to fate‚Äîeverything Rome must overcome to exist.

His death is just: He killed Pallas, refused peace, and fought against divine will. Aeneas correctly executes him.

His tragedy is necessary: Individual claims (however legitimate) must yield to collective destiny. Rome's foundation requires sacrifice.

Evidence: The epic consistently associates Turnus with furor (9.760, 12.894); his death allows the promised peace (12.791-842).

Pessimistic Reading

Turnus is unjustly killed: His claims are legitimate; he's a victim of divine caprice (Juno's manipulation) and fate's cruelty.

His death exposes pietas's cost: Aeneas becomes what he opposed‚Äîa man of furor‚Äîin the very moment of victory.

His tragedy indicts empire: Rome is founded on the destruction of the admirable. The epic ends in violence, not transcendence.

Evidence: Turnus's dignified final speech (12.931-938); Aeneas kills in rage, not justice (12.946-947); the epic ends with death, not celebration.

The Ending's Ambiguity

The Aeneid's final lines are crucial:

"ast illi solvuntur frigore membra
vitaque cum gemitu fugit indignata sub umbras."

"But his limbs dissolved in cold,
and his life fled with a groan, resentful, to the shades below."

Key words: frigore (cold‚Äîdeath, but also emotional coldness?), indignata (resentful/unworthy‚Äîis death unjust or is Turnus indignant?).

The epic ends on Turnus's death, not Rome's founding. This has troubled readers for 2,000 years. Why end with the antagonist's tragedy rather than the protagonist's triumph?

Connections to Dido
Turnus's death mirrors Dido's in significant ways: both are victims of fate, both display furor, both are sympathetically portrayed, and both die resenting their fate (Dido curses; Turnus's life is "indignata"). The pattern suggests Rome is built on the destruction of the worthy‚Äîan uncomfortable foundation myth.

Synthesizing the Interpretations

The strongest essay responses don't choose one interpretation exclusively but acknowledge the tension:

  • Turnus's death is both necessary (fate demands it) and tragic (he didn't deserve it morally)
  • Aeneas acts both rightly (avenging Pallas, serving destiny) and wrongly (killing in furor, not piety)
  • The epic both celebrates Rome's foundation and mourns what was destroyed to achieve it

This complexity is what makes the Aeneid profound. Simple readings miss Virgil's sophistication.

Exam Strategy: Turnus Questions
For "Is Turnus a sympathetic character?" or "How does Virgil present Turnus?" questions: (1) Establish his heroic qualities with textual evidence; (2) Explain his furor and role as antagonist; (3) Discuss the Achilles parallel; (4) Analyse his death scene in detail; (5) Conclude by addressing both pro-Augustan and pessimistic readings, showing the ambiguity is intentional. This demonstrates sophisticated understanding.
