3.1 Aeneas - The Conflicted Hero

üìö Topic 3: Characterisation ‚è±Ô∏è 60 min üìñ Virgil's Aeneid

Understanding Aeneas: Rome's Reluctant Founder

Aeneas is NOT like Achilles or Odysseus. He's not the strongest warrior or the cleverest hero. What makes him special‚Äîand what makes him DIFFICULT‚Äîis that he's defined by duty, not by personal glory. He's the man who keeps going even when he doesn't want to.

Why Aeneas Matters for Exams
OCR requires you to understand "characterisation" and "pietas" in depth. Aeneas embodies both. He's the protagonist of ALL prescribed books (2, 4, 6, 10, 12), so you MUST know him inside out. Every essay about themes, divine intervention, fate, or leadership connects back to him.

The genius of Virgil's characterisation is that Aeneas is SYMPATHETIC but also FRUSTRATING. He makes you uncomfortable. He abandons Dido. He kills suppliants. He's often passive and uncertain. Yet he's supposed to be the model Roman hero‚Äîand somehow, he is.

Arma virumque cano, Troiae qui primus ab oris
Italiam fato profugus Laviniaque venit
litora‚Äîmultum ille et terris iactatus et alto
vi superum, saevae memorem Iunonis ob iram...
‚Äî Aeneid 1.1-4 (I sing of arms and the man who first from Troy's shores, exiled by fate, came to Italy and Lavinian coasts‚Äîmuch buffeted on land and sea by violence of the gods, because of cruel Juno's unforgetting wrath...)

What the Opening Tells Us

  • "profugus": Exile/refugee‚ÄîAeneas is displaced, not triumphant
  • "fato": By fate‚Äîhe doesn't CHOOSE this journey; it's imposed
  • "iactatus": Buffeted/tossed about‚Äîhe's a victim of forces beyond his control
  • "vi superum": Violence of the gods‚Äîdivine powers use him for their purposes
  • "saevae memorem Iunonis ob iram": Juno's anger‚Äîone goddess's grudge shapes his entire life

Why This Opening Is Revolutionary

Compare this to Homer's openings. The Iliad starts with ANGER (Achilles' rage). The Odyssey starts with a MAN OF MANY TURNS (Odysseus's cleverness). But the Aeneid starts with FATE and SUFFERING.

Aeneas isn't angry like Achilles or resourceful like Odysseus. He's enduring. He survives. He continues. That's his heroism‚Äîand it's much less glamorous than Greek heroes.

The Central Problem: A Passive Protagonist?

One of the biggest criticisms of Aeneas is that he's PASSIVE. Things happen TO him more than he makes them happen. Gods give him orders. Fate pushes him forward. He often seems like a puppet of destiny rather than an active agent.

Evidence He's Passive

  • Venus has to rescue him in battle (Book 2)
  • Mercury has to wake him up in Carthage (Book 4)
  • He needs Sibyl's guidance in underworld (Book 6)
  • He hesitates before killing Turnus (Book 12)
  • He weeps constantly and expresses uncertainty

Counter-Evidence: He's Active

  • Organises Troy's defence during fall (Book 2)
  • Rescues his father and son from burning Troy
  • Makes the hard choice to leave Dido (Book 4)
  • Volunteers for dangerous katabasis (Book 6)
  • Fights ferociously when Pallas dies (Books 10-12)

Exam Strategy

Don't just pick a side. The BEST essays acknowledge BOTH aspects. Aeneas is passive in that fate drives him‚Äîbut active in that he CHOOSES to obey fate despite his personal desires. The tension between these creates his character's depth.

Pietas: Aeneas's Defining Virtue

If you learn ONE WORD for the Aeneid, make it pietas. It's Aeneas's essential quality‚Äîwhat makes him the ideal Roman hero. But it's NOT a simple concept.

What Pietas Means

  • Duty to the gods: Respecting divine will, performing rituals, obeying divine commands
  • Duty to family: Protecting and honouring parents, caring for children, maintaining family line
  • Duty to community/state: Putting the collective good above personal happiness
  • Respect for fate: Accepting what must be, even when it hurts
  • Self-control: Suppressing personal emotions and desires for higher purpose

Pietas is usually translated as "duty" or "piety," but it's bigger than both. It's about recognising that you're part of something larger than yourself‚Äîfamily, nation, cosmic order‚Äîand acting accordingly, even at great personal cost.

...insignem pietate virum...
‚Äî Aeneid 1.10 (a man outstanding in pietas)

Virgil calls Aeneas "pius Aeneas" (pious/dutiful Aeneas) more than 20 times. It's his standing epithet‚Äîlike "swift-footed Achilles" or "grey-eyed Athene." His identity IS pietas.

Why Pietas Makes Him Different

Greek heroes prioritise personal glory (kleos). Achilles chooses death for eternal fame. Odysseus tricks and schemes to get home. But Aeneas prioritises DUTY over both glory and personal desire.

He doesn't WANT to leave Troy. He doesn't WANT to leave Dido. He doesn't particularly WANT to found Rome. But he does it all anyway‚Äîbecause pietas demands it. This makes him the model Roman, not the model Greek hero.

The Cost of Pietas

Here's where Virgil gets interesting: he shows you what pietas DESTROYS as well as what it builds. Being "pius Aeneas" comes at a devastating personal cost.

What Aeneas Loses to Pietas

  • His wife Creusa: Lost in the chaos of Troy's fall while he prioritised father and son (Book 2)
  • His lover Dido: Abandoned in Carthage to obey divine command‚Äîleading to her suicide (Book 4)
  • His companions: Palinurus, Misenus, many others die on the journey (Books 5-6)
  • His surrogate son Pallas: Dies under his protection in battle (Book 10)
  • His own happiness: He weeps constantly, expresses longing for death, never finds peace
At pius Aeneas, quamquam lenire dolentem
solando cupit et dictis avertere curas,
multa gemens magnoque animum labefactus amore
iussa tamen divum exsequitur...
‚Äî Aeneid 4.393-396 (But dutiful Aeneas, though he longs to ease her grief with consolation and turn aside her cares with words, groaning heavily and shaken in spirit by his great love, nevertheless carries out the gods' commands...)

Critical Interpretation

Notice Virgil shows us Aeneas's inner conflict: "shaken in spirit by his great love" but "nevertheless carries out the gods' commands." He's not cold or unfeeling‚Äîhe's torn apart. Pietas demands that he suppress his feelings, not that he lacks them. This makes him more sympathetic... and more tragic.

Aeneas's Flaws: The Unheroic Hero

This is where it gets uncomfortable. Aeneas is the protagonist‚Äîthe "good guy"‚Äîbut he does things that are morally questionable, sometimes downright brutal. Virgil doesn't hide this. He shows you the dark side of pietas and Roman destiny.

For Essays: Don't Avoid This
Weaker essays portray Aeneas as a simple hero. STRONGER essays acknowledge his flaws and debate whether they undermine or complicate his heroism. The ambiguity is intentional‚ÄîVirgil wants you to think about the moral cost of empire.

Flaw #1: Excessive Rage (Furor)

Aeneas is supposed to embody pietas‚Äîreason, duty, self-control. But repeatedly, he loses control and becomes consumed by furor (irrational rage). This is especially problematic because furor is what he's supposed to OPPOSE.

Key Moments of Furor

  • Book 2: During Troy's fall, Aeneas goes on a suicidal rampage‚Äîattacking Greeks recklessly, endangering his mission
  • Book 10: After Pallas's death, Aeneas becomes a killing machine‚Äîsacrificing prisoners, refusing mercy
  • Book 12: In the final duel, even after Turnus surrenders, Aeneas kills him in rage
Furor iraque mentem
praecipitant, pulchrumque mori succurrit in armis.
‚Äî Aeneid 2.316-317 (Rage and fury drive my mind headlong, and it occurs to me that it is beautiful to die in arms)

The Pallas Rampage (Book 10.510-605)

  • Aeneas takes EIGHT captives alive specifically to sacrifice them at Pallas's funeral‚Äîhuman sacrifice
  • He refuses to accept surrenders: "Pallas is the one who sacrifices you!" he shouts
  • He kills suppliants who beg for mercy‚Äîsomething even Achilles rarely does
  • The narrator describes him as "fervidus" (seething), "acer" (fierce), filled with "dolor" (grief/rage)

Why This Is Problematic

The Aeneid sets up pietas vs furor as its central opposition. Pietas = Roman order, reason, duty. Furor = destructive passion, chaos, madness. Juno embodies furor; Jupiter represents order.

But when Aeneas becomes furor-driven, what does that mean? Is he betraying his own principles? Is Virgil showing that even the "good guys" can become monsters? Or is some furor necessary for empire-building? Virgil leaves this deliberately unresolved.

Flaw #2: Abandoning Women

Aeneas's relationships with women are... not great. He loses Creusa, abandons Dido, and barely interacts with his destined bride Lavinia. There's a pattern here, and it's troubling.

Creusa (Book 2)

  • Lost in chaos of Troy's fall
  • Aeneas doesn't notice she's gone until it's too late
  • Her ghost appears to release him from guilt
  • He moves on relatively quickly

Dido (Book 4)

  • They become lovers (marriage from her view)
  • He leaves without proper farewell or explanation
  • His speech to her is cold and legalistic
  • She commits suicide; he sails away
Illa solo fixos oculos aversa tenebat
nec magis incepto vultum sermone movetur
quam si dura silex aut stet Marpesia cautes.
‚Äî Aeneid 6.469-471 (She [Dido's shade], turned away, kept her eyes fixed on the ground and was no more moved in expression by his speech than if she were hard flint or Marpesian rock standing there)

Defence vs Critique

Defence: Aeneas is following divine commands. His mission requires sacrifice. He does feel guilt (see Book 6 encounter). Personal relationships can't override duty to Rome's future.

Critique: Duty becomes an excuse for emotional cowardice. He could have handled Dido's departure better. The "ends justify means" logic justifies causing immense suffering to innocent people.

Flaw #3: Uncertainty and Passivity

For a hero, Aeneas spends a LOT of time unsure of what to do, needing divine intervention to make decisions, and expressing despair. Compare this to decisive heroes like Achilles or assertive leaders like Agamemnon (flawed as he is).

Key Moments of Uncertainty

  • Book 2: During Troy's fall, Anchises refuses to flee. Aeneas has to wait for divine sign (snake around Iulus's head) before Anchises agrees
  • Book 4: Settles in Carthage, building Dido's city. Mercury has to literally wake him up and remind him of his mission
  • Book 8: Terrified before final battles. Venus has to bring him divine armour to boost confidence
  • Book 12: Hesitates before killing Turnus‚Äîhas to see Pallas's belt to find motivation

Compare to Greek Heroes

  • Achilles makes instant decisions‚Äîfight or withdraw, kill or spare (usually kill)
  • Odysseus is proactive‚Äîschemes, plans, never waits for divine prompting
  • Aeneas reacts more than he acts‚Äîresponds to crises rather than creating momentum

Why Virgil Wrote Him This Way

This isn't accidental weakness‚Äîit's deliberate characterisation. Aeneas represents a NEW kind of hero for a new age (Augustus's Rome, not Homer's Greece).

Roman values emphasised collective good over individual glory, obedience to divine/state authority over personal initiative, endurance over aggressive action. An uncertain, dutiful hero who keeps going despite doubt is MORE Roman than a confident, glory-seeking warrior.

That doesn't mean you have to LIKE this characterisation‚Äîbut understand it's intentional.

Aeneas's Character Arc: From Troy to Latium

Unlike Greek heroes who are fairly static (Achilles is always rage-driven; Odysseus is always clever), Aeneas DEVELOPS throughout the epic. He starts as a traumatised refugee and becomes (supposedly) a confident leader and warrior. Track this evolution‚Äîit's crucial for essays.

Stage 1: The Traumatised Survivor (Books 1-2)

Where We Meet Him

Book 1: Storm drives Trojans to Carthage. Aeneas is despairing: "O three and four times blessed, those who had the luck to fall before their fathers' eyes beneath Troy's high walls!" He wishes he'd died at Troy‚Äînot exactly inspiring leadership.

Book 2: The Fall of Troy (Flashback)

  • Initial response: Suicidal courage‚Äî"Let us die and rush into the thick of battle!" (2.353)
  • Sees Greeks infiltrate via wooden horse: Too late to save city; tries to organise resistance anyway
  • Witnesses Priam's murder: Thinks of killing Helen in revenge‚Äîstopped by Venus
  • Flees with father, son, household gods: Loses wife Creusa in chaos
Furor iraque mentem
praecipitant, pulchrumque mori succurrit in armis.
‚Äî Aeneid 2.316-317 (Rage and fury drive my mind headlong, and it occurs to me that it is beautiful to die in arms)

At this stage, Aeneas is reactive, emotional, driven by furor more than pietas. He needs constant divine guidance (Venus appears repeatedly) and hasn't yet accepted his mission. He's a survivor, not yet a leader.

Stage 2: The Reluctant Wanderer (Books 3-5)

The Long Journey

Books 3-5 narrate seven years of wandering‚Äîfalse starts, dead ends, losses. Aeneas tries to settle in Thrace (haunted), Crete (plagued), Carthage (divine intervention forces departure). His father Anchises dies in Sicily (Book 3), removing his moral anchor.

Key Developments

  • Learning to read divine signs: Gradually understands prophecies and omens without constant prompting
  • Gaining confidence as leader: By Book 5, organises funeral games, makes decisions about who stays/goes
  • Still uncertain about destination: "Italy" is vague; doesn't yet understand what he's building
  • Emotional connections endanger mission: Carthage shows he can be distracted from duty

The Carthage Test (Book 4)

Carthage is Aeneas's biggest test of pietas. He's happy. He's in love. He's helping build a city (what he's SUPPOSED to do eventually). Why not stay?

Because it's the WRONG city. Mercury reminds him: you're not building Carthage for Dido; you're supposed to build Rome for Ascanius. Staying would mean betraying his son's future.

When Aeneas leaves Dido, he proves his pietas‚Äîbut at devastating human cost. This is the moment he truly chooses duty over personal desire.

Stage 3: The Enlightened Leader (Book 6)

Book 6 is the turning point. Aeneas descends to the underworld‚Äîa dangerous journey to the land of the dead‚Äîto meet his father's shade and learn about Rome's future. He emerges transformed.

What He Learns in the Underworld

  • Sees the consequences of furor: Dido in the Fields of Mourning, still wounded by love; Deiphobus mutilated by Helen's betrayal
  • Meets Anchises in Elysium: Father explains reincarnation, Roman destiny, future heroes
  • Views the pageant of heroes: Sees Romulus, Augustus, all of Rome's future greatness
  • Receives Rome's mission statement: "Remember, Roman, to rule the peoples with your power‚Äîthese will be your arts‚Äîto impose the custom of peace, to spare the conquered, and to war down the proud" (6.851-853)
Tu regere imperio populos, Romane, memento
(hae tibi erunt artes), pacique imponere morem,
parcere subiectis et debellare superbos.
‚Äî Aeneid 6.851-853 (Remember, Roman, to rule peoples with your command‚Äîthese will be your arts‚Äîto establish the custom of peace, to spare the defeated, and to war down the proud)

After this katabasis, Aeneas understands his mission fully. He's not just finding a new home‚Äîhe's founding an empire. The suffering makes sense now (supposedly). He emerges ready to fight for Latium.

Stage 4: The Warrior King (Books 7-12)

The Italian War

Books 7-12: War breaks out in Latium. Juno stirs up opposition. King Latinus offers his daughter Lavinia, but Queen Amata and her favourite Turnus resist. Three years of brutal warfare follow.

Aeneas as Warrior (Books 10-12)

  • More decisive in battle: Commands troops effectively, makes tactical decisions
  • Forms alliances: Secures Evander's support, allies with Etruscans
  • Takes on Achillean qualities: After Pallas's death, becomes aristeia-driven killing machine
  • But still needs divine help: Venus brings armour; divine intervention saves him multiple times

Has He Really Changed?

Book 12's ending is controversial. Aeneas defeats Turnus in single combat. Turnus begs for mercy. Aeneas hesitates‚Äîthen sees Pallas's belt on Turnus and kills him in fury.

Is this the completion of his character arc (becoming a decisive warrior-king)? Or is it regression to furor (letting rage override mercy)? Virgil ends the epic on this ambiguous note‚Äîthe final word is "shadows" as Turnus's soul flees to the underworld. Not exactly triumphant.

Five Key Moments That Define Aeneas

For close analysis questions, you need SPECIFIC EXAMPLES. Here are five crucial scenes that show different aspects of Aeneas's character‚Äîmemorise these for essays.

Moment 1: Carrying Anchises (Book 2.707-729)

'Ergo age, care pater, cervici imponere nostrae;
ipse subibo umeris nec me labor iste gravabit;
quo res cumque cadent, unum et commune periclum,
una salus ambobus erit.'
‚Äî Aeneid 2.707-710 (So come, dear father, place yourself upon my neck; I will carry you on my shoulders and this labour will not burden me; however things fall out, there will be one danger shared by both, one salvation for us both.)

Why This Matters

  • Visual symbolism: Aeneas literally carries the past (father) and future (son by hand) out of burning Troy
  • Family pietas: Prioritises father's safety over his own‚Äîcould flee faster alone
  • Three generations: Anchises, Aeneas, Ascanius = past, present, future of Rome
  • Household gods: Anchises carries the Penates (household gods)‚Äîreligious continuity from Troy to Rome
  • This image appears on coins: Augustus used this scene in propaganda‚Äîthe icon of Roman pietas

The Lost Wife

Crucially, Creusa gets lost during this escape. Aeneas doesn't notice until they reach the meeting point. When he goes back to find her, he finds only her ghost.

Her ghost releases him from guilt: "Don't weep for me. The great Mother of the Gods keeps me here. A royal wife awaits you in Hesperia." Even the loss of his wife serves divine plan.

This establishes a pattern: Aeneas's personal relationships are sacrificed to his mission. His character is defined by what he loses.

Moment 2: The Storm Speech (Book 1.94-101)

Exemplo Aeneae solvuntur frigore membra;
ingemit et duplicis tendens ad sidera palmas
talia voce refert: 'O terque quaterque beati,
quis ante ora patrum Troiae sub moenibus altis
contigit oppetere!'
‚Äî Aeneid 1.92-96 (Immediately Aeneas's limbs are loosened with cold; he groans and stretching both hands to the stars speaks such words: "Oh three and four times blessed, those who had the luck to die before their fathers' faces beneath Troy's high walls!")

What This Reveals

  • "solvuntur frigore membra": His limbs are loosened with cold/fear‚Äîhe's terrified, physically weak
  • "ingemit": He groans‚Äîemotional expression, not stoic silence
  • "O terque quaterque beati": Envies the dead‚Äîwishes he'd died at Troy
  • Compares to Diomedes: "At least Diomedes killed you with his strong right hand [if I'd died at Troy]"‚Äîwishes for glorious death in battle, not drowning in anonymous storm

Not Your Typical Hero Speech

Compare this to Odysseus in a storm (Odyssey 5): Odysseus strips naked and swims to shore, never considering giving up. He acts.

Aeneas despairs. He wishes for death. He has to be saved by Neptune, not by his own resourcefulness. This immediately establishes him as a different kind of hero‚Äîmore vulnerable, more human, more reliant on divine help.

Some see this as weakness. Others see it as realism‚Äîhe's a traumatised refugee, not a confident adventurer. Both readings are valid.

Moment 3: Leaving Dido (Book 4.393-396)

At pius Aeneas, quamquam lenire dolentem
solando cupit et dictis avertere curas,
multa gemens magnoque animum labefactus amore
iussa tamen divum exsequitur...
‚Äî Aeneid 4.393-396 (But dutiful Aeneas, though he longs to ease her grief with consolation and turn aside her cares with words, groaning heavily and shaken in spirit by his great love, nevertheless carries out the gods' commands...)

The Key Words

  • "pius": First word‚Äîdefines him by duty even in this emotional moment
  • "quamquam... cupit": Though he WANTS to console her‚Äîhe has feelings!
  • "multa gemens": Groaning heavily‚Äîhe's in pain
  • "magnoque animum labefactus amore": Shaken in spirit by great love‚Äîthis is costing him
  • "tamen": NEVERTHELESS‚Äîthe crucial word. Despite all this, he obeys
  • "iussa divum exsequitur": Carries out the gods' commands‚Äîduty wins

Sympathetic Reading

  • Virgil shows his inner conflict
  • He doesn't WANT to hurt her
  • Pietas requires personal sacrifice
  • He's torn between love and duty
  • His grief proves he's not cold

Critical Reading

  • Feeling bad doesn't excuse harm
  • He leaves without proper farewell
  • His speech to her is legalistic, cold
  • Pietas becomes excuse for cowardice
  • "Following orders" justifies cruelty

For Essays

Don't just pick one reading. The BEST essays say: "Virgil presents both perspectives. The word 'tamen' (nevertheless) encapsulates the tension‚ÄîAeneas feels deeply but acts according to duty. Whether this makes him admirable or cruel depends on whether you prioritise collective mission over individual suffering."

Moment 4: The Pallas Rampage (Book 10.510-605)

When Aeneas learns that Pallas (young son of his ally Evander, under his protection) has been killed by Turnus, he goes berserk. This is Aeneas at his most Achillean‚Äîand most disturbing.

What Aeneas Does

  • Takes eight young Italian captives alive‚Äî"to send as offerings to the shade of Pallas"‚Äîhuman sacrifice planned
  • Magus begs for mercy, offering ransom‚ÄîAeneas kills him anyway: "Pallas sacrifices you!"
  • Haemonides begs to bury his father's body‚ÄîAeneas refuses: "This wound will settle that"
  • Tarquitus son of Faunus begs for life‚ÄîAeneas mocks him, kills him, leaves body for animals
  • Narrator describes him as "fervidus" (seething), swept along by "ira" (rage)
'Pallas te hoc vulnere, Pallas
immolat et poenam scelerato ex sanguine sumit.'
‚Äî Aeneid 10.493-494 (Pallas sacrifices you with this wound, Pallas, and takes vengeance from your wicked blood)

Why This Is Significant

  • Parallels Achilles's aristeia: Like Achilles after Patroclus's death, Aeneas becomes a killing machine
  • Violates Roman values: Romans valued mercy to the defeated ("parcere subiectis"‚Äîspare the conquered). Aeneas doesn't
  • Human sacrifice: This is a Greek practice (Achilles sacrifices Trojans at Patroclus's funeral), not typically Roman
  • Furor overcomes pietas: In this moment, rage drives him‚Äîthe opposite of what he's supposed to embody
  • Sets up final scene: His killing of Turnus echoes this‚Äîrevenge for Pallas justifies murder of suppliant

The Ambiguity of Heroism

Is this justified? Pallas died under Aeneas's protection‚Äîhe promised Evander to keep him safe and failed. The grief and guilt are real. Turnus killed Pallas brutally and took his belt as a trophy.

But does that justify refusing mercy to suppliants? Refusing burial rites? Planning human sacrifice? Virgil doesn't tell you. He shows you what "heroism" looks like in its brutal reality and lets you judge.

Moment 5: Killing Turnus (Book 12.930-952)

The epic's final scene. Aeneas has defeated Turnus in single combat. Turnus begs for mercy. Aeneas hesitates. Then he sees Pallas's belt on Turnus and explodes in fury. The final word is "shadows" as Turnus dies.

Ille, oculis postquam saevi monimenta doloris
exuviasque hausit, furiis accensus et ira
terribilis: 'Tune hinc spoliis indute meorum
eripiare mihi? Pallas te hoc vulnere, Pallas
immolat et poenam scelerato ex sanguine sumit.'
Hoc dicens ferrum adverso sub pectore condit
fervidus; ast illi solvuntur frigore membra
vitaque cum gemitu fugit indignata sub umbras.
‚Äî Aeneid 12.945-952 (He [Aeneas], after his eyes drank in this memorial of savage grief and the spoils, blazing with fury and terrible in anger: "Are you to be snatched from me, dressed in the spoils of my people? Pallas sacrifices you with this wound, Pallas, and takes vengeance from your cursed blood." Saying this, seething, he buries the sword in his breast; but his limbs are loosened with cold and his life flees, resentful, with a groan to the shades below.)

Breaking Down the Ending

  • "saevi monimenta doloris": Memorial of savage grief‚ÄîPallas's belt triggers memory of loss
  • "furiis accensus": Blazing with furies‚Äîdivine madness, not reason
  • "ira terribilis": Terrible in anger‚Äîhe becomes monstrous
  • "fervidus": Seething‚Äîsame word used in Book 10 rampage
  • "vitaque... fugit indignata sub umbras": Life flees, RESENTFUL, to the shades‚ÄîTurnus dies angry, not reconciled

How Should This Ending Make You Feel?

  • Triumphant? Aeneas has won. Rome's destiny is secured. Justice for Pallas
  • Uneasy? He kills a suppliant in rage. Not exactly pietas. Turnus had offered truce
  • Tragic? Everyone has lost something. Victory is hollow. Final word is "shadows"‚Äîdeath, darkness
  • Ambiguous? Virgil deliberately doesn't resolve this. You decide what kind of hero Aeneas is

For Exam Essays

The ending is controversial and deliberately ambiguous. Don't assert that it's definitely triumphant OR definitely problematic. The best essays acknowledge the ambiguity: "Virgil ends with Aeneas acting on furor (rage), not pietas (duty), suggesting even the ideal Roman hero contains contradictions." Then explore what that means.

The Critical Debate: Is Aeneas a Good Hero?

Scholars have argued about Aeneas's character for 2,000 years. Is he the ideal Roman hero or a morally compromised figure? Did Virgil celebrate him or critique him? Understanding both sides is essential for high-level essays.

Why This Matters for Exams
OCR mark schemes reward "critical engagement" and "awareness of scholarly debate." Don't just summarise what happens‚ÄîANALYSE what it means and acknowledge that reasonable people disagree. The best essays present multiple interpretations, support them with evidence, then argue for one (or explain why ambiguity is intentional).

Position 1: Aeneas as Ideal Hero (Pro-Augustan Reading)

The Argument

Virgil wrote the Aeneid to glorify Augustus and Rome. Aeneas is the model Roman‚Äîdutiful, pious, self-sacrificing. His flaws make him human and relatable, but his core virtue (pietas) is never in doubt. The epic celebrates the cost of empire but ultimately justifies it.

Evidence for This View

  • Constant divine approval: Jupiter confirms Aeneas's mission repeatedly. Venus protects him. Fate itself drives him
  • "pius Aeneas": His defining epithet‚ÄîVirgil calls him dutiful/pious 20+ times
  • Book 6 vision: Anchises shows him Rome's glorious future‚Äîall this suffering produces Augustan peace
  • His suffering proves his virtue: He endures loss after loss but never abandons his mission
  • Comparison to Turnus: Turnus is driven by furor; Aeneas (mostly) by pietas. Pietas wins, as it should

Historical Context

Virgil wrote during Augustus's reign (29-19 BCE). Rome had just emerged from civil wars. Augustus presented himself as bringer of peace, restorer of traditional values, divinely ordained ruler.

The Aeneid explicitly connects Aeneas to Augustus‚ÄîAnchises points to Augustus in the underworld procession: "This is the man, this one, whom you have so often heard promised to you, Augustus Caesar, son of a god" (6.791-792).

From this perspective, Aeneas's struggles justify Augustus's rule. Look what it cost to build Rome. Look how much Aeneas sacrificed. Augustus is the fulfilment of this sacrifice‚Äîthe peace that makes it worthwhile.

Position 2: Aeneas as Problematic Hero (Pessimistic Reading)

The Argument

Virgil subtly critiques Aeneas and the imperial project he represents. Pietas becomes an excuse for cruelty. Duty justifies destroying innocent people (Dido, Turnus). The cost of empire is TOO HIGH‚ÄîVirgil shows you the human wreckage and lets you question whether it's worth it.

Evidence for This View

  • Sympathetic victims: Dido is noble and tragic‚Äîwe feel her suffering more than Aeneas's mission. Same with Turnus
  • The ending: Final word is "shadows"‚Äînot triumphant. Aeneas kills in rage, not justice
  • Human sacrifice: Aeneas plans to sacrifice prisoners‚Äîmorally disturbing act, uncommented-upon by narrator
  • His passivity: Constantly needs divine prompting‚Äîis he heroic or just obedient?
  • Virgil's ambiguity: If he wanted straightforward celebration, why make Aeneas so conflicted and his actions so questionable?

The Pessimistic Tradition

This reading gained prominence in 20th century, especially after WWI and WWII. Scholars who'd seen the cost of empires (British, Nazi, Soviet) were suspicious of imperial propaganda.

They noticed: Virgil COULD have ended with triumphant celebration. Instead, he ends with a suppliant killed in rage. He COULD have made Dido villainous. Instead, she's sympathetic. He COULD have made Aeneas confident and decisive. Instead, he's uncertain and conflicted.

From this view, Virgil is subtly subverting Augustus's propaganda‚Äîshowing that empire is built on suffering, violence, and moral compromise. He celebrates Rome... but "with tears" (lacrimae rerum‚Äîthe tears of things).

Position 3: "Two Voices" / Intentional Ambiguity

This is the most sophisticated modern view, popularised by Harvard professor Adam Parry. The idea: Virgil wrote with TWO VOICES simultaneously‚Äîone celebrating empire, one mourning its cost. Both are real. The tension is intentional.

How This Works

Public voice: Rome's destiny is glorious. Aeneas's pietas is admirable. Augustus is divinely ordained. The suffering produces peace. This is what the emperor wants to hear.

Private voice: Dido is destroyed. Turnus dies resentful. Countless Italians die for Aeneas's "destiny." The hero ends in rage, not reason. This is what Virgil actually feels.

Both voices are present in the text. Virgil doesn't reconcile them‚Äîhe lets them exist in tension. This makes the Aeneid more complex and realistic than straightforward propaganda OR straightforward critique.

Sunt lacrimae rerum et mentem mortalia tangunt.
‚Äî Aeneid 1.462 (There are tears for things and mortal matters touch the mind) [Aeneas seeing images of Troy's fall in Carthage]

For High-Level Essays

This "two voices" approach is excellent for exams because it shows sophisticated thinking: "Virgil presents Aeneas as both ideal Roman hero (pius, dutiful, destined) AND morally ambiguous figure (abandons Dido, kills suppliants, acts on rage). These aren't contradictions‚Äîthey're intentional complexity. Empire requires both pietas and furor, sacrifice and violence. Virgil doesn't hide this tension; he makes it the poem's central theme."

How to Use This in Essays

APPROACH 1
Present Both Sides
"Some scholars see Aeneas as ideal Roman hero (evidence). Others see him as morally problematic (evidence). Both readings are supported by the text."
APPROACH 2
Argue for One
"While some read Aeneas sympathetically, the text better supports a critical view (evidence and argument)." OR reverse it‚Äîbut acknowledge counterarguments.
APPROACH 3
Embrace Ambiguity
"Virgil deliberately creates an ambiguous hero. Aeneas's contradictions reflect the contradictions of empire itself‚Äîglorious and terrible, destined and destructive."
APPROACH 4
Historical Evolution
"Ancient readers saw Aeneas as ideal. Post-WWI readers saw him as problematic. Modern scholars see intentional complexity. All readings illuminate the text."

What NOT to Do

DON'T write as if there's only one "correct" reading. DON'T ignore evidence that complicates your argument. DON'T just summarise‚ÄîANALYSE. DON'T forget that Virgil wrote 2,000 years ago in a different culture‚Äîbut also that great literature transcends its context and speaks to us now.

Final Thoughts: Why Aeneas Matters

Whether you find Aeneas admirable, frustrating, or fascinatingly ambiguous, he represents something important: the cost of civilisation.

Every major achievement‚Äîbuilding cities, founding nations, establishing peace‚Äîrequires sacrifice. Personal desires must sometimes yield to collective needs. Heroes aren't always likeable or morally pure‚Äîthey're people who keep going despite doubt, pain, and loss.

Aeneas isn't Achilles (who fights for glory) or Odysseus (who fights to go home). He's fighting for something that doesn't exist yet‚Äîa future Rome, a destiny he'll never see, a civilisation that will be built on his suffering.

That makes him Rome's perfect founder: uncertain, conflicted, grief-stricken, but enduring. He doesn't win because he's the strongest or cleverest. He wins because he keeps going.

The Bottom Line for Exams
Know Aeneas deeply: his pietas, his flaws, his journey, his key moments, and the critical debate. Be able to analyse specific passages showing his character. Most importantly: understand that ambiguity and complexity are features, not bugs. Virgil wrote a complicated hero for a complicated epic about empire's glory and cost.
