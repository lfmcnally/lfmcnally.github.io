How to Answer 8-Mark Questions
Master the detailed response questions in Myth and Religion
What are 8-Mark Questions?
▼

8-mark questions are detailed response questions that test both your knowledge (AO1) and your ability to analyze and evaluate (AO2). They appear throughout the Myth and Religion paper and require you to:

  • Use a source as your starting point (image or text extract)
  • Add your own knowledge from the topics you've studied
  • Make judgments and evaluate different viewpoints
  • Write in paragraph form with clear arguments
'Greek gods were too human-like to inspire genuine religious awe.' How far do you agree with this assessment? Use Source A [showing anthropomorphic Greek gods] as a starting point and your own knowledge in your answer.
[8]
What the Examiners are Looking For
▼

8-mark questions test two Assessment Objectives equally - you need both to reach the top levels:

AO1: Knowledge & Understanding (4 marks)
Demonstrate accurate knowledge of classical sources and their contexts
You can show this through:
  • Specific details from the source
  • Names of gods with correct Greek forms
  • Iconography and symbols (Zeus's thunderbolt, Athena's owl)
  • Mythological stories and their context
  • Religious practices and beliefs
  • Epithets and roles of gods
AO2: Analysis & Evaluation (4 marks)
Analyze, interpret and evaluate to form judgments
You can show this through:
  • Arguing for/against the statement
  • Weighing up different viewpoints
  • Drawing conclusions from evidence
  • Explaining why something was significant
  • Making connections between examples
  • Reaching a balanced judgment
Key Point: The examiners use a "best fit" approach - you need roughly equal amounts of both AO1 and AO2 to reach the higher levels. A purely descriptive answer won't score highly, even with lots of knowledge.
Mark Scheme Levels
▼

Understanding the levels helps you target the right grade. Here's what examiners look for at each level:

Level 4
7-8 marks
  • Consistently accurate and detailed knowledge of sources and wider context
  • Well-argued response supported by range of well-selected evidence
  • Includes critical analysis, interpretation and evaluation
  • Shows very good understanding of cultural contexts
Level 3
5-6 marks
  • Accurate knowledge using both source and own knowledge
  • Focused response supported by range of evidence
  • Includes relevant analysis, interpretation and evaluation
  • Shows good understanding of contexts
Level 2
3-4 marks
  • Sound, mostly accurate knowledge using source and own knowledge
  • Engages with general topic supported by limited evidence
  • Includes some analysis, interpretation and evaluation
  • Shows some understanding of contexts
Level 1
1-2 marks
  • Limited knowledge - may only use source material
  • Little attempt at basic explanation
  • Includes isolated analysis, interpretation and evaluation
  • Shows limited understanding of contexts
How to Structure Your Answer
▼
Follow These 5 Steps
1
Address the Question
Give your initial judgment on the statement - do you agree, disagree, or think it's partially true?
2
Use the Source
Pick specific details from the source and explain what they show about the topic
3
Add Your Knowledge
Bring in examples from your studies that support or challenge the statement
4
Consider Alternatives
Show you understand different viewpoints - what might someone argue on the other side?
5
Reach a Conclusion
Make a final judgment based on the evidence you've presented
Student Examples
▼

Let's look at how different students approached our example question, and see what levels their answers achieved:

Student A - Level 1 Response
1-2 marks
Greek gods looked like humans and had human emotions like being jealous and angry. In the source you can see Zeus looking like a man with a beard. This meant people could relate to them because they were like normal people. The gods were impressive because they had special powers that humans didn't have.
Examiner Commentary:
This response shows limited knowledge and only really uses the source material. There's little attempt to engage with the specific question about whether gods were "too human-like to inspire religious awe." The candidate makes basic observations but doesn't develop them or use specific examples from their own knowledge. No analysis of what "religious awe" might mean or evaluation of different perspectives.
Student B - Level 2 Response
3-4 marks
I disagree with this assessment because Greek gods were both human-like and divine. In the source, Zeus appears with his thunderbolt which shows his divine power over the sky and weather. Although the gods looked like humans, they had superhuman abilities that would have inspired awe. For example, Athena sprang fully grown from Zeus's head, and Poseidon could cause earthquakes. The gods also lived forever unlike humans. However, it is true that the gods had human emotions - Zeus was often unfaithful to Hera, and the gods could be petty and jealous like in the Trojan War.
Examiner Commentary:
This response engages with the question and shows sound knowledge using both the source and own examples (Athena's birth, Poseidon's powers). The candidate shows some understanding of the balance between human and divine characteristics. However, the analysis is quite basic and doesn't fully develop the concept of "religious awe" or explain why these characteristics would inspire worship.
Student C - Level 3 Response
5-6 marks
I partially agree with this assessment, as Greek gods combined human characteristics with divine powers in ways that could both inspire and diminish religious awe. The source shows Zeus with distinctly human features but holding his divine thunderbolt, representing this duality. The anthropomorphic nature of Greek gods made them accessible to worshippers - people could understand divine anger through Zeus's rages or divine wisdom through Athena's counsel. This relatability was crucial for Greek religion's transactional nature, where mortals offered sacrifices expecting divine favor in return. However, the gods' human flaws could indeed reduce their awesome nature. Zeus's numerous affairs, depicted in myths like the abduction of Europa, showed him as lustful rather than morally perfect. Similarly, the petty quarrels between gods in the Iliad presented them more as powerful humans than transcendent deities. Yet their immortality, immense power, and ability to control natural forces like earthquakes (Poseidon) or the hunt (Artemis) maintained their divine status worthy of worship.
Examiner Commentary:
This is a focused response that uses accurate knowledge from both the source and wider study. The candidate understands the concept of anthropomorphism and relates it to Greek religious practice. Good use of specific examples and some attempt to analyze both sides of the argument. The understanding of transactional religion shows good contextual knowledge.
Student D - Level 4 Response
7-8 marks
This assessment oversimplifies the sophisticated nature of Greek religious thought. While Greek gods were indeed anthropomorphic, this human-like quality served specific religious and cultural functions rather than diminishing their capacity to inspire awe. The source demonstrates this complexity - Zeus appears with recognizably human features, yet his divine attributes (the thunderbolt, his commanding posture) clearly mark him as transcendent. The anthropomorphic nature of Greek gods actually enhanced their religious power by making the divine comprehensible to human worshippers. Through familiar emotions and motivations, mortals could understand divine will and participate in the transactional relationship central to Greek religion. Epithets like Zeus Xenios (protector of hospitality) or Athena Promakhos (defender in battle) showed gods' specialized roles in human society while maintaining their divine authority. Furthermore, Greek religious awe often stemmed precisely from the gods' unpredictable blend of human emotion and divine power. Demeter's grief in the Homeric Hymn creates both relatable maternal anguish and terrifying famine, demonstrating how human-like emotions amplified rather than reduced divine impact. The gods' immortality, their ability to appear in multiple forms simultaneously, and their control over natural forces maintained clear boundaries between mortal and divine. Even their moral flaws served religious purposes - Zeus's affairs explained natural phenomena and heroic lineages, while divine conflicts in myths like the Trojan War provided frameworks for understanding human suffering. Rather than being "too human," Greek gods achieved a sophisticated balance that made the divine accessible while preserving its essential otherness.
Examiner Commentary:
This is a consistently accurate and detailed response showing very good understanding of Greek religious concepts. The candidate demonstrates critical analysis by challenging the premise of the question and providing well-selected evidence. Outstanding use of specific examples (epithets, the Homeric Hymn to Demeter) and sophisticated evaluation of how anthropomorphism functioned in Greek religion. Shows understanding of cultural contexts and reaches a well-argued conclusion.
Top Tips and Common Mistakes
▼
Always Use the Source
Pick specific details from images or texts - don't just describe what you see, explain what it shows about the topic
Address the Question
Make sure every paragraph relates back to the specific question asked - don't just write everything you know about the topic
Show Different Views
The best answers consider multiple perspectives - what would someone who disagrees with you argue?
Use Specific Details
Names, epithets, symbols, specific myths - the more precise your knowledge, the higher your AO1 marks
Explain Significance
Don't just state facts - explain WHY they matter for answering the question
Manage Your Time
Plan for about 12-15 minutes per 8-mark question - quick plan, then write fluently
Remember: Quality over Quantity

It's better to make fewer points well-supported with evidence than to list many facts without explanation. Focus on depth rather than breadth.
