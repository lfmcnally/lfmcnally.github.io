2.3 The Twelve Labours of Heracles

Learning Objectives v1

By the end of this section, you will understand the twelve labours and their significance, how Heracles overcame each challenge, the representation of the labours on the Temple of Zeus at Olympia, and Heracles' role as founder of the Olympic Games.

From Madness to Redemption

Following the murder of his family in a fit of madness sent by Hera, Heracles sought purification at the Oracle at Delphi. The Pythia commanded him to serve his cowardly cousin King Eurystheus of Tiryns for twelve years and perform whatever labours were demanded.

Originally ten labours, two were later discounted by Eurystheus (the Hydra because Heracles had help, and the Augean Stables because he was paid), requiring two additional tasks. These labours would transform Heracles from a broken man guilty of the worst crime into the greatest hero of antiquity.

"The Pythia told him to dwell in Tiryns, serving Eurystheus for twelve years and to perform the labours imposed upon him, and thus, she said, when the labours were accomplished, he would be immortal."
‚Äî Pseudo-Apollodorus, Library 2.4.12

The labours progressed in difficulty and scope. The first six were relatively local to the Peloponnese, while the later labours took Heracles to the edges of the known world and even beyond - to the underworld itself.

1
Nemean Lion ü¶Å
Chief Difficulty:
Impenetrable golden skin - no weapon could pierce it
How Heracles Succeeded:
Strangled it with his bare hands, then used its own claws to skin it. Wore the pelt as armour thereafter.

Location: Nemea, northeast Peloponnese

Significance: The lion skin became Heracles' iconic garment, making him invulnerable to weapons. This first labour established his method - when conventional weapons fail, use strength and ingenuity.

2
Lernaean Hydra üêç
Chief Difficulty:
When one head was cut off, two more would grow back
How Heracles Succeeded:
With help from nephew Iolaus, burnt the neck stumps to prevent regeneration. Dipped arrows in its poisonous blood.

Location: Lake Lerna, near Argos

Significance: The Hydra's blood became a deadly poison Heracles used on his arrows. Eurystheus discounted this labour because Iolaus helped, showing the arbitrary nature of the tasks.

3
Ceryneian Hind ü¶å
Chief Difficulty:
Sacred to Artemis, incredibly swift, had to be captured alive
How Heracles Succeeded:
Pursued it for a full year, finally captured it while it slept. Apologised to Artemis for the necessity.

Location: Ceryneia, Achaea

Significance: This labour required patience rather than strength, showing Heracles could exercise restraint. The golden antlers made it unique among deer.

4
Erymanthian Boar üêó
Chief Difficulty:
Enormous size, ferocious temperament, had to be captured alive
How Heracles Succeeded:
Chased it into deep snow where it tired, then captured it. Carried it on his shoulders to Eurystheus.

Location: Mount Erymanthus, Arcadia

Significance: When Heracles arrived with the boar, Eurystheus was so frightened he hid in a bronze jar - a recurring comic element showing the king's cowardice.

5
Augean Stables üèõÔ∏è
Chief Difficulty:
3,000 cattle, 30 years of filth, had to clean in one day
How Heracles Succeeded:
Diverted two rivers (Alpheus and Peneus) through the stables, washing them clean instantly.

Location: Elis, western Peloponnese

Significance: This labour required ingenuity over strength. Eurystheus discounted it because Augeas had offered payment, though Augeas later refused to pay.

6
Stymphalian Birds ü¶Ö
Chief Difficulty:
Man-eating birds with bronze beaks and metallic feathers they could shoot
How Heracles Succeeded:
Used bronze castanets from Athena to startle them into flight, then shot them with his arrows.

Location: Lake Stymphalia, Arcadia

Significance: This labour showed divine assistance - Athena provided the noise-makers. The birds were sacred to Ares, adding divine conflict to the task.

7
Cretan Bull üêÇ
Chief Difficulty:
Father of the Minotaur, breathed fire, terrorised Crete
How Heracles Succeeded:
Wrestled it into submission, rode it across the sea to Greece, presented it to Eurystheus.

Location: Crete

Significance: This bull was sent by Poseidon and later became the father of the Minotaur. After Heracles released it, it became the Marathon Bull that Theseus would later capture.

8
Mares of Diomedes üê¥
Chief Difficulty:
Four mares trained to eat human flesh, incredibly savage
How Heracles Succeeded:
Fed their master Diomedes to them, which calmed them. Bound their mouths and led them back.

Location: Thrace

Significance: The poetic justice of feeding the cruel king to his own man-eating horses showed Heracles could be ruthless when necessary. The tamed horses were later released and bred normal offspring.

9
Belt of Hippolyta ‚öîÔ∏è
Chief Difficulty:
Queen of the Amazons, Hera warned them Heracles came to attack
How Heracles Succeeded:
Hippolyta was initially willing to give the belt, but Hera's interference forced a battle. Heracles won the belt in combat.

Location: Themiscyra, land of the Amazons

Significance: This labour involved the mythical warrior women, the Amazons. The belt was a symbol of Hippolyta's authority as queen, requested by Eurystheus' daughter.

10
Cattle of Geryon üêÑ
Chief Difficulty:
Geryon had three heads and three bodies joined at the waist
How Heracles Succeeded:
Killed Geryon with a poisoned arrow through all three bodies. Herded the cattle back across Europe.

Location: Erytheia, far western edge of the world

Significance: This labour took Heracles to the edge of the world. He set up the Pillars of Heracles (Gibraltar) to mark his journey. The return journey with the cattle became an epic trek across Europe.

11
Apples of the Hesperides üçé
Chief Difficulty:
Location unknown, guarded by immortal dragon Ladon and the Hesperides
How Heracles Succeeded:
Held the sky while Atlas fetched the apples. Tricked Atlas into taking the sky back by asking him to hold it while he adjusted his cloak.

Location: Garden of the Hesperides, far west beyond the sunset

Significance: These golden apples were Hera's wedding gift from Gaia. This labour required Heracles to literally hold up the heavens, showing his divine-level strength.

12
Cerberus üêï
Chief Difficulty:
Three-headed hellhound, had to capture without weapons from the Underworld
How Heracles Succeeded:
Wrestled Cerberus into submission with his bare hands, with Hades' permission. Returned him after showing Eurystheus.

Location: The Underworld

Significance: The ultimate labour - Heracles literally conquered death by entering the Underworld and returning. He was initiated into the Eleusinian Mysteries to prepare for this journey.

The metopes (square panels between triglyphs) decorated the outer colonnade of the temple, built c. 470-456 BCE. These sculptures, now in the Olympia Museum, depicted Heracles' labours as a complete cycle of redemption.

Heracles: Founder of the Olympic Games

üèÉ
The Sacred Grove: After completing his labours, Heracles established the Olympic Games at Olympia to honour his father Zeus. He marked out the sacred Altis grove and established the stadium.
üìè
The Stadium: According to tradition, Heracles measured out the original stadium length - 600 feet - by placing one foot in front of the other. This is why a stadium (stadion) became a standard Greek measurement.
üåø
The Sacred Olive: Heracles brought the wild olive from the land of the Hyperboreans to Olympia. Winners received crowns made from its branches - the most prestigious prize in the Greek world.
üèÜ
The First Competition: Heracles challenged his four brothers to a race and crowned the winner with an olive wreath, establishing the tradition of athletic competition in honour of the gods.
"Heracles... established the Olympic festival and contest, the prize for victory, and the sacrifices... He conquered all the wrestling matches and won all the crowns, for no one dared to compete against him because of his exceeding prowess."
‚Äî Diodorus Siculus, Library of History 4.14.1-2

The Meaning of the Labours

Personal Redemption

The labours transformed Heracles from a man guilty of killing his family into a purified hero. Each labour represented a step in his journey from guilt to redemption, from mortality toward immortality.

Civilising Force

By defeating monsters and clearing dangerous beasts, Heracles made the world safer for humanity. He represents civilisation triumphing over the wild, order defeating chaos.

Geographic Scope

The labours took Heracles from local challenges in the Peloponnese to the edges of the earth and even to the Underworld. This progression showed his growing power and the universal scope of his heroism.

Divine Testing

Though initiated by Hera's hatred, the labours ultimately proved Heracles worthy of divinity. By succeeding where any mortal would fail, he earned his place among the gods.

From Labour to Glory

The Twelve Labours stand as the greatest series of heroic deeds in Greek mythology. They showcase every aspect of heroism:

  • Physical strength: Wrestling lions, holding up the sky
  • Cleverness: Diverting rivers, tricking Atlas
  • Perseverance: Pursuing the hind for a year
  • Courage: Descending to the Underworld
  • Humility: Serving a cowardly king for twelve years

The representation of these labours on the Temple of Zeus at Olympia, the most important Panhellenic sanctuary, shows their central importance to Greek culture. Here, at the site where Heracles founded the Olympic Games, his labours were immortalised in stone, inspiring athletes and pilgrims for centuries.

Through these impossible tasks, Heracles proved that even the greatest sin could be redeemed through suffering and service. His labours became a template for heroism - showing that true greatness comes not from avoiding failure, but from accepting responsibility and working toward redemption.
