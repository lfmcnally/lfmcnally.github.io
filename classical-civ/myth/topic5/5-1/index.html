Step 1 — How was it founded?
Who founded the Panathenaia?
AnswerTraditionally founded by Erichthonios (a mythical early king, born from the earth) or by Theseus when he unified the towns of Attica into a single state. It was reorganised on a grand scale by the tyrant Peisistratos in 566 BC.
What were the two forms?
AnswerThe Lesser Panathenaia was held every year. The Greater Panathenaia was held every four years, like the Olympics — it was far more elaborate, with additional athletic, musical, and equestrian contests.
When was it held?
AnswerDuring the Athenian month of Hekatombaion (roughly July/August), timed for Athena's birthday. The festival lasted around six days.
Who was honoured?
AnswerAthena Polias — “Athena of the City” — patron goddess and divine protector of Athens. Her cult statue in the Erechtheion on the Acropolis was the focus of worship.

Step 2 — What happened day by day?
1Days 1–2 — Athletic Contests
Footraces, wrestling, boxing, pankration (all-in fighting), and pentathlon. Events were divided by age: boys, youths, and men. Victors won Panathenaic amphorae filled with sacred olive oil from Athena's own trees — an extremely valuable prize.
Open to all Greeks, not just Athenians
2Day 3 — Musical & Rhapsodic Contests
Recitation of Homer's Iliad and Odyssey by rhapsodes. Musical contests on the aulos (double-pipe) and kithara (lyre). Held in the Odeon, a roofed concert hall built by Pericles.
Peisistratos made Homeric recitation compulsory
3Day 4 — Tribal Contests
Lampadedromia (torch relay race), boat race, pyrrhic dance (armed war dance), and euandria (manly beauty contest). The ten Athenian tribes competed against each other — boosting civic pride and rivalry.
These were uniquely Athenian events
4Day 5 — The Pannychis
An all-night celebration with singing, dancing, and sacrifices on the Acropolis. This was a deeply religious vigil — the city stayed awake through the night to honour Athena before the climax of the festival at dawn.
Held on the eve of Athena's birthday
5Day 6 — The Great Procession & Sacrifice
The grand procession (pompe) began at the Kerameikos (city gate), wound through the Agora, and climbed to the Acropolis. A newly woven sacred peplos was carried on a ship-cart (like a sail). The climax: a hecatomb of 100 cattle sacrificed at Athena's great altar, followed by a public feast for the whole city.
The peplos depicted the Gigantomachy — gods defeating giants

Step 3 — What sacrifices were made?
What was the central sacrifice?
AnswerThe hecatomb — the ritual slaughter of 100 cattle at Athena's great altar on the Acropolis. This was the largest and most expensive sacrifice in the Athenian calendar.
How were the animals prepared?
AnswerThe cattle were garlanded (decorated with ribbons and garlands) and led in the great procession through the city to the altar. They had to appear to go willingly — a sign the gods accepted the sacrifice.
What happened to the meat?
AnswerThe meat was divided and distributed among the citizens of Athens. For many ordinary Athenians, this was a rare chance to eat meat — making the festival a genuine social occasion, not just a religious one.
What was the gods' portion?
AnswerThe thigh bones wrapped in fat were burned on the altar for Athena — the smoke rose to the gods. The people ate the rest. This division follows the mythical precedent set by Prometheus when he tricked Zeus.
Key point The hecatomb was an enormous expense and demonstrated the supreme importance of Athena to Athens. Sacrificing 100 cattle was a statement of civic wealth and devotion that no other festival could match.

Step 1 — Why was it important?
Civic Identity
ExplanationThe festival united all Athenians — citizens, metics (resident foreigners), and even freed slaves could participate. It created a shared experience that reinforced what it meant to be Athenian.
Honouring the Patron Goddess
ExplanationThe festival maintained Athena's favour through the principle of do ut des — “I give so that you may give.” By offering sacrifices and gifts, Athenians expected divine protection and prosperity in return.
Political Display
ExplanationAthens used the festival to show its wealth and power to visitors from across Greece. The scale of the hecatomb, the quality of the contests, and the beauty of the procession all projected Athenian superiority.
Social Bonding
ExplanationThe public feast after the hecatomb brought the whole community together. Sharing sacrificial meat was both a religious and social act — it created solidarity and a sense of belonging.
Cultural Celebration
ExplanationAthletic, musical, and poetic contests celebrated Athenian excellence (arete). Winning at the Panathenaia brought glory to both the individual and their tribe.
Religious Obligation
ExplanationFailure to honour Athena properly could bring divine displeasure. The Greeks believed neglecting the gods risked crop failure, plague, or military defeat. The festival was not optional — it was a duty.

Step 2 — How was Athena worshipped?
The Peplos
ExplanationA richly woven robe depicting the Gigantomachy (gods defeating the giants). It was woven over 9 months by the arrephoroi — young girls chosen from noble families. On the final day it was carried through the city and presented to Athena's ancient cult statue in the Erechtheion.
The Procession
ExplanationThe entire city came together in the pompe — from the Kerameikos, through the Agora, to the Acropolis. Every part of Athenian society had a role: kanephoroi (basket-bearers), horsemen, elders, soldiers, metics carrying trays. The procession itself was an act of worship — a visual display of the city honouring its goddess.
Contests in Athena's Honour
ExplanationExcellence in competition pleased the gods. Athletic and musical contests were not secular entertainment — they were offerings. Performing at your best in Athena's name was itself a form of worship.
The Hecatomb
ExplanationThe greatest possible sacrifice — 100 cattle — showed Athena's supreme importance. The burning of thigh bones sent the offering to the heavens. The communal feast that followed bound worshippers together in shared piety.
Prize Amphorae
ExplanationPanathenaic amphorae depicted Athena on one side and the contest event on the other. They were filled with sacred olive oil from trees said to descend from Athena's original gift to Athens. The prizes literally connected Athena to the contests.

Step 2 — Practice Questions
1. Describe how the Great Panathenaia was founded. (4 marks)
Model Answer Points
  • Traditionally founded by Erichthonios (or Theseus) in mythical times
  • Theseus credited with unifying Attica and establishing the festival
  • Reorganised by Peisistratos in 566 BC to include athletic and musical contests
  • Two forms: Lesser (annual) and Greater (every 4 years)
2. Describe what happened on each day of the Great Panathenaia. (8 marks)
Model Answer Points
  • Days 1–2: Athletic contests (footraces, wrestling, boxing, pankration, pentathlon); victors won amphorae of sacred olive oil
  • Day 3: Musical and rhapsodic contests — Homer recitation, aulos, kithara; held in the Odeon
  • Day 4: Tribal contests — lampadedromia (torch relay), boat race, pyrrhic dance, euandria; ten tribes competed
  • Day 5: Pannychis — all-night celebration with singing, dancing, sacrifices on Acropolis
  • Day 6: Great Procession from Kerameikos through Agora to Acropolis; sacred peplos on ship-cart; hecatomb of 100 cattle; public feast
3. Explain why the Great Panathenaia was important to the Athenians. (8 marks)
Model Answer Points
  • Civic identity: united citizens, metics, and freed slaves in a shared experience
  • Religious duty: maintained Athena's favour through do ut des; failure risked divine displeasure
  • Political display: showed Athens' wealth and power to visitors from across Greece
  • Social bonding: public feast after the hecatomb brought the community together; rare chance for ordinary Athenians to eat meat
  • Cultural celebration: athletic, musical, and poetic excellence honoured Athena and Athenian arete
  • Link to identity: the peplos depicted the Gigantomachy — Athens saw itself as the defender of order against chaos, just as Athena fought the giants
