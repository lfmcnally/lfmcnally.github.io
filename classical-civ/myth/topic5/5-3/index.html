Step 1 — What were its origins?
When was it held?
AnswerAnnually on 15 February — so ancient that even the Romans were unsure of its exact origins.
What was the Lupercal?
AnswerA cave on the Palatine Hill where the she-wolf (lupa) suckled Romulus and Remus.
Which god was honoured?
AnswerLupercus (sometimes identified with Faunus or Greek Pan) — god of shepherds, flocks and fertility.
Who may have founded it?
AnswerSome said Evander, a mythical Greek settler who arrived in Italy before the Trojan War.
What does the name mean?
AnswerMay come from lupus (wolf) or luere per caprum ('to purify by means of a goat').
What is februatio?
AnswerFebruary comes from februa ('instruments of purification') — the Lupercalia was a purification festival to cleanse the city before the new year (originally March).

Step 2 — What happened at the Lupercalia?
1
1The Gathering
The Luperci priests assembled at the Lupercal cave on the Palatine Hill. There were two colleges of Luperci — the Fabiani and the Quintiliani. Julius Caesar later added a third college, the Juliani.
2
2The Sacrifice
Goats and a dog were sacrificed at the cave. The goat symbolised fertility; the dog symbolised purification and guardianship.
3
3The Smearing
The sacrificial knife was touched to the foreheads of two noble young Luperci. The blood was then wiped away with milk-soaked wool. The young men had to laugh during this ritual.
4
4The Feast
The Luperci ate goat meat at the Lupercal cave — a communal feast at the most sacred site.
5
5The Run
The Luperci stripped naked (or wore goatskin loincloths), cut the goatskins into strips called februa, and ran around the ancient boundary of the Palatine, striking anyone they met.
6
6Women and the Thongs
Women of rank deliberately positioned themselves along the route to be struck by the februa. Being struck was believed to promote fertility and ease childbirth.
“At the Lupercalia… young men run through the city naked, striking those they meet with shaggy thongs. Many women of rank purposely get in their way…” Plutarch, Life of Caesar 61

Step 3 — What sacrifices were made?
What animals were sacrificed?
AnswerGoats (primary — associated with fertility) and a dog (connected to purification and guardianship).
Where did the sacrifice take place?
AnswerAt the Lupercal cave itself — the most sacred spot on the Palatine Hill.
What was the blood-and-milk ritual?
AnswerBlood smeared on foreheads = death and sacrifice; wiped with milk-soaked wool = new life and purity. The two together symbolised the cycle of death and rebirth.
What were the februa?
AnswerGoatskin thongs (strips) that carried the purifying and fertilising power of the sacrifice into the city as the Luperci ran their route.
How was this different from Greek sacrifice?
AnswerNo offerings were burned for the gods — a distinctly Roman ritual focused on direct physical contact with sacred materials rather than sending smoke to the heavens.

Step 1 — Why was it important?
Connection to Rome's Founding
ExplanationDirectly linked to Romulus, Remus and the she-wolf — the Lupercalia celebrated the very origins of Rome itself.
Purification (Februatio)
ExplanationCleansed the city as the old year ended and the new one (originally beginning in March) approached. A fresh start for all of Rome.
Fertility
ExplanationIn a society with high infant mortality, promoting fertility was vital to the survival of families and the state. The striking with februa was believed to bless women with children.
Tradition (Mos Maiorum)
ExplanationSo ancient it was considered essential to maintain — abandoning it would mean breaking with the ancestors and risking divine displeasure.
Community Participation
ExplanationThe whole community was involved — the Luperci running through the streets, women along the route, crowds watching. It brought Romans together.
Remarkable Survival
ExplanationStill celebrated in the 5th century AD, long after Rome became Christian. Pope Gelasius I finally banned it in 494 AD — a testament to its deep hold on Roman identity.

Step 2 — How was Lupercus worshipped?
Physical Ritual
ExplanationLupercus was worshipped through direct physical ritual, not formal temple worship. The Lupercalia was intensely physical and embodied — blood, running, striking and feasting.
The Luperci Priests
ExplanationDedicated servants of the god — young men of aristocratic families who became the living instruments of Lupercus's power during the festival.
The Sacred Run
ExplanationBy tracing the boundary of Rome's oldest settlement on the Palatine, the Luperci renewed the city's protective boundary — a physical act of divine guardianship.
Striking with Februa
ExplanationThe goatskin thongs distributed the god's blessing — this was how worshippers received divine favour for fertility and purification.
Sacrifice of Goats
ExplanationHonoured Lupercus as a pastoral god of flocks and herds — the goat was his sacred animal and its sacrifice the central act of worship.
The Lupercal Cave
ExplanationThe most sacred site — the ritual always centred here, tying worship of Lupercus permanently to Rome's very foundations.
Ritual Laughter
ExplanationThe young Luperci had to laugh when blood was smeared on their foreheads — this may have symbolised joy, vitality and the life-force that Lupercus embodied.
Explain Skill Emphasise the physical, embodied nature of worship — not quiet prayer or temple offerings, but blood, running, striking and laughter.

Step 2 — Practice Questions
1. Describe the origins of the Lupercalia. (4 marks)
Model Answer The Lupercalia was an ancient Roman festival held annually on 15 February. It took place at the Lupercal cave on the Palatine Hill — the site where, according to legend, the she-wolf suckled Romulus and Remus. The festival honoured Lupercus, a god of shepherds and fertility sometimes identified with Faunus. Some Romans believed it was founded by Evander, a mythical Greek settler. The name may derive from lupus (wolf) or from luere per caprum ('to purify by means of a goat'), reflecting its dual nature as a festival of both foundation myth and purification.
2. Describe what happened at the Lupercalia. (8 marks)
Model Answer The Luperci priests gathered at the Lupercal cave, organised into two colleges (Fabiani and Quintiliani). Goats and a dog were sacrificed there — the goat for fertility and the dog for purification. Blood from the sacrificial knife was smeared on the foreheads of two young noble Luperci, then wiped off with milk-soaked wool; the young men had to laugh during this. After feasting on goat meat at the cave, the Luperci stripped naked (or wore goatskin loincloths) and cut the goatskins into strips called februa. They then ran around the boundary of the Palatine Hill, striking anyone they met. Women of rank deliberately positioned themselves along the route to be struck, as this was believed to promote fertility and ease childbirth. Plutarch describes how “many women of rank purposely get in their way.”
3. Explain why the Lupercalia was important to the Romans. (8 marks)
Model Answer The Lupercalia was important because it was directly connected to Rome's founding myth — the cave where Romulus and Remus were suckled. It served as a purification ritual (februatio) that cleansed the city before the new year. In a society with high infant mortality, its fertility rites were vital. It embodied mos maiorum (the custom of the ancestors) — so ancient that abandoning it would mean breaking with tradition and risking the gods' displeasure. It involved the whole community, from aristocratic Luperci to women along the route, binding Romans together. Its remarkable survival into the 5th century AD (banned only in 494 by Pope Gelasius I) shows just how deeply it was woven into Roman identity.
