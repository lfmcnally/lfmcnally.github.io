Step 1 — What were its origins?
Who was Saturn?
AnswerGod of agriculture, wealth, and time. His temple at the foot of the Capitoline Hill also served as the state treasury (aerarium).
When was it held?
AnswerBegan 17 December, eventually extended to last up to a week (through 23 December).
What was the Golden Age?
AnswerSaturn once ruled Italy during the Aurea Aetas — a mythical time of peace, equality, abundance, no slavery, and no private property.
Who established it?
AnswerTraditions credited Tullus Hostilius (7th century BC) or said it predated Rome's founding.
Why this time of year?
AnswerAround the winter solstice — celebrating the return of longer days and hope of agricultural renewal.

Step 2 — What happened at the Saturnalia?
1Opening Ceremony
A public sacrifice at the Temple of Saturn, followed by a great public banquet (convivium publicum) open to all.
2The Greeting
"Io Saturnalia!" rang through the streets — the standard greeting used by everyone everywhere during the festival.
3Change of Dress
Togas were set aside for colourful, informal synthesis garments. The pilleus (felt cap of freedom) was worn — a symbolic abolition of social distinctions.
4Role Reversal
Masters served their slaves at dinner. Slaves spoke freely, dined with masters, and gave orders. This re-enacted the equality of the Golden Age.
5Gambling
Dice games, normally illegal, were freely permitted. All classes played together.
6Gift-Giving (Sigillaria)
Romans exchanged small clay or wax figurines (sigillaria), candles (cerei), writing tablets, and dice. Extra days were added to the festival specifically for this.
7Feasting and Drinking
Lavish meals were shared, wine flowed freely, and normal rules of modesty were relaxed.
8Mock King
A Saturnalicius Princeps was chosen by lot. He gave absurd commands that had to be obeyed.
9Public Holiday
Schools closed, courts didn't sit, and no wars were declared.
"It is now the month of December, when the greatest part of the city is in a bustle. Loose reins are given to public dissipation..." — Seneca, Epistles 18.1

Step 3 — What sacrifices were made?
Where was the central sacrifice?
AnswerAt the Temple of Saturn in the Roman Forum on 17 December.
What was unusual about the sacrifice?
AnswerPerformed ritu Graeco (in the Greek manner) — the priest's head was uncovered, unlike normal Roman practice of covering the head. This is evidence of the festival's great antiquity.
What happened to Saturn's statue?
AnswerHis feet were normally bound with woollen bands. At Saturnalia, these were ceremonially removed, symbolising the liberation of the Golden Age.
What was the lectisternium?
AnswerA sacred banquet for the gods — couches were set out and a meal laid before Saturn's statue, as if the god were dining.
Were there private sacrifices?
AnswerYes — individual households made private offerings and libations to Saturn.

Step 2 — Practice Questions
1. Describe the origins of the Saturnalia. (4 marks)
Model Answer The Saturnalia was an ancient Roman festival held in honour of Saturn, the god of agriculture, wealth, and time. It was held around the winter solstice, beginning on 17 December and eventually lasting up to a week. The festival was believed to recall the Golden Age (Aurea Aetas), when Saturn ruled Italy and there was peace, equality, and abundance with no slavery or private property. Tradition credited its establishment to Tullus Hostilius in the 7th century BC, though some sources said it predated Rome's founding.
2. Describe what happened at the Saturnalia. (8 marks)
Model Answer The Saturnalia opened with a public sacrifice at the Temple of Saturn, followed by a great public banquet (convivium publicum) open to all. The greeting "Io Saturnalia!" rang through the streets. Romans set aside their togas for colourful synthesis garments and wore the pilleus (felt cap of freedom), symbolically abolishing social distinctions. In a dramatic role reversal, masters served their slaves at dinner, and slaves could speak freely and give orders — re-enacting the Golden Age. Dice games, normally illegal, were freely permitted. Romans exchanged gifts called sigillaria (small clay figurines), candles, writing tablets, and dice. Lavish feasting and drinking took place, with normal rules relaxed. A Saturnalicius Princeps (mock king) was chosen by lot and gave absurd commands. Schools closed, courts did not sit, and no wars were declared.
3. Explain why the Saturnalia was important to the Romans. (8 marks)
Model Answer The Saturnalia was the most popular festival in the Roman calendar — Catullus called it "the best of days." It served as a vital safety valve in Rome's hierarchical society, allowing tensions between masters and slaves to be released in a controlled, festive way. The festival had deep agricultural significance, as Saturn was the god of sowing and the timing around the winter solstice celebrated the completion of autumn planting and the hope of renewal. Celebrating was part of mos maiorum — the ancestral customs that defined Roman identity. The gift-giving and public feasting strengthened community bonds across all social classes. Honouring Saturn was also a religious duty, maintaining his favour for the prosperity of agriculture and the state. The Saturnalia's legacy extended far beyond Rome: many of its traditions — gift-giving, feasting, greenery, and candles — were absorbed into Christmas celebrations.
