Step 2 — What happened day by day?
1Day 1: The Proagon (Preview)
A public preview held in the Odeon. Playwrights presented their actors unmasked and announced the subjects of their plays. This built excitement and allowed audiences to choose which performances to attend.
☾Day 1 Evening: The Eisagoge (Bringing-In)
The wooden cult statue of Dionysus was carried from its temple to the road leading to Eleutherae, then brought back to Athens by torchlight. The epheboi (young citizen-soldiers) accompanied it with singing and dancing — re-enacting Dionysus’s original arrival in the city.
2Day 2: Grand Procession (Pompe)
Citizens and metics (resident foreigners) marched through Athens carrying phalloi — large model phalluses sacred to Dionysus. A bull, garlanded with ribbons, was led to sacrifice. Wine was carried and drunk freely. The procession displayed the city’s wealth, piety and unity.
♪Day 2 After: Dithyrambic Contests
10 choruses of 50 men and 10 choruses of 50 boys — 1,000 performers in total — competed singing dithyrambs: choral songs and dances in honour of Dionysus. Each of the 10 Athenian tribes entered one men’s and one boys’ chorus.
3–5Days 3–5: Tragic Contests
Three playwrights each presented a tetralogy: three tragedies plus a satyr play. The great tragedians — Aeschylus, Sophocles and Euripides — competed here. Judges were selected by lot to prevent bribery, and a winner was crowned with ivy.
6Day 6: Comic Contests
Five comedies were performed in a single day. Aristophanes was the most famous comic playwright. Comedy could openly mock politicians, generals and public figures — a remarkable freedom of speech unique to Athenian democracy.

Step 3 — What sacrifices were made?
What was sacrificed?
AnswerA bull in the orchestra (dancing area) of the Theatre of Dionysus before performances.
How was it prepared?
AnswerGarlanded with ribbons, led in the grand procession.
What were the libations?
AnswerDrink offerings of wine poured to Dionysus before every performance — wine was his sacred gift.
Why was sacrifice necessary?
AnswerBlood consecrated the performance space as sacred ground. Without it, performances could not proceed.
What was the religious meaning?
AnswerReminded the audience that plays were religious acts, not mere entertainment — every performance was an offering to Dionysus.

Step 1 — Why was it important?
Democratic Showcase
ExplanationDisplayed Athens’ organisation, wealth and cultural achievement to its own citizens and the wider Greek world.
Imperial Display
ExplanationAllies brought tribute, which was publicly displayed in the theatre — a statement of Athenian power and dominance.
Civic Participation
ExplanationMassive involvement: citizens served as actors, chorus members, choregoi (wealthy sponsors), and audience. The festival was a communal effort.
Religious Duty
ExplanationHonouring Dionysus maintained his favour and the city’s prosperity. Neglecting the god risked divine punishment.
Cultural Prestige
ExplanationProduced the greatest works of Western literature — the tragedies and comedies that are still studied and performed today.
Social Cohesion
ExplanationPrisoners were released for the festival. The theorikon fund paid poor citizens’ tickets, ensuring everyone could attend regardless of wealth.
International Audience
ExplanationHeld in spring as the sailing season reopened, attracting visitors from across the Greek world who witnessed Athenian greatness first-hand.

Step 2 — How was Dionysus worshipped?
Drama as worship
ExplanationDramatic performances were acts of worship — rituals performed in his honour. The god was believed to be present, watching from his seat in the theatre.
Procession & symbols
ExplanationThe procession honoured him by re-enacting his arrival and displaying his symbols: phalloi, wine and ivy.
Dithyrambs
ExplanationDithyrambs were choral hymns specifically praising Dionysus — 1,000 performers sang and danced in his honour.
The Eisagoge
ExplanationThe Eisagoge re-created Dionysus’s mythical journey from Eleutherae each year, symbolically bringing the god into the city afresh.
Sacred precinct
ExplanationThe Theatre of Dionysus was a sacred precinct — a temple stood beside the theatre, and the priest of Dionysus had a front-row seat (prohedria).
Ivy & wine
ExplanationIvy wreaths and wine — both sacred to Dionysus — were worn and drunk as acts of worship throughout the festival.
God of transformation
ExplanationDionysus was the god of transformation, masks and performance — drama was his domain. Wearing masks and becoming another character was itself a Dionysiac act.

Step 2 — Practice Questions
1. Describe how the City Dionysia was founded. (4 marks)
Model Answer The City Dionysia was established by the tyrant Peisistratos around 534 BC. He brought the cult of Dionysus Eleuthereus from Eleutherae, a town on the Athenian-Boeotian border, to Athens. A wooden cult statue was placed in a temple near the Theatre of Dionysus on the south slope of the Acropolis. The festival was held annually in the month of Elaphebolion (late March/early April). The first winner of the tragic competition was Thespis in 534 BC, who invented the actor by adding a single speaker separate from the chorus.
2. Describe what happened at the City Dionysia. (8 marks)
Model Answer The festival began with the Proagon, a public preview in the Odeon where playwrights presented their actors unmasked and announced their subjects. That evening, the Eisagoge took place: the cult statue of Dionysus was carried to the road to Eleutherae and brought back by torchlight with singing and dancing by the epheboi. The next day, a grand procession (Pompe) passed through Athens — citizens and metics carried phalloi, a garlanded bull was led to sacrifice, and wine was carried and drunk. After the procession, dithyrambic contests were held with 10 choruses of 50 men and 10 of 50 boys (1,000 performers). Over the following three days, three tragedians each presented a tetralogy (three tragedies plus a satyr play). Judges were selected by lot. On the final day, five comedies were performed. The bull was sacrificed in the orchestra before performances, and libations of wine were poured to Dionysus.
3. Explain why the City Dionysia was important to the Athenians. (8 marks)
Model Answer The City Dionysia was important for several reasons. Religiously, it honoured Dionysus Eleuthereus through sacrifice, procession, dithyrambs and dramatic performance — maintaining the god’s favour. Politically, it was a democratic showcase: Athens displayed its wealth, organisation and cultural achievement to citizens and international visitors. The public display of allied tribute in the theatre was an imperial statement of power. Socially, the festival unified the city — prisoners were released, and the theorikon fund ensured even the poorest could attend. Culturally, the tragic and comic competitions produced the greatest works of Western literature by Aeschylus, Sophocles, Euripides and Aristophanes. The massive civic participation as actors, chorus members, choregoi and audience made the Dionysia the single most important event in the Athenian calendar.
