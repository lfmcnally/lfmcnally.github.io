Hercules and Achelous

The Wrestling Match for Deianira
Ovid, Metamorphoses Book 9, Lines 1-88

1-3Theseus, the hero, reputed son of Neptune, asked Acheloüs why he had sighed,
4-5and the reason for his damaged forehead: to which the Calydonian river-god, his
6-7uncut hair wreathed with reeds, replied: 'You ask something painful of me. Who
8-9wants to recall the battles he has lost? But, I will tell it as it happened: since the
10-11shame of being beaten is no less than the honour of having fought. It is a great
12consolation to me that the victor was so famous.

13-14If her name has ever come to your notice, Deianira was once the most beautiful
15-16girl, and the jealous hope of many suitors. When, with them, I entered Oeneus's
17-18house, her father, and the man I sought as my father-in-law, I said: "Accept me
19-20as your son-in-law, son of Parthaon." Hercules, scion of Alceus, said the same.
21-22The others gave way before the two of us. Hercules declared that he could
23-24offer Jove as his bride's father-in-law, spoke of his famous labours, and of how
25-26he had survived what his stepmother, Juno, had prescribed for him. On my side
27-28I said: "It would be shameful for a god to concede to a mortal" – He was not yet a
29-30god – "In me you see the lord of the waters, that flow in winding rivers, through
31-32your kingdom. As your son-in-law I would not be a stranger sent from a foreign
33-34shore, but a native, and wedded to your own interests. Only don't let it harm my
35-36case that Queen Juno does not hate me, and all the punishment of the labours,
37she demanded, passed me by!"
• • •
38-39"Now, listen, Hercules, you, son of Alcmena: Jupiter, whose child you boast of
40-41being, is either wrongly called your father, or is truly a wrongdoer. You seek your
42-43father in a mother's adultery. Choose whether you prefer this fiction of Jove as
44-45a father, or to be born the son of shame." As I spoke, he gazed at me fiercely, all
46-47the while, and unable to act like a man and control his blazing anger, he merely
48-49replied in these words: "My right hand is more powerful than my tongue. As
50-51long as I beat you at wrestling, you can win the talking", and he came at me
52-53ferociously. I was ashamed to retreat, after my words: I took off my green robes;
54-55put up my arms; held my hands, fingers curved, in front of my chest in fighting
56-57stance; and readied my limbs for the match. He caught up dust in the hollow of
58-59his hands and threw it over me, and, in turn, was, himself, gilded by the yellow
60-61sand. Now he caught at my neck, or you might think he caught me, now at my
62-63legs, now at my loins: and attacked me from every side. My weight protected
64-65me, and his attempts were useless. I was like a massive pile that the roaring
66-67flood assaults with all its might: it remains, secure in its own bulk.
• • •
68-69We pulled away for a moment, returned to the conflict, and stood firm,
70-71determined not to concede. Foot was set against foot, and I pushed at him, with
72-73my chest full forward, fingers locked with fingers, and head to head. I have seen
74-75two strong bulls come together like that, when they try for the sleekest heifer
76-77in the pasture as their prize in the contest. The herd watches in fear, not sure to
78-79which one victory will grant overriding supremacy. Three times without success
80-81Hercules tried to push my gleaming chest away from him. At the fourth attempt,
82-83he broke my grip, loosed himself from my constricting arms, and with a blow of
84-85his hand – certainly, I myself confess it is the truth – he turned me about, and
86-87clung, with all his weight, to my back.
