What is a Sanctuary?

A sanctuary was a holy area located in a town or city that contained one or more temples and altars to the gods. Famous examples include Olympia and the Acropolis in Athens.

Sacred Space and Purification

Sanctuaries had a freshwater source at the entrance so that Greeks could wash their hands and feet. This was crucial to avoid religious pollution of the holy sites, which would discourage favour from the gods.

Temple Functions Within Sanctuaries

A temple was dedicated to a specific god and housed a cult statue of that deity. Temples gave gods a residence on earth, which was important for gaining favour in their eyes.

Crucially, only priests were allowed into temples. The general public worshipped at the altar outside the temple, not inside the building itself.

Community and Political Functions

Beyond religious purposes, sanctuaries served crucial community functions:

  • Festival venues: Major religious celebrations brought the community together
  • Political gatherings: Important civic decisions were often made in sacred spaces
  • Economic centres: Markets and trade often occurred around sanctuaries
  • Civic identity: Local sanctuaries helped define community belonging and pride
  • International diplomacy: Major sanctuaries like Olympia hosted people from across the Greek world

Sanctuary (Greek: hieron)

A holy area containing temples, altars, and other sacred buildings dedicated to one or more gods.

Sanctuaries could be:

  • Urban: Within cities (like the Athenian Acropolis)
  • Rural: In the countryside (like Olympia)
  • Panhellenic: Serving all Greeks (the "Big Four": Olympia, Delphi, Nemea, Isthmia)

Olympia

One of the most famous sanctuaries in the ancient world, located in the Peloponnese.

Key features:

  • Sacred to Zeus
  • Site of the Olympic Games (from 776 BC)
  • Temple of Zeus with its famous chryselephantine statue
  • International peace during the games (Olympic Truce)

The Athenian Acropolis

The sacred hill overlooking Athens, crowned with temples to Athena.

Major buildings:

  • The Parthenon (Athena Parthenos)
  • The Erechtheion (Athena Polias)
  • Temple of Athena Nike
  • The Propylaea (monumental gateway)

Religious Pollution (miasma)

A state of spiritual contamination that made a person unfit for religious activities.

Pollution could be removed through:

  • Ritual washing with water
  • Waiting for specific time periods
  • Specific purification ceremonies
  • Offering sacrifices

Cult Statue

A statue of a deity that was believed to house the god's presence on earth.

Famous examples:

  • Athena Parthenos in the Parthenon (12m tall, gold and ivory)
  • Zeus at Olympia (one of the Seven Wonders)
  • Athena Polias on the Acropolis (olive wood, very ancient)

Greek Sanctuary Characteristics

Key features:

  • Emphasis on purification and cleanliness
  • Open-air worship at altars
  • Major festivals bringing communities together
  • Sacred groves and natural features
  • Treasuries holding dedications from cities

Roman Sanctuary Characteristics

Key features:

  • Focus on maintaining pax deorum (peace of the gods)
  • State-controlled religion
  • Temples on high podia (platforms)
  • Formal rituals and correct procedures
  • Integration with political power

Temple Functions

Temples served multiple purposes:

  • Divine residence: Housing the cult statue
  • Treasury: Storing valuable offerings
  • Status symbol: Demonstrating city wealth and piety
  • Political tool: Showing divine approval of leaders

Altars

The most important element for worship - more crucial than the temple itself.

Types:

  • Block altars: Simple stone platforms
  • Monumental altars: Large decorated structures
  • Portable altars: For temporary ceremonies

Worship Functions

Sanctuaries provided spaces for various religious activities:

  • Daily offerings and prayers
  • Major festivals and processions
  • Life-cycle ceremonies (birth, marriage, death)
  • Seeking oracles and divine guidance

Purification Practices

Essential for maintaining the sanctity of sacred spaces:

  • Washing hands and feet at entrances
  • Wearing clean, appropriate clothing
  • Abstaining from certain activities beforehand
  • Ritual sprinkling with holy water

Community Functions

Sanctuaries as social centres:

  • Religious festivals bringing people together
  • Athletic competitions and cultural events
  • Political assemblies and civic meetings
  • Trade and economic activity

Treasury Functions

Sanctuaries as secure storage:

  • Valuable offerings from individuals and cities
  • War spoils dedicated to gods
  • State treasuries (like in the Parthenon)
  • Sacred objects and ritual equipment

üß≠ Temple Orientation

Greek temples were carefully aligned east-west for religious and practical reasons:

  • Eastern entrance: Welcomed the rising sun
  • Western rear: Faced the setting sun
  • Symbolic meaning: Connected to daily cycle of light/darkness
  • Practical benefit: Morning light illuminated the cult statue
  • Altar alignment: Positioned on the same axis as the temple

üìê Temple Elevation

Temples were deliberately raised above ground level:

  • Stylobate: The top step platform supporting columns
  • Stereobate: The lower foundation steps
  • Visual impact: Made temples appear more impressive
  • Sacred separation: Elevated gods above mortal level
  • Practical drainage: Protected building from water damage

üèõÔ∏è Colonnade (Peristyle)

The surrounding columns served multiple purposes:

  • Structural support: Held up the roof and entablature
  • Weather protection: Sheltered walls from rain
  • Aesthetic beauty: Created rhythm and visual harmony
  • Sacred enclosure: Defined the temple's sacred space
  • Processional space: Allowed movement around the temple
