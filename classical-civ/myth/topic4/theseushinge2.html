Why do we study Theseus as a foundation story for Athens?

1
He brought the separate communities of Attica together under Athens as one city-state.
That's right ‚úì

This is the synoikismos ("dwelling together"). Before Theseus, there were many separate communities across Attica, each with their own leaders and councils. Theseus united them all under Athens as a single city-state. That act of political unification is why the Athenians treated him as their founder hero.

2
He became the first king of Athens and created its laws and system of government.
Close, but not quite

Theseus was a king of Athens, so this sounds right. But he wasn't the first king (his father Aegeus ruled before him), and the key point is that he unified the communities, not that he created laws. This answer confuses political reform with the act of bringing people together. The foundation story is about unity, not legislation.

3
He proved Athens was the strongest city in Greece by defeating the Minotaur on Crete.
Common misconception

The Minotaur is Theseus's most famous story, so it's tempting to pick this. Defeating the Minotaur was a great achievement and it did free Athens from the tribute. But it's a story about personal heroism, not about founding or building a city. The reason we call Theseus a foundation story is specifically because he unified the separate communities of Attica into one city-state.

4
He made Athens powerful by completing his labours and removing threats from the region.
Nearly there, but...

The labours did make the region safer, and that did help Athens. But making a place safer isn't the same as founding it. Lots of heroes defeated monsters and bandits across Greece. What makes Theseus special to Athens is the synoikismos: he took separate, independent communities and brought them together as one united city-state. That's the foundation story.
