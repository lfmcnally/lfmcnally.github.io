Common misconception

The kylix looks decorative to us, but it was a functional object designed to be used. Ancient Athenians drank from it. Thinking of it as decoration projects a modern idea (like hanging a plate on a wall) onto an ancient context.

2
It was made to honour Theseus at a religious festival.
Common misconception

Because you've studied temples and the naming of Athens, it's natural to connect Theseus with religious practice. But the kylix was not a religious offering or cult object. It was used in a social setting (the symposium). Theseus was celebrated here through storytelling over wine, not through worship.

3
It was made for drinking wine at a symposium.

A kylix is a drinking cup. It was used at a symposium, a drinking party for elite Athenian men where they would drink wine, discuss ideas, and tell stories. The images of Theseus would have been seen and discussed as the guests drank.

4
It was made to teach young Athenians about their history.
Common misconception

This projects a modern idea onto the ancient world. We use images in textbooks to teach, but the kylix wasn't made for children or for education. It was made for adult men at a symposium. The stories were entertainment and a celebration of Athenian identity, not a teaching resource.

Why is the Minotaur shown in the centre (tondo) of the kylix rather than around the edge with the other labours?

It was the first adventure Theseus went on.
Common misconception

The Minotaur was actually after the six labours on the road. Theseus completed all six labours travelling to Athens, and only then volunteered to go to Crete. The centre placement is about importance, not chronological order.

2
It was his most important achievement and the tondo is the most prominent position.
That's right âœ“

The tondo (centre) is the most significant position on a kylix. The Minotaur was Theseus's greatest deed because it freed Athens from the tribute. The artist made a deliberate choice to place it centrally, and the image would be gradually revealed as the drinker emptied the cup.

3
The Minotaur was the biggest scene so it needed more space in the middle.
Common misconception

This treats the layout as a practical decision about fitting images in. But the tondo is actually a small circular space. The artist chose to place the Minotaur here because of significance, not size. The arrangement is a deliberate artistic and cultural choice about what matters most.

4
The artist painted the Minotaur first and then added the labours around it afterwards.
Common misconception

This assumes the layout is about the order the artist worked in. But the composition of the kylix was planned in advance. Ancient potters and painters designed the whole layout before painting. The placement is a deliberate artistic decision, not an accident of process.

Hinge Question 3
After: Independent work on source questions

Checking: I can explain what the kylix tells us about how the Athenians viewed Theseus.

What is the most important reason the Athenians placed Theseus's adventures on a kylix?

ğŸ–ï¸ Hold up 1, 2, 3, or 4 fingers to show your answer.
1
Theseus was a real person and they wanted to remember what he actually did.
Common misconception

Theseus is a mythological figure. The Athenians may have believed he existed, but the stories about him are myths. The kylix isn't a factual record. It's a celebration of what Theseus represented to the Athenians: their identity, their values, and their pride as a city.

2
The stories were exciting and would entertain guests at the symposium.
Nearly there, but...

Entertainment was part of the symposium, so this isn't wrong exactly. But it stays on the surface. The choice of Theseus specifically is what matters. There were many exciting myths they could have used. They chose Theseus because he was Athens's hero, and his deeds reflected the values Athenians were proud of.

3
The kylix was expensive and the scenes of Theseus made it worth more money.
Common misconception

Fine pottery was valuable, but the scenes weren't chosen to increase the price. The imagery was chosen because of its cultural significance. An Athenian symposium guest would value the kylix for what it said about shared identity and values, not simply as a luxury item to show off wealth.

4
Theseus represented Athenian values like bravery, justice, and cleverness, and the symposium was where citizens celebrated their shared identity.
That's right âœ“

The kylix connected the act of drinking at a symposium with Athenian civic pride. Theseus was Athens's founding hero, and his adventures showed the qualities the Athenians valued: courage, poetic justice, skill, and selflessness. Placing him on a drinking cup meant these values were seen, discussed, and celebrated at the heart of Athenian social life.
