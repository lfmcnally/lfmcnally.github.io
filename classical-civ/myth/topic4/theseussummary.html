Part I

Early Life

The boy who grew up not knowing he was a prince

Theseus was raised in Troezen, a city in the southern Peloponnese, by his mother Aethra. His father was Aegeus, king of Athens, though some versions of the myth say the god Poseidon was his true father. This possible divine parentage made him a potential demi-god, like Heracles.

Before Aegeus returned to Athens, he left his sword and sandals hidden beneath a heavy rock. He told Aethra that when their son was old enough and strong enough to lift the rock, he should take the sword and sandals and travel to Athens to claim his birthright as prince.

When Theseus was old enough, he lifted the rock, took the sword and sandals, and set out for Athens. Rather than taking the safe sea route, he chose the dangerous coastal road, which was plagued by bandits and monsters.

Why the dangerous road?

Theseus wanted to prove himself as a hero. He was inspired by the stories of Heracles, who had won fame through his twelve labours. Theseus wanted to achieve similar glory by clearing the road of villains, and in doing so, prove he was worthy of being recognised as the son of Aegeus and as a prince of Athens.

Part II

The Journey to Athens

Six labours on the coastal road from Troezen

On his journey from Troezen to Athens, Theseus encountered six villains who terrorised travellers along the road. He defeated each one, often turning the villain's own method of killing against them. This pattern is sometimes called 'poetic justice', and it was important to how the Athenians understood Theseus as a hero: he was not merely strong, but clever and just.

The six labours, in order, were:

Labour 1

Periphetes, the Club-Bearer

Epidaurus

Periphetes was a son of Hephaestus who lurked on the road near Epidaurus. He carried a huge bronze club and used it to beat travellers to death.

Theseus took the club from Periphetes and killed him with it. He kept the club as a weapon and carried it with him for the rest of his journey, just as Heracles carried his lion skin.

Note on the kylix

Periphetes is often identified as the labour not shown on the Theseus Kylix. The kylix depicts five labours around the tondo; Periphetes is commonly the one omitted, perhaps because his defeat (club-beating) was less visually distinctive than the others.

Labour 2

Sinis, the Pine-Bender

Isthmus of Corinth

Sinis was known as the pine-bender. He would bend two pine trees down to the ground and tie a traveller between them. When he released the trees, they sprang apart and tore the victim in two.

Theseus used Sinis's own method against him. He bent the pine trees and catapulted Sinis to his death in exactly the same way Sinis had killed his victims.

Labour 3

The Crommyonian Sow (Phaia)

Crommyon, near Corinth

The Crommyonian Sow was a monstrous wild pig that terrorised the area around Crommyon. It was sometimes called Phaia after the old woman who was said to have raised it.

Theseus hunted the creature and killed it with his sword.

How is this labour different?

This is the only labour where the opponent is a beast rather than a human villain. Because it is an animal, Theseus cannot use 'poetic justice' here. He simply has to overpower and kill it. This labour is more like one of Heracles's labours, several of which involved defeating dangerous creatures.

Labour 4

Skiron

The Scironian Cliffs

Skiron sat on the cliffs along the coastal road and forced travellers to kneel and wash his feet. While they were kneeling at the edge of the cliff, he kicked them off into the sea, where a giant sea turtle waited below to devour them.

Theseus refused to wash Skiron's feet, threw him off the cliff, and let him be eaten by his own turtle. Once again, the villain met the same fate he had inflicted on others.

Labour 5

Kerkyon

Eleusis

Kerkyon was a powerful wrestler who stood on the road near Eleusis and challenged every traveller to a wrestling match. He was immensely strong and killed all those who lost to him, which was everyone.

Theseus accepted the challenge but defeated Kerkyon through superior skill and technique rather than brute strength. He lifted Kerkyon into the air and threw him to the ground, killing him.

What does this tell us?

This labour shows that Theseus was not just physically strong. He was skilful and clever. The Athenians valued intelligence and technique alongside courage, and this story illustrated those qualities in their hero.

Labour 6

Procrustes (Damastes)

Near Athens, on the Sacred Way

Procrustes (also called Damastes) pretended to be a generous host. He offered travellers a bed to sleep in. But when they lay down, if they were too short for the bed, he stretched their bodies to fit. If they were too tall, he cut off their legs or feet until they matched the bed exactly. No one ever fitted, and all his guests died.

Theseus forced Procrustes to lie on his own bed and then cut him to fit it, giving him the same treatment he had given his victims.

The pattern of poetic justice

Procrustes is the last of the six labours before Theseus reaches Athens. By this point, the pattern of poetic justice is very clear. In labour after labour, Theseus defeats each villain using their own method of killing. This tells us that the Athenians valued a hero who brought fitting punishment to wrongdoers. It was not enough to simply be strong; a hero should ensure that the punishment fits the crime.

Part III

Theseus and the Minotaur

The greatest deed — Crete and the Labyrinth

After arriving in Athens and being recognised by his father Aegeus, Theseus learned of the terrible tribute that Athens was forced to pay to King Minos of Crete. Every nine years (or, in some versions, every year), Athens had to send seven young men and seven young women to Crete, where they were sent into the Labyrinth and devoured by the Minotaur, a creature that was half-man and half-bull.

This tribute had been imposed because Minos's son Androgeos had been killed during a visit to Athens (either murdered or killed while fighting the Bull of Marathon). Minos held Athens responsible and demanded the tribute as punishment.

Theseus volunteered to go as one of the fourteen young Athenians. Before entering the Labyrinth, Ariadne, the daughter of King Minos, fell in love with him and gave him a ball of string (sometimes called a 'clew') so that he could find his way back out after killing the Minotaur.

Theseus entered the Labyrinth, found the Minotaur at the centre, and killed it with his bare hands. He then followed the thread back out of the Labyrinth and escaped from Crete with the other Athenian youths and with Ariadne.

Why is this Theseus's greatest deed?

The Minotaur was not just a personal challenge like the labours on the road. By killing it, Theseus freed Athens from a humiliating tribute and saved the lives of the young Athenians who would have been sent to die. This made him a protector and saviour of his city, which is why the Athenians honoured him as their greatest hero.

Part IV

The Theseus Kylix

Prescribed source — British Museum 1850,0302.3

Object
A kylix: a wide, shallow drinking cup with two handles
Technique
Attic red-figure pottery
Date
c.440–430 BC (mid-5th century BC)
Location
British Museum, London (accession number 1850,0302.3)
Original use
Drinking wine at a symposium (a drinking party for elite Athenian men)

The kylix shows the adventures of Theseus. In the tondo (the circular scene in the centre of the cup), Theseus is shown defeating the Minotaur. Around the inside of the cup, five of his six labours are depicted (Periphetes is usually the one omitted).

Why is the Minotaur in the centre?

The tondo is the most important and prominent position on a kylix. The Minotaur was Theseus's greatest achievement, so it belongs in the most significant spot. As the drinker emptied the cup, the Minotaur scene would be gradually revealed as the wine level dropped, creating a dramatic visual experience. The labours around the edge would have been visible from the start, showing the journey that led to the climactic encounter in the centre.

Why put Theseus on a drinking cup?

The symposium was an important social occasion for wealthy Athenian men, where they drank wine, discussed ideas, and told stories. Placing Theseus's deeds on a kylix meant that his heroic achievements would be seen, discussed, and celebrated during these gatherings. It connected the act of drinking with Athenian identity and civic pride. The owner of this kylix was displaying his cultural knowledge and his connection to Athens's founding mythology.

What does the kylix tell us about Athenian values?

The way Theseus is depicted on the kylix tells us what the Athenians admired in a hero. They valued bravery (he chose the dangerous road and volunteered against the Minotaur), justice (he punished each villain in a fitting way), cleverness (he defeated Kerkyon through skill rather than brute strength), and selflessness (he risked his life to free Athens from the tribute). Theseus is shown as a civilising force who cleared the roads of dangers and protected ordinary people.
