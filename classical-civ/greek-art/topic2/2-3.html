The High Classical Period

The High Classical period (c. 450-400 BCE) represents the pinnacle of Greek sculptural achievement. This was the age of Pericles in Athens, the construction of the Parthenon, and the development of the 'Classical ideal' - the perfect balance of naturalism and idealisation.

Two sculptors exemplify this period's achievements: Polykleitos of Argos, who theorised the perfect proportions of the human body, and Myron of Athens, who captured the body in dynamic motion.

Remember
The original bronze statues by Polykleitos and Myron are lost. We know them only through Roman marble copies. Always mention this in essays - it affects our understanding of the works.

Doryphoros (Spear-Bearer)
Original by Polykleitos c. 450-440 BCE | Roman marble copy | Height: 212 cm | Naples National Archaeological Museum

Roman marble copy of Polykleitos' Doryphoros - the embodiment of his Canon. Image: Wikimedia Commons

The Canon
Polykleitos wrote a theoretical treatise called the Canon ('rule' or 'measure') establishing ideal proportions. The Doryphoros was created as a demonstration of these principles - it was literally called "the Canon".
Contrapposto
Perfected contrapposto: weight on right leg, left leg relaxed. This creates a complex series of opposing tensions throughout the body - what the Greeks called chiastic balance.
Chiastic Balance
The body shows a cross-pattern (chiasmus): right side - engaged leg, relaxed arm; left side - relaxed leg, engaged arm (holding spear). Opposites balance across the body.
Proportions
Mathematical relationships between parts: the head is 1/7 of total height (later artists like Lysippos would change this). Every part relates proportionally to every other part.

Polykleitos' Theory

Polykleitos believed that beauty arose from symmetria - the commensurability of parts. Each part of the body should bear a mathematical relationship to every other part and to the whole.

Key Quote (Galen, 2nd century CE)
"Beauty consists in the proportions, not of the elements, but of the parts, that is to say, of finger to finger, and of all the fingers to the palm and the wrist, and of these to the forearm, and of the forearm to the upper arm, and of all the parts to each other."

Idealisation: The Doryphoros represents no specific individual but the ideal male body - a perfect athlete in the prime of youth. This balance of naturalism (anatomically accurate) and idealisation (perfected beyond nature) defines Classical art.

Discobolus (Discus Thrower)
Original by Myron c. 460-450 BCE | Roman marble copy | Height: 155 cm | Multiple copies exist (best: Palazzo Massimo, Rome)

The Lancellotti Discobolus - considered the best Roman copy of Myron's original. Image: Wikimedia Commons

Captured Movement
Shows the athlete at the moment of maximum tension - the backswing before throwing. This split-second is frozen eternally. Myron was famous for capturing decisive moments of action.
Composition
The body forms a complex spiral or torsion. The torso twists while the face remains calm. Despite the extreme pose, there's a sense of balance and control - potential energy about to be released.
Expression
The face is serene, almost emotionless, despite the physical effort. This contrast between bodily action and facial calm is characteristic of Classical sculpture - ethos (character) over pathos (emotion).
Viewpoint
Designed primarily for frontal viewing (unlike later Hellenistic works). The spiral composition works best from a single optimal viewpoint - a characteristic of Classical relief-like thinking.

Myron's Achievement

While Polykleitos focused on the standing figure at rest, Myron was celebrated for capturing the body in motion. Ancient sources praised his ability to show fleeting moments of athletic action.

Technical challenge: Representing violent movement while maintaining balance, harmony, and the dignified calm expected of Classical art. The Discobolus solves this by showing maximum physical tension with minimal emotional expression.

Bronze vs Marble
The original bronze would have shown this pose more convincingly - bronze is self-supporting. Roman marble copies required the ugly tree-stump support you see in copies. This significantly changes the visual effect.

The Classical Ideal

High Classical sculpture represents a perfect balance of opposing qualities:

  • Naturalism + Idealisation: Anatomically accurate yet perfected beyond nature
  • Movement + Stability: Dynamic poses that maintain equilibrium
  • Individual + Universal: Specific bodies that represent timeless ideals
  • Physical + Spiritual: Athletic bodies expressing moral excellence

Comparing Polykleitos and Myron

Polykleitos (Doryphoros)
Focus on proportion and harmony at rest. Mathematical theory (Canon). Subtle movement through contrapposto. The body in perfect equilibrium.
Myron (Discobolus)
Focus on capturing dynamic action. Dramatic torsion and spiral composition. Body at moment of maximum tension. Movement frozen in time.
Essay Point
Both sculptors work within Classical principles - idealised bodies, calm expressions, balance and harmony. But they approach these ideals differently: Polykleitos through mathematical proportion at rest, Myron through dynamic equilibrium in action.

Historical Context

The High Classical period coincides with the Athenian golden age under Pericles (c. 461-429 BCE). This was an era of:

  • Athenian imperial power and wealth
  • Democratic confidence and civic pride
  • Major building projects (Parthenon, 447-432 BCE)
  • Philosophical developments (Socrates, Sophists)

Classical art reflects this confidence - a belief in human capacity, rational order, and achievable perfection. The idealized athlete embodied Greek values of arete (excellence) and kalos kagathos (beautiful and good).
