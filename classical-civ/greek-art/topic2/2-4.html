The Late Classical Period

The Late Classical period (c. 400-323 BCE) saw a shift from the austere idealism of High Classical art towards softer, more sensuous forms. Praxiteles of Athens was the leading sculptor of this era.

Key developments include:

  • Softer, more languid poses with pronounced S-curves
  • Greater emotional expression and intimacy
  • The first monumental female nude
  • More elongated proportions
  • Dreamy, introspective facial expressions

Hermes and the Infant Dionysus
Attributed to Praxiteles, c. 340 BCE (possibly Roman copy) | Marble | Height: 213 cm | Archaeological Museum of Olympia

Hermes with the infant Dionysus - possibly the only surviving original work by Praxiteles. Image: Wikimedia Commons

The S-Curve
Extreme contrapposto creates a sinuous S-curve through the body. Hermes leans heavily on a support (tree trunk with drapery), creating a languid, relaxed pose unprecedented in earlier sculpture.
Surface Treatment
Famous for soft, almost melting transitions between forms. Marble is polished to a luminous sheen. Light plays across the surface creating subtle gradations - sfumato in stone.
Expression
Dreamy, distant gaze - eyes looking beyond the viewer. Soft, idealized features. The expression suggests an inner world, private emotion, quite different from Classical detachment.
Narrative Moment
Hermes holds the infant Dionysus while resting during his journey to deliver the baby god to the nymphs of Nysa. His raised right arm probably held grapes to amuse the child.

Original or Copy?

This statue was found at Olympia where Pausanias (2nd century CE) reported seeing a work by Praxiteles showing Hermes with Dionysus. Some scholars believe this is the original; others argue it's a Roman copy. Key evidence:

Arguments for Original
Found in location Pausanias described; exceptional quality; some technical features unusual in Roman copies.
Arguments for Copy
Highly polished surface may be Roman taste; some scholars date the style later; tool marks suggest Roman techniques.
Aphrodite of Knidos
Original by Praxiteles c. 350 BCE | Known through Roman copies | Best copies: Vatican, Munich

Roman copy of the Aphrodite of Knidos - the first monumental female nude in Greek art. Image: Wikimedia Commons

Revolutionary Nudity
The first monumental nude female figure in Greek art. Earlier korai and goddesses were always clothed. This broke a fundamental taboo and created a new artistic tradition.
Narrative Context
Aphrodite prepares to bathe - she sets down her robe on a water jar (hydria). This provides a "justification" for the nudity while the goddess's gesture acknowledges being seen.
Modest Gesture
One hand covers her pubic area - the Venus pudica pose that would influence Western art for centuries. This gesture simultaneously conceals and draws attention to her nudity.
Fame
Ancient sources called it the finest statue ever made. The city of Knidos (Asia Minor) displayed it in a circular temple viewable from all sides - unusual for the time. Tourists came from across the Mediterranean.

Cultural Significance

The Aphrodite of Knidos represents a fundamental shift in how the female body could be represented in Greek art. It established:

  • The female nude as a legitimate artistic subject
  • The Venus pudica pose (modest Venus) - copied endlessly
  • A more intimate, personal relationship between viewer and statue
  • The goddess as an object of aesthetic (and erotic) contemplation
Ancient Reception
Ancient sources record that men fell in love with the statue; one allegedly left a stain on it. While these stories may be exaggerated, they indicate the powerful effect this new kind of image had on ancient viewers.

Characteristics of Praxitelean Style

S-Curve Pose
Exaggerated contrapposto creating sinuous curves through the body, often requiring external support.
Soft Modelling
Gentle transitions between forms; no sharp divisions. Creates impression of soft, living flesh.
Elongated Proportions
Figures are more slender and elegant than High Classical canon. Smaller heads relative to body.
Dreamy Expression
Introspective, distant gazes. Figures seem lost in private reverie rather than engaging viewer.

Comparing High and Late Classical

Polykleitos (High Classical):

  • Mathematical proportions (Canon)
  • Self-supporting poses
  • Athletic, muscular bodies
  • Balanced, rational expression

Praxiteles (Late Classical):

  • Elongated, elegant proportions
  • Leaning poses requiring support
  • Softer, more sensuous bodies
  • Dreamy, emotional expression
