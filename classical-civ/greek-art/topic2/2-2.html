2.2 Early Classical - The Severe Style

Topic 2: Freestanding Sculpture 45 min Prescribed Sources

Prescribed Sources in This Lesson

This lesson covers key Early Classical sculptures:

  • Kritios Boy (c. 480 BCE) - Acropolis Museum, Athens
  • Riace Bronzes (Warrior A and B) (c. 460-450 BCE) - Museo Nazionale della Magna Grecia, Reggio Calabria

What is the Severe Style?

The Severe Style (also called Early Classical) marks the transition from Archaic to Classical sculpture, roughly 480-450 BCE. This period coincides with the Persian Wars and their aftermath.

Key characteristics of the Severe Style include:

  • Abandonment of the Archaic smile for serious, contemplative expressions
  • Introduction of contrapposto (weight shift)
  • More naturalistic anatomy
  • Increased interest in movement and psychological depth
Historical Context
The Persian sack of Athens in 480 BCE destroyed the Archaic sculptures on the Acropolis. The subsequent Greek victory at Salamis and Plataea led to a new confidence and the Classical period. The solemnity of the Severe Style may reflect the gravity of these wartime experiences.

Kritios Boy
c. 480 BCE | Marble | Height: 117 cm | Acropolis Museum, Athens

The Kritios Boy - a revolutionary work marking the end of the Archaic period. Image: Wikimedia Commons

Contrapposto
For the first time in Greek sculpture, the weight is clearly on one leg (the left). The right leg is relaxed, knee slightly bent. This creates a subtle S-curve through the body - the first true contrapposto.
Anatomy
The contrapposto affects the whole body naturally: the right hip drops, the spine curves, the shoulders respond. The body is understood as an integrated system of muscles and bones.
Expression
No Archaic smile. The face is serious, introspective, almost melancholic. The head turns slightly, breaking frontal rigidity. This suggests inner psychological life.
Hair
Short, cap-like hair with simple curls - more naturalistic than the elaborate Archaic patterns. Practical and youthful rather than decorative.

Revolutionary Importance

The Kritios Boy is often called the first truly Classical work because of its introduction of contrapposto - the natural weight shift that would define Greek sculpture for centuries.

Why Contrapposto Matters
Contrapposto transforms the human figure from a rigid symbol into a living, breathing presence. The weight shift creates a sense of potential movement - the figure could step forward at any moment. It also allows for infinite variations in pose, freeing sculptors from the kouros formula.

Named after: The sculptor Kritios, who worked with Nesiotes on the Tyrannicides group. The attribution is based on style rather than inscription.

Riace Bronzes (Warriors A and B)
c. 460-450 BCE | Bronze | Height: c. 2m | Museo Nazionale della Magna Grecia, Reggio Calabria

The Riace Bronzes - rare original Greek bronzes discovered underwater in 1972. Image: Wikimedia Commons

Discovery
Found by a scuba diver off the coast of Riace, Italy in 1972. Probably being transported to Rome when the ship sank. Rare surviving original Greek bronzes.
Material
Hollow-cast bronze using the lost-wax technique. Copper for lips and nipples, silver for teeth, bone and glass paste for eyes. Shows the polychrome nature of Greek bronze sculpture.
Warrior A (The Younger)
More dynamic pose, intense expression, originally held spear and shield. Wild, flowing hair and beard. Veins visible on arms. Possibly depicts a hero or god.
Warrior B (The Elder)
Calmer expression, originally wore a Corinthian helmet pushed back. More contained pose but equally powerful anatomy. Different artistic hand - possibly different sculptors.

Technical Excellence

The Riace Bronzes demonstrate the technical achievements possible in bronze that were difficult or impossible in marble:

  • Unsupported poses: No need for tree stumps or struts - bronze is self-supporting
  • Fine detail: Veins, individual curls of hair, expressive musculature
  • Inlaid materials: Copper lips, silver teeth, glass eyes create lifelike appearance
  • Lost-wax casting: Allowed hollow construction, reducing weight and cost
Why Most Bronzes Are Lost
Bronze was valuable and could be melted down for reuse (weapons, coins, new statues). Most Greek bronzes were destroyed in antiquity. The Riace Bronzes survive only because they were lost in a shipwreck. This reminds us that Roman marble copies often represent lost bronze originals.

Anatomical Achievement

The Riace warriors display extraordinary anatomical knowledge:

Musculature: Every muscle is accurately observed - the abdominal muscles, the serratus anterior on the ribs, the complex back muscles. The bodies show both strength and realistic proportions.

Contrapposto perfected: The weight shift is pronounced, creating dynamic asymmetry. The bodies twist naturally, suggesting potential movement while maintaining equilibrium.

Individual character: Despite idealization, each warrior has distinct features. This combination of ideal and individual points toward later developments in portraiture.

The Severe Style: Key Developments

From Archaic to Classical
The Severe Style bridges the Archaic and High Classical periods. The rigid frontality and decorative stylization of Archaic art gives way to naturalistic anatomy, psychological depth, and dynamic poses - while maintaining idealized beauty.

Technical advances: Contrapposto revolutionizes the representation of the standing figure. Bronze casting reaches new levels of sophistication. Sculptors demonstrate detailed knowledge of human anatomy.

Expressive changes: The Archaic smile disappears, replaced by serious, contemplative expressions. Figures seem capable of thought and emotion. This psychological dimension becomes central to Classical art.

Historical context: The Persian Wars (490-479 BCE) are the backdrop. Greek victory over Persia led to immense confidence and resources, especially in Athens. The solemnity of Severe Style may reflect wartime experiences; its dynamism anticipates the triumphal Classical period.

Essay Comparison Points

When comparing Archaic and Severe Style works:

  • Pose: Rigid, symmetrical kouros formula → contrapposto weight shift
  • Expression: Archaic smile → serious, introspective
  • Anatomy: Stylised patterns → naturalistic modelling
  • Movement: Static frontality → potential energy, implied motion
  • Hair: Elaborate decorative patterns → simpler, more natural forms
