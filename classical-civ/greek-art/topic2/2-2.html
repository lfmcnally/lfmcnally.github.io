2.2 Early Classical - The Severe Style

Topic 2: Freestanding Sculpture 45 min Prescribed Sources

Prescribed Sources in This Lesson

This lesson covers the prescribed Early Classical (Severe Style) sculptures:

  • Delphic Charioteer (c. 478-474 BCE) - Delphi Archaeological Museum
  • Artemision Zeus/Poseidon (c. 460 BCE) - National Archaeological Museum, Athens

Also covered: The Kritios Boy as an important transitional work marking the introduction of contrapposto.

What is the Severe Style?

The Severe Style (also called Early Classical) marks the transition from Archaic to Classical sculpture, roughly 480-450 BCE. This period coincides with the Persian Wars and their aftermath.

Key characteristics of the Severe Style include:

  • Abandonment of the Archaic smile for serious, contemplative expressions
  • Introduction of contrapposto (weight shift)
  • More naturalistic anatomy
  • Increased interest in movement and psychological depth
Historical Context
The Persian sack of Athens in 480 BCE destroyed the Archaic sculptures on the Acropolis. The subsequent Greek victory at Salamis and Plataea led to a new confidence and the Classical period. The solemnity of the Severe Style may reflect the gravity of these wartime experiences.

Delphic Charioteer
c. 478-474 BCE | Bronze | Height: 180 cm | Delphi Archaeological Museum

The Delphic Charioteer - one of the finest surviving Greek bronzes. Image: Wikimedia Commons

Context
Part of a larger votive offering commemorating a chariot victory at the Pythian Games. The complete group included the chariot, four horses, and possibly a groom. Dedicated by Polyzalos, tyrant of Gela in Sicily.
Pose
Stands holding the reins, depicted just after victory. Despite the drama of racing, the figure is calm and composed. Slight turn of the head breaks strict frontality. Weight subtly shifted.
Dress
Wears the xystis - the long charioteer's robe tied at the waist with a belt. The vertical folds create a column-like effect, emphasising stability and dignity. The garment's pleats are rendered with extraordinary precision.
Expression
The face exemplifies Severe Style restraint. Calm, dignified expression despite the intensity of competition. Inlaid eyes (copper lashes, onyx irises, glass paste whites) create uncanny lifelikeness.

Severe Style Characteristics

The Charioteer embodies key Severe Style features:

  • Seriousness: No Archaic smile - expression is grave, focused, noble
  • Restraint: Emotional intensity contained; dignity maintained
  • Naturalism: Face is individualised yet idealised; veins visible on feet
  • Technical excellence: Bronze allows fine detail; polychrome inlays add realism
Victory and Commemoration
Athletic victory brought immense prestige to both the victor and their city. Wealthy patrons commissioned elaborate victory monuments at Panhellenic sanctuaries like Delphi and Olympia, advertising their piety and success to all Greece.
Artemision Zeus (or Poseidon)
c. 460 BCE | Bronze | Height: 209 cm | National Archaeological Museum, Athens

The Artemision Bronze - a god in the act of throwing, recovered from the sea. Image: Wikimedia Commons

Discovery
Found in a shipwreck off Cape Artemision, northern Euboea, in 1926-1928. Like the Riace Bronzes, it survives because it was lost at sea, avoiding the melting-down that destroyed most ancient bronzes.
Identity Debate
Zeus throwing a thunderbolt or Poseidon throwing a trident? The missing weapon makes identification uncertain. Most scholars favour Zeus based on the powerful, commanding pose associated with the king of the gods.
Pose
Captured at the moment of maximum tension before the throw. Arms extended wide, weight balanced between both legs. The figure fills space dynamically - a major departure from the closed kouros form.
Anatomy
Idealised athletic body with perfectly defined musculature. The muscles are taut, responding to the action. Naturalistic yet heroically proportioned - this is a god, not a mortal.

Revolutionary Action Pose

The Artemision Zeus represents a revolutionary achievement in capturing the body in motion:

  • Dynamic extension: Arms spread wide, occupying maximum space
  • Frozen moment: The split-second of potential energy before release
  • 360-degree composition: Designed to be viewed from multiple angles
  • Divine power: The pose conveys superhuman strength and authority
Bronze vs Marble
This pose would be nearly impossible in marble - the extended arms would snap off. Bronze's tensile strength allows unsupported limbs and dynamic poses. This is why we must imagine the original bronze versions of works we know only as Roman marble copies.

Comparison with the Charioteer

Both the Charioteer and Artemision Zeus are Severe Style bronzes, but they show different aspects of the period:

Delphic Charioteer
Static, restrained, vertical emphasis. Clothed. Depicts a mortal after victory. Shows Severe Style calm and dignity. Column-like stability.
Artemision Zeus
Dynamic, active, horizontal extension. Nude. Depicts a god in action. Shows Severe Style mastery of movement. Explosive energy contained.
Kritios Boy
c. 480 BCE | Marble | Height: 117 cm | Acropolis Museum, Athens

The Kritios Boy - a revolutionary work marking the end of the Archaic period. Image: Wikimedia Commons

Contrapposto
For the first time in Greek sculpture, the weight is clearly on one leg (the left). The right leg is relaxed, knee slightly bent. This creates a subtle S-curve through the body - the first true contrapposto.
Anatomy
The contrapposto affects the whole body naturally: the right hip drops, the spine curves, the shoulders respond. The body is understood as an integrated system of muscles and bones.
Expression
No Archaic smile. The face is serious, introspective, almost melancholic. The head turns slightly, breaking frontal rigidity. This suggests inner psychological life.
Hair
Short, cap-like hair with simple curls - more naturalistic than the elaborate Archaic patterns. Practical and youthful rather than decorative.

Revolutionary Importance

The Kritios Boy is often called the first truly Classical work because of its introduction of contrapposto - the natural weight shift that would define Greek sculpture for centuries.

Why Contrapposto Matters
Contrapposto transforms the human figure from a rigid symbol into a living, breathing presence. The weight shift creates a sense of potential movement - the figure could step forward at any moment. It also allows for infinite variations in pose, freeing sculptors from the kouros formula.

Named after: The sculptor Kritios, who worked with Nesiotes on the Tyrannicides group. The attribution is based on style rather than inscription.

The Severe Style: Key Developments

From Archaic to Classical
The Severe Style bridges the Archaic and High Classical periods. The rigid frontality and decorative stylization of Archaic art gives way to naturalistic anatomy, psychological depth, and dynamic poses - while maintaining idealized beauty.

Technical advances: Contrapposto revolutionizes the representation of the standing figure. Bronze casting reaches new levels of sophistication. Sculptors demonstrate detailed knowledge of human anatomy.

Expressive changes: The Archaic smile disappears, replaced by serious, contemplative expressions. Figures seem capable of thought and emotion. This psychological dimension becomes central to Classical art.

Historical context: The Persian Wars (490-479 BCE) are the backdrop. Greek victory over Persia led to immense confidence and resources, especially in Athens. The solemnity of Severe Style may reflect wartime experiences; its dynamism anticipates the triumphal Classical period.

Essay Comparison Points

When comparing Archaic and Severe Style works:

  • Pose: Rigid, symmetrical kouros formula → contrapposto weight shift
  • Expression: Archaic smile → serious, introspective
  • Anatomy: Stylised patterns → naturalistic modelling
  • Movement: Static frontality → potential energy, implied motion
  • Hair: Elaborate decorative patterns → simpler, more natural forms
