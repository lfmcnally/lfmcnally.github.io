2.5 Hellenistic Sculpture

Topic 2: Freestanding Sculpture 45 min Lysippos and Later Developments

Prescribed Sources in This Lesson

  • Apoxyomenos (Scraper) by Lysippos (c. 330 BCE) - Vatican Museums

Also covered: The Laocoon group as an important example of later Hellenistic dramatic style.

The Hellenistic Period

The Hellenistic period (323-31 BCE) begins with Alexander the Great's death and ends with Rome's conquest of Egypt. Greek culture spread across a vast area from Egypt to Central Asia, creating new artistic centres and patrons.

Key characteristics of Hellenistic sculpture:

  • Dramatic emotion (pathos) replaces Classical restraint
  • Theatrical compositions designed for multiple viewpoints
  • Virtuoso technique - technical display becomes valued
  • Diverse subjects - old age, children, foreigners, everyday life
  • Extreme naturalism including ugliness and suffering

Apoxyomenos (The Scraper)
Original by Lysippos c. 330 BCE | Roman marble copy | Height: 205 cm | Vatican Museums

Roman copy of Lysippos' Apoxyomenos - an athlete scraping oil and dirt from his body. Image: Wikimedia Commons

New Proportions
Lysippos established new, more slender proportions: the head is 1/8 of body height (vs Polykleitos' 1/7). The body appears taller, more elegant, less stocky.
Three-Dimensional Space
Arms extend forward into the viewer's space - revolutionary! Earlier sculpture stayed within an imaginary frame. This figure reaches out, engaging real space.
Multiple Viewpoints
Designed to be viewed from many angles, not just frontally. The composition changes as you walk around - no single "correct" viewpoint.
Everyday Moment
Shows an athlete after competition, scraping off oil and sweat with a strigil. Not an heroic action but a mundane, human moment - characteristic of later Greek interests.

Lysippos and Alexander

Lysippos was court sculptor to Alexander the Great - only he was permitted to create portraits of the king. He was extraordinarily prolific; ancient sources credit him with 1,500 works (all lost).

Lysippos' Innovation
"Lysippos said that while other sculptors made men as they were, he made them as they appeared to be." This suggests interest in optical effects and how sculpture appears to the eye, not just mathematical proportions.
Antikytheran Youth
c. 340-330 BCE | Bronze | Height: 196 cm | National Archaeological Museum, Athens

The Antikytheran Youth - a bronze masterpiece recovered from the sea. Image: Wikimedia Commons

Discovery
Recovered from a shipwreck off the island of Antikythera in 1900-1901, along with the famous Antikythera mechanism. One of few original Greek bronzes to survive antiquity.
Identity
The figure's identity is debated: possibly Perseus holding Medusa's head, Paris with the Apple of Discord, or a mortal athlete. The right arm extends forward, holding a now-lost object.
Pose
Like the Apoxyomenos, this figure extends into the viewer's space. The raised arm and gaze directed at an object in the hand creates a contemplative moment. Weight on one leg with relaxed contrapposto.
Attribution
Sometimes attributed to the school of Lysippos due to the slender proportions and spatial composition. Others suggest Euphranor or an unknown master of the late 4th century.

Late Classical or Early Hellenistic?

The Antikytheran Youth dates to the transition between Late Classical and Hellenistic periods. It shows characteristics of both:

  • Classical features: Idealised body, calm expression, restrained emotion
  • Hellenistic features: Extension into space, psychological moment captured, multiple viewpoints
Original Bronze
Unlike the Apoxyomenos (known only through Roman marble copies), the Antikytheran Youth is an original bronze. This allows us to appreciate Greek bronze-working technique directly: the inlaid copper lips, the detailed hair, the subtle surface modelling impossible to reproduce in marble.

Comparison with Apoxyomenos

Both figures extend into the viewer's space with raised arms, yet they differ in character:

Apoxyomenos
Everyday action (scraping). Athletic build. Arms working with a tool. Mundane moment elevated to art.
Antikytheran Youth
Contemplative gaze at object in hand. More youthful, slender. Psychological focus. Possibly mythological subject.
Laocoon and His Sons
Attributed to Hagesandros, Polydoros, and Athenodoros of Rhodes | c. 200 BCE or 1st century CE copy | Marble | Height: 208 cm | Vatican Museums

Laocoon and His Sons - one of the most famous sculptures from antiquity. Image: Wikimedia Commons

The Story
Laocoon was a Trojan priest who warned against accepting the wooden horse. The gods sent sea serpents to kill him and his sons - either as punishment or to ensure Troy's fall.
Extreme Emotion
Laocoon's face shows intense agony - mouth open, brow furrowed, eyes raised. This extreme pathos contrasts dramatically with Classical restraint. Physical and emotional suffering are central.
Complex Composition
Three figures and two serpents interlock in a pyramidal composition of great complexity. The eye moves through tangled bodies, creating dramatic tension.
Virtuoso Technique
Extraordinary technical achievement in rendering anatomy under stress, the texture of the serpents, the complex poses. The sculptors display their skill deliberately.

Discovery and Influence

The Laocoon was rediscovered in Rome in 1506 and immediately recognized as the masterpiece described by Pliny the Elder. Michelangelo was present at its excavation. It profoundly influenced Renaissance and Baroque art.

Pliny's Praise (1st century CE)
"A work to be preferred to all that the arts of painting and sculpture have produced... carved from a single block." (Actually carved from several blocks - Pliny was mistaken.)

Dating debate: Scholars disagree whether this is a Hellenistic original (c. 200 BCE) or a Roman-era copy/creation (1st century BCE-CE). Either way, it exemplifies Hellenistic dramatic aesthetics.

Classical vs Hellenistic: Key Differences

Aspect Classical (5th c. BCE) Hellenistic (3rd-1st c. BCE)
Expression Calm, restrained (ethos) Intense emotion (pathos)
Viewpoint Primarily frontal Multiple viewpoints
Space Self-contained Extends into viewer's space
Subjects Ideal youth, gods, athletes All ages, types, emotions
Body Idealized perfection Realistic, including suffering

The Development of Greek Sculpture: Summary

Archaic (600-480 BCE): Rigid kouros formula, stylized forms, Archaic smile, Egyptian influence. Movement towards naturalism within formula.

Severe/Early Classical (480-450 BCE): Contrapposto introduced, serious expressions, increased naturalism. The Kritios Boy and Riace Bronzes.

High Classical (450-400 BCE): Perfect balance of naturalism and idealization. Polykleitos' Canon, Myron's movement, Parthenon sculptures.

Late Classical (400-323 BCE): Softer forms, S-curves, emotional expression, first female nude. Praxiteles, Lysippos.

Hellenistic (323-31 BCE): Dramatic pathos, theatrical compositions, diverse subjects, virtuoso technique. Laocoon, Dying Gaul, Venus de Milo.
