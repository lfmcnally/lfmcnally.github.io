LESSON 2.1

The Genitive Case

Understanding possession and the 'of' case

More Than Just Subject and Object

Sentences would get rather boring if the only noun cases were nominative and accusative. Fortunately, Latin has three other main cases that add richness and precision to expression:

genitive
used to show possession
e.g. the house of the girl
dative
translated to or for
e.g. I give the book to the master
ablative
translated by, with or from
e.g. we fight with swords

We'll meet each of these in turn, starting with the genitive case.

What is the Genitive Case?

The genitive case is used to demonstrate possession. It shows who owns what, or what belongs to whom. The key thing to remember is that it's the possessor that goes into the genitive, not the thing they possess.

Key Point

In the phrase "the house of the master", the master goes into the genitive, not the house. The house remains nominative or accusative depending on its job in the sentence.

The term genitive is linked to the word genesis (origin). You might think of things that are owned by somebody as (in a sense) originating from them.

Watch Out!

In both puella and dominus the genitive singular and the nominative plural are identical. You need to work out which one you're dealing with from the context (what is happening in the rest of the sentence).

  • puell-ae could be genitive singular OR nominative plural
  • domin-i could be genitive singular OR nominative plural

How to Translate the Genitive

The genitive is translated into English using 'of' or 's (s' for a plural). Both mean exactly the same thing:

villam amicorum amamus.
Method 1: Using 'of'
Translation: We like the house of the friends.
More formal-sounding
villam amicorum amamus.
Method 2: Using 's
Translation: We like the friends' house.
More natural-sounding
Word Order: The genitive noun tends to come immediately after the thing possessed: gladius domini (the master's sword).

Using Context Clues

Use any available clues to help you work out the number and case of a word with an ending that is ambiguous (could be more than one thing). Click each sentence to see how context helps:

servus domini non laborat.
Context Clue: The verb laborat is singular, so you know that the subject of the sentence must be singular.
The subject must be servus, so domini must be genitive singular (the other option, that domini is nominative plural, is impossible because it would need a plural verb).
Translation: The slave of the master is not working. / The master's slave is not working.

Recognizing Declensions

When a noun is listed in a wordlist, the genitive singular (either in full or just the ending) is given too, as well as the gender. The declensions have different genitive endings, so you can work out which declension the noun belongs to:

puella, -ae f
girl
genitive ending -ae, therefore 1st declension
dominus, -i m
master
genitive ending -i, therefore 2nd declension

As you meet new items of vocabulary you will always be given enough information to enable you to work out what you are dealing with: e.g. a noun that goes like puella, or a verb that goes like porto, etc.
