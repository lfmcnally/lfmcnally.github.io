LESSON 1.3

Nominative and Accusative Singular

First declension nouns and their cases

What is a Case?

In Latin, nouns change their endings to show what job they're doing in the sentence. These different forms are called cases. Think of it like giving each noun a uniform that shows its role - just like how a referee wears stripes and a goalkeeper wears different colored gloves!

Today we'll learn about the two most important cases: the nominative (for subjects doing the action) and the accusative (for objects receiving the action).

Key Concept: The ending of a Latin noun tells us what role it plays in the sentence - just like verb endings tell us who's doing the action!

The Two Cases

Case Job in Sentence Think of it as... Example
Nominative Subject The "doer"
The girl walks
Accusative Direct Object The "receiver"
I see the girl

First Declension: puella (girl)

Most first declension nouns are feminine and follow the same pattern as puella. Let's see how the ending changes for different cases:

Case Ending Latin English
Nominative
-a
puell-a
a girl (subject)
Accusative
-am
puell-am
a girl (object)

Notice the key difference: -a for subjects (nominative) and -am for objects (accusative). That extra -m is your clue that something is being done TO this noun!

Putting it Together

Now let's see how cases work in real sentences. Remember: look for the nominative (subject) first, then the verb, then the accusative (object).

Example Sentences
puella ancillam vocat.
The girl calls the slave-girl.
femina villam amat.
The woman loves the house.
ancilla feminam salutat.
The slave-girl greets the woman.
Pro tip: If you see a noun ending in -am, something is happening TO it. If it ends in -a, it's doing the action!
