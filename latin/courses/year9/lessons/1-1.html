Welcome from Mr McNally

Before we begin, here's a short introduction to Classics and why it's such an exciting subject to study:

Latin Is Not Dead

Latin is not a dead language - it's an immortal one. We don't study Latin to help with other subjects or to boost our CVs. We study it because Latin itself is extraordinary.

Latin is a precise, expressive language that trains accuracy and clear thinking. Every word matters, every ending carries meaning, every sentence is crafted with purpose. When you learn Latin, you learn to think with the precision of a lawyer, the logic of a philosopher, and the creativity of a poet.

Through Latin, we read the Romans on their own terms - their history, philosophy, and poetry exactly as they wrote it. No translation can capture Caesar's controlled power, Cicero's rhetorical brilliance, or Virgil's layered meanings. Their world still shapes our debates about power, law, identity, and art.

The Architecture of Latin

Latin is architecturally perfect. Unlike the chaos of English spelling and grammar, Latin follows beautiful, logical patterns. It's a language where everything fits together like a Roman arch - each stone precisely placed, the whole structure elegant and unshakeable.

What Makes Latin Unique
Inflection: Endings show meaning
In English we need word order: "Dog bites man" vs "Man bites dog". In Latin, the endings tell you who's doing what, so words can dance anywhere in the sentence.
Concision: Maximum meaning, minimum words
"Veni, vidi, vici" - three words capture what English needs eight for: "I came, I saw, I conquered"
Precision: Exact expression
Latin has different words for "kill": interficere (kill), necare (murder), occidere (strike down), caedere (slaughter). Each carries different moral weight.
Permanence: Unchanging through centuries
Shakespeare's English needs translation notes. Caesar's Latin, 2000 years old, needs none. Once you learn it, you can read any era.

Meet the Romans

The Romans weren't dusty statues - they were brilliant, complex, sometimes terrible, often admirable people who faced the same questions we do: What is justice? How should we live? What makes a good leader? What is our duty to others?

They built aqueducts and wrote love poetry. They conquered nations and debated philosophy. They were engineers and artists, soldiers and senators, slaves and emperors. Their voices - preserved in Latin - still speak directly to us.

In this course: You'll meet Caesar the general, Cicero the lawyer, Virgil the poet, Pliny watching Vesuvius explode, Catullus writing to his girlfriend, and Marcus Aurelius contemplating life. Real people, real voices, real Latin.

The Gifts of Latin

Latin doesn't promise to make you smarter or help you learn French. It offers something better - it changes how you think:

  • Attention to detail: In Latin, one wrong letter changes everything. You learn to notice what others miss.
  • Logical thinking: Every sentence is a puzzle with a solution. You learn to work systematically.
  • Cultural depth: You'll understand references others don't even notice are references.
  • Intellectual confidence: If you can master Latin, you can master anything.
  • Connection to the past: You join an unbroken chain of scholars stretching back millennia.
The real gift: Latin teaches you to slow down, pay attention, and think deeply. In a world of tweets and TikToks, that's revolutionary.

Your Journey Begins

Over the coming months, you'll discover that Latin isn't about memorising tables or chanting "amo, amas, amat" (though we'll do a bit of that too!). It's about entering a world of precision and beauty, logic and creativity, ancient wisdom and timeless questions.

In our next lesson, you'll write your first Latin sentences. You'll discover how Latin verbs work - how one tiny ending can tell you exactly who's doing what. You'll begin to think like a Roman.

Remember: Every Latin scholar started exactly where you are now. Caesar learned these same patterns as a boy. Virgil practised these same endings. You're joining an ancient tradition. Welcome to Latin!
