Welcome from Mr McNally

Before we begin, here's a short introduction to Classics and why it's such an exciting subject to study:

Why Study Latin?

Latin lets you enter a world that has shaped our own. It is the language of Roman politics, law, myth and art, and it speaks in many voices: the cut and thrust of Cicero's rhetoric, Tacitus' cool moral analysis, Virgil's wide-ranging epic in the Aeneid, Catullus' sharp and self-aware love poetry. We study Latin to meet that world on its own terms, to handle a precise and demanding language, and to read great literature in the words its authors chose.

The Three Ls

L
Language
  • Learn a finely structured system where meaning lives in endings and word order
  • Build habits of accuracy, attention to detail and clear reasoning
  • Tackle real problems of translation and interpretation, not exercises made up to be easy
L
Literature
  • Read Roman authors in their own voices, not only in paraphrase
  • Study style, metre, rhetoric and narrative technique with evidence on the page
  • Ask what texts say about power, freedom, identity, love, duty and grief
L
Life
  • Think with the Romans about government, citizenship, war and justice
  • Trace ideas that still shape Europe and the wider world
  • Carry a durable curiosity that enriches study, work and culture long after exams

Origins

Latin began in Latium around Rome, attested in inscriptions from the early first millennium BCE. From humble beginnings among farmers and shepherds, it would grow to shape the world.

Classical Latin

Standardised in the late Republic and early Empire, shaped by writers such as Cicero, Caesar, Virgil, Horace and Ovid. This is the Latin we study - the language at its most refined and powerful.

Latin and Empire

Everyday speech across the West developed into the Romance languages. Written Latin remained the common language of administration and literature, uniting diverse peoples under Roman law and culture.

Late Antique and Medieval Latin

The language of scholarship, law, philosophy, theology and science across Europe. Every educated person thought and wrote in Latin - it was the internet of the medieval world.

Renaissance to Enlightenment

Humanists revived classical style. Latin stayed the medium of much learned writing and international correspondence. Newton wrote his Principia in Latin; Linnaeus named all living things in Latin.

Latin in Schools

From medieval grammar schools to Victorian public schools, Latin sat at the heart of education. Twentieth-century reforms broadened the curriculum, but Latin remains a rigorous, widely respected subject with thriving GCSE and A Level courses.

A Note on Pronunciation

Latin has been spoken for nearly three millennia, and its pronunciation has evolved through different periods and places. We use the reconstructed Classical pronunciation - the result of rigorous philological work comparing inscriptions, Greek transliterations, and the comments of ancient grammarians.

This means we pronounce Latin as Cicero and Caesar would have heard it in the Senate. You'll also encounter Ecclesiastical pronunciation in choral music and church texts - equally valid, simply from a different historical moment. Understanding both enriches your engagement with texts across the centuries.

A matter of scholarship: The differences in pronunciation reflect Latin's continuous life through history. Each era left its mark on how the language sounds, from the Forum to the medieval monastery to the Renaissance court.

Fun fact: Julius Caesar pronounced his name "YOO-lee-oos KAI-sar" not "JOO-lee-us SEE-zer". The letter 'v' was pronounced like 'w', so 'veni, vidi, vici' sounded like "WAY-nee, WEE-dee, WEE-kee"!

Your Journey Begins

This term, you'll start with the foundations - verbs that bring Latin to life, nouns that build sentences, and the logical patterns that make everything click. You won't just learn about Latin; you'll think in Latin.

By Christmas, you'll be reading real Latin sentences. By next summer, simple stories. By the time you finish the course, you'll have the keys to 2,000 years of literature, from Caesar's campaigns to Newton's physics, all in the original language.
