The Ablative Case

Completing the five main cases - 'by', 'with', and 'from'

What is the Ablative Case?

The ablative case is the last of the five main Latin cases. It has a range of uses and meanings. When it appears by itself (without a preposition), it can mean 'by', 'with', or 'from'.

The term "ablative" comes from part of a Latin verb meaning "I take away". This captures one important thing the ablative expresses: the idea of separation - things being taken away or moved from somewhere.

Etymology: Ablative → ab- (away) + -lative (carrying) = "carrying away". This case often expresses how things are separated, removed, or distinguished from each other.

Key Observations:
  • The ā in puell-ā is pronounced long (unlike the short a in the nominative)
  • Dative and ablative singular of dominus are identical: domin-o
  • Dative and ablative plural are the same for both declensions: -is

The Ablative of Instrument

On its own, the ablative can only be used with inanimate objects (non-living things). This is called the ablative of instrument - it shows what tool or method is used to do something.

gladio pugnat
Ablative of instrument
Translation: He fights with a sword
The sword is the instrument/tool used for fighting
aqua villam lavant
Using 'with' or 'by'
Translation: They wash the house with water
Water is the means/instrument of washing
Remember: The ablative of instrument is only used with things, not people. If you want to say "by a person", you need the preposition a/ab + ablative.

Word Order with the Ablative

The ablative usually comes after the dative and before any adverbs and the verb. Here's the standard Latin word order pattern:

Standard Word Order

1. (Time adverb)
2. Subject
3. Object
4. Dative
5. Ablative
6. (Adverb)
7. Verb
cibum servo gladio do
Example in word order
cibum (object) servo (dative) gladio (ablative) do (verb)
Translation: I give food to the slave with a sword
Notice: object → dative → ablative → verb

Practice Examples

Let's see the ablative in action! Click each sentence to reveal the breakdown and translation. Pay attention to what tools or instruments are being used:

Ablative of Instrument
gladio hostes necat.
Breakdown: gladio (with a sword - abl sg), hostes (enemies - acc pl), necat (kills - 3rd sg)
Translation: He kills the enemies with a sword.
aqua manus lavamus.
Breakdown: aqua (with water - abl sg), manus (hands - acc pl), lavamus (we wash - 1st pl)
Translation: We wash our hands with water.
servi terra villam aedificant.
Breakdown: servi (slaves - nom pl), terra (with earth/soil - abl sg), villam (house - acc sg), aedificant (build - 3rd pl)
Translation: The slaves build the house with earth.
pecunia amicos iuvamus.
Breakdown: pecunia (with money - abl sg), amicos (friends - acc pl), iuvamus (we help - 1st pl)
Translation: We help our friends with money.
Pattern Recognition: Notice how the ablative often appears just before the verb, showing the means or instrument by which the action is performed.

Quick Practice

Identify the Case and Translate
vino ancillas iuvat
vino: ablative singular (with wine)
Translation: He helps the slave-girls with wine
puellae libris discunt
puellae: nominative plural (the girls)
libris: ablative plural (with books)
Translation: The girls learn with books
domino auxilio venimus
domino: dative singular (to the master)
auxilio: ablative singular (with help)
Translation: We come to the master with help
