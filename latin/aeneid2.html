§1 · Lines 268–280
The Dream of Hector
268
Tempus erat quo prima quies mortalibus aegris
It was the time when first sleep begins for weary mortals
What this line does
Virgil opens with the deepest sleep of the night — deliberately, because he is about to shatter it. The calm is not accidental: it makes the horror that follows worse.
269
incipit et dono divum gratissima serpit.
and steals over them, most welcome, as the gift of the gods.
What this line does
The sound of this line enacts falling asleep — the sibilance of serpit is one of the most discussed sound effects in Latin poetry. It also frames sleep as a divine gift, which makes what happens inside it all the more devastating.
270
in somnis, ecce, ante oculos maestissimus Hector
In sleep — look — before my eyes, most sorrowful Hector
What this line does
This is the moment of apparition — and Virgil controls it precisely. We get the emotional register (most sorrowful) before the identification (Hector). By the time the name arrives, we already know this will be devastating.
271
visus adesse mihi largosque effundere fletus,
seemed to stand before me, pouring out abundant tears,
What this line does
The key construction: videor + infinitive. Hector seemed to stand before him, seemed to pour out tears. The seeming matters — it is a dream. But also: two infinitives, and students always miss the second one.
272
raptatus bigis ut quondam, aterque cruento
dragged by the chariot as once he was, and dark with bloody
What this line does
The Homeric allusion arrives. Dragged behind the chariot — this is the desecration from Iliad 22-24 that Virgil's audience knew in their bones. He does not explain it. He trusts the audience to bring their Homer.
273
pulvere perque pedes traiectus lora tumentis.
dust, and pierced through his swollen feet with thongs.
What this line does
The accusative of respect — the trickiest construction in §1, and tumentis is the word students most reliably get wrong. It looks like a genitive singular. It is not.
274
ei mihi, qualis erat, quantum mutatus ab illo
Alas — in what a state he was, how greatly changed from that
What this line does
The narrator breaks through. Ei mihi is not grammar — it is grief. Aeneas at Dido's court, years after the event, loses his composure in the retelling. Then the great contrast begins: quantum mutatus.
275
Hectore qui redit exuvias indutus Achilli
Hector who returned, dressed in the spoils of Achilles,
What this line does
The name Hectore arrives at the start of this line — delayed from illo at the end of 274. Then immediately we pivot to his glory: who returned, wearing Achilles' own armour. The contrast with lines 272-273 is the emotional core of the passage.
276
vel Danaum Phrygios iaculatus puppibus ignis!
or who hurled Trojan fire at the Greek ships!
What this line does
The second image of Hector's glory — hurling Trojan fire at the Greek fleet. The exclamation mark is Virgil's own. This is Aeneas's grief as punctuation.
277
squalentem barbam et concretos sanguine crinis
His beard was matted and his hair clotted with blood,
What this line does
The catalogue of degradation continues — beard, hair, wounds. Virgil refuses to hurry past the physical reality. The accumulation is deliberate. If you are going to found Rome on this suffering, you need to feel its full weight.
278
vulneraque illa gerens, quae circum plurima muros
and he bore those many wounds which around the walls
What this line does
Illa — those famous wounds. Virgil points at the Iliad without explaining it. His audience knew exactly which wounds. This is writing that assumes you have done the reading.
279
accepit patrios. ultro flens ipse videbar
of his native land he had received. Of his own accord, weeping, I seemed
What this line does
Ultro — of his own accord, first, without being asked. Aeneas spoke before Hector said anything. The emotion is prior to the encounter. This is one of the most precise psychological observations in the poem.
280
compellare virum et maestas expromere voces:
to be addressing the hero and giving voice to sorrowful words:
What this line does
The passage ends on the threshold of speech. Not on what Aeneas says, but on the effort of saying it. Expromere — to wring the words out — is the finest word choice in §1. The colon holds us there, in the moment before words come.
§2 · Lines 298–308
The Fall of Troy: the Simile
298
Diverso interea miscentur moenia luctu,
Meanwhile the city walls are engulfed on all sides in widespread grief,
What this line does
The section opens in media res — Troy is already engulfed. Virgil's first word is Diverso (widespread, from all directions): the disaster is already total before Aeneas has even woken up.
299
magis atque magis, quamquam secreta parentis
and more and more, although the secluded house of father
What this line does
More and more — the sounds are growing. Even the secluded house of Anchises, set back and screened by trees, cannot keep the noise out. The growing sound mirrors the growing reality.
300
Anchisae domus arboribusque obtecta recessit,
Anchises, covered and sheltered by trees, had withdrawn,
What this line does
Three words of shelter and protection across two lines: secreta, arboribusque, obtecta. Virgil establishes how protected this house should be precisely so that the encroaching sounds are more ominous.
301
clarescunt sonitus armorumque ingruit horror.
the sounds grow louder and the terror of arms presses in.
What this line does
The sounds grow louder and the horror presses in physically. Ingruit is a strong, physical verb — horror bears down like a weight. This is where we fully leave the gentle world of the dream and enter the catastrophe.
302
excutior somno et summi fastigia tecti
I am shaken out of sleep and climb to the ridge of the highest roof,
What this line does
Excutior somno — 'I am shaken out of sleep'. The contrast with serpit (269) is the whole transition from peace to catastrophe in two words. Then immediately: he climbs to the roof to see.
303
ascensu supero atque arrectis auribus asto:
and stand with ears pricked up:
What this line does
Arrectis auribus — ears pricked up — is an animal image applied to Aeneas. Like a frightened animal, every sense straining. Then asto: I stand. The colon holds the moment before comprehension.
304
in segetem veluti cum flamma furentibus Austris
just as when flame falls upon a cornfield with the south winds raging,
What this line does
Veluti cum — the flag for an epic simile. Virgil enters a double comparison borrowed from Homer. Fire in a cornfield with raging south winds: natural, unstoppable force destroying human cultivation.
305
incidit, aut rapidus montano flumine torrens
or a swift mountain torrent fed by a stream
What this line does
The second simile begins: a mountain torrent. Two images, not one. The doubling is epic convention but also deliberate: one image is not enough for Troy.
306
sternit agros, sternit sata laeta boumque labores
levels the fields, levels the thriving crops and the labours of the oxen
What this line does
Sternit...sternit — anaphora that hammers the relentlessness of destruction. Fields, then crops (laeta — thriving!), then the labours of the oxen. Virgil measures the loss not just in things but in work.
307
praecipitisque trahit silvas; stupet inscius alto
and drags the forests headlong; the shepherd, unknowing, stands amazed on a height
What this line does
The simile ends on the shepherd — stupet inscius — standing amazed, not knowing. This is Virgil's most resonant image: not the destruction itself but a human being who doesn't yet understand what he is hearing.
308
accipiens sonitum saxi de vertice pastor.
catching the sound from the summit of the crag.
What this line does
Pastor — shepherd — is the last word of the comparison. The shepherd hears the sound from the crag. The simile and the reality are identical: hearing without yet seeing.
§3 · Lines 308–317
Troy Burns; Aeneas Acts
308
tum vero manifesta fides Danaumque patescunt
Then indeed the proof is manifest, and the treacheries of the Greeks lie open.
What this line does
Tum vero — Virgil's formula for decisive revelation. The simile resolves into brutal reality. The proof of the Greeks' treachery is now manifest, undeniable. We pivot from the shepherd on his crag to Troy burning.
309
insidiae. iam Deiphobi dedit ampla ruinam
Already the great house of Deiphobus has collapsed, with fire gaining the upper hand;
What this line does
Deiphobus is named — a specific neighbour. The destruction moves from abstract to intimate. Naming the man whose house falls first makes the disaster human-scale and personal.
310
Volcano superante domus, iam proximus ardet
already the next one is burning;
What this line does
Volcano superante — Vulcan (fire) used by metonymy, gaining the upper hand. Proximus ardet — the next house is burning. The spread is systematic, house by house. The fire is winning.
311
Ucalegon; Sigea igni freta lata relucent.
Ucalegon is alight; the broad Sigean straits gleam with fire.
What this line does
Ucalegon — just the name, no verb. Then the sea reflecting Troy's flames: the destruction has reached cosmic scale, visible across the water. The section moves from the intimate (named neighbours) to the immense.
312
exoritur clamorque virum clangorque tubarum.
The shouting of men and the blaring of trumpets rises up.
What this line does
Clamorque virum clangorque tubarum — the double -que construction and the hard consonants make the line clang and clatter. Virgil is making sound through the sounds of his words.
313
arma amens capio; nec sat rationis in armis,
Out of my mind I seize my weapons; nor is there enough reason in the arming,
What this line does
Arma amens capio — the most important line in §3. Amens: out of his mind. Virgil is honest. This is not heroic arming. Then: nec sat rationis in armis — Aeneas himself admits there was no rational basis for it.
314
sed glomerare manum bello et concurrere in arcem
but my spirit burns to mass a band for war and rush to the citadel
What this line does
Glomerare, concurrere — historical infinitives. The grammar strips away the finite verb and gives the actions raw urgency. These are impulses, not plans. The speed of the grammar enacts the frenzy.
315
cum sociis ardent animi; furor iraque mentem
with my companions; fury and rage drive the mind
What this line does
Ardent animi — our spirits burn. The emotional fire mirrors the physical fire of Troy. Furor iraque — the two forces driving the mind. Virgil names them both: frenzy and rage working together.
316
praecipitat, pulchrumque mori succurrit in armis.
headlong, and it comes to mind that dying in arms is beautiful.
What this line does
Pulchrumque mori succurrit in armis — the most resonant line of §3. The Homeric heroic ideal, stated with full force. Dying in battle is beautiful. The impersonal succurrit is crucial: the thought comes to him, he does not choose it.
§4 · Lines 624–637
Troy Sinks in Flames
624
Tum vero omne mihi visum considere in ignis
Then indeed it seemed to me that all of Troy was sinking in flames
What this line does
Tum vero again — the third decisive moment of realisation. Aeneas sees the whole city sinking into fire. The visum est construction returns from §1: the vision is so vast it has the quality of a terrible dream.
625
Ilium et ex imo verti Neptunia Troia:
and Neptune's Troy being overturned from its very foundations:
What this line does
Ex imo verti — overturned from the very foundations. And Neptunia Troia: Neptune built Troy's walls, according to myth. The city built by a god is falling. The irony is deliberate.
626
ac veluti summis antiquam in montibus ornum
just as when farmers press on to uproot an ancient ash-tree on the high mountains,
What this line does
Ac veluti — the epic simile opens. An ancient ash-tree on a high mountain, being felled by farmers with repeated axe-blows. Antiquam: age is the first thing Virgil establishes. The tree's age is Troy's age.
627
cum ferro accisam crebrisque bipennibus instant
hacked at with iron and with repeated double-headed axes,
What this line does
Cum ferro accisam crebrisque bipennibus — the farmers keep at it with repeated double-headed axes. Accisam (hacked at) and crebris (repeated) insist on the sustained, relentless nature of the assault.
628
eruere agricolae certatim illa usque minatur
and she all the while threatens to fall, and with her summit shaken
What this line does
Illa usque minatur — she, the tree, keeps threatening to fall. The feminine pronoun personifies the tree throughout. She resists. The simile's power comes from this resistance.
629
et tremefacta comam concusso vertice nutat,
she trembles as to her foliage and sways — until gradually overcome
What this line does
Tremefacta comam — trembling as to her foliage. Coma (hair) applied to the tree's leaves — the tree has hair. Accusative of respect returns from §1. Concusso vertice: her summit shaken by the blows below.
630
vulneribus donec paulatim evicta supremum
she has groaned for the last time and, torn from the ridges,
What this line does
Paulatim evicta — gradually overcome. Paulatim is the key word of the whole simile. The fall is not sudden but slow, worn down over time. This is how great things are destroyed.
631
congemuit traxitque iugis avulsa ruinam.
dragged down her ruin.
What this line does
Congemuit — gave a deep groan. The tree's death-sound. Trees do not groan — by giving it this sound, Virgil gives the tree a death like a warrior's. The simile closes on ruinam: ruin, the last word before we return to Aeneas.
632
descendo ac ducente deo flammam inter et hostis
I go down, and with a god guiding me, through flame and enemies
What this line does
Descendo ac ducente deo — I go down, with a god guiding me. This is the theological pivot of Book 2 and one of the most important moments in the whole poem. Before: amens, furor. After: divine guidance, divine protection. Rome becomes possible here.
633
dant tela locum flammaeque recedunt.
I make my way: weapons give way, and flames draw back.
What this line does
Dant tela locum flammaeque recedunt — weapons give way and flames draw back. The physical world yields to the divinely guided Aeneas. This is ducente deo made visible: the world literally opens up for him.
634
Atque ubi iam patriae perventum ad limina sedis
And when at last I reached the threshold of my ancestral home
What this line does
Perventum (est) — arrival was made. Impersonal passive of a verb of motion: no personal subject. A likely exam target. He has arrived at the threshold of his ancestral home — the limina, the charged boundary space.
635
antiquasque domos, genitor, quem tollere in altos
and the ancient halls — my father, whom above all I hoped to carry
What this line does
Genitor — the elevated word for father. Aeneas has arrived at his father's house and his father is the first and absolute priority. The repeated primum (635-636) enacts this priority: above all else, his father.
636
optabam primum montis primumque petebam,
first to the mountains and first sought out —
What this line does
Optabam primum montis primumque petebam — first I wished, and first I sought. The anaphora of primum insists on the absolute priority of his father's rescue. This is pietas — duty to family — in action.
637
abnegat excisa vitam producere Troia
refuses to prolong his life with Troy destroyed.
What this line does
Abnegat — refuses. One word. Absolute. After all of Aeneas's effort, his father refuses to leave. The emotional crisis of the next section begins here, in the bluntness of this single verb.
