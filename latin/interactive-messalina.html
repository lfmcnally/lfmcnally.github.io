Section 1: Lines 1-7

Messalina novo et quasi insano amore incensa est. nam in C. Silium, iuventutis Romanae pulcherrimum, ita exarserat ut Iuniam Silanam, nobilem feminam, matrimonio eius, exturbaret liberoque adultero potiretur. neque Silius flagitii aut periculi nescius erat: sed intellexit exitium, si abnueret, fore certum et, si consentiret, nonnullam facinoris celandi spem esse; simulque se magna praemia accepturum. igitur placuit neglegere futura praesentibus frui.
Section 1 Translation: Messalina was inflamed with a new and almost insane love. For she had burned with love for C. Silius, the most handsome of the Roman youth, to such an extent that she drove Junia Silana, a noble woman, from her marriage, and got possession of an unrestricted adulterer. Silius was not unaware of the disgrace or the danger: but he realised that ruin would be certain if he rejected her, and if he agreed, there was some hope of concealing the crime; and at the same time he would receive great rewards. Therefore he decided to ignore future events and enjoy the present circumstances.

Section 2: Lines 8-17

illa non furtim sed multis cum comitibus ventitat domum, egredienti adhaeret, dat opes honoresque: postremo servi, liberti, paratus principis apud adulterum saepe videbantur. at Claudius matrimonii sui ignarus. iam Messalina propter facilitate adulteriorum ad novas libidines versa est. Silius, sive fatali insania an ipsa pericula remedium imminentium periculorum ratus, abrumpi dissimultationem urgebat: quippe non exspectandum, dum princeps senesceret.
Section 2 Translation: She visited the house frequently, not secretly, but with many companions, clung to him when he went out, gave him honours and gifts: finally, slaves, freedmen and furnishings of the emperor's were seen often at the house of the adulterer. But Claudius was unaware of the state of his own marriage. Now Messalina, because of the easiness of the adulteries, turned to unusual lusts. Silius, whether because of his fateful folly or thinking that the very dangers were a cure for the imminent dangers, pressed for the concealment to be cast aside. Certainly, they should not wait whilst the emperor grows old.

Section 3: Lines 17-26

se caelibem, orbum, nuptiis et adoptando Britannico paratum. eandem Messalinae potentiam mansuram esse, addita securitate, si praevenirent Claudium, qui insidiis incautus sed ad iram celer esset. Messalina, non amore in maritum, sed verita ne Silius summa adeptus se sperneret, diu haesitavit; sed tandem persuasum. nomen enim matrimonii concupivit ob magnitudinem infamiae. nec ultra morata quam dum sacrificii gratia Claudius Ostia proficisceretur, cuncta nuptiarum sollemnia celebrat.
Section 3 Translation: He was single, childless, ready for marriage, and adopting Britannicus. For Messalina the same power would remain with added security if they forestalled Claudius, who was oblivious to treachery but swift to anger. Messalina hesitated for a long time, not because of love for her husband, but fearing that Silius, if he obtained supreme power, might reject her; but finally she was persuaded. For she craved the title of marriage because of the magnitude of the disgrace. Not delaying any further than until Claudius set out for Ostia for a sacrifice, she celebrated all the rites of marriage.

Section 4: Lines 27-38

igitur domus principis inhorruit, maximeque ei qui potentiam habebant timuereunt ne res verterentur: spem tamen habebant, si Claudio de atrocitate sceleris persuasissent, Messalinam posse opprimi sine quaestione damnatam; sed periculum esse ne ille defensionem audiret, neve clausae aures etiam confitenti non essent. Narcissus, occasiones quarenes, cum Caesar diu apud Ostiam moraretur, duas eius paelices largitione et promissis perpulit delationem subrire. exim Calpurnia (id alteri paelici nomen), ubi datum est secretum, ad genua Caesaris provoluta nupsisse Messalinam Silio exclamat; altera paelice haec confirmante, Calpurnia cieri Narcissum postulat.
Section 4 Translation: Therefore the emperor's household shuddered, and particularly those who held any power feared lest the state be overturned. Nevertheless they had some hope, that if they convinced Claudius about the enormity of the crime, Messalina could be condemned without a trial and overthrown. But there was a danger that Claudius might listen to her defence or that his ears might not be closed to her, even if she confessed. Narcissus, looking for opportunities, since Caesar was delaying for a long time at Ostia, compelled two of his concubines by means of largesse and promises to submit an accusation. Then Calpurnia (that was the name of one of the two concubines), when she was given a secret audience, fell down at the knees of Caesar, shouted that Messalina had married Silius; when the other concubine confirmed this, Calpurnia demanded that Narcissus be summoned.

Section 5: Lines 39-49

Qui 'discidiumne tuum' inquit 'novisti? Nam matrimonium Silii vidit populus et senatus et milites; ac nisi celeriter agis, tenet urbem maritus.' non solum rumor interea, sed undique nuntii ad Messalinam contendunt, qui Claudium omnia cognovisse et venire promptum ultioni adferrent. igitur Messalina Lucullianos in hortos, Silius dissimulando metui ad forum digrediuntur. Illa tamen, quamquam res adversae consilium eximerent, ire obviam et aspici a marito statim constituit, quod saepe ei fuerat subsidium. misitque ut Britannicus et Octavia in complexum patris irent.
Section 5 Translation: He said 'Do you know about your divorce? For the people, the senate and the soldiers have seen Silius' marriage. And unless you act quickly, the husband will control the city.' Meanwhile, there was not only rumour, but from all sides messengers were hastening to Messalina to report that Claudius knew everything and was coming eager for revenge. Therefore, Messalina and Silius departed in different directions, she to the gardens of Lucullus, he to the forum to hide his fear. She, however, although unfavourable circumstances were taking away time for deliberation, immediately decided to meet and be seen by her husband, which had often been a help to her. And she sent instructions that Britannicus and Octavia should go to embrace their father.

Messalina
The empress Messalina
Character Introduction
Opening with her name immediately establishes her as the focus and sets up her agency in the narrative.
Effect:
Tacitus places Messalina at the center of the action from the very first word, emphasizing her role as the driving force behind the scandal.
novo et quasi insano amore
"with a new and almost insane love"
Alliteration + Hyperbole
The repetition of 'n' sounds (novo...insano) creates alliteration, while "quasi insano" uses hyperbole to emphasize the extremity of her passion.
Effect:
The alliteration makes the phrase memorable and rhythmic, while the hyperbole immediately signals that this is not ordinary love but dangerous obsession that will lead to disaster.
incensa est
"was inflamed/set on fire"
Metaphor
Fire imagery suggests passion that burns out of control and destroys everything in its path.
Effect:
The fire metaphor foreshadows destruction and suggests Messalina's passion is dangerous and consuming, setting the tone for the tragic events to follow.
C. Silium
"Gaius Silius"
Historical Figure
Gaius Silius was a real Roman politician and the object of Messalina's obsession.
Effect:
Using his full formal name (praenomen + nomen) gives gravity and historical authenticity to the account.
iuventutis Romanae pulcherrimum
"the most handsome of Roman youth"
Superlative
"pulcherrimum" is the superlative form of "pulcher" (beautiful/handsome).
Effect:
The superlative emphasizes Silius's exceptional attractiveness, explaining why Messalina became so obsessed and making her actions seem more understandable (though not excusable).
exarserat
"she had burned with love"
Pluperfect + Fire Imagery
Continues the fire metaphor from "incensa" - this burning had already occurred and intensified.
Effect:
The pluperfect tense shows this passion had been building over time, while the fire imagery reinforces the dangerous, consuming nature of her obsession.
ut...exturbaret
"so that she drove out..."
Result Clause
"ut" + imperfect subjunctive shows the consequence of her burning passion.
Effect:
The result clause emphasizes how her obsession led directly to destructive actions - she destroyed another woman's marriage to get what she wanted.
liberoque adultero potiretur
"and take possession of an unrestricted adulterer"
Alliteration
The 'p' sounds in "potiretur" connect with "pulcherrimum" earlier, creating sonic links.
Effect:
"libero adultero" is almost oxymoronic - a "free adulterer" suggests someone available for her exclusive use, showing her desire to possess him completely.
Silius flagitii aut periculi nescius erat
"Silius was not unaware of the disgrace or danger"
Litotes
"neque...nescius" (not unaware) is a double negative emphasizing that he fully understood the risks.
Effect:
This shows Silius was not a naive victim but made a calculated decision despite knowing the dangers, making him complicit in the scandal.
intellexit
"he realized"
Indirect Statement Introduction
Introduces the complex indirect statement showing his reasoning process.
Effect:
Takes us inside Silius's mind to show his calculating, rational analysis of an impossible situation.
exitium, si abnueret, fore certum
"that ruin, if he refused, would be certain"
Conditional Clause + Hyperbaton
"si abnueret" is embedded within the main clause, while "certum" emphasizes the certainty of destruction.
Effect:
The embedded conditional and strong "certum" show he had no real choice - refusal meant certain death.
si consentiret, nonnullam facinoris celandi spem esse
"if he agreed, there was some hope of concealing the crime"
Contrast + Euphemism
Contrasts with the certainty of death; "nonnullam...spem" (some hope) is deliberately vague.
Effect:
The contrast between "certain ruin" and "some hope" shows his impossible dilemma - death vs. a slim chance of survival through compliance.
magna praemia accepturum
"he would receive great rewards"
Material Motivation
Shows he wasn't just motivated by fear but also by greed and ambition.
Effect:
Reveals Silius's character - he's not just a victim but someone willing to profit from a dangerous liaison with the emperor's wife.
neglegere futura praesentibus frui
"to ignore future events and enjoy present circumstances"
Balanced Infinitives + Antithesis
Two infinitives balance each other; "futura" vs "praesentibus" creates antithesis between future and present.
Effect:
The balanced structure mirrors his balanced decision, while the antithesis shows his choice to live for the moment despite future consequences - a fatally shortsighted decision.
illa non furtim sed multis cum comitibus ventitat domum
"she visited the house frequently, not secretly, but with many companions"
Antithesis + Frequentative Verb
"non furtim sed" creates stark contrast, whilst "ventitat" (frequentative of venio) emphasises repeated visits.
Effect:
Shows Messalina's brazen confidence and escalating recklessness - she's no longer trying to hide the affair.
egredienti adhaeret
"clung to him when he went out"
Physical Metaphor
"adhaeret" (sticks to/clings to) suggests obsessive, possessive behaviour.
Effect:
Creates an image of desperate, clingy behaviour that would be inappropriate for an empress in public.
dat opes honoresque
"gave him honours and gifts"
Zeugma + Climax
Single verb "dat" governs both nouns, building to the most shocking revelation about imperial property.
Effect:
Shows how she's abusing her imperial position to shower her lover with state resources.
servi, liberti, paratus principis apud adulterum saepe videbantur
"slaves, freedmen, and imperial furnishings were often seen at the adulterer's house"
Tricolon + Metonymy
Three groups escalating in significance; "paratus" represents all imperial luxury.
Effect:
The climactic "paratus principis" shows she's essentially moving the imperial household to her lover's house - the ultimate scandal.
Claudius matrimonii sui ignarus
"Claudius was unaware of his own marriage"
Dramatic Irony
Claudius goes to perform religious duties whilst his wife commits the ultimate sacrilege.
Effect:
The contrast between Claudius's piety and Messalina's impiety heightens the dramatic tension.
cuncta nuptiarum sollemnia celebrat
"she celebrated all the rites of marriage"
Totality + Religious Language
"cuncta" emphasises completeness; "sollemnia" refers to sacred rites.
Effect:
Shows this wasn't a secret ceremony - it was a full, public wedding, making the bigamy unmistakable.
igitur domus principis inhorruit
"therefore the emperor's household shuddered"
Personification + Onomatopoeia
The house itself "shudders"; "inhorruit" suggests both fear and physical trembling.
Effect:
Creates a vivid image of terror spreading through the palace as news of the marriage spreads.
maximeque ei qui potentiam habebant timuereunt ne res verterentur
"and particularly those who held power feared lest the state be overturned"
Fear Clause + Political Metaphor
"ne...verterentur" expresses their fear; "res" can mean both "things" and "the state".
Effect:
Shows this isn't just a marital scandal - it threatens the entire political order.
spem tamen habebant, si Claudio de atrocitate sceleris persuasissent, Messalinam posse opprimi sine quaestione damnatam
"nevertheless they had hope, that if they convinced Claudius about the enormity of the crime, Messalina could be condemned without a trial and overthrown"
Conditional Hope + Legal Language
Complex conditional structure; "sine quaestione" refers to summary execution.
Effect:
Shows they're planning Messalina's immediate execution without due process - the crime is so clear.
periculum esse ne ille defensionem audiret, neve clausae aures etiam confitenti non essent
"there was a danger that he might listen to her defence or that his ears might not be closed to her, even if she confessed"
Double Negative + Metaphor
"neve...non" creates complex negation; ears "closed" suggests deliberate deafness.
Effect:
Shows they fear Claudius's weakness for Messalina might overcome even clear evidence of her guilt.
Narcissus, occasiones quarenes
"Narcissus, looking for opportunities"
Character Introduction
Shows Narcissus as actively seeking the right moment to act.
Effect:
Establishes Narcissus as the calculating mastermind behind Messalina's downfall.
Caesar diu apud Ostiam moraretur
"Caesar was delaying for a long time at Ostia"
Temporal Clause
"diu" emphasises the extended time, giving Narcissus opportunity to plan.
Effect:
Creates tension - time is running out whilst Claudius remains unaware and distant.
duas eius paelices largitione et promissis perpulit delationem subrire
"compelled two of his concubines by means of largesse and promises to submit an accusation"
Corruption + Legal Term
Shows bribery; "delationem" is the technical term for formal accusation.
Effect:
Reveals the complex web of palace intrigue - even Claudius's lovers are pawns in the game.
Calpurnia (id alteri paelici nomen), ubi datum est secretum, ad genua Caesaris provoluta nupsisse Messalinam Silio exclamat
"Calpurnia (that was the name of one of the two concubines), when she was given a secret audience, fell down at the knees of Caesar, shouted that Messalina had married Silius"
Dramatic Scene + Parenthesis
Vivid physical description; parenthetical identification adds realism.
Effect:
Creates a dramatic tableau of supplication and revelation - the moment truth breaks through.
Calpurnia cieri Narcissum postulat
"Calpurnia demanded that Narcissus be summoned"
Passive Infinitive
"cieri" shows formal summoning to give evidence.
Effect:
Shows Calpurnia following Narcissus's script - she knows to call in the main witness.
'discidiumne tuum' inquit 'novisti?
"'Do you know about your divorce?' he said"
Rhetorical Question + Dramatic Irony
Opens with shocking news phrased as a question - Claudius doesn't even know he's divorced.
Effect:
Maximises the shock value - Narcissus reveals Claudius has been unknowingly divorced by his wife's remarriage.
Nam matrimonium Silii vidit populus et senatus et milites
"For the people, the senate and the soldiers have seen Silius's marriage"
Tricolon + Public Witnesses
Three groups representing all Roman society - everyone knows except Claudius.
Effect:
Emphasises Claudius's complete humiliation - the entire state has witnessed his wife's bigamy.
ac nisi celeriter agis, tenet urbem maritus
"and unless you act quickly, the husband will control the city"
Urgency + Political Threat
"celeriter" emphasises speed; "tenet urbem" suggests political control.
Effect:
Makes clear this is about political survival - Silius could seize power through his marriage.
non solum rumor interea, sed undique nuntii ad Messalinam contendunt
"meanwhile, there was not only rumour, but from all sides messengers were hastening to Messalina"
Escalation + Spatial Imagery
"non solum...sed" creates escalation; "undique" suggests messages from everywhere.
Effect:
Shows how quickly news spreads in Rome - Messalina will soon know Claudius has been informed.
Claudium omnia cognovisse et venire promptum ultioni adferrent
"that Claudius knew everything and was coming eager for revenge"
Perfect Infinitive + Characterisation
"cognovisse" shows completed knowledge; "promptum ultioni" reveals his mindset.
Effect:
Creates urgency and fear - Claudius isn't just informed, he's actively seeking vengeance.
igitur Messalina Lucullianos in hortos, Silius dissimulando metui ad forum digrediuntur
"therefore, Messalina and Silius departed in different directions, she to the gardens of Lucullus, he to the forum to hide his fear"
Chiastic Structure + Contrast
Their destinations contrast - she seeks luxury, he tries to maintain normality.
Effect:
Shows their different responses to crisis - she retreats to comfort, he attempts to brazen it out.
Illa tamen, quamquam res adversae consilium eximerent, ire obviam et aspici a marito statim constituit
"She, however, although unfavourable circumstances were taking away time for deliberation, immediately decided to meet and be seen by her husband"
Concessive Clause + Character Insight
"quamquam" introduces the obstacle; shows her quick decision-making under pressure.
Effect:
Reveals Messalina's strategic thinking - she knows her best weapon is her personal influence over Claudius.
quod saepe ei fuerat subsidium
"which had often been a help to her"
Relative Clause + Pattern
"saepe" shows this is her established method of managing Claudius.
Effect:
Suggests Messalina has repeatedly manipulated Claudius through personal appeals - but will it work this time?
misitque ut Britannicus et Octavia in complexum patris irent
"and she sent instructions that Britannicus and Octavia should go to embrace their father"
Emotional Manipulation
Using their children as a weapon to soften Claudius's anger.
Effect:
Shows Messalina's calculating ruthlessness - even her own children become tools in her desperate strategy.

"abrumpi" (to break off/snap) suggests violent, sudden action rather than gradual revelation.

"non exspectandum" shows urgent necessity; "senesceret" euphemistically refers to Claudius's death.

Political Calculation
Shows the marriage is partly about securing the succession through adoption.
Effect:
Reveals this isn't just about passion - it's a political coup involving the imperial succession.

Indirect Statement
Shows Silius's promise that she won't lose power by changing husbands.
Effect:
Reveals Messalina's primary concern is maintaining her power and status, not love.

Conditional + Euphemism
"praevenirent" euphemistically refers to preventing Claudius from acting first.

Contrasts Claudius's naivety with his capacity for sudden, violent retribution.
