Who actually governs?
The rise of Agrippina and the sidelining of Britannicus · AD 50–52
Agrippina Nero Claudius Britannicus Pallas
🏛
The central question running through Annals XII
Claudius holds the title of emperor. Who actually governs?
Tacitus's task — and yours — is to trace where real power sits.
📖
The Big Picture

These chapters tell the story of how Agrippina the Younger manoeuvres her son Domitius — soon renamed Nero — into position to succeed Claudius, at the direct expense of Claudius's biological son, Britannicus.

Agrippina has already achieved the extraordinary by marrying her uncle. Now she engineers Domitius's formal adoption, secures him military honours and public titles, and strips Britannicus of every supporter he has — tutors, freedmen, officers — until he is effectively isolated inside his own household.

Tacitus presents this as a study in how power actually works: accumulated quietly, through marriage, influence, bribery, and personnel management, rather than through any open exercise of force. Claudius is largely passive throughout. Agrippina is the animating intelligence.

💡 Keep asking: who persuades whom, and why? Every persuasion in these chapters conceals someone whose interests it serves.

25
Chapter 25 — The Adoption of Domitius

Agrippina cannot simply ask Claudius to adopt her son — that would expose her ambition too plainly. So she uses Pallas, a powerful imperial freedman who is also her ally, to make the case. Claudius is sensitive about dynastic stability and susceptible to flattering comparisons with great emperors. Pallas exploits both: he frames the adoption as sound constitutional practice following Augustus and Tiberius, not as something Agrippina wants.

That Claudius then addresses the senate in terms that almost exactly mirror Pallas's argument is Tacitus's quiet way of showing the reader something Claudius cannot see: he is not deciding, he is executing someone else's plan. The detail that Domitius is three years older than Britannicus matters too — this installs a rival with a head start, not merely a companion.

26
Chapter 26 — Nero Named, Agrippina Augusta

The formal steps proceed quickly and correctly: senate vote, law, name-change, title. Everything looks legitimate. But Tacitus's choice of quibus patratis — when these things had been done — treats epoch-shifting constitutional acts as mere paperwork. The coolness is deliberate: he refuses to editoralise on the proceedings themselves, which makes the pivot to Britannicus land harder.

The title Augusta had previously been held by Livia — Augustus's wife — and carried quasi-imperial prestige. Agrippina receiving it formalises what has already been true in practice: she is the dominant force in the household. Britannicus, watching his support network shrink while his stepmother's honours multiply, is given just two words by Tacitus: intellegens falsi. He understands the pretence. Tacitus gives him intelligence rather than a speech — and that restraint is what makes the moment affecting.

41a
Chapter 41a — Nero Enters Public Life

The toga virilis was normally given to boys around fourteen or fifteen; granting it early to Nero is a deliberate political acceleration. Tacitus signals the reasoning immediately: quo capessendae rei publicae habilis videretur — so that he might seem ready for public office. One word, videretur, tells you this is about managed perception.

The accumulation of titles and donatives that follows — future consulship, proconsular power, princeps iuventutis, gifts to soldiers and people — is a succession programme dressed as a series of individual honours. The circus scene is its culmination: Nero in triumphal dress, Britannicus in a child's toga. The crowd is not just watching a ceremony; it is being shown a picture of the future and invited to treat it as settled.

41b
Chapter 41b — Britannicus Isolated

Having secured the formal succession through Nero's adoption and public elevation, Agrippina now addresses the informal one: destroying every relationship Britannicus has. The officers and freedmen around him are removed not because they have done anything wrong but because their loyalty to him is itself a threat. The language Tacitus uses — remoti fictis causis, removed on invented grounds — is stated as plain fact, without elaboration. The bluntness is the critique.

When Britannicus calls Nero "Domitius" — refusing to acknowledge the adoption — Agrippina inflates a domestic irritation into a constitutional crisis. She tells Claudius that the decisions of the senate and people are being reversed inside the household. This is a wild exaggeration of a naming incident, and Tacitus marks it as such with quasi crimina: complaints dressed as accusations, without the substance. Claudius responds as he always does — by being persuaded.

42
Chapter 42 — Agrippina Consolidates Power

The Praetorian Guard was the most sensitive military institution in Rome — the force that decided, in practice, who ruled. Replacing its commanders is therefore the most audacious move in the set text, and Agrippina frames it as a matter of military efficiency: two prefects divide loyalty, one would improve discipline. The real reason is that the sitting prefects are associated with Messalina's legacy and therefore potentially loyal to Britannicus. Burrus, their replacement, is competent — but gnarum tamen cuius sponte praeficeretur: well aware who had placed him there.

The carriage privilege to the Capitol — previously reserved for priests — makes her status visually and symbolically imperial. Tacitus then lists her fourfold connection to emperors — daughter, sister, wife, mother — as a plain statement of unprecedented fact. She saves her senate ally Vitellius when he is prosecuted, but does so minis magis quam precibus: by threat rather than appeal. Even her acts of protection are coercive.

43
Chapter 43 — Famine and the Fragile State

This is the only point in your set text where Tacitus leaves the palace entirely, and the shift is deliberate. The chapter opens with bad omens — birds on the Capitol, earthquakes, a crowd trampling its weakest members in panic — and then arrives at something more concretely serious: Rome has fifteen days of grain left. Claudius is surrounded by an angry mob in the Forum and can only escape through a military cordon.

That image of the emperor fleeing his own people is important. The palace chapters have been about power accumulated quietly through ceremony, adoption, and personnel management. Chapter 43 shows what all that power-play has failed to address: the city cannot feed itself. The juxtaposition is Tacitus's implicit argument.

He then steps back further still. Italy once supplied Rome's armies on campaign across the empire; now Rome doesn't even farm efficiently, dependent entirely on Africa and Egypt. The sentence navibusque et casibus vita populi Romani permissa est — the life of the Roman people has been entrusted to ships and to chance — is a structural critique of the Principate, not just a comment on a bad harvest. The word casibus, accidents, placed next to navibusque, ships, suggests that human technology offers no protection against randomness. The system is fragile in a way no emperor can fix.

52
Chapter 52 — Scribonianus and atrox et inritum

Chapter 52 is a brief chapter but it contains some of Tacitus's most characteristic writing. Furius Scribonianus is exiled for consulting astrologers about the emperor's fate — the charge is essentially treason by implication, since wanting to know when an emperor will die is a way of planning for succession without authorisation. His mother Vibia is implicated. Claudius is then praised for sparing them, given that Scribonianus's father had once actually led a rebellion against Rome. Genuine clemency, and Tacitus acknowledges it as such.

The next sentence records that Scribonianus died in exile shortly afterwards, quickly, seu casu seu veneno — by accident or by poison — and Tacitus adds: ut quisque credidit, vulgavere, people spread the news as each chose to believe. He refuses to adjudicate. This is not laziness — it is a technique. In imperial Rome, the truth about a convenient death is always deniable, and Tacitus distributes the uncertainty across the populace to show that everyone knows the ambiguity and no one can resolve it.

The chapter ends with the senate passing a decree expelling astrologers from Italy: atrox et inritum. Fierce and pointless. Astrologers had been expelled from Rome repeatedly across the Principate and had always returned; everyone knew the decree was unenforceable. The two adjectives placed directly together — without explanation, without apology — compress the entire institutional absurdity into a single phrase. The senate can pass severe legislation; it cannot make it work.

⚠️ The key political fact
Agrippina is simultaneously Claudius's niece and his wife — a marriage so unusual that Rome had to pass a special decree permitting uncle-niece unions. Her son Nero therefore becomes, through adoption, both Claudius's stepson and adopted heir, displacing his biological son Britannicus.
🔑 Why this matters for Annals XII
The competition between Nero and Britannicus is constitutional. Whichever boy the emperor publicly favours, whichever accrues the right titles and profile, will succeed him. Agrippina's programme in these chapters is about making the answer to that question obvious and irreversible before Claudius dies.
