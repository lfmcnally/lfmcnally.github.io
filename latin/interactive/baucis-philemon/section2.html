Baucis and Philemon: Welcoming the Gods

📕 Ovid Metamorphoses 8.637-650 ⏱️ 40 min 📊 GCSE Latin

Learning Objectives

In this lesson, you will read how the elderly couple Baucis and Philemon welcome disguised gods into their humble home. You'll explore Ovid's poetic techniques, learn key vocabulary about hospitality and daily life, and understand how this story illustrates Roman values.

The Story So Far

Jupiter and Mercury have disguised themselves as mortal travellers to test human hospitality. They've knocked on a thousand doors in a Phrygian town, but every household has turned them away. Finally, they arrive at the tiny cottage of Baucis and Philemon, an elderly couple who, despite their poverty, welcome the strangers warmly.

This passage shows the moment the gods enter the humble dwelling and Baucis begins preparing a meal for them, demonstrating the ideal of Roman hospitalitas (hospitality).

Why This Passage Matters
This is one of Ovid's most charming stories, showing that virtue and kindness matter more than wealth. The detailed description of domestic life gives us a vivid picture of how ordinary Romans lived, whilst the poetic style demonstrates Ovid's genius for storytelling.

Historical and Cultural Context

Roman Hospitality: The Romans took hospitality (hospitalitas) very seriously. Turning away a traveller was considered deeply shameful, especially since you never knew if a stranger might be a god in disguise (as in this story!).

Humble Living: Ovid's detailed description of the cottage—with its thatched roof, single room, and simple furniture—shows us how poor rural Romans lived. The contrast between their poverty and their generosity makes the story more powerful.

Fire and Hearth: The hearth fire was the centre of Roman domestic life. It was sacred to Vesta, goddess of the hearth. Keeping the fire going (as Baucis does here) was an important daily task.

Ovid's Metamorphoses: This story comes from Book 8 of Ovid's Metamorphoses, a collection of myths about transformation. Written around 8 CE, it became one of the most influential works in Western literature.

The Moral of the Story
This tale teaches that the gods reward genuine kindness regardless of wealth. When the rest of the town is destroyed by flood for their inhospitality, Baucis and Philemon are saved and granted their wish to die together. Their cottage is transformed into a temple, and they become trees that grow intertwined.
