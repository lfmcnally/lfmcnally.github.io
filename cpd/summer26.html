Answer to Q1 · Worth lingering on

Two ways to handle a mixed class.

Differentiation is a sorting exercise.

Based on prior attainment and perception, pupils are sent toward different goals with different tasks. Decisions are fixed in advance. Expectations diverge. Bias is baked in.

Adaptive teaching is a scaffolding exercise.

Everyone aims at the same challenging goal. Teaching responds, in the moment, to evidence of what pupils need. Groupings are flexible and temporary. Expectations stay high for all.

Step One · Before the lesson

Prepare & resource responses before the lesson.

Cognitive load in the moment is incredibly high. If you want a real chance of responding well, you need the responses already loaded.

The "if... then..." structure

If most pupils misunderstand, then I'll need a different, more accessible explanation.
If understanding is not quite secure, then I'll need additional practice tasks.
If understanding is strong, then I'll need to extend and deepen.

What "resourcing" looks like

Prepare these in advance so they're ready when needed:

  • Examples and non-examples
  • Analogies and alternative visuals
  • Worked examples, partially completed
  • Practice questions at graded difficulty
  • Simpler alternative explanations
  • Scaffolds, prompts, chunking
  • Back-pocket questions and 'thunks'
Step Two · In the lesson

Check for understanding — all pupils, at once.

A single pupil answering in a discussion tells you about one pupil. A hinge question tells you about the whole room.

The non-negotiables of a hinge

  • Stimulus carefully crafted against learning intentions
  • Responses taken from all pupils, quickly and simultaneously
  • You can analyse the pattern of responses and act on it

See the Clifton bespoke Walkthru "Implement Formative Hinges: Elicit Evidence of Progress" for full detail.

What you're looking for

One of three patterns will emerge:

Pattern A · all or most misunderstand
Pattern B · varied understanding
Pattern C · all or most understand
Step Three · Response A

All or most misunderstand.

This almost certainly means instruction has been ineffective. Gaps must be filled and misconceptions must be overwritten. Move on and you'll teach on sand.

Re-explain

Different, more accessible:

  • Simpler terms
  • Remove redundant or superfluous information
  • Additional examples and non-examples
  • Analogies
  • Additional or alternative visuals

Re-model

  • Live model, narrating your thinking
  • Use a worked example
  • Chunk the process — model each chunk, practise, then move on

The goal is not to cover content. The goal is to replace the faulty schema with a correct one.

Step Three · Response B

Varied understanding across the class.

Some are there, some are not. This is the most common pattern, and the most pedagogically rich. Three tools: support, rehearsal, peers.

Support
Scaffolding
Worked examples, partially completed examples, criteria, sentence starters, graphic organisers. Directs attention and relieves working memory.
Rehearsal
More practice
Similar contexts, variations on a theme. Pupils may simply need more reps to embed the process and link it to existing knowledge.
Peers
Flexible grouping
Temporary groupings based on evidence. Mixed pairs for peer tutoring. Same-level groups for targeted support or rehearsal.
Step Three · Response C

All or most understand.

Don't waste the opportunity. You have a choice — and "move on" is only one of them.

Move on

Understanding is secure. Introduce and explain the next concept or topic.

Legitimate. But consider whether "cover more" is always the best use of the time you've just earned.

Deepen, extend, transfer

  • Back-pocket questions or 'thunks'
  • Elaborative interrogation
  • Pupils generate and explain misconceptions
  • Rehearsal for transfer (new or unfamiliar contexts)

This also frees you to provide 1:1 support to any pupil who doesn't yet understand.

A worked example · before you start Task 4

What "resourcing a response" actually looks like.

One hinge question. Three if-thens. One of them resourced properly. Here's mine, from last term.

Context

Year 9 Latin · ablative case.

Pupils have met the ablative for time when and means / instrument. Today's lesson introduces place where (in / sub + abl.).

My hinge, with misconceptions pre-thought for each distractor:

In villā magnā habitat.
Why is villā in the ablative?
  • A time when → hasn't attended to in; defaulting to most recent use taught.
  • B means / instrument → ignoring the preposition; sees any ablative noun as "by/with".
  • C place where ✓ → recognises in + abl. as locative function.
  • D subject → case confusion; treating ablative singular -ā as nominative first declension.
All/most misunderstand
If most pick B (means) because that's what they've done all term, then I re-teach via contrast: two sentences side by side, one genuine means, one place where. Resourced: pre-made slide with sagittā pugnat / in silvā pugnat, matching visuals, the question "what does the preposition tell you?"
Varied understanding
If the class is split roughly half right, half wrong, then pair secure pupils with insecure pupils; each pair takes three English sentences and identifies which Latin ablative use each would need. Resourced: paired worksheet (6 sentences), sentence-starter scaffold on the back for the insecure partner.
All/most understand
If they're there, then I extend into a fourth use (ablative of manner) and have them hypothesise what the pattern is, before I name it. Resourced: back-pocket slide with three examples of manner usage, no English gloss, the prompt "what do these three have in common?"
Task Four

Individual planning

~10 min

Now it's your turn. Take the hinge question you brought with you.

Step One
Write your three if... then... statements
One for each pattern: all misunderstand, varied, all understand. Be specific to your hinge question.
Step Two
Choose one
Pick the statement that feels most useful or most urgent for your next lesson with that class.
Step Three
Resource it properly
A more accessible explanation. A narrated worked example. Alternative visuals. A set of practice tasks for encoding. Back-pocket questions. Whatever the "then" demands.

By the end of this task

You walk out with one hinge question, three if-then statements, and one resourced response, ready to use in a real lesson.

CPD booklet is on your table for reference. Use the worked example from the previous slide as a model if you need one.

If you finish early — resource a second one.

Common challenges from the floor

Click a question. Esc or × to return to the deck.

Isn't this just differentiation rebranded?
+
No — and that's the whole point of slide five. Differentiation sets different goals for different pupils based on perception or prior attainment. Adaptive teaching keeps the goal the same for everyone and adjusts the support dynamically, based on real-time evidence. One sorts pupils; the other scaffolds them. The language is deliberately distinct in the literature (Pearce, Mould, EEF) precisely because differentiation baked in bias and low expectations for pupils deemed "less able".
Doesn't all this prep massively increase my workload?
+
Upfront, yes — a bit. But two things make it manageable. First, the resources you prepare are reusable: a good worked example for introducing the ablative works every year, not just once. Second, the alternative is higher cost: re-teaching whole topics, dealing with cemented misconceptions, pupils lost by year 11. Preparing a second explanation and a back-pocket question is the workload reduction, paid upfront. You don't need all three responses for every lesson — just for the critical-point hinge you've identified.
What if my "if-then" prep doesn't match what actually happens?
+
That's fine — and actually quite normal, especially at first. The value of the prep isn't that you'll always use what you've made. It's that the act of preparing makes you more responsive: you've already thought about the possible misconceptions, so you recognise them faster when they appear. Over time, your predictions get better. And if a pattern emerges that you hadn't planned for, you've got the headspace to improvise because the lower-cognitive-load scenarios are already handled.
How is a hinge question different from just asking the class?
+
Three things. All pupils answer, not just the keen ones or the ones you call on. Simultaneously, so no pupil is influenced by another's answer. And the responses are analysable — you can see the pattern in the room, not just the mode. Mini-whiteboards, ABCD cards, Plickers, or a well-designed multiple-choice on Forms all work. "Does everyone understand?" is not a hinge question — it's a hope.
Is this realistic for a full timetable?
+
You don't responsively teach every moment of every lesson — you responsively teach at critical points. One well-chosen hinge per lesson, sometimes one every few lessons, is the ambition. The Walkthru is not saying "do all of this all the time"; it's saying "when you do check understanding, have the response ready." Most teachers find that once the habit is in, it saves time elsewhere because they stop teaching into a void.
Doesn't "same goal for all" ignore SEND needs?
+
The opposite. Kirsten Mould — SEND specialist — places adaptive teaching at the heart of high-quality teaching for all, precisely because it keeps expectations high for SEND pupils and adjusts the route, not the destination. Specific EHCP provision sits alongside this, of course. The old model of "simpler task for the SEND pupil" was well-intentioned but corrosive: it cemented a ceiling. Adaptive teaching is about removing the ceiling while thickening the floor.
Why can't I just use prior attainment data?
+
Prior attainment is useful context but a poor predictor of moment-to-moment understanding. A pupil who aced the last topic may be completely lost in this one; a pupil who struggled last term may be thriving. Decisions based on live evidence outperform decisions based on stored assumptions — and they're less vulnerable to bias. Prior attainment tells you where someone has been, not where they are.
Isn't moving on when they understand sometimes the right call?
+
Absolutely, yes. "Move on" is a legitimate Response 3c — it's listed first in the Walkthru for a reason. The point of the slide is just that it's a choice, not the only choice. If you've got time and you've got understanding, there's real value in using that space for transfer and depth. But if curriculum pressure means moving on is right today, that's a responsive decision too.
What if I freeze in the moment and can't pick a response?
+
This is why step one exists. The prep is the antidote to the freeze. You're not trying to improvise a whole pedagogical strategy in real time — you're picking between 2-3 pre-loaded options. And if you do freeze, a perfectly legitimate move is to buy time: "right, some of you have this and some don't — take two minutes to explain your thinking to your partner," which also gives you peer-tutoring coverage while you decide. Freezing becomes rarer with practice, and in the meantime, having any "if-then" ready is better than none.
How does this differ from Wiliam's formative assessment work?
+
It builds directly on it. Wiliam's five strategies are the foundation — particularly "engineering effective classroom discussions" and "providing feedback that moves learning forward." The Walkthru you're using today is really the "what do I do with the evidence" half of Wiliam's framework, sharpened into a planning and in-lesson tool. Wiliam himself has moved in this direction, advocating that both teachers and learners make the responsive adjustments — which is where Corno's self-regulation endpoint comes in.
Trickier challenges
Isn't this just another CPD fad that will be gone in two years?
+
Fair challenge — we've all sat through sessions on strategies that didn't survive the decade. The honest answer is that the label "adaptive teaching" is recent, but the underlying idea — check understanding, adjust accordingly — is as old as teaching itself. Socratic questioning, Vygotsky's ZPD, Rosenshine's checking for student understanding, Wiliam's formative assessment — this is all one continuous lineage. What's new is the attempt to make it concrete and teachable through Walkthrus. If the vocabulary changes in five years, the practice underneath it won't.
I've been teaching for twenty years. Isn't this what good teachers already do?
+
Yes — and that's exactly why we're doing it. Experienced teachers do this intuitively, which is brilliant for them but terrible for the profession, because intuition can't be transferred to ECTs or shared across a department. The point of codifying it isn't to patronise people who already do it well. It's so that the next generation of teachers have the vocabulary and structure to learn faster, and so that what the best teachers do implicitly becomes something we can all do deliberately. If a session feels like it's teaching you to suck eggs, then it's working — it means you recognise good practice when you see it.
My subject doesn't have a clean hinge question — how does this apply to me?
+
This is the most legitimate subject-specific challenge, and the honest answer varies by subject. In English, the hinge might be a short written response that surfaces a misconception about a text's meaning. In Art, it might be a structured peer critique using specific criteria. In PE, it might be a modelled skill where you observe form in real time. The principle isn't "use a multiple-choice" — it's elicit evidence from all pupils, quickly, in a way you can analyse. The MCQ hinge is just the cleanest example. If your subject resists it, your department is the best place to work out the equivalent. Worth a cross-departmental conversation — I'd be interested to compare with Art and Drama especially.
This feels very behaviourist — what about deeper learning?
+
Understandable concern. The Walkthru framing can make it sound reductive — check understanding, deploy response, done. But deep learning and responsive teaching aren't in tension; they're interdependent. You can't foster genuine depth if half your class has a fundamental misconception you haven't noticed. Response 3c — extending and deepening when understanding is secure — is where the richer pedagogy happens: elaborative interrogation, transfer to unfamiliar contexts, generating misconceptions. Responsive teaching clears the ground; depth is what you build on it.
Doesn't this reduce teaching to delivering algorithms?
+
The Walkthru looks algorithmic — if X then Y — but the craft sits in the quality of each response, not the logic tree. A weak teacher with a perfect "if-then" structure still delivers weak teaching. The framework gives you a place to put your pedagogical judgement, but it doesn't replace it. Choosing which analogy, which worked example, which back-pocket question — that's where expertise lives, and no framework will ever touch it. Think of it less as an algorithm, more as a scaffold for professional decision-making.
What's the evidence base that this actually works?
+
Honest answer: the specific term "responsive teaching" is relatively new (Fletcher-Wood, 2018) so there's no 30-year longitudinal study on it as a labelled practice. But the components are some of the most evidenced moves in education: formative assessment (Hattie effect size ~0.7), feedback (~0.7), scaffolding, worked examples, Rosenshine's principles. The EEF's guidance on adaptive teaching draws on this same body of work. What's less well-evidenced is the specific Walkthru format as packaging — but then, that's just a delivery mechanism for practices that are independently well-supported.
What about the pupil who pretends to understand in the hinge?
+
Real issue. Hinges are harder to fake than hands-up questioning but not foolproof — a well-designed multiple-choice can still be guessed. Three mitigations. Design your distractors carefully: a good hinge has wrong answers that map to specific misconceptions, so the choice reveals the type of error even if the pupil gets it right. Use multiple hinges across a topic, not just one; a pupil who bluffs one will usually slip on another. And follow up individually with pupils whose responses don't match their usual pattern. The hinge isn't a polygraph; it's a fast, imperfect signal that beats hoping for the best.
How do I do this with a class of 30 on a Friday afternoon?
+
Pragmatically. Friday afternoon, year 9, damp classroom — nobody's delivering platinum-standard responsive teaching in that slot, and pretending otherwise is how CPD loses credibility. The realistic ambition is: one hinge, one prepared response, pitched at whatever you think is most likely to be needed. Even that is more than nothing. The framework is a ceiling to aim at across a half-term of lessons, not a floor you hit every period. Be kind to yourself about which lessons you bring the full toolkit to.
What if the whole department isn't on board with this?
+
Responsive teaching works at the individual classroom level — you can do it in your room without your colleagues doing it in theirs, and your pupils will benefit. That said, it becomes more powerful when a department shares hinge questions and resources, so start small: one colleague, one topic, one shared resource. If you can point to pupils in your class doing better on a given misconception, the culture shifts. Evidence beats advocacy for persuading reluctant colleagues — and persuading the whole department isn't your responsibility in a 45-minute INSET. Tend your own garden first.
Does this approach work for pupils with significant SEMH needs or trauma?
+
Responsive teaching is about academic response, but the same principle — observe, adjust, respond in the moment — is core to trauma-informed practice. For SEMH pupils, your "if-then" thinking might sometimes be non-academic: "if this pupil is dysregulated, then this isn't the moment for a hinge question; then my response is regulation first." The Walkthru is built around academic understanding, but the responsive disposition extends naturally. Complex SEMH needs warrant specialist input beyond this framework, and our SENDCo and pastoral teams should always be in the loop.
Why are we doing this when we should be talking about [other priority]?
+
The honest response is that pedagogy, pastoral care, wellbeing, workload, behaviour, assessment — these all deserve attention, and no single INSET can cover all of them. This strand has been this year's focus because we chose one of the three pillars of adaptive teaching per term, which gave us coherence across the year. If this feels like the wrong priority given what's happening in your corridor right now, that's worth raising separately — but it doesn't undermine the value of today's content. Good responsive teaching supports every other priority: fewer misconceptions means fewer lost pupils, means fewer behaviour issues, means less marking rework. It's not competing with the other thing you care about; it's upstream of it.
